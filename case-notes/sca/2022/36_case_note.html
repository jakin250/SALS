Supreme Court of Appeal

Biyela v Minister of Police (1017/2020) [2022] ZASCA 36

After successfully claiming R160,000 in damages from the Minister of Police for his unlawful arrest in the Durban Magistrate’s Court, the Minister appealed the decision to the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 36

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

This appeal concerns the unlawful arrest and detention of the appellant, Sandile Biyela. On 18 May 2012, Biyela was arrested without a warrant by members of the South African Police Service (SAPS) for alleged intimidation. Following his arrest, he was held at the Durban Central Police Station and appeared in court on 21 May 2012. The charges against him were later postponed, and he was released ([paras 1-4]).

After successfully claiming R160,000 in damages from the Minister of Police for his unlawful arrest in the Durban Magistrate’s Court, the Minister appealed the decision to the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court. The majority of the court overturned the initial judgment, leading to Biyela’s appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([paras 2-3]).

During the initial proceedings, the Minister’s plea was described as a "bare denial," which resulted in the appellant bearing the burden of proving his wrongful arrest. However, the respondent later amended its plea to acknowledge that Biyela was indeed arrested without a warrant and set forth the circumstances that led to his arrest ([paras 5-7]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue presented in this case is whether the arrest and detention of Biyela by SAPS members were lawful. This question encompasses two key sub-issues: (1) whether the officers had reasonable suspicion to justify the arrest, and (2) the impact of hearsay evidence on the determination of that reasonable suspicion ([paras 1, 32]).

R – Rules

Under Section 40(1)(b) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, a peace officer may arrest a person without a warrant if they reasonably suspect that the person has committed an offence listed in Schedule 1 ([para 30]). For the arrest to be deemed lawful, the following criteria must be satisfied:

  1. The arresting officer must be a peace officer.
  2. The officer must have formed a suspicion regarding a Schedule 1 offence.
  3. The suspicion must be based on reasonable grounds ([paras 31-32]).

Furthermore, the court noted in Duncan v Minister of Law and Order that "reasonable suspicion" is more than mere conjecture; it requires a factual basis upon which the suspicion is grounded ([para 34]). The admissibility of evidence is not a requisite for forming reasonable suspicion ([para 35]).

A – Application

The SCA evaluated the facts within the framework provided by the statutory rules. The majority found that the arresting officers acted on information provided by Constable Saunders, who had been monitoring CCTV footage and reported that a group of individuals in a white minibus was engaging in unlawful activities ([paras 13-14]).

The court rejected Biyela's argument that the officers could not have formed a reasonable suspicion based on hearsay evidence. Instead, it concluded that even if the information later proved to be inadmissible, it could still serve as a basis for the officers' reasonable suspicion at the time of the arrest ([paras 23, 38]). They placed particular emphasis on the credibility of the arresting officers, finding their testimonies to be consistent and corroborated ([paras 24-25]).

Conversely, the dissenting opinion argued that while the officers had a duty to show the grounds for their suspicion, they failed to produce the original report from Saunders during the trial. It contended that the absence of corroborative evidence undermined the validity of the officers’ claims. The dissent highlighted significant contradictions and inability to recall specific details of the events surrounding the arrest ([paras 27-28, 41-43]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA ultimately upheld Biyela's appeal and determined that his arrest was indeed unlawful. The court criticized the lack of substantial grounds presented by the Minister to justify the arrest and agreed with the trial court's finding that the respondent had failed to meet their onus in this regard ([para 45]). The dissenting opinion, while acknowledging the procedural errors, maintained that the presence of contradictory testimonies and the lack of concrete evidence from the SAPS should lead to a dismissal of the appeal on the grounds of insufficient proof to establish lawful arrest ([paras 39, 43-44]).

In summary, the SCA's judgment reinforces the necessity for law enforcement officials to substantiate claims of reasonable suspicion with credible evidence and underscores the protection of personal liberty against unjust arrest and detention.
