Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: South African Riding for the Disabled Association v Regional Land Claims Commissioner and Others [2017] ZACC 4

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case revolves around the South African Riding for the Disabled Association (the Association), which occupies Erf 142 Constantia, Cape Town, under a lease agreement for 34 years. The property was previously state-owned and was eventually ordered to be transferred to Mr. Sedick Sadien and Mr. Ebrahim Sadien (collectively, the Sadiens) as compensation for land they lost due to apartheid-era discrimination.

In December 2012, the Land Claims Court awarded a smaller piece of land to the Sadiens, later adjusting this by ordering the transfer of Erf 142 in February 2013. Notably, the Association alleged that it had made improvements valued at R7.5 million on Erf 142 but was not compensated for this enhancement when the Land Claims Court ordered its transfer.

The Association sought to intervene in the proceedings, applying for rescission of the court's varied order under section 35(11) of the Restitution of Land Rights Act (the Act), asserting that it had a direct and substantial interest in the matter, particularly regarding entitlement to “just and equitable compensation” under section 35(9) of the Act. The Land Claims Court dismissed the application on the grounds that the Association had no interest in the subject-matter, prompting the Association to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([paras 1–19]).

### I – Issues
The main legal question in this case concerns whether the Association had a “direct and substantial interest” in the Land Claims Court proceedings, which would entitle it to intervene. Specifically, it addresses the need for lawful occupiers of state land, like the Association, to receive just and equitable compensation when their occupied land is ordered to be transferred, despite the dismissal of their intervention request ([paras 2–6], [15]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework for this case is primarily the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994, especially sections 35(9) and 35(11):

- **Section 35(9)** provides that lawful occupiers of state land are entitled to “just and equitable compensation” when such land is granted to claimants for restitution ([paras 13–14]).
  
- **Section 35(11)** allows the court to rescind or vary orders under certain conditions ([para 7]).

The test for intervention in judicial proceedings requires the applicant to prove a direct and substantial interest in the subject matter, a principle that necessitates a pre-decision hearing before the Court issues an order that may affect affected parties ([paras 9–11]).

### A – Application
The Constitutional Court found that, although the Land Claims Court initially deemed the Association without interest in the Sadiens' claims for land restitution, it incorrectly dismissed the Association's right to seek compensation as conferred by section 35(9). The Court highlighted that the varied order issued by the Land Claims Court did indeed transfer the property to the Sadiens without the requisite consideration of the Association's entitlement to compensation. 

Evidence established that the Association had a substantial interest in receiving compensation for the improvements made on Erf 142. The Court concluded that a lawful occupier such as the Association should have the opportunity to intervene in the proceedings for this limited purpose, thereby necessitating a reassessment of its claims concerning just and equitable compensation ([paras 12–18]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately granted the Association leave to appeal, upholding its right to intervene solely for the purpose of determining compensation. It set aside the order of the Land Claims Court and remitted the matter for consideration of compensation owed to the Association. The Court concluded that the Regional Land Claims Commissioner would be responsible for the costs of both the Land Claims Court and the Constitutional Court, emphasizing that the Commissioner had misinterpreted the scope of the Association’s rights under the Restitution Act ([paras 21–22]).

This ruling underscores the importance of recognizing and safeguarding the rights of lawful occupiers during land restitution processes and affirms their entitlement to just and equitable compensation when their occupied land is subject to transfer orders. Consequently, the case signals a pivotal stance in balancing restitution claims with the rights of existing lawful occupiers.
