Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Notes: Minister of Water and Environmental Affairs v Kloof Conservancy (106/2015) [2015] ZASCA 177

## F – Facts

The case revolves around the duties of the Minister of Water and Environmental Affairs (the Minister) in relation to the **National Environmental Management: Biodiversity Act 10 of 2004 (NEMBA)**, specifically the obligation to publish a national list of alien and invasive species (AIS) and corresponding regulations. The timeline for publishing the list was set at 24 months from the commencement of NEMBA on September 1, 2004, with a deadline on August 31, 2006 ([paras 2–3]). 

Kloof Conservancy, a registered non-profit organization focused on biodiversity protection, initiated legal proceedings asserting that the Minister had failed to publish the AIS list and regulations in a timely manner. Kloof brought two applications before the KwaZulu-Natal Local Division of the High Court seeking to compel the Minister to fulfill her statutory obligations and to invalidate the interim regulations published in July 2013, which they believed failed to comply with NEMBA ([paras 5–6]).

The High Court ruled on October 22, 2014, declaring the Minister’s prior failures unlawful and unconstitutional but the Minister had, by that time, published a final AIS list and regulations. The High Court also imposed an obligation on the Minister to ensure compliance with NEMBA by all organs of State ([paras 9–10]).

The Minister appealed the parts of the High Court's order which imposed the obligation to ensure compliance with NEMBA ([paras 10–11]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal questions raised in this case include:

1. **Whether the High Court had the jurisdiction to impose a general obligation on the Minister to ensure compliance with NEMBA among all organs of State** ([para 10]).
2. **Whether the orders issued by the High Court were consistent with the constitutional principles of separation of powers and co-operative government** ([para 20]).
3. **Whether the obligations imposed by the High Court were sufficiently defined and practical for the Minister to execute effectively** ([paras 13–14]).

## R – Rules

1. **Legislation**: The case centers around the provisions of NEMBA which outlines the role of the Minister in managing biological diversity, including sections requiring the publication of lists and regulations pertaining to AIS ([paras 1–4]).

2. **Constitutional Principles**: Section 40 of the South African Constitution delineates functions across three spheres of government: national, provincial, and local, establishing a co-operative governance framework. This principle is echoed in sections 41 and 76 of the Constitution, which calls for adherence among all government levels ([paras 10–11]).

3. **Case Law**: The court reviewed previous cases that underscore the rules of co-operative governance and the delineation of governmental functions, such as **Johannesburg Municipality v Gauteng Development Tribunal** and **Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality v Dada** ([paras 11, 20]).

4. **Judicial Precedents on Clarity**: The court referenced significant decisions emphasizing the need for clarity and certainty in judicial orders, as laid out in **Mazibuko NO v Sisulu NO** and **Minister of Home Affairs v Scalabrini Centre** ([paras 14–15]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the High Court's orders could not stand for several reasons:

1. **Misinterpretation of Jurisdiction**: The orders imposed by the High Court incorrectly presupposed a hierarchical governmental structure rather than acknowledging the co-operative dynamics established by the Constitution. This misunderstanding implied that the national government could supervise and enforce compliance among all spheres of government, which contradicted established co-operative principles ([paras 10–11]).

2. **Practicality and Vagueness**: The High Court’s directives required the Minister to undertake extensive monitoring to ensure compliance among potentially thousands of entities (e.g., 278 municipalities). This obligation was deemed vague and impractical, failing to provide the Minister with clear parameters within which to operate, thereby rendering the court's order susceptible to contempt accusations ([paras 12–13]).

3. **Existence of Valid Regulations**: The 2014 AIS list and regulations published by the Minister created legally binding obligations that were not adequately considered by the High Court. The Supreme Court noted that the High Court order inadvertently created conflicting compliance timelines, disregarding statutory schemes designed to manage AIS effectively ([paras 15–16]).

4. **Separation of Powers**: The responsibility for the management of public resources and the operational functions of different government tiers rests with the executive. The orders extended beyond the constitutional mandate, intruding upon executive functions and, thus, raising concerns about overreach in judicial intervention ([para 19]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by the Minister, thereby setting aside paragraphs c. and d. of the High Court’s order ([para 25]). The judgment reinforced important principles regarding the relationship between various government levels, emphasizing the significance of adhering to co-operative governance. It highlighted the need for clarity in judicial mandates to facilitate effective compliance without infringing on statutory obligations. This case serves as a notable example concerning the judicial approach to environmental governance, mandates, and the interspherical dynamics of governmental authority in South Africa. 

In summary, the decision emphasizes the delicate balance required in achieving environmental objectives while respecting the autonomy and operational distinctions of different governmental tiers, underlining the need for legislative clarity and functional demarcation in South African environmental law.
