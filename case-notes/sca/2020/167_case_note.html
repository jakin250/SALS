Vukeya v Ntshane and Others

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 167 (11 December 2020) Court: Supreme Court

Neutral Citation: Vukeya v Ntshane and Others (Case no. 518/2019) [2020] ZASCA 167 (11 December 2020)
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Date of Judgment: 11 December 2020
Coram: Maya P, Dambuza, Mocumie and Plasket JJA, Goosen AJA

F - Facts

[1] The appellant, Mafemani Collet Vukeya, appealed against a judgment from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, which ordered the cancellation of the deed of transfer of a property sold by a deceased spouse, Wilson Mkhatshane Ntshane, to the appellant without the consent of his wife, Shalate Nelly Ntshane, the first respondent. According to the high court ruling, the deed dated 19 May 2009 was null and void due to the absence of the wife's consent, a requirement under section 15(2)(a) of the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984 (MPA).

[3] The first respondent was married to the deceased in community of property since 13 May 1980 and was unaware that the property had been sold until her appointment as the executrix of the deceased’s estate in September 2013. The appellant contended he purchased the property in good faith, believing the deceased was unmarried.

[4] The appellant provided evidence that both the deed of sale and the transfer documents indicated the deceased was unmarried. However, the first respondent argued the appellant was obligated to verify the deceased’s marital status before proceeding with the purchase.

[6] The high court rejected the appellant's claims and ruled the transfer invalid, indicating that true ownership could not be established without the wife's consent, which neither the deeds registry nor the appellant could reasonably ascertain.

I - Issues

[9] The core issue for determination in the appeal was whether the appellant could invoke the protections afforded to third-party purchasers under section 15(9)(a) of the MPA, considering it was not in dispute that the first respondent never provided her consent. The appeal examined if the appellant's lack of knowledge about the deceased's marriage sufficed to validate the sale under the deemed consent provision of the MPA.

R - Rules

[8] The relevant rules derive from the MPA, particularly sections 15(2)(a) and 15(9)(a). Section 15(2)(a) stipulates that spouses married in community of property may not alienate or burden joint estate property without mutual consent. In contrast, section 15(9)(a) states that if a spouse enters a transaction without the required consent and the third party is unaware and could not reasonably have known about the lack of consent, the contract is deemed valid.

[10] The Supreme Court previously examined section 15(9)(a) in Marais NO and Another v Maposa and Others, highlighting the balance between protecting the non-consenting spouse and upholding the rights of bona fide third-party purchasers. Moreover, the court reiterated that a third party must substantiate their plea for deemed consent by proving their ignorance and inability to reasonably know about the lack of consent.

A - Application

[10-20] The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the high court erred in not applying section 15(9)(a) correctly. Despite the first respondent's claims, the appellant was deemed to have acted in good faith, supported by documentation indicating the deceased’s marital status as unmarried. The court noted there was no compelling evidence that the appellant should have conducted further inquiries to discern the deceased's marital status given the circumstances.

The judgment referenced the case of Mulaudzi v Mudau and Others, equating the factual intersection in terms of contractual underpinnings regarding consent to sell joint estate property. The court underscored that since the first respondent's claims regarding the appellant's knowledge were insufficiently substantiated, the protections under section 15(9)(a) were activated, again validating the sale.

C - Conclusion

Based on the above reasoning, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, thereby validating the sale agreement due to the appellant's lack of knowledge regarding the first respondent's consent. The court's decision set aside the high court's order and dismissed the application with costs, reaffirming the legal adequacy and enforceability of contracts undertaken in good faith considerate of the protections against abuses highlighted in MPA. This ruling clarified the scope of section 15(9)(a) and reaffirmed the necessity for reasonable inquiry to determine marital status in property transactions involving the sale of immovable property when consent is statutory prerequisite.

Implications: This judgment reinforces the need for due diligence by third-party purchasers in property transactions while simultaneously balancing the rights of spouses in community of property environments. The reiteration that absences of consent could still lead to enforceable transactions if third parties act bona fide reaffirms the significance of contractual agreements in property law, emphasizing the imperative of legislative provisions like the MPA in protecting both parties' interests while fostering equitable economic transactions.
