Constitutional Court

Electoral Commission of South Africa v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others [2018] ZACC 46

The case concerns an urgent application by the Electoral Commission of South Africa (the Commission) seeking the extension of a suspension of a declaration of invalidity originally issued by this Court in Electoral Commission v Mhlope [2016] ZACC 15.

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 46

F – Facts

The case concerns an urgent application by the Electoral Commission of South Africa (the Commission) seeking the extension of a suspension of a declaration of invalidity originally issued by this Court in Electoral Commission v Mhlope [2016] ZACC 15. In the Mhlope case, this Court found that the Commission's failure to record all available voters' addresses on the national common voters’ roll violated its rule of law obligations under section 1(c) of the Constitution ([1]–[2]).

The initial order declared the Commission’s omission invalid but granted a suspension until 30 June 2018 for the Commission to fulfil its obligations regarding voter address recording. By the application date, the Commission reported that, despite substantial efforts, it still faced a significant shortfall in obtaining all addresses that were reasonably available as of 17 December 2003—the date certain amendments to electoral law came into effect ([3]–[4]).

In its application, the Commission sought a further suspension of the declaration, citing the need to ensure the upcoming elections in 2019 are conducted fairly and without legal challenges regarding address compliance. The Court noted that, as of May 2018, the Commission projected that approximately 21% of registered voters remained without complete address information ([30]–[39]).

The case involved various respondents, including political parties and representatives of state institutions, some of whom opposed the extension of the Court's suspension due to concerns over the integrity and fairness of the electoral process ([10]–[11]).

I – Issues

The Court had to address the following chief legal questions:

  1. Is it just and equitable to grant the extension of the suspension of the declaration of invalidity of the Commission's duties with respect to voter address recording? This involves evaluating the Commission's compliance with the Mhlope order and whether it has made reasonable efforts to fulfil its obligations to ensure free and fair elections ([4]–[6]).
  2. What are the implications of extending the suspension on the electoral process, particularly regarding public confidence in elections and the integrity of the voters' roll? The Court needed to assess how the requested extension would affect both the current electoral cycle and future elections ([36]–[39]).
  3. What considerations should inform the Court's decision on the necessary conditions that could be attached to any extension, if granted? This includes evaluating the balance between the necessity for urgency in the electoral process and adhering to constitutional obligations ([64]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal principles derive from the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, particularly section 1(c) concerning the rule of law, and section 172(1)(b), which empowers courts to issue "just and equitable" orders, including suspending declarations of invalidity ([4]–[5]). The previous case law, particularly the Zondi case, emphasizes the factors the Court must consider when deciding on extensions of such declarations, including the interests of justice, compliance prospects, and the need for finality in litigation ([4]–[40]).

The Court also reaffirmed the importance of the Electoral Act 73 of 1998, which mandates accurate voter registration and the inclusion of registered voters’ addresses ([12]–[13]). In discussing the Commission's responsibilities, the Court highlighted its duty to take reasonable steps to record available addresses on the national voters' roll and emphasized the importance of maintaining the integrity of the electoral process ([72]–[76]).

A – Application

The Court conducted a thorough examination of the Commission's reports and actions post-Mhlope, concluding that while substantial progress in obtaining voter addresses had been made, a significant shortfall remained. The Commission confessed to its inability to fully comply with the initial order, acknowledging the limitations imposed by resource constraints and the administrative challenges inherent in addressing the unique South African electoral landscape ([30]–[39]).

The Commission's argument for an extension focused on logistical considerations, asserting that addressing the missing voters' information should not negatively impact the upcoming elections. However, other parties raised valid concerns about potential electoral fraud and the risk of challenges to the election results based on the presence of missing addresses on the voters' roll ([40]–[42]).

In evaluating the potential implications of granting the extension, the Court deliberated on the wider public interest and the constitutional imperatives of ensuring free and fair elections. It expressed apprehension that allowing an extension could inadvertently legitimize shortcomings in the voters' roll, undermining public confidence in the electoral process ([52]–[60]). The Court weighed these concerns against the Commission's assurances that it would continue efforts to rectify address deficiencies and improve compliance with electoral law.

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Court granted a conditional extension of the suspension of the declaration of invalidity until 30 November 2019. The extension's conditions included:

  1. The Electoral Commission was required to obtain and record on the national common voters' roll all addresses that were reasonably available as of 17 December 2003 ([66]).
  2. The Commission was to file bi-monthly compliance reports detailing its progress regarding voter address collection ([66]).
  3. Any voters without adequate addresses must be flagged and required to provide addresses on voting day ([66]).

The Court's decision emphasized balancing the need for electoral integrity with practical administrative realities, reiterating its commitment to uphold the constitutional project while addressing the complexities arising from the unique challenges faced by the Commission ([118]–[130]).

This case serves as a vital reminder of the importance of meticulous planning and administrative transparency within electoral bodies to safeguard democracy and uphold the public's trust in electoral processes.
