Cross-Border Road Transport Agency v Central African Road Services (Pty) Ltd and Another [2015] ZACC 12

Constitutional Court 2015 [2015] ZACC 12

F – Facts

On 31 March 2011, the Minister of Transport promulgated amendment Regulations under the Cross-Border Road Transport Act 1998, significantly increasing the permit fees payable to the Cross-Border Road Transport Agency (the Agency) by operators in the cross-border road transport sector ([1]). Subsequently, Central African Road Services (Pty) Ltd and Deernam (Pty) Ltd challenged these Regulations in the North Gauteng High Court, claiming they were unconstitutional ([2]). The High Court, presided by Makgoka J, declared the Regulations invalid on several grounds, including inconsistent publication in only one language, violations of procedural fairness, inadequate public consultation, and failure of the Agency's board to consider the draft Regulations properly ([3]).

The High Court's ruling suspended the declaration of invalidity for six months, allowing the Minister and the Agency to republish the Regulations ([4]). However, the Minister failed to publish new valid regulations within the suspension period, leading to disputes over whether the invalidity applied retrospectively from the promulgation date or prospectively from the end of the suspension ([5]). On 1 October 2013, Road Services sought an urgent application to compel actions by the Agency and to confirm the retrospective nature of the invalidity, which resulted in an order from Heaton-Nicholls J affirming the retrospective effect of the invalidity and indicating that existing permit fees should be used until new regulations were validly promulgated ([6]–[8]).

The Agency pursued an appeal to the Constitutional Court seeking to overturn this ruling ([10]). The pertinent question was whether legal principles surrounding the operation of suspended orders of constitutional invalidity and a court’s power to vary those orders had been properly applied ([11]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues before the Constitutional Court centered on:

  1. What principles govern the operation of orders of constitutional invalidity that are suspended when the suspension period lapses without corrective legislation.
  2. Whether the court has the authority to vary a final order previously made, particularly concerning its retrospective effect ([11]).

R – Rules

The Constitutional Court considered the following legal standards and precedents:

  1. Doctrine of Objective Constitutional Invalidity: Under this doctrine, a law is deemed invalid if it contradicts the Constitution. Notably, this principle allows the declaration of invalidity to take retrospective effect unless a court explicitly modifies this outcome ([13]–[16]).
  2. Section 172 of the Constitution: This section empowers courts to make orders that are just and equitable, which includes the ability to limit the retrospective effect of declarations of invalidity and to delay such declarations ([15]).
  3. Precedent from Ferreira v Levin: The Court referenced established case law affirming the principles of constitutional invalidity and highlighted that courts possess discretionary power to temper the effects of such declarations ([13],[14]).
  4. Finality of Courts’ Orders: General legal principles assert that once a court has ruled, its orders are final, creating a presumption against the ability to amend such decisions unless specific grounds, like errors or ambiguities, are present ([38],[39]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court analyzed the lower court's orders and the implications of the suspension period that had elapsed without the requisite regulatory actions being taken by the Minister. The Court noted that while the initial order from Makgoka J broadly declared the invalidity of the Regulations, it was also significant that the order suspended the invalidity without clear instructions limiting the retrospective effect ([27]).

The Court reaffirmed that generally, the declaration of legal invalidity results in immediate retrospective effect unless the Court explicitly limits this in the order ([20],[34]). By examining the original order, the Court found that it was silent on retrospectivity concerning most paragraphs, indicating that said silence could not be interpreted as an oversight but would instead imply that no limitation on retrospectivity was intended ([31]–[34]).

In terms of the arguments posited by the Agency to request a variation of the order, the Court rejected this claim on the basis that a court does not typically possess the authority to alter its own final orders once the suspension period has expired. This principle is rooted in ensuring legal certainty and maintaining the rule of law ([38]–[44]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court affirmed the dismissal of the Agency’s appeal, holding that the declaration of constitutional invalidity operated retrospectively from the date of the Regulations’ promulgation (31 March 2011) due to the lack of any explicit limitation set by the lower court ([53]–[54]). Thus, the invalid Regulations had been nullified from that point onward. The decision reinforces the automatic retrospective effect of declarations of invalidity under constitutional jurisdiction unless explicitly modified by the court. Additionally, it delineates the limitations of courts in varying their own final orders, emphasizing the importance of clarity and finality in judicial determinations.

This case underscores essential constitutional principles regarding legitimacy, the role of regulatory bodies, and adherence to procedural norms, imposing significant implications for legislative and administrative practices going forward.
