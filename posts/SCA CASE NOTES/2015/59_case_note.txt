Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Ndwambi v The State 611/2013 [2015] ZASCA 59

## F – Facts ([1]–[12])

The case revolves around Muvhuso Calvin Ndwambi, who was convicted of fraud in relation to the sale of a fake rhino horn on 29 October 2003 at the Shell Ultra City, Kroonstad. The sale was orchestrated as part of a police trap, where the police had received information that Ndwambi’s co-accused wished to sell a rhino horn ([1]). The police, led by Inspector Oberholzer, set up surveillance and arrested both Ndwambi and his co-accused when a transaction was initiated at the petrol station ([2]–[5]).

The co-accused presented a wrapped item as a rhino horn, and after negotiations, she was arrested, while Ndwambi, who denied involvement, was also apprehended. Searches revealed that Ndwambi was a police officer and he claimed he was merely giving her a lift ([5]–[6]). The trial court found Ndwambi and his co-accused guilty, subsequently dismissing their appeals ([1]). 

Ndwambi contended that the State did not prove all the elements required for a fraud conviction, particularly the intent to deceive and the aspect of prejudice ([2]), arguing that he neither knew the item was fake nor participated with fraudulent intent.

## I – Issues ([2], [12], [13])

The primary legal issues pertain to:

1. Whether the evidence presented was sufficient to establish Ndwambi's intent to deceive, a requisite element of fraud.
2. Whether the State demonstrated the requisite level of prejudice, actual or potential, in the fraudulent transaction ([18]–[21]).

## R – Rules ([13]–[22])

Fraud is established if it can be shown that a false representation was made knowingly, without belief in its truth, or recklessly ([14]). The case referred primarily to the definition articulated in **Derry v Peek** ([14]), which highlighted that fraud encompasses deceitful misrepresentations made with reckless disregard for their truth.

Moreover, **Ex parte Lebowa Development Corporation Ltd** defined fraud through dolus eventualis, where an accused knowingly exposes another to a risk of falsehood ([15]). 

Prejudice in fraud does not require the deceived party to have acted on the false representation. What counts is the deceiver's intent, as established in **R v Dyonta & another**, which clarified that potential prejudice suffices ([20]). 

## A – Application ([11]–[22])

The court evaluated Ndwambi's role in the illicit transaction. The evidence showed that he was present, watched over the transaction, and had extensive communication with his co-accused in the lead-up to the event, which belied his claim of ignorance ([10]). The court found that the trial court acted correctly in rejecting Ndwambi's account as improbable and inconsistent with the facts ([8], [9]).

Regarding intent, the evidence from the appellant's co-accused represented that the item was a rhino horn and that them attempting to sell it for a significant sum established a prima facie case of deception ([17]). Furthermore, the Court maintained that the objectively fraudulent nature of the representation was evident, regardless of the actual outcome of the transaction ([22]).

In terms of prejudice, the court assessed that the risk of harm—financial and ethical concerns regarding illegal trade—was substantial, confirming that the intent to deceive was clearly present in Ndwambi's actions and participation ([22]).

## C – Conclusion ([23], [24])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Ndwambi's appeal, affirming the conviction of fraud. The Court held that his actions clearly illustrated fraudulence, as both the intent to deceive and potential prejudice were sufficiently established. The conviction was upheld partly because Ndwambi's status as a police officer highlighted the breach of trust involved, hence justifying the six-year imprisonment sentence ([23]).

This case underscores the legal principle that intent can be inferred from conduct and situational context, alongside a reaffirmation of the principle that the law examines actions through the perspective of the deceiver. The dissenting opinion raised concerns about the nature of evidence and burden of proof regarding intent but ultimately did not prevail, marking a significant affirmation of the original ruling ([24]).
