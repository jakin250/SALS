Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2003

# Case Note: FIRAC Method

## Facts

[1] This case presents an application for leave to appeal against the judgment of the Northern Cape High Court, stemming from the applicant, Christopher Lance Mercer, being convicted in the Kuruman Magistrates’ Court in September 2001. The conviction related to a violation of sections 31(1) and 44(1)(a) of the Nature and Environmental Conservation Ordinance 19 of 1974 (Cape) (the 1974 Ordinance), which pertain to the unlawful harbouring of specified animal species without requisite permits. Prior to his conviction, Mercer had various permit applications denied by nature conservation authorities due to the classification of three caracals as potential problem animals under the Problem Animal Control Ordinance 26 of 1957 (Cape) (the 1957 Ordinance). 

[2] Following the conviction, Mercer appealed to the High Court, contesting both his conviction and sentence while also challenging the constitutionality of the 1974 Ordinance as a whole. Initially scheduled for a hearing on 9 September 2002, the High Court hearing was postponed until 3 March 2003. Prior to this hearing, Mercer attempted to access the Constitutional Court directly for relief, a move the Court dismissed as premature, emphasizing that the High Court should first address the matter. 

[3] At the subsequent hearing, Kgomo JP dismissed Mercer’s appeal but recognized the substantial efforts and financial investments made by the applicant and his partner in operating the Kalahari Raptor Centre. Consequently, the High Court replaced the original substantial fine with a caution and discharge, reflecting on their community service and commitment to wildlife. 

[4] Mercer, undeterred, applied to the Supreme Court of Appeal for leave to appeal the High Court's confirmation of his conviction. This application was also unsuccessful. Subsequently, Mercer applied to the Constitutional Court for leave to appeal against the High Court’s confirmation of his conviction and sought condonation for the delay in filing his application, which was 21 days late. Condonation would only be granted if it served the interests of justice, evaluated primarily by the reasons given for the delay, the merits of the appeal, and any public interest in the matter.

[5] The State Attorney indicated that proposed legislation (National Environmental Management: Biodiversity Bill) was forthcoming, aimed at enhancing environmental rights per Section 24 of the Constitution and introducing a controlled permit system for dealing with specific species. This raised questions about the public interest in resolving Mercer’s appeal.

## Issues

[6] The principal legal questions before the Court revolved around: 
1. Whether the Northern Cape Problem Animal Control Ordinance, No. 26 of 1957, was unconstitutional and invalid.
2. Whether the administrative decisions denying Mercer the necessary permits constituted an improper exercise of discretion.
3. Whether Mercer was entitled to claim that he had not received a fair trial based on the prosecution's actions under the 1974 Ordinance.

## Rules

[7] The legal framework involved included the following statutes:
- **Nature and Environmental Conservation Ordinance 19 of 1974 (Cape)**, specifically prohibiting the possession of certain animals without a permit.
- **Problem Animal Control Ordinance 26 of 1957 (Cape)**, defining specific animals as 'problem animals'.
The ruling drew on precedents that stipulate a failure to pursue appropriate avenues of recourse invalidates appeals regarding constitutional challenges to legislation not actively contested in prior courts (see, e.g., *Shongwe v S* [2003] 8 BCLR 858 (CC)).

## Application

[8] The Court elaborated that Mercer’s request to declare the 1957 Ordinance unconstitutional was misplaced, as he had not demonstrated a sufficient connection between the two ordinances to challenge the former in lieu of a conviction under the latter. The Court posited that his appeals were unwarranted since the 1957 Ordinance had not been a contested matter in the High Court, asserting that his request was an improper attempt at direct access for a separate ordinance that was not subject to challenge in this context.

[9] Further, the contention that the 1974 Ordinance itself was unconstitutional was assessed. The applicant failed to pursue specific relief or contentions raised in prior hearings. The High Court’s decision outlined that the permit requirement implied a necessary regulation of wildlife interaction, reflecting a balance of freedoms and preserving ecological integrity. The absence of a thorough challenge to this reasoning indicated a lack of substantive grounds for claim against the 1974 Ordinance.

[10] Regarding the denial of the permit application, the Court reiterated that the correct course of action would be to seek a review of the administrative decision instead of framing it within a criminal appeal context, which was not suitable for the remedies he sought. 

[11] Finally, the question of a fair trial was evaluated. Mercer’s assertions of unfairness lacked substantive foundation since he was charged in accordance with the provisions of the ordinance under which he had been convicted. Claims around the constitutionality of prosecution processes had no evidentiary support indicating prosecutorial impropriety, leading the Court to reaffirm that the prosecution's conduct did not infringe upon due process. 

## Conclusion

[12] The Constitutional Court dismissed Mercer’s application for condonation on the basis of lacking prospects of success in his appeal. The Court underscored that no compelling public interest necessitated the appeal's determination, especially in light of forthcoming legislation that sought to regulate biodiversity and animal protection. The majority ruling articulated that the absence of any legitimate argument distinguishing the case from previous legal frameworks closed the door on any viable route for a successful appeal against his conviction. 

In summary, *Mercer* serves as a salient case illustrating the delicate interplay between environmental law enforcement, administrative procedures, and the rights of wildlife sanctuaries. The consideration of public interest and prospective success in appeals underlines constitutional adherence while ensuring the integrity of ecological statutes.
