Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: M v M (1305/2021) [2023] ZASCA 33

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–20])

The case concerns a dispute between Mr. I M (the appellant) and Mrs. A M (the respondent) following their divorce. They were married in community of property on December 4, 1995, and divorce proceedings were initiated by the appellant on September 15, 2015, in the Springs Regional Court. The appellant sought, among other relief, a forfeiture of benefits from the marriage based on allegations of the respondent's extramarital affair. 

The respondent countered these allegations, asserting that the appellant engaged in infidelity and was abusive. She did not initially seek forfeiture but instead sought the division of the joint estate. In 2017, the appellant amended his claim to include 50% of the respondent's pension interest from the Government Employees Pension Fund (GEPF). The respondent contended this claim was unjust, arguing she would be prejudiced because the appellant had withdrawn a significant pension amount prior to the divorce and refused to account for the balance.

During the proceedings, the regional court ruled that the respondent bore the burden to prove that the appellant would unduly benefit from retaining his pension interest. The regional court ultimately dismissed the appellant's request for pension interest and awarded each party their own costs. Dissatisfied with this decision, the respondent appealed to the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which overturned the regional court's ruling, concluding that the appellant's engagement in domestic violence warranted forfeiture of his benefits.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 20–19])

1. **Whether the high court misdirected itself by granting forfeiture of all patrimonial benefits when this was not sought by the respondent.**
2. **Whether the appellant's alleged misconduct, specifically his failure to account for his pension payout, constituted substantial misconduct meriting forfeiture of his entitlement to the pension interest.**
3. **Whether the respondent bore the onus of proving that the appellant would unduly benefit if the order for forfeiture was not granted.**

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 21–23])

The case hinges on the interpretation of sections 7 and 9 of the Divorce Act 70 of 1979, which govern the division of pension interests upon divorce. Specifically, Section 7(7) states that pension interest is deemed part of each party's assets in divorce proceedings, while Section 9 allows for forfeiture of patrimonial benefits based on substantial misconduct if it would otherwise lead to undue benefit.

Relevant precedent includes:
- **Botha v Botha 2006 (4) SA 144 (SCA)**, illustrating the principles surrounding forfeiture based on misconduct.
- **Wijker v Wijker 1993 (4) SA 720 (A)**, emphasizing that the presence of any one of the factors can justify a forfeiture order.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 24–32])

The Supreme Court of Appeal noted that the regional court had correctly placed the burden on the respondent to prove undue benefit in the context of forfeiting the appellant's pension interest. The court found that substantial misconduct relating to financial disclosure had not been established by the respondent, despite her claims regarding the appellant's failure to account for the pension funds.

The Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted that while allegations of infidelity and abusive behavior were present, the respondent failed to establish how these constituted substantial misconduct relevant to forfeiture. It further reaffirmed that any misconduct did not rise to the level necessary for forfeiture, emphasizing that the appellant's expenditures post-divorce were demonstrably for the benefit of the joint estate, such as home renovations and payment of debts—this demonstrated a lack of undue benefit to the appellant.

Moreover, the Supreme Court found fault in the high court's interference, arguing it substituted findings that were not part of the original findings from the regional court. This change was deemed incorrect as the high court moved beyond the scope of the relief sought by the respondent.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 33–35])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the regional court's findings and overturned the high court's decision. The court concluded that the respondent did not meet the burden of proof required to justify forfeiture of the appellant’s pension interest, given the lack of substantial evidence of misconduct. The ratio decidendi emphasized that simply alleging misconduct is insufficient without proof of its impact on the division of assets.

The appeal was upheld with each party responsible for its own costs, reinforcing the principle that the distribution of assets must be approached with fairness and substantiated claims. This ruling clarifies the burden of proving substantial misconduct in divorce proceedings and its necessary connection to claims of undue benefit, outlining the court's discretion in interpreting these elements.
