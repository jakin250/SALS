Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Moko v Acting Principal of Malusi Secondary School and Others [2020] ZACC 30

**F – Facts:**  
The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa concerns Mr. Johannes Moko, a Grade 12 learner at Malusi Secondary School in Limpopo. On 25 November 2020, he arrived at the school to sit for the Business Studies Paper 2 examination but was barred from entry by the Acting Principal, Mr. Tlou Mokgonyana, because he had not attended certain extra lessons ([paras 7–8]). Mr. Moko’s subsequent attempts to have guardians present failed, ultimately leading to his failure to sit for the examination. This resulted in him being informed that he could only write the supplementary examination in May 2021, which he argued would hinder his right to further education, as it would delay his ability to apply to higher education institutions ([paras 9–12]). The initial application to the High Court for urgent relief was struck from the roll on technical grounds of lack of urgency ([para 11]). Consequently, Mr. Moko sought direct access to the Constitutional Court for an urgent determination of his right to education ([paras 12–20]).

**I – Issues:**  
The primary legal issue revolves around whether the actions of the first respondent, which resulted in Mr. Moko missing his examination, constituted a violation of his rights under section 29(1) of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to basic and further education ([para 25]). The issues also encompass whether the Court should grant direct access given the urgency and frustrations faced in lower courts ([paras 16–22]).

**R – Rules:**  
The Court referred directly to section 29 of the South African Constitution, which stipulates the right to basic and further education ([para 2]). It emphasized the immediate realisation of the right to basic education compared to the progressive realisation of further education, relying on precedents such as *Juma Musjid* and *Pridwin* regarding the interpretation of basic education ([paras 26–34]). Relevant legal principles outlined the necessity for urgent legal intervention in matters concerning fundamental rights, as established in the case of *Mazibuko* and further reiterated in *Christian Education South Africa* ([paras 16–19]).

**A – Application:**  
The Court analyzed the actions of the first respondent against the established obligations under section 29 of the Constitution. It began by determining whether the matric examinations constituted a part of basic education ([para 26]). Concluding that indeed they do, the Court firmly upheld the notion that acting principals, as organs of the state, have both negative and positive obligations to facilitate educational rights, which includes not obstructing access to examinations ([paras 34–35]). The actions of the first respondent, specifically sending Mr. Moko home and preventing him from entering the examination room without justifiable grounds, breached his right to basic education and could not be defended by the absence of a valid policy or reason for such actions ([paras 32–35]).

**C – Conclusion:**  
The Court found in favor of Mr. Moko, declaring the first respondent’s conduct as unconstitutional and a violation of section 29(1) of the Constitution. The Court ordered that Mr. Moko be allowed to write the missed examination by 15 January 2021 and mandated the second to fifth respondents to ensure this opportunity, thus affording him a timely venue for his further education ([paras 48–50]). Joint and several costs against the respondents were ordered, emphasizing accountability within the state’s educational obligations ([para 46]). The implications of this decision reinforce the transformative power of education and the imperative for educational officials to adhere to constitutional mandates, illustrating the judiciary's commitment to uphold fundamental rights in the context of South Africa's evolving democratic framework ([paras 47–48]).
