Bukelwa Nolizwe Holomisa v Sango Patekile Holomisa and Another [2018] ZACC 40

The Magistrate ruled in favor of the First Respondent, decreeing the divorce and distributing the joint estate despite the Applicant's absence, which stemmed from procedural irregularities by her attorney.

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 40

FIRAC Method

F – Facts:

The case arises from a marriage solemnized on December 16, 1995, in Mqanduli, Transkei, between Bukelwa Nolizwe Holomisa (the Applicant) and Sango Patekile Holomisa (the First Respondent). Their marriage was contested regarding its property regime—specifically, whether it was in or out of community of property. The First Respondent sought a divorce in the Mthatha Regional Court, asserting that their marriage was out of community of property, which the Applicant contested, claiming it was in community of property.

The Magistrate ruled in favor of the First Respondent, decreeing the divorce and distributing the joint estate despite the Applicant's absence, which stemmed from procedural irregularities by her attorney. The High Court confirmed this ruling, and the Supreme Court of Appeal reversed the findings, declaring the marriage out of community of property but failed to address the constitutional issue concerning the Divorce Act 70 of 1979, specifically section 7(3) which limited property redistribution benefits only to specific marriages recognized by law during apartheid.

The Applicant sought recourse from the Constitutional Court, advocating for the inclusion of her situation under the provisions of section 7(3) of the Divorce Act, arguing it was unconstitutional and discriminatory towards women married under the former Transkei legislation ([paras 5–10, 11–12]).

I – Issues:

The key legal issues before the Constitutional Court included:

  1. Whether section 7(3) of the Divorce Act 70 of 1979 was constitutionally valid, particularly given its exclusion of women married under the Transkei Marriage Act from property redistribution rights.
  2. The appropriateness of granting direct access to the Constitutional Court for this constitutional challenge without prior adjudication in lower courts.
  3. The validity of the distinctions made regarding property rights among women married under differing legal frameworks in South Africa ([paras 24–28]).

R – Rules:

The court considered several legal rules and precedents, focusing primarily on:

  • Equality Clause: Section 9(1) of the Constitution of South Africa ensures that everyone is equal before the law and has the right to equal protection and benefit of the law.
  • Constitutional Invalidity: The court referenced previous judgments regarding discriminatory laws and the need to ensure that the Constitution is upheld to eradicate laws from the apartheid era that perpetuate inequities ([paras 23–24, 29]).
  • Rules concerning Direct Access: The Constitutional Court typically grants direct access in exceptional circumstances and prefers that cases go through lower courts for comprehensive adjudication ([paras 25–29]).

A – Application:

In its application of the law, the Constitutional Court unanimously found that the exclusion of women married under the Transkei Marriage Act from the benefits of section 7(3) of the Divorce Act was a clear violation of the constitutional equality guarantee. The court determined that the differentiation lacked a rational basis and thus did not serve any legitimate governmental purpose ([paras 23–24, 27]).

The court reflected on the complex legislative history leading to the establishment of the provisions in question—specifically how past apartheid policies had created a void in equitable access to marital property rights for women in the former homelands compared to the broader national context. The ruling acknowledged that such inequalities were not only unjust but also a legacy of South Africa's socio-political history ([paras 22–30]).

Concerning the question of direct access, the court held that while the procedural lapses were noteworthy, they did not bar the court from hearing the matter due to the immediate need to address the apparent and gross inequality faced by women like the Applicant. The court concluded that any delay in rectifying this inequality would further perpetuate the discrimination ([paras 25–28, 30]).

C – Conclusion:

The Constitutional Court held that:

  1. Section 7(3) of the Divorce Act 70 of 1979 was declared constitutionally invalid to the extent that it excluded spouses married under the Transkei Marriage Act from its ambit.
  2. A suspension of the declaration of invalidity was imposed for 24 months to allow Parliament to remedy the legislative gap.
  3. The court provided for interim measures, mandating that section 7(3) be interpreted to include marriages under the Transkei Marriage Act without ante-nuptial contracts ([paras 35]).

The implications of this ruling are significant, as they not only restore rights to spouses in similar situations to the Applicant but also highlight the need for legislative reform to address the remnants of apartheid-era laws that continue to affect the country's legal landscape. This decision reaffirms the Constitutional Court's role in correcting historical injustices and ensuring the challenges faced by vulnerable populations are addressed under the current legal framework ([paras 30–34]).
