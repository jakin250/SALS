Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: Du Preez N O v The Member of the Executive Council for Health of the Eastern Cape Province (1032/2022) [2024] ZASCA 147 

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts ([1]–[3], [20]–[43])

The case involves an application for reconsideration of a petition for leave to appeal concerning a claim for medical negligence damages arising from injuries sustained by a minor child (PN) during birth at Dora Nginza Hospital, Gqeberha, on 25 October 2009. The applicant, Wynand du Preez, acting as executor of PN's estate, contended that PN suffered a brain injury resulting in cerebral palsy due to the negligence of hospital staff during the delivery ([2]).

The High Court initially dismissed the claim on 17 June 2022, stating that while PN had suffered a severe brain injury leading to cerebral palsy, the evidence did not prove negligence by the medical staff employed by the MEC for Health ([2]–[4]). The applicant alleged negligence on three counts: improper monitoring during labor, excessive fundal pressure applied to the abdomen, and failure in resuscitation efforts ([5]).

Following the dismissal, the applicant sought further review, which was initially rejected by the Supreme Court of Appeal on 12 September 2022. The Acting President of the Supreme Court of Appeal later granted reconsideration on 19 December 2022, allowing for oral argument on the merits of the case ([1], [3]).

Tragically, PN passed away on 7 May 2023, and the executor, Mr. Du Preez, became the applicant in this matter ([3]). The new evidence sought to be introduced by the applicant included a letter from Dr. van der Walt, a doctor involved in PN's resuscitation efforts, claiming that severe bradycardia was present, thus asserting the ineffectiveness of the resuscitation by hospital staff ([7]–[18]).

### I – Issues ([4], [26], [41], [42])

The predominant issues for determination were:
1. Whether the application met the necessary threshold of “exceptional circumstances” required for the reconsideration of an appeal denied by two Supreme Court judges under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([1], [20]).
2. If negligence could be established on the part of the medical staff concerning the care provided during the labor and delivery of PN, and if such negligence caused or contributed to her brain injury ([4], [20]).
3. The relevance and admissibility of the new evidence submitted by the applicant, claimed to counteract the defenses raised by the MEC ([7], [19]).

### R – Rules ([1], [12], [26], [38])

The legal framework relevant to this case includes:
- Section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which stipulates conditions under which leave to appeal may be reconsidered. Specifically, it allows for a reconsideration when it is shown that refusal of leave did not sufficiently consider that exceptional circumstances exist ([1], [20]).
- The standard applicable to claims of medical negligence, which requires the plaintiff to prove that the medical staff breached the standard of care expected in the circumstances, and that this breach caused the injury ([41], [42]).
- Criteria for the admission of new evidence, as established in *S v de Jager* and *Rail Commuters Action Group*, which includes requirements for the evidence being relevant, weighty, and not previously available ([11], [12]).

### A – Application ([20]–[41])

In considering the application for reconsideration, the Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the evidence presented, particularly addressing the claims regarding the alleged negligence by the hospital staff. The Court noted that the applicant's arguments, chiefly based on expert opinion, largely reiterated points made at trial without compelling new evidence or demonstrating substantive misdirection by the High Court ([41], [42]).

The Court identified that PN was delivered in a compromised state, as established by medical records indicating a low Apgar score and required resuscitation immediately post-delivery ([23]–[25]). It further emphasized that the medical experts agreed that PN suffered from severe brain injury during the second stage of labor, denying the link between any alleged negligent resuscitation by hospital staff and the injury incurred ([34], [39]).

The attempts to introduce the letter from Dr. van der Walt were dismissed by the Court on the grounds that the letter constituted hearsay and lacked the formal weight of a sworn affidavit. The Court determined that the letter did not materially influence the outcome of the case, reinforcing the earlier High Court's conclusions that established facts regarding the medical treatment of PN at birth pointed towards no negligence ([19], [41]).

### C – Conclusion ([42]–[44])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the application for leave to appeal, concluding that the applicant did not meet the stringent criteria for “exceptional circumstances” necessary for reconsideration under section 17(2)(f). The Court held firm to the principle that negligence in medical cases must be proved with substantive evidence, and it affirmed that the High Court had sufficiently evaluated the evidence provided ([42], [44]).

The ruling underscores the weighty burden placed on plaintiffs in medical negligence cases to substantiate claims with clear evidence of both a breach in the duty of care and a causal link to the injuries sustained. The implications of this case highlight the difficulties faced by claimants in appealing medical negligence determinations where factual evaluations had been rigorously assessed by trial courts. 

This judgment solidifies the judicial reluctance to interfere with factual findings unless a clear error is evident and serves as a reminder of the high threshold needed to overturn decisions made at lower court levels.
