Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Mogale and Others v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others [2023] ZACC 14

## F – Facts
The case of **Mogale and Others v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others** concerns the constitutionality of the **Traditional and Khoi-San Leadership Act 3 of 2019** (hereafter "TKLA"). The applicants — led by Constance Mogale — argued that the Act was passed without adequate public participation, in violation of **sections 59(1)(a)**, **72(1)(a)**, and **118(1)(a)** of the South African Constitution, which mandate legislative bodies to facilitate public involvement in their processes ([1]–[13]). 

The TKLA was introduced in the National Assembly on **21 September 2015**. Despite claims that public hearings were conducted, the applicants' monitors documented significant deficiencies throughout the public participation processes held across the provinces from **February 2016** to **August 2017**. These included inadequate notice, insufficient translation of documents, and the exclusion of diversely affected voices ([5]–[12]). The Act was ultimately signed into law by the President on **20 November 2019**, despite earlier concerns raised by the applicants regarding the law's inadequacies ([12]).

The applicants filed this case on **20 December 2021**, challenging the constitutionality of the TKLA based on the deficient legislative process through which it was enacted, alleging that such defects rendered the Act invalid ([12]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues before the Court were:
1. Did the applicants possess standing to challenge the validity of the TKLA, given their attempts at engaging in the public participation process?
2. Did the Parliament and its constituent legislatures fulfill their constitutional obligation to facilitate reasonable public involvement in the legislative process leading to the enactment of the TKLA?
3. What is the appropriate remedy, if any, should the Court find the TKLA to be unconstitutional? ([13]).

## R – Rules
The rules governing this case center around the constitutional obligation of Parliament to facilitate public involvement in legislative processes as established in:
- **Section 59(1)(a)** of the Constitution mandates the National Assembly to facilitate public engagement.
- **Section 72(1)(a)** mandates the National Council of Provinces (NCOP) to provide a similar facilitation.
- **Section 118(1)(a)** applies to provincial legislatures, obligating them to ensure public participation ([32]).

In preceding case law, particularly **Doctors for Life International v Speaker of the National Assembly** and **Land Access Movement of South Africa v Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces**, the Constitutional Court emphasized that public involvement must be meaningful and capable of influencing legislative decisions ([34]).

The Court developed a standard in **Doctors for Life**, positing that reasonable public involvement requires:
1. Affording the public adequate notice of proposed legislation.
2. Providing accessible materials in relevant languages.
3. Ensuring that legislative bodies actively engage with the submissions made ([34]–[38]).

## A – Application
The Constitutional Court analyzed the public participation process in detail, noting numerous deficiencies. The Court evaluated the evidence presented by the applicants, documenting the lack of proper notice and preparation time for public hearings, insufficient materials provided to participants, and minimal engagement with input received ([53]–[80]).

Overall, the Court found that Parliament failed to adequately facilitate public involvement:
- **Notices for hearings** were insufficient, often communicated by word of mouth and only a day in advance, which did not allow meaningful preparation ([61]).
- **Pre-hearing education** was not conducted in many provinces, leading to misunderstanding among participants regarding the purpose of the hearings ([64]).
- Attendance at hearings was disproportionately skewed towards traditional leaders over ordinary citizens, effectively silencing diverse community voices ([73]).

Conclusively, the Court ruled that these cumulative deficiencies pointed to a failure to reasonably comply with constitutional mandates regarding public participation, leading to the conclusion that the TKLA was adopted in a manner inconsistent with the Constitution ([82]–[84]).

## C – Conclusion
The Court held that **the TKLA is unconstitutional and invalid due to Parliament's failure to facilitate adequate public involvement as mandated by the Constitution.** However, due to the potential disruption that an immediate declaration of invalidity could cause, the Court suspended the order of invalidity for **24 months** to allow Parliament to rectify the legislative process ([87]).

This decision underscores the importance of participatory democracy and the duty of legislative bodies to engage meaningfully with the public. It highlights that legislation affecting disadvantaged communities must be approached with particular sensitivity and inclusiveness, ensuring that all stakeholders have the opportunity to contribute to the conversation ([46]–[47]).

By directing the costs against the opposing parties, the Court also emphasized accountability in the legislative process, thereby reinforcing its commitment to upholding constitutional standards in lawmaking ([86]).
