Phillipa Susan van Zyl NO v Getz (548/19) [2020] ZASCA 84

B was born to T T and L T (her parents), and she suffers from significant mental disorders, rendering her financially dependent ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 84

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The matter at hand involves an appeal by Phillipa Susan van Zyl, acting as curatrix ad litem for her granddaughter B T ("B"), against Keith Getz, the executor of the estate of B's deceased grandfather, S T ("S"). The core of the dispute arises from B's claim for maintenance against S's estate, which was summarily dismissed based on established common law principles regarding the duty of support owed by grandparents to their grandchildren.

  1. B was born to T T and L T (her parents), and she suffers from significant mental disorders, rendering her financially dependent ([2]).
  2. Upon divorce in 2001, a court order required L to maintain B, but he emigrated and ceased all financial support or contact ([15d], [15e]).
  3. Following S's death in 2004, his entire estate was bequeathed to N T, B's grandmother, who subsequently also passed away ([3], [16]).
  4. B’s claim for maintenance was not upheld by the estate on the grounds of the common law decision in Barnard NO v Miller (1963), which ruled against the enforceability of support obligations on a grandparent's deceased estate ([3], [5.4]).
  5. Van Zyl sought to declare the Barnard principle unconstitutional under sections 39(2) and 173 of the South African Constitution, arguing that it infringed upon B's rights as a vulnerable child in need of support ([4]).

I – Issues

The central legal question was whether the common law should be developed to recognize a duty of support on the part of a grandparent's deceased estate towards their grandchildren under certain circumstances:

  1. Whether the common law rule, which denies the estate of a grandparent liability for a grandchild's maintenance, is inconsistent with constitutional rights, particularly the child’s rights to dignity and support as established in sections 10 and 28(2) of the Constitution ([1], [4]).
  2. Whether the courts can or should develop common law principles when those principles may have broad implications for familial obligations and succession policies ([39], [54]).

R – Rules

The court evaluated the existing common law principles surrounding maintenance obligations vis-à-vis familial relationships and distinguished between obligations established during the lifetime of a person versus those that carry over to a deceased's estate:

  1. Common law dictates:
  • The duty of support primarily rests with parents, which remains enforceable against a deceased parent's estate ([20]).
  • The notable exceptions arise under judicial interpretations in cases such as Carelse v Estate de Vries and the controversial claim made in Lloyd v Menzies, recognizing potential liability of grandparents’ estates under specific contexts (though disputed) ([19], [23], [29]).
  1. Constitutional provisions under:
  • Section 39(2), which directs courts to promote the Constitution's spirit when developing the law ([40]).
  • Whole frameworks from past court rulings underscore the necessity for thorough judicial reasoning and the potential for legislative amendment versus judicial law creation ([12], [56]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal examined the facts surrounding B's claim and assessed both the procedural and substantive failures present in the High Court's judgment:

  1. The SCA noted the significant delay and lack of reasons for the lower court's decision, which violated due process and judicial duty, thereby rendering subsequent appeal processes difficult and opaque ([9], [10]).
  2. The court stated that the separation of issues under Rule 33(4) might not achieve justice where interrelated family obligations arise, suggesting the original High Court did not sufficiently consider the complexities of the familial situation ([50]).
  3. Critically, the court concluded that the facts presented failed to adequately establish L's inability or unwillingness to support B through evidence of his financial status or further efforts made by T to ascertain L's whereabouts ([47], [49]).
  4. The court explicitly declined to instigate the development of common law regarding grandparent estate liability in maintenance, asserting that such broad changes extend beyond judicial powers and encroach upon legislative domain ([59]).

C – Conclusion

The court dismissed the appellant’s appeal, affirming the legal position laid out in Barnard that grandparents’ estates are not obligated for grandchild maintenance post-mortem, due to insufficient evidence and reasoning underpinning the claim.

  1. The judgment emphasized that any substantial change in familial support obligations should originate from legislative amendments rather than judicial reinterpretations, particularly given the profound implications such shifts would denote to existing family law structures and the rights of heirs ([59]).
  2. Acknowledging the critical nature of B's circumstances, the court nevertheless upheld prevailing legal principles and cautioned against judicial overreach by reiterating that the complexities and innovations in familial support must be judiciously legislated rather than adjudicated ([60]).
  3. Additionally, the court's directive for a report to be sent to the Judicial Service Commission regarding the lower court's conduct signified serious concerns about judicial efficiency and accountability ([60]).

Implications

This case reflects on the intersections between familial obligations under common law, constitutional rights, and the roles of judicial versus legislative powers in evolving legal doctrines. It serves as a commentary on how courts must navigate rights and responsibilities while ensuring that there is sufficient evidence and rationale to back significant legal transitions, especially in delicate domains affecting children's welfare. The ruling reinforces the conservative view on estate obligations, maintaining a cautious approach to changes in family law pending clear legislative guidance.
