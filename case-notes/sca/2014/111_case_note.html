Supreme Court of Appeal

Barnard & Others v The Registrar of Medical Schemes (628/13) [2014] ZASCA 111

The case involves the appellants—trustees of the Medshield Medical Scheme (the scheme)—and the respondent, the Registrar of Medical Schemes.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 111

F – Facts

The case involves the appellants—trustees of the Medshield Medical Scheme (the scheme)—and the respondent, the Registrar of Medical Schemes. The scheme, one of the largest medical schemes in South Africa, had about 207,000 beneficiaries. On October 2, 2012, the registrar approached the North Gauteng High Court seeking an urgent order to place the scheme under provisional curatorship due to several irregularities in its governance and administration ([paras 1–3]). The court granted a provisional curatorship, and a final order was confirmed by Murphy J on November 15, 2012 ([paras 5–6]).

The registrar’s application was based on several grounds outlined in the Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998 (the MS Act) and the Financial Institutions (Protection of Funds) Act 28 of 2001 (the FI Act). Key concerns included the trustees’ failure to comply with the MS Act and scheme rules, irregularities in broker management agreements, improper dual roles within the BOT, questionable election practices for trustees, and a lack of cooperation with regulatory demands ([paras 7–14]).

The appellants appealed against this final order, contending that the appointment of a curator was not justified ([para 6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the appointment of a curator was justified based on the claims of material irregularities raised by the registrar ([para 41]). The court needed to determine:

  1. Whether material irregularities warranting the appointment of a curator were present?
  2. Whether less intrusive alternatives existed to address the concerns without resorting to curatorship?
  3. Whether the registrar had followed appropriate procedures in bringing the application for curatorship?

R – Rules

The court relied heavily on statutes relevant to the governance of medical schemes, specifically:

  • Section 56(1) of the MS Act: This provision allows the registrar to apply for the appointment of a curator if material irregularities come to light or if the scheme is not in sound financial condition ([para 8]).
  • Section 5(1) and (2) of the FI Act: These sections outline the registrar’s powers to seek curatorship based on evidence showing that such an appointment is desirable to address identified issues ([para 10]).

Precedents were also referenced, notably Executive Officer FSB v Dynamic Wealth Ltd and Others ([para 13]), where the court articulated the requirements for establishing “good cause” for curatorship under the FI Act.

A – Application

The court evaluated the evidence presented by the registrar against the backdrop of the statutory requirements. It found multiple substantive failures in the governance of the scheme:

  1. Disregard of MS Act Provisions: The trustees were found to have breached multiple provisions, including engaging non-accredited brokers and failing to terminate non-compliant contracts ([paras 15–20]).
  2. Elections and Governance Violations: Allegations of misconduct during the election of trustees were substantiated, indicating that the Sapling representatives had manipulated the election process to secure governance for their benefit ([paras 31–33])—especially given they were not long-term members of the scheme prior to their election ([para 34]).
  3. Lack of Cooperation with Regulatory Oversight: The court noted the BOT’s confrontational stance toward regulatory inspections and the registrar, which highlighted a broader unwillingness to comply with governance norms ([paras 37–39]).

The cumulative effect of these issues was determined to pose risks to the beneficiaries’ interests, justifying the appointment of a curator ([para 42]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court’s decision to appoint a curator for the Medshield Medical Scheme. The ruling emphasized both the necessity for immediate action to protect the interests of beneficiaries and the absence of viable alternatives to curatorship, given the systemic issues in governance and compliance ([paras 48–50]). The court concluded that these material irregularities justified the registrar's request, leading to the dismissal of the appeal with costs, including those incurred by dual counsel ([paras 51–52]).

This decision illustrates the court's commitment to vigorous oversight of medical schemes, reinforcing the responsibilities of trustees to adhere strictly to legal standards in the administration of funds and management practices that affect beneficiaries.
