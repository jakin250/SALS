Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: Slinger v The State (233/13) [2013] ZASCA 197

## F – Facts

The case arises from criminal proceedings related to events that occurred on 22 July 1998, at Eldorado Park. The appellant, Boy Wilken ‘Willem’ Slinger, along with co-accused, was charged in the Vereeniging Regional Court on multiple counts, specifically four counts of robbery with aggravating circumstances, three counts of illegal possession of a firearm, two counts of kidnapping, and one count of illegal possession of ammunition. Throughout the protracted trial, which lasted several years, the two co-accused of the appellant passed away, leaving Slinger as the sole defendant.

In May 2002, the trial court convicted the appellant on three of the four robbery counts, both kidnapping counts, and one count of illegal possession of a firearm. Following his conviction, Slinger immediately fled, causing a delay of 4.5 years before his rearrest and subsequent sentencing on 28 November 2006 to an effective term of 23 years’ imprisonment ([1]–[2]).

The appellant subsequently sought leave to appeal against his convictions and sentences, which were denied by the trial court and the High Court on 5 May 2008. However, on 17 October 2011, he successfully persuaded the High Court to grant him leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) specifically against the refusal of his petition ([2]).

## I – Issues

The central issue before the SCA was whether the High Court had correctly refused the appellant's application for leave to appeal. This determination hinged upon whether there were reasonable prospects of success for the appellant’s appeal concerning his convictions and sentences ([3]–[4]). 

The appeal did not encompass the merits of the original convictions or sentences directly but focused explicitly on the procedural correctness of the High Court's earlier refusal ([3]). 

## R – Rules

In determining whether to grant leave to appeal, the SCA relied on established legal principles concerning the threshold necessary for such an application. The appellant was required to demonstrate more than mere arguability—he needed to provide a sound and rational basis justifying a conclusion that an appellate court could reasonably arrive at a different decision than that made by the trial court ([4]).

The court also reaffirmed key legal precedents relevant to robbery charges, joint possession, and the evaluation of witness evidence, particularly with respect to single witness identification and the implications thereof in criminal law. Notable cases included *Matshona v S*, *S v Smith*, and *S v Mbuli*, which provide guidance on the standards of evidence required for convictions based on joint criminal enterprise and possession ([1], [4], [11]).

## A – Application

The SCA meticulously analyzed the implications of the evidence presented at the trial, particularly the testimony of the key witness, Mr. Bevin Kyde. Kyde identified the appellant as a participant in the armed robbery events, asserting his involvement during the robbery of ATM cash. The trial court had previously accepted this identification, despite Kyde being a single witness. 

However, the SCA noted potential flaws in the identification process and the state's inability to conclusively link the appellant to significant parts of the criminal activities, given that he only appeared on the scene after the initial robbery had taken place. Consequently, there emerged a reasonable prospect that another court could find that the evidence against the appellant was insufficient for conviction ([6]–[10]).

Moreover, the appellate court considered the legality of the appellant's conviction for illegal possession of a firearm, which was predicated on a finding of joint possession related to the actions of his co-accused. This reasoning, however, was found to be flawed under the principles established in *S v Mbuli*, which contravened the earlier *Khambule* case, reflecting a significant evolution in legal understanding on the matter ([12]).

The analysis culminated in the conclusion that the appellant had reasonable grounds for his appeal against convictions for robbery and kidnapping. The court underscored that both the trial court and the High Court had erred in denying the appellant leave to appeal on these substantial bases ([10]–[12]).

## C – Conclusion

The SCA held that the High Court had incorrectly refused the appellant leave to appeal, given the reasonable prospects of success evident from the analysis of the trial's evidentiary standards and the identification processes employed. Therefore, the SCA set aside the High Court’s order dismissing Slinger’s appeal application and granted such leave to appeal against both convictions and sentences ([13]–[15]). This ruling has significant implications for the appellant's right to challenge the validity of his convictions and reflects broader considerations regarding the fairness of legal processes in South African criminal law. 

In a broader context, the case underscores crucial jurisprudential issues surrounding the reliability of witness testimony, the standards for joint possession in criminal enterprises, and the procedural guarantees afforded to accused persons, all vital for maintaining integrity in the judicial process.
