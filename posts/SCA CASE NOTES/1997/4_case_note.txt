Source Folder: 1997

# FIRAC Case Note: Farouk Jasat v The State (Case No 154/96)

**F – Facts:**
The appellant, Farouk Jasat, an attorney in Pietermaritzburg, was convicted by a regional court for "housebreaking with intent to trespass and trespass" ([para 2]). In April 1993, his colleague, Attorney Baboo Akoo, who had allowed him access to a filing cabinet in his office, abandoned his practice and fled to the United Kingdom. Subsequently, Akoo advised his articled clerk, Chutterpaul, via phone to remove certain items from the office. On 5 April 1993, the Natal Law Society intervened, changed the locks, and refused to grant Jasat access until procedural formalities were completed ([paras 1–5]).

On 6 April 1993, a suspicious incident unfolded when witness Pienaar encountered two men near Akoo's office, one of whom was carrying a box of files ([paras 6–8]). Pienaar and security guard Dlamini reported their observations of signs of forced entry, later leading to police involvement and revelations of missing office items ([paras 9–10]). Jasat's vehicle was identified as parked near the scene, linking him to the incident.

The trial court ruled in favor of the prosecution based on eyewitness accounts and circumstantial evidence, despite Jasat claiming an alibi, which the court found unconvincing ([paras 10–11]).

**I – Issues:**
The main legal questions central to the appeal included:
1. Whether housebreaking with intent to commit trespass was a legally competent verdict given the circumstances ([para 12]).
2. Whether the appellant was prejudiced when convicted of a lesser charge without prior indication ([paras 13–14]).
3. Whether the evidence satisfactorily established a contravention of the Trespass Act 6 of 1959 ([paras 16–17]).

**R – Rules:**
Key legal rules and precedents supporting the court's decision included:

1. **Trespass Act 6 of 1959**: Specifically, Section 1(1)(a) which states that a person without permission enters or is present in a building and commits an offence ([para 11]).
  
2. **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**: Sections regarding competent verdicts aligned with the charge ([para 14]).

3. **Principles of Evidence**: The credibility and impartiality of witnesses, particularly regarding identification ([para 10]), and the obligation for an accused to foresee the possibility of various outcomes when defending against a charge ([para 14]).

**A – Application:**
The court evaluated the trial court's findings, emphasizing the eyewitness accounts and circumstantial evidence against Jasat, including the suspicious presence of his vehicle and the unauthorized access to Akoo's office. The court reiterated that the appellant's alibi lacked credibility given his legal expertise, and it was reasonable to conclude he was involved in a break-in ([paras 10–19]).

Further, the court examined the issue of whether the change of charges constituted prejudice:
- It determined that being an experienced lawyer, Jasat could not claim surprise by the conviction on a lesser charge since he was always aware that the facts might support such a verdict ([para 14]).
- Assertions concerning the burden of proving lack of permission were found unpersuasive, as evidence suggested Jasat had no lawful right to access the premises ([paras 18–19]).

**C – Conclusion:**
The appeal was dismissed, with the court modifying the conviction to accurately reflect the nature of the offence as "housebreaking with intent to contravene section 1(1)(a) of the Trespass Act, 1959" ([para 20]). 

The judgment underlined the importance of being precise in formal charges and the validity of circumstantial evidence in establishing guilt. The implications reinforce the critical interaction between legal formalism and substantive justice in criminal proceedings, particularly for accused parties with professional legal backgrounds.
