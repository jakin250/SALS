Mokone v Tassos Properties CC and Another [2017] ZACC 25

In this case, Ntswaki Joyce Mokone (the applicant) entered into a written lease agreement with Tassos Properties CC (the first respondent) on 1 March 2004, which included a right of first refusal to purchase the leased premises if Tassos decided to sell ([2]–[3]).

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 25

F – Facts

In this case, Ntswaki Joyce Mokone (the applicant) entered into a written lease agreement with Tassos Properties CC (the first respondent) on 1 March 2004, which included a right of first refusal to purchase the leased premises if Tassos decided to sell ([2]–[3]). The initial lease period was one year, expiring on 28 February 2005, but it was renewed orally under the same terms until 3 May 2006, when a handwritten endorsement extended the lease to 31 May 2014 ([2]–[4]).

In the meantime, on 15 July 2009, Tassos sold the leased premises to Blue Canyon Properties 125 CC (the second respondent), resulting in a transfer on 1 March 2010. Mokone became aware of the sale and sought to exercise her right of pre-emption by notifying Tassos in writing on 27 January 2012, but her right was rebuffed ([5]–[7]). Consequently, Mokone initiated action against both Tassos and Blue Canyon in the High Court, claiming that the endorsement extending the lease also extended her right of pre-emption ([8]–[9]).

The High Court concluded that the right of pre-emption was not extended with the lease and postponed further proceedings due to unresolved issues. Leave to appeal was subsequently denied by both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal, necessitating the appeal to the Constitutional Court ([11]).

I – Issues

The Constitutional Court addressed several critical legal issues:

  1. Whether leave to appeal should be granted in both applications ([15]).
  2. Whether the right of pre-emption was renewed together with the lease when it was extended on 3 May 2006 ([15]).
  3. Whether the endorsement extending the lease needed to comply with the formalities of the Alienation of Land Act ([15]).
  4. Whether Mokone could resist eviction despite the lease’s expiration and the due notice to vacate ([15]).

R – Rules

  • Common Law Principles: The Court aligned its reasoning with English law principles that categorize a right of pre-emption as a collateral condition, separate from the landlord-tenant relationship. This principle posits that unless there is explicit evidence of intent to include collateral terms in a lease extension, they do not automatically renew with the lease ([20]–[24]).
  • Alienation of Land Act: The relevant provisions stipulate that an alienation must be in writing and signed by the parties involved; however, the Court noted that a right of pre-emption does not effectuate an alienation until exercised ([44]–[47]).
  • Judicial Discretion in Stay of Proceedings: The principles surrounding judicial discretion to stay proceedings were examined, particularly in accordance with interests of justice, as enshrined in Section 173 of the Constitution ([66]–[69]).

A – Application

  1. Leave to Appeal: The Court found that the issues raised had significance beyond this particular case and involved questions of law that warranted consideration at a higher level, thus granting leave to appeal ([15]–[19]).
  2. Renewal of Right of Pre-emption: The Court rejected the High Court's rigid interpretation that viewed collateral terms, such as the right of pre-emption, as automatically unrenewed upon lease extension. Instead, the Court adopted a more nuanced interpretation focused on the parties’ actual intentions at the time of lease extension, arguing that ordinary laypersons would interpret "extension" to include all terms of the agreement, including the right of pre-emption ([20]–[40]).
  3. Compliance with the Alienation of Land Act: The Court reasoned that the endorsement's lack of signature by Mokone was not relevant to the validity of her right of pre-emption under the Alienation of Land Act since the right could only be exercised through a later agreement that would then require compliance with such formalities ([44]–[56]).
  4. Eviction Proceedings: The Court ruled that it was just and equitable to stay eviction proceedings against Mokone pending the final resolution of her assertion of the right of pre-emption, citing considerations of justice and the potential for her eviction to be unjust if her right was ultimately upheld ([70]–[72]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ruled in favor of Mokone, stating that the extension of the lease also extended her right of pre-emption. The orders of the High Court were set aside, and the matter was remitted for the determination of unresolved issues ([75]). The implications of this ruling redefined the understanding of lease agreements and pre-emption rights within South African law—emphasizing the need for common law to evolve alongside statutory expectations while promoting equitable outcomes in property relations. The case highlights a significant judicial shift towards interpreting contracts in a manner that reflects the intentions of ordinary people rather than rigid legal formalism.

The Court’s approach affirms its commitment to ensuring fairness and justice in contractual relations and to providing necessary protections for parties asserting rights under potentially nuanced circumstances.
