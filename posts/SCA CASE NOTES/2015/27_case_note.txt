Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Medirite (Pty) Limited v South African Pharmacy Council

**Neutral Citation:** Medirite v South African Pharmacy Council (197/2014) [2015] ZASCA 27 (20 March 2015).

## F – Facts

The case originated from the appellant, Medirite (Pty) Limited, which operates pharmacies within the premises of supermarkets owned by Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd. This business model, successful since 2003, includes 129 licensed pharmacies utilizing the flow of foot traffic generated by the supermarkets ([para 2]). 

The South African Pharmacy Council (first respondent) issued rules under the Pharmacy Act 53 of 1974, obligating pharmacy premises to be distinctly demarcated from other business premises ([para 1]). On 2 March 2012, the Council amended its Rules Relating to Good Pharmacy Practice (GPP) to include stricter requirements mandating that pharmacy premises maintain a permanent and solid demarcation (specifically a wall from floor to ceiling), which could significantly alter the existing configuration of Medirite’s pharmacies ([para 5]). The appellant objected, citing substantial construction costs (estimates of R200,000) and potential disruption to its business model as primary concerns ([para 6]).

Despite the appellant's attempts to engage with the Council to withdraw the amendment, these efforts were unavailing, leading to the filing of review proceedings based on the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([paras 7-8]). The high court dismissed this application, prompting the appellant to appeal ([para 1]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal questions before the court included:

1. Did the amendment of the GPP Rules by the South African Pharmacy Council constitute rational administrative action in accordance with PAJA?
2. Were the requirements regarding permanent wall demarcation reasonable in light of the potential adverse impacts on pharmacy operations and the business model of the appellant?
3. Did the first respondent provide sufficient justification for the necessity of such stringent definitions of "pharmacy" premises? ([paras 9-11]).

## R – Rules

The court's evaluation was largely framed by the provisions of PAJA, particularly section 33 of the Constitution, which mandates that administrative actions be lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair ([para 9]). The factors for evaluating the rationality of the administrative action included:

- The necessity of regulatory requirements vs. the impact on those regulated ([para 21]).
- Whether the administrative action was guided by relevant considerations and whether it took into account the possible effects on affected parties ([paras 21-24]).
- The requirement in section 6(2) of PAJA asserting that a decision can be reviewed if it is not rationally connected to the information before the administrator ([para 10]).

Prominent cases, such as *Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Environmental Affairs* ([2004] ZACC 15), and the principles of proportionality and reasonableness as articulated by the Constitutional Court were also referenced. These stipulate that decisions must not be excessively burdensome or unjustifiably invasive ([paras 20-21]).

## A – Application

In reviewing the amendment's introduction of strict requirements regarding pharmacy demarcation, the court placed significant emphasis on the absence of supporting evidence for why such a requirement was deemed necessary. The Council had not previously raised concerns regarding the existing compliance by Medirite’s pharmacies with the demarcation rule ([paras 11-12]). Historical inspections revealed that no violations had been noted, suggesting that existing practices already satisfied the need for demarcation ([para 11]).

Financial projections demonstrating the modification's economic impact strengthened the appellant's argument that the requirements would result in irrational business burdens ([para 6]). The court highlighted that under PAJA, the first respondent’s rationale for the wall lacked factual backing and was seen as arbitrary ([paras 16-17]). 

Furthermore, the Council could have explored less invasive alternatives, which would mitigate adverse impacts yet still achieve the goal of demarcation ([para 22]). This failure to thoroughly consider proportionality ultimately rendered the amendment unreasonable in its implementation ([para 23]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the South African Pharmacy Council's amendment introducing stringent demarcation requirements was neither rational nor reasonable and thus should be set aside ([para 23]). As a result, the court ruled in favor of the appellant, allowing the appeal with costs, including the costs of two counsels ([para 24]).

This case illustrates the critical importance of reasoned and evidence-based administrative decision-making, particularly within regulated industries. It emphasizes the principle that while regulatory bodies must enforce compliance, they are also bound by constitutional standards requiring rationality and proportionality in their regulatory approaches. The ruling has significant implications for the regulatory landscape governing pharmacy practices in South Africa, particularly for pharmacies operating within other commercial contexts.
