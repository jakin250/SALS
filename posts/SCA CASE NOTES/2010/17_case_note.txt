Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH
Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of Majomatic 115 (Pty) Limited v Kouga Municipality (303/09) [2010] ZASCA 17

## F – Facts
The case Majomatic 115 (Pty) Limited v Kouga Municipality revolved around the validity of a contract of sale entered into by Kouga Municipality (the Municipality) with Majomatic 115 (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) for the alienation of certain municipal property in November 2003. The case was heard by the Supreme Court of Appeal on 23 February 2010 and the judgment was delivered on 18 March 2010.

The Municipal Ordinance 20 of 1974 (Cape) governed the procedures for the alienation of municipal property, particularly outlined in sections 124 and 172. On 14 November 2003, an advertisement was placed inviting tenders for the sale of various properties, including the property of interest, which specified a submission deadline of 28 November 2003. However, the advertisement failed to mention that objections to the sale could be lodged or the proper channels for lodging such objections, ultimately undermining compliance with the several procedural prerequisites mandated by the Ordinance.  
  
At the heart of the controversy was whether the legal procedures outlined per the Ordinance were still in effect following the implementation of the new Constitution. The initial judgment by Schoeman J of the Eastern Cape High Court stated that compliance with said provisions was necessary for a lawful contract conclusion ([1]-[2]).

## I – Issues
The principal legal issue was whether the procedures prescribed by sections 124 and 172 of the Municipal Ordinance 20 of 1974 were still in force in November 2003, or whether they had been impliedly repealed by Item 2 of Schedule 6 of the Constitution. Specifically, the court needed to ascertain if compliance with these provisions was essential for the validity of the contract of sale that took place in November 2003 ([1]-[5]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal authorities for this case stemmed from the Municipal Ordinance 20 of 1974, specifically:

1. **Section 124(1) and (2)**, which required the Municipality to adhere to proper procedures for the alienation of municipal property, including the advertisement of intentions to sell and consideration of any objections.
2. **Section 172(1) and (2)**, which mandated the Municipality to invite tenders before entering contracts for the sale of goods or the execution of any works exceeding a prescribed financial threshold.

Additionally, the interpretation of **Item 2 of Schedule 6 of the Constitution** was pivotal. It stipulates that any law in force prior to the Constitution's enactment remains valid unless amended, repealed, or inconsistent with the Constitution ([6]-[7]).

Precedents from previous cases, such as **CDA Boerdery (Edms) Bpk v Nelson Mandela Metropolitan Municipality**, were also cited, outlining the survival of certain "old order" legislation unless inconsistent with the new constitutional framework ([7]-[8]).

## A – Application
The court concluded that the argument presented by the Appellant, asserting that the Ordinance had been entirely repealed, failed to hold under the scrutiny of Item 2 of Schedule 6 of the Constitution. The court affirmed that existing legislation would remain operative unless explicitly repealed or inconsistent with constitutional provisions. This interpretation led to the conclusion that only the unconstitutional portions of sections 124(1) could be rendered void, while remaining portions, particularly section 124(2), maintained their validity.

The argument made by the Appellant that alternative legislation (such as sections 10G(5) and 10G(11) of the Local Government Transition Act 209 of 1993) provided a framework for property alienation proved unpersuasive, as these sections were deemed either irrelevant or non-specific regarding the procedures for alienation of municipal property. The court reinforced the necessity for compliance with the Ordinance procedures and underscored that the failure to adhere to such requirements led to the invalidity of the contract ([5]-[13]).

Additionally, contrasting arguments from the Second and Third Respondents highlighted a counterpoint regarding the survival of oversight powers, yet these were determined as distinguishable from the case at hand.

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, validating the lower court's position that the failure to comply with the procedural requirements of the Municipal Ordinance invalidated the sale of the property. The court's decision reaffirmed the significance of adhering to established statutory requirements for the alienation of municipal property, underlining that old legislative frameworks retain their effect unless explicitly extinguished by constitutional enactments or incompatible with them ([14]-[15]).

This ruling underscores the delicate balance between pre-existing legislation and new constitutional frameworks in South African law and the critical importance of compliance with procedural regulations. The implications of this judgment stress that municipalities must carefully observe statutory mandates to avoid invalidating prospective contracts with private entities.
