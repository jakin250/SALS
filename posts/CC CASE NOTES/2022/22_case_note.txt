Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

### FIRAC Case Note: e.tv (Pty) Limited v Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies and Others; Media Monitoring Africa and Another v e.tv (Pty) Limited and Others [2022] ZACC 22

**Facts**: 

This case arises from the urgent appeal concerning South Africa’s transition from analogue to digital television broadcasting. Initially, a deadline for the analogue switch-off was set for 31 March 2022, following a prolonged public participation process initiated in 2007 to implement a Broadcasting Digital Migration Policy (BDM Policy) ([1]–[10]). The applicants, e.tv, a leading private broadcaster, and two non-profit organizations, Media Monitoring Africa (MMA) and SOS Support Public Broadcasting, sought to challenge both the original switch-off date and the registration cut-off date for set-top boxes (STBs), which was set for 31 October 2021. The two organizations contended that millions of South Africans still depended on analogue signals and would be cut off from broadcasting services if the switch-off proceeded without further consultation and the provision of STBs ([11]–[16]).

In previous High Court proceedings, the applicants sought a range of relief that included declaring the Minister’s actions unconstitutional due to inadequate provision for affected households. The High Court ultimately extended the switch-off date to 30 June 2022 but dismissed the application for more extensive relief, leading to the current appeal ([20]–[22]).

**Issues**: 

1. Does this Court have jurisdiction to hear the matter? 
2. Should leave to appeal be granted directly to this Court on an urgent basis? 
3. Were the rights to social assistance and freedom of expression infringed as a result of the Minister's decisions? 
4. What is the nature of the Minister’s power in determining the analogue switch-off date, and did the Minister act rationally?
5. What is the appropriate remedy given the circumstances?

**Rules**: 

The court’s authority is rooted in its power to review constitutional matters as per section 167(3) of the Constitution of South Africa. Moreover, the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) sets forth the requirements for administrative actions involving public consultation ([24]–[27]). Jurisprudence related to procedural rationality was drawn upon, including the criteria from *Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of SA* and *Affordable Medicines Trust*, establishing that any exercise of public power must be rational and lawful ([40]–[44]).

Precedents such as *Democratic Alliance* and *KwaZulu-Natal Joint Liaison Committee* were invoked to argue that the Minister’s obligations to consult were integral to the legality of her decisions ([16], [24], [28]).

**Application**: 

The Constitutional Court found that the ministerial decisions lacked procedural rationality as there was no meaningful consultation with key stakeholders impacted by the analogue switch-off date and STB registration cut-off ([52]–[53]). The court underscored that rationality in this context necessitated a reasonable opportunity for public input in determining critical timelines that could affect service access for millions of citizens. The failure to provide adequate notice and an opportunity to register for STBs before the deadlines set by the Minister constituted a breach of procedural obligations ([60]–[67]).

The Minister attempted to justify the decisions based on historical participation thresholds and previous awareness campaigns, but the Court found the methods inadequate in light of the urgency and gravity of the implication for the large swath of the populace reliant on analogue broadcasting ([68]–[72]).

The decision to impose the 31 October registration cut-off was particularly scrutinized as it was deemed irrational, given the historical context that left large numbers of the affected population unaware and unprepared ([78]–[80]). The Court also noted that digital migration must endeavor to ensure that vulnerable populations are not disproportionately adversely affected ([73]–[74]).

**Conclusion**: 

The Court upheld the appeal, concluding that both the determination of the analogue switch-off date and the STB registration deadline were unconstitutional and invalid. It set aside both decisions, reinstating the need for the Minister to engage in genuine consultation before re-establishing a new switch-off date ([103]). The judgment further highlighted the principle of legality and procedural fairness in administrative decisions, emphasizing the necessity to consider the socio-economic consequences of public policy decisions impacting access to essential services, like broadcasting ([100], [110]).

The Court granted the Minister costs in the appeal while applying the *Biowatch* principle to favour the applicants in terms of costs owed, recognizing their role in protecting public interest, despite the commercial underpinnings of one of the applicants ([107]–[110]).
