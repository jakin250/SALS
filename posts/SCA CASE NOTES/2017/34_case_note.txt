Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Mudau v The State ([2017] ZASCA 34)

## F – Facts:
In this case, Nndwambi Mudau (the first appellant) and Tshivhangwalo Rathogwa Mudau (the second appellant) were convicted in the Limpopo Local Division of the High Court for murder, attempted murder, and robbery committed with aggravating circumstances ([1]). The trial court convicted them based on witness testimony and confessions made by both appellants. The key evidence was provided by Mr. Raymond Lishivha and Ms. Portia Budeli, who testified that two assailants entered a shack, resulting in the death of Mr. Robert Mandiwana and serious injuries to Mr. Lishivha ([3]). The testimonies established the context of the crime but could not identify the assailants directly.

After their initial conviction in 2003 and a life sentence imposed on the murder charge, the appellants sought to appeal their convictions and sentences. Leave to appeal was granted on 6 December 2012. During the trial, the State sought to introduce confessions purportedly made by the appellants, but the defense objected on grounds that they were not freely and voluntarily given, alleging coercion and improper procedures in the taking of these confessions ([4],[5]).

## I – Issues:
The primary legal issues in this appeal revolve around the admissibility of the confessions as evidence, whether there were misdirections by the trial court, and whether the rights of the appellants to a fair trial were infringed ([2],[8]). Specific concerns included:
1. Whether the trial court erred in admitting the confessions given that they allegedly failed to satisfy the statutory requirements outlined in the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([4],[9]).
2. Whether the trial court improperly shifted the onus of proof regarding the voluntariness of the confessions to the appellants ([23]).
3. The overall impact of these issues on the right to a fair trial as guaranteed by the Constitution ([29]).

## R – Rules:
The legal framework governing the admissibility of confessions includes:
1. **Section 217 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**: This section mandates that confessions must be made freely and voluntarily, and a confession’s validity depends on the satisfaction of these conditions by the prosecution ([9],[10]).
2. **Section 219 of the same act**: This provision states that confessions made by one accused cannot be used against co-accused persons, thus ensuring fair treatment and separate assessments of each accused’s guilt ([12],[28]).
3. **Section 35(5) of the South African Constitution**: This section requires that evidence obtained in violation of the rights in the Bill of Rights should be excluded, especially if its admission would render the trial unfair ([12]).

The court also made reference to case law, including *R v Becker*, which defined confessions as unequivocal admissions of guilt and highlighted the need for thorough adherence to statutory requirements ([10]).

## A – Application:
In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal identified significant flaws in how the trial court addressed the confessions. Notably:
- **Admissibility Issues**: The court found multiple omissions and contradictions in the recording of the confessions, indicating that the strict requirements of Section 217 were not met. Specifically, questions relating to the voluntariness and presence of coercion were inadequately documented ([15],[21]).
- **Trial Within a Trial**: The court criticized the trial judge for incorrectly placing the burden of proof on the appellants during the trial within a trial concerning the voluntariness of the confessions. Instead, the onus was on the State to prove that the confessions were admissible ([23]).
- **Bias and Perception of Impartiality**: The remarks made by the trial judge implied bias against the second appellant and raised concerns about the fairness of the proceedings. This is significant in maintaining judicial impartiality, as established in various case law ([25],[27]).
- The court concluded that the confessions, having been ruled inadmissible, could be deemed the sole basis for the convictions, leading to their inevitable overturning ([29]).

## C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that there were serious procedural irregularities and misdirections during the trial, ultimately infringing upon the appellants' rights to a fair trial as prescribed by the Constitution ([29]). The admission of the confessions was found to be erroneous due to their failure to meet statutory and constitutional standards, and upon this basis, the appeal was upheld. 

The court vacated the appellants' convictions for murder, assault with intent to commit grievous bodily harm, and robbery with aggravating circumstances, instead declaring both appellants not guilty and discharging them on all charges ([30]). This case underscores the paramount importance of adhering to procedural safeguards in the criminal justice system to protect individual rights and ensure fair trials.
