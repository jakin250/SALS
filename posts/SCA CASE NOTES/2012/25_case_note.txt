Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Notes for Kogana v SBV Services (384/11) [2012] ZASCA 25

## F – Facts
The present case arises from a robbery occurring on 28 June 2007, involving SBV Services (Pty) Ltd, a security company contracted to deliver cash to ATMs. Glenville Mervin Kogana, the appellant and supervisor of the security crew, was involved in a cash delivery where the crew was ambushed by armed robbers. 

On the date of the robbery, Kogana was supervising a crew of four tasked with delivering R5 084 000 in cash using an armored vehicle. Upon arrival at a service station ATM, the crew followed standard procedure, which included securing the premises and taking necessary precautions before unloading cash ([paras 1–6]). 

During the unloading operation, Kogana observed a diesel spill, which distracted the crew, potentially leading to inadequate security measures. While he attempted to address the diesel cap issue, the robbery unfolded: as Kogana and colleagues were overwhelmed, the robbers stole cash and fled in their vehicle ([paras 8–10]). Subsequently, SBV initiated legal proceedings against Kogana in the Eastern Cape High Court, alleging he neglected his duties leading to the loss ([paras 11–12]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue was whether there was a causal link between Kogana’s alleged breach of duty and the resultant robbery and loss of money. Specifically, the court needed to determine whether the breach of secure procedures by Kogana caused the loss of money, thus establishing liability ([paras 13–14]). Secondary issues included the adequacy of SBV's shift from a delictual claim to one based on contractual obligations involving Kogana’s duties and Kogana's claimed defense against the allegations of negligence ([paras 12–13]).

## R – Rules
The foundational legal rule pertains to the necessity of establishing causation in claims of negligence and breach of duty. The law requires that a plaintiff demonstrate that the defendant's actions were a proximate cause of the injury or loss ([para 14]). The relevant standard for causation in this context is whether the money would "probably not have been stolen had [Kogana] properly performed his duties" ([para 14]).

Moreover, standard reference cases include *Lillicrap, Wassenaar and Partners v Pilkington Brothers (SA) (Pty) Ltd* and *AB Ventures Ltd v Siemens Ltd*, which delineate circumstances under which contractual duties can give rise to liability for damages through failure to fulfill said duties ([para 12]).

## A – Application
The court rigorously analyzed the factual circumstances surrounding the robbery, closely examining witness testimony and the consistency of the operational procedures followed by SBV's crew. It was noted that Kogana had two primary duties: to ensure that Mr Mdlalisa was positioned strategically and to prevent Mr Dunywa from exiting the vehicle ([paras 16–20]). 

However, the court found deficiencies in the arguments presented by SBV regarding causation. Specifically, while an expert testimony suggested that proper positioning of guards might have deterred robbers, the court emphasized that this was speculative and did not take into account the possibility of insider knowledge possessed by the robbers ([para 21]). 

The court also highlighted that the evidence did not concretely support the assertion that had Kogana properly executed his duties, the crew could have successfully averted the robbery. The nature of the ambush suggested that the robbers had likely anticipated the crew's standard operational behavior ([para 22]).

Ultimately, the court ruled that SBV failed to meet the burden of proof necessary to establish a causal nexus between Kogana's alleged negligence and the loss incurred. Consequently, the claim could not succeed under either the delictual or contractual framework as the evidence fell short of establishing that the robbery would not have occurred if Kogana had adhered to proper security protocols ([para 22]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Kogana's appeal, concluding that the evidence did not substantiate a causal link between his actions and the theft of funds. Consequently, the order of the lower court was set aside, and Kogana was absolved of liability for the loss, with costs awarded in his favor ([paras 23]).

The judgment underscores the burden of proof required for claims of negligence, particularly in circumstances involving the interplay of multiple variables, including the unexpected actions of criminals. This case serves as an important reference regarding causation in negligence within the context of employment duties in the security industry and highlights the challenges plaintiffs face in establishing a direct link between breaches of duty and resultant losses.
