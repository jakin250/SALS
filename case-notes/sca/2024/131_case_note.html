FIRAC Case Note: The Public Protector of South Africa v The Chairperson of the Section 194(1) Committee and Others (627/2023) [2024] ZASCA 131

F – Facts

The case arises against the backdrop of the removal proceedings against the former Public Protector, Ms Busisiwe Mkhwebane, initiated by the National Assembly. Mkhwebane was first appointed Public Protector in 2016. On February 21, 2020, a motion was submitted by Ms Natasha Mazzone of the Democratic Alliance to investigate Mkhwebane's misconduct under section 194(1) of the South African Constitution; this motion was subsequently approved by the National Assembly and referred to an independent panel for preliminary assessment ([paras 2–3]).

On March 16, 2021, based on a report from the independent panel, the National Assembly resolved to continue with the inquiry regarding Mkhwebane's alleged incompetence and misconduct ([paras 3–4]). Mkhwebane subsequently filed an application in the Western Cape High Court on November 7, 2022, seeking various forms of relief against the actions taken by the first and second respondents, which included the dismissal of her recusal application and the alleged violation of her rights during the inquiry ([paras 6–7]).

The High Court ruling on May 13, 2023, dismissed Mkhwebane's application, stating that a piecemeal review would not be conducive and that no exceptional circumstances were shown ([paras 8–9]). Following this ruling, Mkhwebane filed for an urgent appeal, which the High Court granted leave for, under conditional applications for cross-appeals concerning costs ([para 9]).

After the completion of the inquiry, the committee recommended her removal, which the National Assembly adopted on September 11, 2023. Mkhwebane was officially removed by Presidential action on September 13, 2023, and was succeeded by Ms Kholeka Gcaleka ([paras 10–11]).

The issues escalated further when Mkhwebane's authority to pursue the appeal became contentious, given her removal from office and the question of whether the original proceeding was valid. On March 8, 2024, attempts at substituting Mkhwebane as the appellant were made, claiming a change of status under Rule 15 of the Uniform Rules of Court ([paras 14–15]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues at stake included:

  1. Standing: Whether Mkhwebane retained the standing to appeal post her removal from office and whether the Public Protector's office had authorized the ongoing litigation ([paras 12–14]).
  2. Application of Rule 15: Whether Rule 15 of the Uniform Rules of Court applied in matters following the grant of judgment, allowing for a party's substitution ([paras 17–23]).
  3. Determination of mootness: Whether the appeal had practical effect given Mkhwebane's removal and subsequent events ([paras 30–34]).
  4. Nature of the appeal: If the appeal raised discrete legal questions or merely dealt with moot procedural issues ([paras 35–41]).

R – Rules

The court considered several legal principles and statutory provisions:

  1. Constitutional framework: Section 194 of the Constitution governs the removal process for the Public Protector and outlines the basis for such inquiries ([para 3]).
  2. Uniform Rules of Court (Rule 15): This provides for substitution of parties in cases of change of status but was scrutinized to determine if it applies post-judgment or in the context of this case ([paras 17–18]).
  3. Precedents: The court’s interpretation rested heavily on precedents concerning substitution, standing, and the necessity for concrete controversies in deciding appeals ([paras 36–41]). The court pointed to past decisions emphasizing that appeals should not address academic questions or hypothetical scenarios ([para 33]).

A – Application

In applying these rules to the current situation, the court examined Mkhwebane's standing, determining that:

  • Given her removal, she could not automatically be deemed to still represent the Public Protector's interests in the appeal.
  • The court held that Uniform Rule 15 does not permit a mere substitution notice to validate a new party in an ongoing appeal when essential authority is lacking ([paras 23–24]).

Thus, the notice filed under Rule 15 by Mkhwebane was insufficient since it did not constitute a substantive application nor did it prove adequate authority to act on behalf of the Public Protector. The court concluded that Mkhwebane's actions did not meet the requirements of the law to engage in appellate proceedings effectively.

Furthermore, the appeal was determined to be moot due to the irreversible nature of Mkhwebane's removal and the completion of the National Assembly's proceedings, which negated the purpose of the appeal ([paras 30–34]). The court highlighted that no practical effect could derive from deciding Mkhwebane's appeal, as no effective relief could be granted, reaffirming the fundamental principle that courts do not render advisory opinions on moot matters ([para 35]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately struck the appeal from the roll, noting that it was improperly before the court due to issues surrounding standing and authority to appeal. The decision was accompanied by a cost order against Mkhwebane, reinforcing the court’s position that frivolous appeals not only consume judicial resources but also affect other genuinely deserving cases ([paras 49–50]). This ruling underscores the critical requirement for litigants to adhere to procedural norms and demonstrate valid standing when appearing before courts, particularly in matters of significant public interest involving constitutional offices.
