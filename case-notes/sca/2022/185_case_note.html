Supreme Court of Appeal

Vantage Goldfields SA (Pty) Ltd and Others v Arqomanzi (Pty) Ltd

The adopted business rescue plans, intended to restore the companies' solvency, were not implemented, primarily due to complications in securing third-party funding ([paras 6–7]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022

F – Facts

The case revolves around Vantage Goldfields SA (Pty) Ltd, Vantage Goldfields Limited, and Lombard Insurance Company Limited as appellants against Arqomanzi (Pty) Ltd as the respondent ([paras 1–4]). The background is steeped in the tragic collapse of the Lily Mine on February 5, 2016, resulting in the fatalities of three mine employees and the subsequent placement of the owning company, Mako,香港 Imperial Mining Company (Pty) Ltd (MIMCO), into business rescue on April 4, 2016, under the relevant framework of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 ([paras 5–6]). The Vantage companies followed similar procedures, and business rescue practitioners were appointed.

The adopted business rescue plans, intended to restore the companies' solvency, were not implemented, primarily due to complications in securing third-party funding ([paras 6–7]). After attempts by Flaming Silver Trading 373 (Pty) Ltd to acquire equity funding failed, the practitioners declared the plans as 'failed' and invited new funding offers. Arqomanzi submitted an offer, but the practitioners informed the creditors that they intended to unilaterally amend the previously adopted business rescue plans ([paras 8–12]).

Arqomanzi contested this unilateral amendment through urgent applications for interdicts against the practitioners from proceeding with the amended plans ([paras 13–14]). The high court confirmed a rule nisi, declaring that the practitioners lacked the authority for such amendments and that they could not disregard previous court orders ([paras 14–16]).

I – Issues

The principal legal question before the court was whether the high court was correct in interdicting the business rescue practitioners from implementing the amended business rescue plans, underlining the prohibition against unilateral amendments to adopted plans ([paras 2, 3, 15]). Additionally, the case raised procedural questions concerning the legitimacy of the costs orders against Lombard Insurance Company Limited, which participated in the application proceedings ([paras 31–39]).

R – Rules

The legal framework pertinent to this case is enshrined within the Companies Act 71 of 2008, particularly sections concerning business rescue planning, the rights of creditors, and the limitations on unilateral amendments ([paras 21–25]). Notably, there is no statutory provision allowing the unilateral amendment of an adopted business rescue plan ([paras 22, 24]). The court referred to several precedents, including Knoop v Gupta and Booysen v Jonkheer Boerewynmakery, underscoring that business rescue practitioners operate under the obligation to adhere to adopted plans and cannot take actions extending beyond statutory boundaries ([paras 23–26]).

The court also cited the principles governing interdicts, emphasizing that a party seeking an interdict must demonstrate a clear right, well-grounded apprehension of harm, and that the harm can’t be adequately compensated by damages ([para 28]).

A – Application

The court assessed whether the business rescue practitioners were authorized to amend the adopted plans based on a clause within those plans. It was found that while the practitioners may have included a provision allowing for amendments, such provision does not confer upon them the authority to contravene the Companies Act. The adopted plans must be discussed and voted upon by creditors, and any unilateral amendments would undermine that democratic process ([paras 20–27]).

In addressing Arqomanzi's standing, the court noted that the previous Roelofse order recognized it as a creditor of VGL, entitling it to protection under the circumstances ([paras 29–30]). Therefore, Arqomanzi established a clear right which justified the interdict against the practitioners, confirming the high court's imposition of a rule nisi ([paras 30–31]).

As for the appeal concerning the costs awarded against Lombard Insurance, the court affirmed that Lombard, by actively participating in the proceedings, voluntarily accepted the associated risks of cost liabilities ([paras 31–39]). The court concluded that it would not second-guess the high court’s discretion unless clear misdirection was evident, which was not the case here ([paras 36–39]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the high court's confirmation of the rule nisi, effectively barring the business rescue practitioners from implementing the amended business rescue plans on the grounds of lack of authority under the Companies Act. This decision reinforces the statutory framework designed to protect creditors and maintain the integrity of business rescue processes in South Africa ([paras 41]).

The court also dismissed the appeal by Lombard Insurance Company concerning the costs order, affirming that active participation in litigation carries the inherent risk of cost implications. This precedent serves to underline that affected parties who choose to engage in such proceedings do so with the understanding of potential financial consequences, reinforcing the principles of accountability and equitable treatment in business rescue contexts ([paras 39–41]).
