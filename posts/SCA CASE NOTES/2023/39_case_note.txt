Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service and Another v Richards Bay Coal Terminal (Pty) Ltd [2023] ZASCA 39

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History** ([paras 1–6])

In the case of *Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service and Another v Richards Bay Coal Terminal (Pty) Ltd*, the core issue involved whether Richards Bay Coal Terminal (RBCT) was confined to a statutory appeal under section 47(9)(e) of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 (CEA) or could also challenge a tariff determination by the Commissioner of the South African Revenue Service (SARS) through judicial review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), and the principle of legality.

RBCT sought to challenge a tariff determination that concluded it had wrongfully claimed rebates exceeding R7 million for non-qualifying activities from 2013 to 2017. Following an investigation and a subsequent letter of demand from SARS, RBCT filed an internal appeal which was rejected by the Excise Appeal Committee (EAC). Subsequently, RBCT sought to appeal this decision and, alternatively, to review the determination and order the production of the decision record. SARS resisted this latter request, arguing the review was not competent as RBCT's remedy was confined to the statutory appeal.

The KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court ruled in favor of RBCT, compelling SARS to provide the record of decision. SARS appealed against this order.

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions** ([paras 1–2, 17])

The essential legal question revolved around the applicability of a dual remedy system in statutory contexts, specifically whether a taxpayer could pursue both a wide appeal and a review under judicial mechanisms. The specific constitutional and statutory concerns included:

1. Whether the statutory remedy under the CEA precludes the taxpayer’s right to seek judicial review in cases concerning tariff determinations.
2. The implications of both administrative justice principles under the Constitution and the provisions of PAJA regarding the transparency and accountability of administrative actions associated with tax law.

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon** ([paras 10–22])

The judgments relied upon in reaching the decision included:

1. The principles articulated in *Competition Commission of South Africa v Standard Bank* ([2020] ZACC 2), which affirmed that orders compelling respondents to disclose records in review proceedings are indeed appealable and that jurisdiction must first be determined before enforcing such disclosure.
2. The *BCE Food Service Equipment v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service* ([2017] ZAGPJ HC 243), where the court held that statutory appeal mechanisms, such as those prescribed in the CEA, do not oust the right to pursue a review under PAJA.
3. The significant constitutional principles established in *Zondi v MEC for Traditional and Local Government Affairs* ([2005] ZACC 2), which indicated that provisions must be construed consistently with PAJA unless expressly stated otherwise. 

These cases collectively establish the boundaries and interrelations between statutory appeal rights and judicial reviews under PAJA, emphasizing the legislative intent that does not aim to remove the judicial oversight of administrative actions.

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law** ([paras 23–28])

In applying the law to the facts of this case, the court reiterated that while section 47(9)(e) provides a statutory appeal route, it does not negate the right to review under PAJA. The court construed RBCT's situation within the broader constitutional framework, emphasizing that the right to just administrative action must be respected irrespective of statutory procedures. 

The judgment confirmed that administrative decisions could, indeed, be subject to judicial scrutiny to ensure they are not only correct in their outcomes but also lawful and procedurally fair. The court established that denying disclosure of the decision record effectively undermines RBCT’s ability to mount a proper challenge, thus infringing on the right to just administrative action guaranteed under section 33 of the Constitution.

Moreover, the necessity for transparency in administrative governance was highlighted as a core aspect of maintaining public trust and ensuring accountability. The court's acknowledgment of the right to access the record was positioned within the framework of ensuring that affected parties can effectively argue their cases, thereby reinforcing principles of good governance.

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications** ([paras 29])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed SARS's appeal, affirming the decision of the KwaZulu-Natal High Court. The court established that RBCT holds the right to contest the tariff determination both under the appeal process stipulated in the CEA and through a review under PAJA. The judgment reasserted that statutory provisions may provide specific remedies but do not eliminate the fundamental rights enshrined in the Constitution.

The implications of this decision are significant, as it clarifies the scope of judicial review in tax matters within South Africa, ensuring that remedies under administrative law coexist alongside statutory remedies. This fusion promotes a system where accountability and fairness in administrative actions are genuinely upheld, enhancing the legal framework governing taxpayer rights and administrative conduct within the South African revenue system. Therefore, the decision sets a precedent reinforcing the balance of administrative authority and judicial oversight, thus nurturing a legal landscape where transparency and accountability remain paramount.
