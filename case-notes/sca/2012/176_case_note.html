Supreme Court of Appeal

The National Credit Regulator v Standard Bank of South Africa Ltd (231/12)

The matter in question concerns the appeal by the National Credit Regulator (NCR) against Standard Bank of South Africa Limited (the Bank) regarding the regulation of administration fees on housing loans.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012

F – Facts

The matter in question concerns the appeal by the National Credit Regulator (NCR) against Standard Bank of South Africa Limited (the Bank) regarding the regulation of administration fees on housing loans. Historically, these fees were governed by the Usury Act 73 of 1968, which imposed a cap on the fees a lender could charge in relation to loan administration ([1]).

On the repeal of the Usury Act, the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 came into effect, which set a new framework that also addressed service fees for loans, yet the Bank contended that the regulation under the Usury Act did not survive this transition for existing loans. Consequently, the NCR applied to the South Gauteng High Court for a restraining order against the Bank from charging fees in excess of the cap established by the Usury Act. The lower court dismissed the NCR's application, leading to the current appeal ([2]).

The relevant regulation from the Usury Act specified that moneylenders could not charge more than R5.00 as an administration fee for housing loans unless otherwise specified ([5]). Upon the transition to the National Credit Act, the legislation defined a service fee that could be charged, which was capped at R50.00 per month. Section 105(1) of the National Credit Act grants the Minister the power to prescribe service fee calculation methods. The crux of the matter revolves around whether the maxima established under the old Usury Act remain applicable to existing loans post-enactment of the National Credit Act ([6]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue presented in this case is whether the administration fees regulated under the Usury Act survived the repeal and transition to the National Credit Act concerning existing home loans. Specifically, the court had to consider:

  1. Did the repeal of the Usury Act deregulate the administration fees charged on pre-existing home loans?
  2. Does the transitional provision in the National Credit Act preserve the caps imposed on such fees under the Usury Act? ([1]–[2]).

R – Rules

The court predominantly relied on the provisions of both the Usury Act and the National Credit Act. Key rules relevant to this case include:

  1. Usury Act 73 of 1968, specifically section 5(1)(k) and the provisions outlined in the Schedule, which defined administration fees and imposed limits on what could be charged by lenders ([3], [4], [5]).
  2. National Credit Act 34 of 2005, particularly:
  • Section 101(1)(c) – which stipulates conditions under which a credit agreement may require payment.
  • Section 105(1) – which empowers the Minister to prescribe service fees through consultation with the NCR.
  • The transitional provisions in Schedule 3, particularly paragraph 7(2), which states rights and obligations that existed under the previous Act should be preserved unless explicitly provided for otherwise ([6], [8]).

A – Application

In applying the law, the court examined the transitional provisions, observing that they were explicitly designed to maintain existing rights and obligations from the Usury Act up until proper adaptation under the new National Credit Act. The court reiterated that although the Bank argued that the original limitation on fees did not create a "right" or "obligation" to charge a maximum fee, the overall regulatory scheme indicated a clear intention to retain a measure of regulation over charges made by financial institutions to protect borrowers ([8], [10]).

Counsel for the Bank initially pointed at a possible inadvertence in the drafting of the National Credit Act that led to the voiding of previous fee regulations for existing loans. However, in counterargument, the NCR posited that the statutory obligation of the Bank, stemming from the Usury Act, ought to be preserved and could not simply vanish with the change of legislation. The court acknowledged that the entitlement to a capped fee was indeed a prescriptive limitation meant to protect the interests of borrowers and, crucially, held that the limitation imposed by the Usury Act should similarly apply to service fees under the new framework until the Minister amended it as envisioned by section 105 of the National Credit Act ([11], [12]).

The learned judge articulated that the rights conferred by the previous legislative regime were valid and actionable even under the new regime unless explicitly repealed or altered, which had not occurred. Instead, the apparent intention behind the transitional provisions was to safeguard those consumer rights during the shift in legislative structures ([8], [12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded by ruling in favor of the NCR, thus upholding the appeal against the lower court's dismissal. The court declared that Standard Bank was not entitled to charge administration fees exceeding the cap set by the Usury Act for housing loans existing at the time the National Credit Act came into force, unless a new fee was prescribed by the Minister ([14]).

This decision underscores the principle of regulatory continuity in consumer credit legislation, affirming that the rights and obligations imposed by the Usury Act are preserved under the National Credit Act's transitional provisions. The implications of this ruling are significant for protecting consumers, ensuring that their rights are not eroded by legislative changes and reinforcing the ongoing obligation of financial institutions to act within the bounds of established fee regulations until a lawful amendment is made.
