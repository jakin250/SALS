Bapedi Marota Mamone v Commission on Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims and Others [2014] ZACC 36

The investigation identified a historical rivalry between the factions loyal to Kgoši Sekhukhune I and those of his contemporary, Kgoši Mampuru II.

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 36

F – Facts

The matter before the Constitutional Court of South Africa involves a dispute over the rightful kingship of the Bapedi traditional community in Limpopo province. The applicant, Bapedi Marota Mamone, argues that the kingship should not reside under the lineage of Kgoši Sekhukhune I. This controversy ensued after the Commission on Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims (the Commission), following a comprehensive investigation, concluded that the kingship of the Bapedi was established in accordance with customary law and historically resorts under the lineage of Sekhukhune I ([1]–[3]).

The investigation identified a historical rivalry between the factions loyal to Kgoši Sekhukhune I and those of his contemporary, Kgoši Mampuru II. Both men laid claim to the kingship following the death of Kgoši Sekwati I in 1861. The Commission's report noted that Sekhukhune I assumed kingship through a violent challenge to Mampuru II, who fled upon being confronted ([25]–[30]). It asserted the existence of a "might and bloodshed" rule in Bapedi customary law, arguing that kingship could be obtained by force and was contested through historical incidents ([31]).

Dissatisfied with this conclusion, the applicant pursued a review in the High Court, claiming the Commission's decision was irrational and failed to consider pertinent evidence. This was upheld by the High Court and subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which dismissed the appeal affirming the previous findings ([32]–[36]). The case was then escalated to the Constitutional Court.

I – Issues

The central legal issues stem from the arguments regarding the Commission's application of the Bapedi customary law of succession:

  1. Did the Commission act irrationally by concluding that the kingship resorts to the lineage of Kgoši Sekhukhune I in light of the historical record?
  2. Did the Commission fail to consider relevant facts and evidence in its determination ([39]–[40])?
  3. Is the kingship as claimed by the applicant supported by applicable customary law, and what is the constitutional positioning of the Commission's findings?

R – Rules

The applicable legal principles are rooted primarily in customary law as it applies to Bapedi tradition, reinforced by the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003, especially section 25(3) that mandates the Commission to apply the relevant customary law "as they were when the events occurred" ([19]). The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) also factored significantly, as it provides grounds for review when administrative actions disregard relevant facts or lack rational justification ([60]–[63]).

Critically, the Constitution of South Africa recognizes the institution of traditional leadership under section 211, which mandates that traditional authorities function according to the principles of customary law ([13]–[14]). The Commission is required to exercise a fair, objective, and impartial determination, allowing customary law to play a pivotal role in resolving such disputes ([15]–[16]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court's majority judgment, led by Khampepe J, contended that the Commission's decision was rooted in an extensive investigation involving historical material and evidence that conformed to its mandate under the Framework Act ([67]–[70]). Unlike the applicant's contentions, the Court recognized the legitimacy of the Commission's application of the "might and bloodshed" rule, indicating that historical precedent supported the Commission’s conclusion that kingship could be acquired through such means during the relevant time ([72]–[88]).

The analysis also scrutinized whether the Commission sufficiently considered Kashukola's status or the history of successions leading up to Sekhukhune I’s reign. The judgment found that the applicant's historical challenges did not undermine the Commission's decision nor demonstrate a failure to rationally connect the conclusion with documentary evidence.

Conversely, the dissenting judgment by Jafta J pointed towards an oversight by the Commission in inadequately applying the customary law of succession, arguing that the decision disregarded established Bapedi rules which dictated succession solely through the legitimate offspring of a timamollo, suggesting the Commission's lack of adherence to the customary law as a failure in due process and rationality ([61]–[66]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court dismissed the appeal, affirming the decision of the Commission, and ruled that the Commission did not act irrationally nor ignore relevant facts as the applicant had alleged. It concluded that the Commission's decision was aligned with historical and customary norms which allowed kingship to be established through might while simultaneously reinforcing the importance of deference to the specialist decisions of bodies like the Commission in handling customary disputes ([110]–[111]). The implications of this ruling are significant for the recognition of traditional leadership roles and a clearer framework on how historical conflicts may shape present-day adjudications in customary law.

Overall, this case underscores the interplay between customary law, constitutional mandates, and the role of administrative bodies in resolving leadership disputes within traditional communities in South Africa.
