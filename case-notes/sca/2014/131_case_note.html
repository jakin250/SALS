Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 131 #### F – Facts The appellant, Chumlong Lemth

Case Note: Lemthongthai v S (849/2013) [2014] ZASCA 131

F – Facts

The appellant, Chumlong Lemthongthai, a Thai national, applied for 26 permits under the National Environmental Management: Biodiversity Act 10 of 2004 (NEMBA) to shoot and kill rhinos, claiming they would be used for trophy hunting. However, the actual intention was to trade illegally in the rhino horns, with most of the horns exported after illegal hunting operations ([1]-[2]).

Lemthongthai unlawfully manipulated customs documents to facilitate the export of the rhino horns, altering recipient details contrary to the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES) ([2]). He faced multiple counts of contravening the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 (CEA) and NEMBA ([3]-[5]).

Initially, Lemthongthai pleaded guilty to some counts but later changed his plea to not guilty for all charges, before eventually reverting to a guilty plea for all counts ([8]). The appellant was convicted by the Regional Magistrate, who imposed an aggregate sentence of 40 years’ imprisonment based on the manipulation of the permit system aimed at profiting from illegal rhino horn trade ([10]-[11]).

Upon appeal, the High Court reduced his sentence to 30 years, acknowledging misdirections in the foundational sentencing imposed by the Regional Magistrate but ultimately still imposing a significant penalty based on the severity of the offenses and their implications for conservation efforts ([12]-[15]).

I – Issues

The case raises the following legal issues:

  1. Whether the severity of the sentences imposed by the courts is appropriate given the circumstances of the case.
  2. Whether the High Court erred in its assessments regarding Lemthongthai’s conduct and his manipulative actions in respect of the permit system.
  3. What constitutes a proper balancing of punishment concerning the objectives of legislative protections for biodiversity under the NEMBA and CEA ([16]-[19]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal provisions underpinning this case include:

  1. NEMBA:
  • Section 57(1): Prohibits restricted activities concerning listed and protected species without the necessary permits.
  • Section 101(1)(a): Specifies penalties for failing to comply with certain provisions regarding protected species.
  • Sections 80(1)(i) and 102: Outline penalties for improper use of permits and related documents ([4]-[5]).
  1. CEA:
  • Section 80(1)(i): Criminalizes improper use of permits in trading with protected species, tying sentencing to the value of goods involved or a term of imprisonment ([4]).
  1. Constitutional Framework:
  • Section 24 of the Constitution establishes the right to an environment that is not harmful, reflecting citizens’ rights to biodiversity ([19]).

A – Application

The courts examined Lemthongthai's actions in light of both the statutory provisions and broader conservation mandates. The Regional Magistrate’s original sentencing appeared to disregard the limits imposed by the CEA on individual counts and misanalyzed the impact of the appellant’s admissions, treating his guilty plea as an admission of guilt without acknowledging mitigating factors related to his cooperation with authorities ([10]-[11]).

The High Court's analysis recognized some errors in the lower courts' findings but ultimately leaned towards heavier sentences to deter similar future conduct, emphasizing that the illegal exploitation of endangered species necessitates stringent penalties to protect biodiversity ([12]-[15]). However, the higher court raised concerns about classifying Lemthongthai alongside traditional poachers, suggesting that the circumstances of this case could potentially warrant a less severe penalty ([1], [17]).

Upon appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, it was argued that the imposition of a 30-year sentence was disproportionate when contextualized against the statutory maximums and the nature of the offenses, particularly when considering that the actions resulted from fraudulent permit applications rather than indiscriminate poaching ([20]-[22]).

The court found the High Court's reasoning flawed in specific assumptions about syndicate involvement and manipulative conduct, ultimately concluding that a sentence of 13 years’ imprisonment alongside a R1 million fine was more adequate than the previous sentences imposed ([23]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Lemthongthai's appeal, reducing his sentence to 13 years’ imprisonment and a fine of R1 million. The Court’s decision was underpinned by considerations of proportionality, a recognition of Lemthongthai’s culpability, and a critical evaluation of the district court's previous sentencing misdirections. The ruling highlights the complex interplay between enforcing biodiversity laws and ensuring that penalties serve a deterrent function without being exorbitantly punitive relative to the offenses committed ([23]).

This case serves as a critical reference to the legal obligations surrounding biodiversity conservation in South Africa and the corresponding penalties for contraventions of legislative protections in the context of illegal wildlife trade.
