Pennington and Summerley v The State (CCT 14/97)

Upon their submission, the appellants contended that the absence of any formal rules or legislation regulating appeals from the SCA to the Constitutional Court exempted them from needing to seek the Court's leave for appeal, claiming an inherent right to appeal ([5]–[6]).

Constitutional Court 1997 CCT 14/97

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

The appellants, J.D. Pennington and M.E. Summerley, were convicted in January 1992 by the Witwatersrand Local Division of the Supreme Court on 172 counts of fraud ([1]). The first appellant received a six-year prison sentence, half of which was suspended, while the second appellant was sentenced to effectively seven years ([1]). The appellants appealed unsuccessfully to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which dismissed their appeal on 16 May 1997 ([1]–[2]). Subsequently, on 26 May 1997, the appellants lodged a notice of appeal to the Constitutional Court, citing violations of their rights to human dignity and fair trial as guaranteed by sections 10 and 35(3) of the 1996 Constitution of the Republic of South Africa ([2]). The notice included various grounds of appeal, such as delays in prosecution, lack of legal representation at crucial points during the trial, and the trial judge’s failure to clarify the appellants' rights, arguing these elements rendered the trial unfair and impaired their dignity ([2]).

Upon their submission, the appellants contended that the absence of any formal rules or legislation regulating appeals from the SCA to the Constitutional Court exempted them from needing to seek the Court's leave for appeal, claiming an inherent right to appeal ([5]–[6]). The procedural irregularities and the context of the transition from the interim Constitution to the 1996 Constitution were crucial to the case, especially given that their trial concluded before the new Constitution was enacted ([7]–[8]).

I – Issues

The key issues before the Constitutional Court included:

  1. Jurisdictional Power: Whether the Constitutional Court had jurisdiction to hear appeals from the SCA in the absence of specific legislative provisions permitting such appeals ([9]–[10]).
  2. Application of the Constitution: Whether the appellants could rely on the rights provided under the 1996 Constitution, particularly the right to a fair trial ([28]–[29]).
  3. Delay in Appeal Hearing: The implications of the five-year delay in processing their appeal concerning their constitutional rights to a fair trial ([37]–[39]).
  4. Procedure of the Appeal: The necessity for the Court to regulate its processes and the implications of not having formal rules or legislation in place ([11]).

R – Rules

The relevant constitutional provisions governing the issues included:

  • Section 167(3), (6) and (7) of the 1996 Constitution, confirming the Constitutional Court as the highest court in all constitutional matters and establishing its original and appellate jurisdiction ([3]).
  • Section 168(3), which clarifies that the SCA holds authority over appeals except in constitutional matters ([10]).
  • Section 35(3) of the 1996 Constitution, which enshrines the right to a fair trial ([28]).
  • Item 17 of Schedule 6 of the 1996 Constitution, stipulating that existing proceedings must continue under prior laws unless the interests of justice demand otherwise ([29]).
  • Precedential cases such as S v Mhlungu and Du Plessis v De Klerk, which outline principles regarding the applicability of constitutional provisions to ongoing or completed proceedings ([30]–[34]).

A – Application

The Court ruled that it indeed had the jurisdiction to hear appeals from the SCA concerning constitutional matters ([10]). However, it underscored that no automatic right to appeal existed without leave; thus, the appellants' claims that they could appeal as of right were unfounded ([27], [52]). The Court emphasized that the absence of enabling legislation or rules at the time did not invalidate the established principle requiring leave for appeals to the Constitutional Court ([11], [27]).

The Court explored whether the appellants could utilize the 1996 Constitution for claims of a fair trial despite their trial occurring before its inception. Citing Mhlungu, the Court noted that rights applied as per the legal context during their trial ([34]). It argued that allowing the appellants to leverage modern constitutional rights retroactively would not be in the interests of justice, as their conduct of the trial was deemed fair based on the laws of the time ([35]–[42]).

Regarding the delay, the Court asserted that while appellate delays could contribute to anxiety, they differed from trial delays and did not inherently breach the right to a fair trial ([37]–[39]). The Court ruled that even if the delay were considered an infringement, it would not warrant any substantive relief, especially given the nature of the appellants’ convictions ([41]–[43]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court dismissed the application for leave to appeal ([53]). The judgment confirmed that the appellants had not demonstrated a compelling case based on constitutional grounds that would justify altering the prior decisions made by lower courts. The ruling established the necessity for a clear procedural framework regarding appeals in constitutional matters, insisting on the regime of requiring leave to appeal, which aligns with ensuring the judicial responsibility and preventing frivolous litigation ([52], [53]).

The implications of this case are significant for future cases addressing the transition from the interim Constitution to the 1996 Constitution, particularly concerning procedural rights and the regulation of appeals within South Africa's constitutional framework. Furthermore, it underscores the enduring impact of established legal precedents in determining the applicability of rights construed under previous legal contexts.
