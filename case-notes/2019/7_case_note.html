Long v South African Breweries (Pty) Ltd and Others [2018] ZACC 7 (FIRAC Method)

[2] Following the reporting of these irregularities, an internal investigation by the first respondent revealed discrepancies, including several vehicles remaining unlicensed and unroadworthy.

Constitutional Court 2019 [2018] ZACC 7 (FIRAC Method)

F – Facts

[1] The applicant, Mr. Allan Long, was employed by South African Breweries (PTY) Ltd as the district manager of the Border District since 1 January 2008. His duties included ensuring compliance with legal requirements regarding fleet management. In December 2012, Long became aware of concerning irregularities involving the licensing and maintenance of company vehicles.

[2] Following the reporting of these irregularities, an internal investigation by the first respondent revealed discrepancies, including several vehicles remaining unlicensed and unroadworthy. A critical incident occurred on 10 May 2013, when a trailer managed by Long was involved in a fatal accident, prompting further scrutiny of his management practices. On 15 May 2013, Long was informed he was under investigation for possible gross negligence and dereliction of duties.

[3] By August 2013, Long was formally suspended from his role. Subsequently, he faced three disciplinary charges related to his management failures, encompassing gross dereliction of duty and dishonesty. A disciplinary hearing was held, resulting in Long's acquittal on one charge but conviction on others, leading to his dismissal on 14 October 2013.

[4] Long filed internal appeals, which were unsuccessful. His case proceeded for arbitration at the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA), where the arbitrator ruled that while the suspension was unfair due to a lack of pre-suspension representation, his dismissal was substantively unfair but procedurally fair. The Labour Court later reviewed the CCMA’s decision, ruling that Long’s dismissal was fair and that the suspension was not an unfair labour practice, subsequently ordering Long to bear the costs of the proceedings.

I – Issues

[5] The key legal inquiries in this case revolve around two primary issues: First, whether an employee is entitled to a pre-suspension hearing in cases of precautionary suspension without immediate disciplinary action ([para 23]). Second, whether the Labour Court’s cost order against Long was justified under the Labour Relations Act (LRA) and relevant principles of fairness ([para 20]).

[6] Additionally, subsidiary issues arose concerning the appropriateness of the Labour Court’s substitutions of awards as opposed to remitting the case back to the CCMA, and interpretations of arbitrator findings on procedural fairness and reasonableness within the context of employee dismissal procedures ([para 18]).

R – Rules

[7] The governing legal framework includes the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA), particularly Section 23 concerning fair labour practices and the principles of procedural fairness which mandate that employees should have the right to contest suspensions that are punitive in nature ([para 22]).

[8] The fundamental rule established in precedents such as Mogale City Local Municipality and Mashego explicitly clarifies the nature of precautionary suspensions, distinguishing these from disciplinary actions and stipulating that an employer's duty to afford employees a hearing prior to suspension applies only in contexts where punitive measures are enforced ([para 35]).

[9] With respect to the costs issues, the LRA dictates that costs orders should conform to principles of law and fairness, allowing the courts to exercise discretion based on the nature of the dispute and interactions between the parties ([para 48]).

A – Application

[10] The Constitutional Court evaluated whether the Labour Court’s ruling on the lack of a requirement for a pre-suspension hearing was constitutionally sound, settling that the suspension was precautionary, thus validating the Labour Court’s permission to proceed without such a hearing ([para 37]).

[11] The assessment of whether Long's suspension was justified stemmed from evidence exhibiting a legitimate need for investigation into his management practices. The court agreed with the Labour Court’s finding that no significant prejudice existed to Long, primarily considering he received full salary during his suspension ([para 49]).

[12] In examining the costs order, the Constitutional Court identified a misapplication of discretion by the Labour Court that deviated from established principles where costs typically do not follow the event in labour matters. The court concluded that neither adjudicative body provided sufficient reasoning or consideration of fairness when making adverse costs orders against Long, thus necessitating the set-aside of the Labour Court’s ruling ([para 53]).

[13] Furthermore, it underscored the need for contextual consideration of Long's seniority and the prevailing relationship dynamics between the parties in deliberations about costs, ultimately declaring a no-cost award as most equitable given the various outcomes achieved ([para 59]).

C – Conclusion

[14] The Constitutional Court, in its unanimous ruling led by Theron J, granted condonation for the late filing of the appeal but refused leave to appeal on the substantive merits surrounding Long’s dismissal. It acknowledged the Labour Court's conclusions on the necessity of a precautionary suspension without prior hearing as consistent with the LRA and case law ([para 66]).

[15] Consequently, the Court set aside the Labour Court's costs order, asserting that the exercise of discretion regarding costs was inadequately reasoned and misaligned with the principles of fairness and law articulated within the LRA ([para 65]).

[16] The implications of this judgment signify a reaffirmation of procedural rights concerning suspensions, solidifying a delineation between precautionary and punitive measures, along with a critical examination of costs in the labour context, ultimately advocating for fairness in judicial processes to uphold the integrity of employment relations in South Africa.
