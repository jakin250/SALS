Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Rajivee Soni v The State (Case no 465/2019) [2021] ZASCA 57

### F – Facts
On the night of 13 May 2013, Dr. Bhavish Sewram was shot and killed outside his surgery in Pietermaritzburg. Rajivee Soni, the appellant, was a businessman who had previously been friends with the deceased. A falling out occurred after Soni suspected Sewram of having an affair with his wife ([1]–[5]). Following the murder, Soni was arrested and charged with six counts: the murder of Dr. Sewram, three counts of defeating or obstructing the course of justice, assault with intent to cause grievous bodily harm, and conspiracy to commit murder under s 18(2)(a) of the Riotous Assemblies Act 17 of 1956 ([3]).

After a lengthy trial of over three years, Soni was convicted on several charges. Count 1: murder, led to a 25-year sentence; counts 2 and 4, both for obstructing justice, resulted in 18 months and 2 years, respectively; count 6 for conspiracy to murder led to five years ([4]–[5]). Concurrent sentencing was applied to counts 2 to 5, amounting to an effective 30 years’ imprisonment ([4]).

### I – Issues
This case raised several legal questions:

1. Whether the evidence sufficiently proved Soni's guilt for murder beyond a reasonable doubt according to the doctrine of common purpose and the concept of mandate ([21]).
   
2. How the court would treat the actions of Soni regarding the counts of defeating or obstructing justice, particularly pertaining to false charges levied against Dr. Sewram ([21]–[22]).

3. The handling of evidence against Soni in light of the unavailability of certain State witnesses and the completed cross-examinations ([38], [81]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on various legal principles, including:

1. The doctrine of common purpose, as elucidated in *Thebus and Another v S*, which allows for the attribution of criminal liability to those who jointly engage in criminal acts ([35]).

2. The concept of *mandate*, whereby if one party (the appellant) directs another (agents) to commit an act (murder), liability is attributed to the principal regardless of the agents' awareness of the principal's identity ([37]).

3. The definition of incitement as laid out in s 18(2) of the Riotous Assemblies Act, which penalizes those who incite others to commit crimes, irrespective of the incited party's susceptibility to influence ([97]).

### A – Application
The court engaged in a thorough examination of the evidence presented. For **Count 1 (Murder)**, the court determined that the pattern of Soni's behavior leading to the murder, including the involvement of accomplices prearranged by him to execute the killing, established guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. Evidence showed Soni had orchestrated activities against Sewram, culminating with his murder by hired assassins. Testimony from multiple witnesses supported this characterization ([47]–[67]).

In relation to **Count 6 (Conspiracy to commit murder)**, the court recognized the ineffective nature of the initial conspiracy charge but concluded that Soni’s actions fulfilled the criteria for incitement due to his clear intent to influence Sithebe to commit the murder ([98]–[104]).

Further, for **Counts 2 and 4 (Defeating the course of justice)**, the corroboration by witnesses such as Naidoo and Kisten revealed that Soni had actively sought false charges against Sewram to damage his reputation and obstruct justice ([106]–[113]). Finally, concerning **Count 3**, the court found in favor of the appellant due to insufficient evidence ([115]–[119]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the convictions for murder, obstructing justice in counts 2 and 4, and converted the conviction for conspiracy to one of incitement to murder, thus maintaining Soni's culpability throughout the fundamental crimes attributed to him. Nonetheless, convictions for counts 3 and 5 were set aside. Soni’s effective sentence was adjusted to 23 years and 7 months after taking into account time served while awaiting appeal ([155]).

This case illustrates the intricacies of applying criminal law principles like common purpose and mandate in relation to a coordinated offense resulting in severe outcomes, demonstrating the justice system's capacity to adapt and respond to evident organized criminal behavior.
