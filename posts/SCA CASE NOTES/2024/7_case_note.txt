# FIRAC Case Note: Nabolisa v The Regional Court Magistrate and Another

**Neutral Citation:** Nabolisa v The Regional Court Magistrate and Another (568/2022) [2023] ZASCA 07 (19 January 2024)  
**Coram:** ZONDI and MOKGOHLOA JJA and NHLANGULELA AJA  
**Heard:** 24 August 2023  
**Delivered:** 19 January 2024  

## F – Facts

The case revolves around an appeal by Frank Nabolisa, who along with Natasha Mashiane, faced charges of dealing in cocaine and unlawful possession of paracetamol and methenamine ([1]). After a trial in the Regional Division of Johannesburg, Nabolisa was convicted and sentenced to a cumulative imprisonment term of 30 years, with a forfeiture order for the exhibits ([1]). The appellant sought to challenge this outcome, asserting that his rights to a fair trial had been violated throughout the proceedings. This led him to exhaust various appeal avenues before ultimately bringing a review application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, which was dismissed on 8 April 2021 ([2]–[3]).

The facts of the initial case involve police acting on intelligence reports that led them to a residence in Ebony Park, where they discovered large quantities of suspected cocaine and other substances in suitcases belonging to Mashiane, Nabolisa’s girlfriend ([4]). The forensic analysis of these substances was carried out by Sgt Rodney Machimane, whose findings established the presence of cocaine, paracetamol, and methenamine ([5]).

During the trial, Nabolisa did not cross-examine state witnesses nor testify in his defense, but his co-accused led expert testimony challenging the integrity of the forensic evidence ([6]–[8]). The appellant's review application was premised on several alleged procedural unfairnesses, including the State's failure to disclose certain working documents related to the forensic analysis, improper allocation of onus in trial proceedings, and issues surrounding the cross-examination of defense witnesses ([9]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal questions raised in this case include:

1. Did the high court err in finding that the appellant’s right to a fair trial was not infringed?
2. Was the appellant deprived of his right to challenge the forensic evidence due to the State’s alleged suppression of evidence?
3. Were the procedural irregularities presented by the appellant sufficient to overturn his conviction and sentence?

These issues engage aspects of constitutional law, particularly the rights enshrined in sections 35(3)(i) and 35(3)(l) of the Constitution, which guarantee an accused person's right to a fair trial ([11]).

## R – Rules

The governing legal principles stem from both constitutional law and statutes relevant to criminal procedure. Key points include:

- **Section 35(3)** of the Constitution guarantees the right to a fair trial, with specifics on challenging evidence and not being convicted of a non-existent offence ([11]).
- **Section 38** allows individuals to approach the courts claiming infringements of their rights ([11]).
- **S v Zuma** established that the right to a fair trial is broader than the procedural aspects previously recognized in criminal trials pre-1994, emphasizing substantive fairness ([11]).

Additionally, the procedural rules under the **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977** are relevant, particularly regarding the discovery of evidence ([14]).

## A – Application

In assessing the arguments put forward by the appellant, the court considered the alleged errors asserted against the high court's findings. These included claims regarding the prosecution's alleged suppression of evidence and the integrity of the forensic evidence presented:

- **Suppression of Evidence:** The court analyzed the contention that the working papers of the forensic analysis were withheld. It noted that the documents were found not to be part of the dossier and that the necessary forensic findings were disclosed ([15]).
  
- **Cross-Examination and Evidence Integrity:** The court underscored the appellant's failure to cross-examine Sgt Machimane and engage with his forensic findings during the initial trial. This failure was deemed to strengthen the state's case, as he did not challenge the evidence against him adequately ([17]).

- **Procedural Fairness and Conduct during the Trial:** The Superior Court examined whether the alleged impropriety during the trial, including the prosecutor’s comments, rose to a level that undermined the integrity of the judicial process. It concluded that although the comments were inappropriate, they did not significantly affect the proceedings ([18]).

The appellant's claims regarding the reliability of the forensic analysis were dismissed on the grounds that the defence experts lacked direct and pertinent experience in forensic analysis, which weakened their testimonies ([17]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the high court’s dismissal of Nabolisa’s review application, finding no substantive breach of his fair trial rights. The court concluded that the processes leading to his conviction were conducted appropriately and within the guidelines of fair trial obligations as mandated by the Constitution. The court emphasized that the appellant’s failure to engage effectively with the prosecution's evidence significantly contributed to the dismissal of his claims ([20]).

The decision ultimately reinforces the necessity for accused persons to actively engage in the legal proceedings and to exercise their rights to challenge evidence critically. The court's reaffirmation of the standards for evaluating irregularities in fair trial claims reflects a commitment to uphold judicial integrity while ensuring that procedural rights are vigilantly protected in South African criminal law.
