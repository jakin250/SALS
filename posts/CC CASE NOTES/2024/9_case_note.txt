# Case Note: D H B v C S B [2024] ZACC 9

### FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts:
The case, **D H B v C S B**, revolves around the enforceability of a prenuptial agreement in the context of South African marital law. The applicant, Mr. D. H. B. (hereafter “the applicant”), and the respondent, Mrs. C. S. B. (hereafter “the respondent”), entered into an antenuptial contract prior to their marriage, which was registered on January 22, 2015. This contract specified that their marriage would be out of community of property, excluding the accrual system ([2]).

A further agreement, described as a prenuptial agreement, was concluded on February 20, 2015. Written in Afrikaans, this prenuptial agreement stipulated that upon the dissolution of the marriage (either by divorce or death), the applicant would donate specific assets to the respondent, including a house valued at R1,500,000, a vehicle worth R250,000, monthly medical contributions, and a spousal maintenance payment of R20,000 per month for the rest of her life ([3]).

The couple was married on May 19, 2015. Divorce proceedings were initiated by the applicant on August 8, 2018, at which point the respondent sought enforcement of the prenuptial agreement through counter-claims. The applicant admitted the existence of the prenuptial agreement but contended that its terms were unenforceable due to the antenuptial contract. He claimed abandonment of the prenuptial agreement as a defense, alongside a defense of ingratitude, arguing that the respondent's actions warranted the revocation of the donations stated in the prenuptial agreement ([4]-[5]).

The Regional Court found the prenuptial agreement enforceable, asserting it could coexist with the antenuptial contract, citing the principles of **pacta sunt servanda** (agreements must be kept) and the freedom of contract. However, the High Court reversed this decision, concluding that the prenuptial agreement conflicted with section 7 of the Divorce Act 70 of 1979, potentially encroaching on the court’s discretion in maintenance matters ([8]-[12]). The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) upheld the Regional Court’s findings before the case reached the Constitutional Court ([14]-[20]).

### I – Issues:
The main legal issues presented in this case include:
1. Was the enforceability of the prenuptial agreement in relation to section 7(1) and (2) of the Divorce Act properly considered by the lower courts? ([21])
2. If it was properly considered, was the conclusion by the SCA that the prenuptial agreement was enforceable correct? ([22])

### R – Rules:
This decision involves interpretation and application of several legal principles and statutes:
1. **Divorce Act 70 of 1979** – Particularly sections 7(1) and 7(2) which govern maintenance and the division of assets in divorce proceedings. These sections empower courts to make orders regarding maintenance and asset division based on the parties’ circumstances at the time of divorce, necessitating the court's discretion to be exercised ([7]).
2. **Legal principles of contract** – Including **pacta sunt servanda**, which emphasizes the binding nature of agreements and the autonomy of contracting parties ([9]).
3. **Public Policy Considerations** – Evaluating whether a prenuptial agreement could operate to oust the court’s discretion in maintenance matters derived from section 7 of the Divorce Act ([20]-[22]).

### A – Application:
The Constitutional Court's ruling focused on the nature of the prenuptial agreement and its perceived implications for spousal maintenance. In analyzing the case, the Court determined that while the SCA correctly identified the coexistence of the antenuptial and prenuptial agreements, it failed to adequately appreciate their implications for the court's authority as set out in the Divorce Act ([61]).

Theron J, writing for the majority, indicated that the prenuptial agreement could not be seen as a valid alteration or amendment of the antenuptial contract, as the two agreements served different purposes. The antenuptial contract regulated the matrimonial property regime, while the prenuptial agreement attempted to dictate post-divorce financial obligations ([60]-[62]). Critically, the majority held that the agreement's inherent nature—as a purported donation—fell outside the purview of section 7 and could not oust the court’s discretion in determining maintenance ([63]).

Conversely, Schippers AJ, in dissent, argued that the prenuptial agreement intended to govern maintenance obligations directly conflicted with public policy and the statutory mandate to ensure just and fair maintenance determinations by the courts ([65]-[113]).

### C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled that the appeal from the SCA was to be dismissed, thus reinstating the Regional Court's decision which held the prenuptial agreement enforceable. The Court concluded that the prenuptial agreement did not conflict with existing public policy nor did it infringe upon the court's discretion under the Divorce Act. The majority opinion underscored the importance of allowing parties to craft enforceable agreements while affirming the necessity for courts to retain the authority to make fair financial determinations regarding maintenance ([63]).

This case establishes crucial precedent regarding the enforceability of prenuptial agreements vis-à-vis spousal maintenance, setting boundaries on private agreements' ability to interfere with statutory obligations and judicial discretion under South African family law.
