# Case Note: Mlungwana and Others v The State and Another [2018] ZACC 45

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–35])

The matter concerns an appeal from the High Court of South Africa’s Western Cape Division, which declared section 12(1)(a) of the Regulation of Gatherings Act 205 of 1993 unconstitutional. The applicants, members of the Social Justice Coalition, were arrested for convening a gathering without proper notice, violating this section. They contended that the criminalisation of such gatherings unfairly limited their constitutional right to peaceful assembly as guaranteed in section 17 of the Constitution. The State disputed their claims, arguing that the section's requirements did not infringe upon any rights but were necessary for public order and safety ([paras 1–4]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1–6])

The key legal questions facing the court were:

1. Does the criminalisation of failing to provide notice for gatherings as stipulated in section 12(1)(a) limit the right to assemble peacefully and unarmed under section 17 of the Constitution?
2. If there is a limitation, is that limitation reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society based on human dignity, equality, and freedom? ([paras 1–2]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 1–6])

The court primarily relied on the provisions of the Constitution of South Africa, particularly sections 17 and 36, which govern the rights to assemble peacefully and the limitations of rights, respectively. The court also referenced significant case law, including Garvas, which clarified the extent of the right to assembly and the conditions under which limitations could be justified ([paras 1–4], [42–46]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 42–110])

The court meticulously analyzed whether section 12(1)(a) constituted a limitation of the right in section 17. They concluded that the criminalisation of a failure to give notice indeed had a chilling effect, deterring potential conveners from organizing peaceful gatherings due to fear of prosecution ([paras 42–47]).

In assessing the justification of the limitation, the court weighed:

1. **The Nature of the Right**: The importance of the right to peaceful assembly and its foundational role in a democracy were highlighted ([paras 60–63]).
   
2. **Importance of the Purpose**: Although the objective of maintaining public order was recognized, the court ruled that this could not justify the severe infringement of constitutional rights ([paras 74–81]).
   
3. **Severity of Limitation**: The broad definitions of 'gathering' and 'convener' resulted in a significant scope of criminal liability, creating disproportionate consequences for individuals wishing to assemble peacefully ([paras 82–89]).

4. **Less Restrictive Means**: Several alternative suggestions to incentivise giving notice were proposed and found to be viable without imposing criminal liability, thereby indicating that the limitation was not justifiable as a last resort ([paras 95–100]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 101–112])

The court unanimously declared that section 12(1)(a) of the Regulation of Gatherings Act was unconstitutional, asserting it imposed an unjustifiable limitation on the right to assembly. This decision significantly underscores the importance of the right to freedom of assembly in a democratic society, ruling that the State's argument for public order could not supersede constitutional rights. The court also mandated that any criminal liabilities for failing to give notice were disproportionate and unnecessary, pointing towards legislative reform that would respect citizens’ rights while ensuring order ([paras 111–112]).

The implications of this case extend beyond the specific provision at issue, reinforcing the need for a delicate balance between public order and civil liberties, setting a precedent for future challenges against laws that infringe upon fundamental rights. 

The case further highlighted the importance of developing legal frameworks that support rather than hinder peaceful assembly, ultimately affirming South Africa's commitment to constitutional democracy and civil rights ([paras 101–112]).
