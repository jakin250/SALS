Constitutional Court

Baphalane Ba Ramokoka Community v Mphela Family and Others

The legal intertwining involves the Mphela Family, with Haakdoornbult owned by a Boerdery CC and other respondents, and the Minister for Rural Development and Land Reform as the eighth respondent.

Constitutional Court 2011 [2011] ZACC 15 Judgment Date: 21 April 2011 Judges:

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 75/10
Judgment Citation: [2011] ZACC 15
Judgment Date: 21 April 2011
Judges: Cameron J

FIRAC Analysis

F - Facts

The applicant, the Baphalane Ba Ramokoka Community (the Community), represented by Kgosi Modise Tonse Ramokoka, sought to rescind a judgment from the Constitutional Court in Mphela and Others v Haakdoornbult Boerdery CC and Others [2008] ZACC 5; an earlier ruling by the Supreme Court of Appeal affirming the Mphela family's claim to the Haakdoornbult farm, which they had occupied since 1918 before being forcibly removed in 1962. The Family had received compensation in the form of the Pylkop farm, which is also claimed by the Community under the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 (the Act), with their claims pending before the Land Claims Court.

The legal intertwining involves the Mphela Family, with Haakdoornbult owned by a Boerdery CC and other respondents, and the Minister for Rural Development and Land Reform as the eighth respondent. The Community argued that the Haakdoornbult judgment impinged on its claims to Pylkop, as it had not been a party to the initial proceedings and had no knowledge of them, violating its right of access to the courts as protected by section 34 of the Bill of Rights ([1]-[5]).

I - Issues

The legal questions presented to the court included:

  1. Whether the judgment in the Haakdoornbult case adversely affected the Community’s claim to Pylkop.
  2. Whether the Community was entitled to rescind parts of the Haakdoornbult judgment due to lack of involvement and alleged violation of its rights to access justice ([3], [4], [28]).

R - Rules

The Court considered the following statutory and case law provisions:

  1. Constitution of South Africa, Section 34: Guarantees the right to a fair public hearing before a court.
  2. Restitution of Land Rights Act, Section 2(2): Concerns overcompensation and the entitlement to return of land ([5], [8]).
  3. Uniform Rules of Court, Rule 42: Addresses judicial errors in absence of affected parties, permitting rescission of judgments under certain conditions ([26]).
  4. Common Law Principles: Relating to the courts’ inherent power to correct own orders and the integration of justice in legal reasoning ([27]).

A - Application

The Court found that the judgment in the Haakdoornbult case did not impinge on the Community’s claim to Pylkop. Cameron J stated the findings in the Haakdoornbult judgment exclusively regarded the Haakdoornbult property and did not have any binding effect on the Community’s claims relating to Pylkop. The Community's assertion that the judgment violated its rights due to lack of participation did not persuade the Court, as there was no order made concerning Pylkop in the original judgment. The Court held that the impact of the original judgment was confined to the matters at issue therein, leaving the Community’s claim to Pylkop unaffected ([28]-[32]).

Furthermore, the alternative relief sought by the Community to expunge certain pronouncements from the Haakdoornbult judgment was rejected, as it was determined that the statements did not relate to the Community's interests. The law did not support the expungement of judicial statements divorced from operative orders concerning substantive rights ([29], [30]).

C - Conclusion

The Court ruled to dismiss the Community's application for rescission. It reiterated that the Haakdoornbult judgment did not infringe upon the Community's claims and contained no binding judicial ruling related to Pylkop. Consequently, there was no necessity to alter or rescind the prior judgment ([33]). Additionally, the Family’s call for punitive costs against the Community was rejected due to the lack of frivolousness in pursuing the rescission claim, although the Court held that the Community’s legal advisor should bear the legal costs arising from unfounded allegations against the Family’s legal representatives ([34]-[44]).

This case encapsulates the complexities surrounding land restitution in South Africa and reinforces the importance of procedural participation in judicial proceedings affecting property rights as protected by the Constitution.
