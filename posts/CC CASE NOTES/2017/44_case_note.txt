Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

### FIRAC Case Note: Phakane v S [2017] ZACC 44

#### F – Facts
The applicant, Klaas Lesetja Phakane, was convicted of murder in 2009 for the killing of Ms Matilda Chuene Boshomane. The conviction was based predominantly on the testimony of a key witness, Ms Martha Manamela, who had a romantic relationship with the applicant. The murder occurred in August 2006, but difficulties arose in the trial proceedings when it was discovered the State failed to provide a complete record of the trial, notably omitting the evidence of Ms Manamela, which had proven integral to the conviction. Despite the state's acknowledgment that attempts were made to reconstruct the missing evidence, the Full Court of the High Court determined that the appeal could proceed fairly despite these deficiencies (see [2]–[3], [19]).

The trial court originally sentenced Phakane to 20 years' imprisonment. However, the Full Court subsequently reduced the sentence to 15 years but upheld the conviction. Phakane lodged an application for leave to appeal, arguing that the absence of the complete record constituted a violation of his right to a fair appeal protected under s 35(3) of the Constitution ([4], [20]).

#### I – Issues
The primary issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the State's failure to provide a complete record of the trial proceedings, particularly concerning a key witness's evidence, infringed the applicant's right to a fair appeal as guaranteed by section 35(3) of the Constitution. Additionally, the court needed to determine the implications of this failure on Phakane's conviction ([20], [23]).

#### R – Rules
Section 35(3) of the Constitution of South Africa guarantees every accused person the right to a fair trial, which includes the "right of appeal to, or review by, a higher court." The jurisprudence developed around fair trial rights emphasizes that a complete and accessible trial record is fundamental to allowing a fair appeal ([20], [24]). The court cited prior rulings such as *S v Joubert* and *S v Chabedi*, reinforcing the need for an adequate record for a fair appellate process ([38], [39]).

#### A – Application
The Constitutional Court, led by Zondo J, scrutinized the impact of the incomplete trial record on the fairness of the appeal process. It assessed that the trial court's conviction heavily relied on Ms Manamela’s testimony without accounting for discrepancies between her statement to the police and her in-court testimony ([12], [36]). The absence of this evidence severely hampered the ability of the Full Court to evaluate the conviction adequately.

Zondo J articulated that, while some evidence existed, it predominantly stemmed from Ms Manamela, whose testimony was pivotal to the murder conviction. The court concluded that without her testimony, the Full Court did not possess a suitable basis to fairly adjudicate the appeal ([38], [41]). Consequently, the Constitutional Court found that an incomplete record effectively rendered Phakane’s right to a fair appeal illusory ([38], [41]).

Cameron J, concurring, noted that although this conclusion necessitated vacating the murder conviction, it did not equate to the applicant’s immediate exoneration, indicating potential avenues for recharging the applicant with murder should circumstances permit ([47], [59]). 

Froneman J, also concurring, acknowledged that, while the incomplete records tainted the entirety of the appeal, the matter should be remitted to the High Court for potential retrial to address these concerns adequately ([62], [63]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court granted the applicant leave to appeal, held that his constitutional right to a fair appeal had been violated, and set aside the conviction and sentence. The court ordered Phakane's immediate release from prison due to these failures in ensuring a fair trial. Moreover, it was emphasized that the State could seek to recharge the applicant with murder in the event that justice warranted it ([46], [41]). Ultimately, the ruling underscored the critical importance of complete trial records and their role in protecting fundamental fair trial rights in South Africa's legal system. 

The implications of this case extend to reinforcing judicial accountability and the necessity for the State to ensure that defendants' rights are preserved throughout the judicial process. By highlighting the integral role of complete records in criminal appeals, this landmark ruling serves as a crucial precedent in South African constitutional law.
