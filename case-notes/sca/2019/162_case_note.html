Case Note: Zephan (Pty) Ltd & Others v Noormahomed (1303/18) [2019] ZASCA 162

F – Facts

The appeal in the matter of Zephan (Pty) Ltd & Others v Noormahomed originated from a default judgment delivered by the Gauteng Division of the High Court. The appellants—Zephan (Pty) Ltd and four individuals, including Nicolas Georgiou—were ordered to pay the respondent, Suraiya Begun Noormahomed, the sum of R6,000,000, along with interest and costs, premised on a buy-back agreement regarding shares in Highveld Syndication (Pty) Ltd ("HS22") ([1]–[2]).

The agreement mandated that the first appellant would repurchase shares held by the respondent after five years, promising to pay a premium of 100% upon repurchase. The other appellants guaranteed this obligation. The default judgment was obtained following the first appellant's failure to perform the repurchase obligation after the five-year period expired ([6]).

The appellants filed an application for rescission of the default judgment, arguing that the respondent's claim had been novated by a business rescue plan sanctioned by the court concerning HS22 and related entities. They cited the acceptance of interest payments by the respondent from another company, Orthotouch Ltd, as evidence that the original agreement had been altered ([4]–[5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question before the court was whether the appellants had established a bona fide defense warranting rescission of the default judgment. Specifically, the court needed to determine if the appellants’ assertion that the claim had been novated by the business rescue processes related to HS22 and Orthotouch was meritorious. Furthermore, the appeal challenged whether the court below correctly dismissed the application for rescission based on this defense's viability ([3]–[4]).

R – Rules

The legal standards applicable in this case pertain to rescission of judgment as prescribed in common law. According to established practice, a party applying for rescission must demonstrate:

  1. A bona fide defense to the claim ([10]).
  2. The application must be brought within a reasonable time ([10]).

Precedents cited include Grant v Plumbers (Pty) Ltd (1949) and Chetty v Law Society, Transvaal (1985), which emphasize the necessity of establishing a prima facie defense ([10]).

Significantly, sections of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, particularly sections 129 and 133, regarding business rescue proceedings and legal moratoriums on litigation, were also critically examined ([8]).

A – Application

The appellants unsuccessfully attempted to distinguish their liability by arguing that the respondent’s claim had been extinguished or altered due to the business rescue proceedings concerning HS22. Specifically, they claimed that the arrangement under section 155 of the Companies Act effectively novated their prior agreement with the respondent. However, the court found this assertion legally insufficient ([11]).

The respondent's claim for specific performance was clearly based on a distinct agreement that was unblemished by the business rescue of HS22. The court reasoned that the obligations under the buy-back agreement remained intact and were unaffected by any external arrangements made by HS22 or Orthotouch. Hence, the alleged novation was unfounded because the original agreement, which was independent of the business affairs of HS22, persisted as a valid obligation owed by the first appellant ([12]–[14]).

Despite the appellants’ claims relating to the respondent accepting interest payments, the court emphasized that such payments did not alter her initial rights under the buy-back agreement ([14]). Consequently, the evidence did not demonstrate any triable issue that would warrant rescission ([15]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal against the Gauteng Division's decision, holding that the court below had correctly determined that the appellants failed to establish a bona fide defense. The absence of a viable legal argument to support the notion of novation, combined with the clarity of the previous agreement's terms, reinforced the conclusion that the respondent's claim remained valid and enforceable ([15]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the strict adherence to contractual obligations irrespective of subsequent business operational changes and reaffirms the importance of demonstrating a bona fide defense when seeking rescission of judgments in contractual matters. The dismissal of the appeal with costs, including those for employing two counsel, indicates the court’s view that the appellants' application lacked merit ([1], [15]).
