FIRAC Case Note: Cash Paymaster Services (Pty) Ltd and Others v Freedom Under Law NPC and Others [2022] ZACC 2

F – Facts

This case arises from an application by the provisional liquidators of Cash Paymaster Services (Pty) Ltd (CPS), who sought to vary a court order made on April 1, 2021, and to join the South African Revenue Service (SARS) as a party ([1]–[6]).

CPS, which was contracted to manage social grant payments for the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA), was declared in liquidation on October 16, 2020, following an unsatisfied judgment against it for more than R316 million ([8]). The April 2021 order required CPS to submit audited profit statements to clarify their earnings from the social grant contracts, which the court previously deemed unlawful but suspended a declaration of invalidity to ensure continued payments to beneficiaries ([3]–[4]).

Following their appointment on October 30, 2020, the provisional liquidators contended that CPS was still bound to comply with the April 2021 order, despite being in liquidation. They sought to amend references to CPS in the existing order to reflect its status as an entity "in liquidation" and join SARS due to overlapping issues in an audit relating to CPS’ tax affairs ([7]–[12]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions before the Constitutional Court included:

  1. Whether CPS, while in provisional liquidation, could be compelled to comply with the April 2021 order ([20]).
  2. Whether SARS had a direct and substantial interest justifying its joinder to the proceedings ([12]).
  3. Whether the provisional liquidators could demand variations to the existing order concerning the compliance timeline and responsibilities ([7], [20]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several legal authorities, including:

  • Section 359(1) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973, which states that civil proceedings against a company are suspended upon its winding-up ([11]).
  • Relevant case law, including South African Riding for the Disabled Association v Regional Land Claims Commissioner [2017] ZACC 4, emphasizing the need for a party seeking joinder to demonstrate a direct and substantial interest in the matter ([12]).
  • Previous Constitutional Court rulings that deemed contracts arising from unconstitutional frameworks as valid, pending compliance with judicial processes to equalize situations post-declaration of invalidity ([3], [4]).

A – Application

The court found that:

  • The provisional liquidators failed to demonstrate how their inability to comply with the April 2021 order arose from statutory limitations placed on them due to their provisional status. The order was directed at CPS, and compliance was deemed mandatory, not optional ([24], [25]).
  • The assertion of a joint interest with SARS was merely procedural convenience rather than a legal necessity; thus, joinder was unnecessary ([12], [14]).
  • The existing obligations placed on CPS in the April 2021 order remained intact and enforceable, irrespective of the liquidators' claims regarding their limitation in enforcing compliance ([22], [25]).

The court noted that while CPS was in a transitional status due to liquidation, it maintained its legal identity, rendering the operational aspects of the previous court orders still applicable ([15], [23]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court dismissed the application to vary the existing order and to join SARS, asserting that the provisional liquidators could fulfill the existing obligations imposed by the April 2021 order. The court underscored the need for adherence to judicial mandates, regardless of liquidatory status, emphasizing that compliance with the court’s directives is obligatory and not reliant on the type of liquidator in control ([25]). Thus, the ruling reinforced the commitment to judicial oversight over public financial management, particularly in cases involving state-related operations, ensuring that accountability mechanisms could not be evaded through corporate restructuring processes like liquidation.

The full implications of the case reaffirm the judiciary's role in safeguarding public funds and ensuring that entities do not escape scrutiny or accountability, even under the complexities of corporate law and liquidation processes ([26]).
