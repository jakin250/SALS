Constitutional Court

Penwill v Penwill N.O. and Others [2020] ZACC 17

The applicant, acting in person, challenged the costs awarded against him by the Court’s Taxing Master after his appeal was dismissed.

Constitutional Court 2020 [2020] ZACC 17

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–5])

The case arises from a long-standing and contentious dispute between two siblings, Andrew Donald Jonathan Penwill (the applicant) and Richard Douglas Penwill (the first respondent), over family inheritance and the estate of their deceased parents. The core issue pertains to the taxation of costs following an unsuccessful application for leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, which was dismissed by the Court due to lack of reasonable prospects of success ([para 1]).

The applicant, acting in person, challenged the costs awarded against him by the Court’s Taxing Master after his appeal was dismissed. Despite receiving notice of the taxation process, the applicant did not attend the hearing, citing health issues, and subsequently sought to have certain costs set aside on various grounds, including what he viewed as excessive charges ([paras 2-5]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 6–8])

The primary legal issue concerns the validity and appropriateness of the Taxing Master's assessment of costs in a taxation review process under the rules governing the Supreme Court of Appeal, which the Constitutional Court adopted for this matter. Specifically, the Court needed to determine if the Taxing Master applied the correct tariffs and whether his decisions were substantively justified within the framework of established legal principles governing taxation of legal costs ([paras 6-7]).

Moreover, ancillary to the taxation issue, the applicant sought to set aside a warrant of execution and notice of attachment that arose from the taxed costs. This raised questions regarding execution actions based upon disputed taxation outcomes ([para 20]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 8–19])

The Court relied on Rule 17 of the Supreme Court of Appeal Rules, which governs the taxation of costs, emphasizing the Taxing Master's discretion in determining reasonable costs within prescribed tariffs. The fact that taxation could be reviewed by a court where doubts about the appropriateness of the Taxing Master's decisions arise was also a critical rule guiding this case ([paras 6-8]).

The Court noted prior judgments, including President of the Republic of South Africa v Gauteng Lions Rugby Union, which established that interventions in a Taxing Master's rulings must demonstrate that the Taxing Master was "clearly wrong" in his determinations. The courts generally afford wide discretion to Taxing Masters, allowing for subjective assessments of what constitutes a fair and reasonable fee given the specific context of the case ([paras 15-19]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 10–19])

The Court closely examined five contested items in the bill of costs.

  1. Item 2 and Item 4 involved challenges to the per-page charges for perusal by opposing attorneys. The Taxing Master's rationale for allowing these rates was based on applicable tariff rules, which were upheld as justifiable given that the applicant could not substantiate his proposed rates. The Court found no basis to alter the Taxing Master's decisions regarding these items ([paras 10-12]).
  2. Item 23, which concerned charges related to an affidavit drawn by Richard Douglas Penwill, was more contentious. Here, the Taxing Master had applied the wrong tariff according to Rule Part C 3(c), thus resulting in an inflated charge. The Constitutional Court concluded that the proper amount should be significantly lower, confirming the applicant's contention that the item had been mischaracterized, ultimately deciding that only R384.00 of R1,504.00 should be permitted ([paras 13-16]).
  3. Item 26 involved copying fees and was upheld as justifiable based on the Taxing Master's rationale of adhering to historical tariffs, with the Court emphasizing the lack of evidence from the applicant to contest the permitted rates effectively ([para 17]).
  4. Finally, Item 48, related to counsel fees claimed for work done by Richard Douglas Penwill, was determined by the Taxing Master to be fair and reasonable. The Court, respecting the Taxing Master's discretion, refused to interfere, thereby allowing the full amount claimed ([paras 18-19]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 20–22])

The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled in favor of the applicant in part, substantially reducing the awarded costs for item 23 and setting aside the warrant of execution and notice of attachment that had been issued. This decision highlighted a misapplication of tariff considerations by the Taxing Master while affirming the need for adherence to established procedures in taxation ([paras 21-22]).

The implications of this judgment underscore the balance that must be maintained between ensuring fair compensation for legal services and the necessity for courts to maintain checks on the application of tariffs, especially where costs may significantly impact a litigant's financial standing. It also reinforces the expectation that Taxing Masters clarify their reasoning and adhere to the rules governing their discretion, promoting transparency and accountability in legal costs taxation processes.
