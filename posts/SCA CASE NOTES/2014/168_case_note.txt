Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

### Case Note: Porritt & Another v The NDPP & Others (978/13) [2014] ZASCA 168

#### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts**  
The case involved Gary Patrick Porritt and Susan Hilary Bennett (the appellants) who faced an indictment consisting of over 3000 counts related to contraventions of various acts including the Income Tax Act 1962, Companies Act 1973, and Stock Exchange Control Act 1985, amidst allegations of racketeering and fraud ([para 1]). The prosecution team was composed of Advocates Etienne Coetzee SC and Jan Ferreira. The appellants challenged the title of the prosecutors to act in their case, arguing that their appointment violated the National Prosecuting Authority Act No 32 of 1998 (the NPA Act) (§ 38(1), which requires consultation with the Minister) and breached their right to a fair trial under § 35(3) of the Constitution. The challenge against Coetzee was grounded on his prior involvement with SARS, which had a direct financial interest in the case. Ferreira faced criticism due to his role in drafting an affidavit supporting the liquidation of a company involved with the first appellant ([paras 2-3]).

Initially, the South Gauteng High Court (Mailula J) dismissed the challenge regarding Coetzee's title but upheld the complaint about Ferreira, ordering the removal of both from the prosecution ([para 4]). Although the court did not grant an acquittal, stating that their removal was based on perceived bias and did not imply any wrongdoing, the appellants appealed this decision ([paras 5-8]).

**I – Issues**  
1. What is the proper legal test for removing a prosecutor, particularly in the context of perceived bias, and how does this relate to the appellants’ rights under the Criminal Procedure Act (CPA) and the Constitution? ([para 6])
2. Should an accused be entitled to an acquittal in circumstances where the court has upheld a plea that the prosecutor lacked title to prosecute? ([para 7])

**R – Rules**  
The relevant statutory provisions include § 106(1)(h) of the CPA, allowing for the plea of improper title to prosecute. § 319 of the CPA permits a reservation of a question of law, which the NDPP invoked regarding prosecutorial removal ([paras 8-9]). Principles governing prosecutorial conduct encompass obligations of impartiality and fairness, primarily originating from the Constitution and international guidelines ([para 12]).

The court underscored that prosecutors are distinct from judges, and their roles involve vigorous advocacy for the state while upholding constitutional protections imparted to the accused ([para 11]). As articulated in previous cases, prosecutors must ensure fairness while pursuing justice and cannot be entirely neutral ([para 14]).

**A – Application**  
The appeal highlighted key issues around the nature of prosecutorial conduct and rights to fair trial. The court emphasized that the removal of prosecutors for perceived bias does not automatically render them intrinsically without title to prosecute. It clarified that an apprehension of bias must directly affect the accused's ability to secure a fair trial. The judgment pointed out that bias must be substantiated, not merely speculative, and the high court's misapplication of the test used for judicial officer recusal to assess prosecutors’ conduct was a misstep ([paras 10, 21]).

Regarding the appellants’ demand for acquittal, the SCA concluded that establishing a perception of bias does not imply a full relinquishment of the prosecutors' title. The appellants failed to prove that the prosecutors lacked the requisite impartiality that would dictate an acquittal as delineated in § 106(4) of the CPA ([paras 8, 9]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, holding that the removal of Coetzee and Ferreira was not grounded on their lack of title, but on a misguided perception of bias. The SCA ruled that the appellants were not entitled to acquittal solely based on the successful removal of the prosecutors; such a removal did not imply any impropriety on their part ([paras 23-24]). The court remitted the matter back to the lower court for further proceedings, reaffirming the necessity of maintaining the integrity of the prosecutorial process while ensuring fair trial rights are preserved ([paras 23]).

### Implications  
This case underscores the delicate balance within the criminal justice system between the rights of the accused and the prosecutorial duties to maintain public interest. It reiterates that perceived bias must be substantiated and viewed within the context of the role of prosecutors, distinct from that of judges, and indicates that while the right to a fair trial is paramount, it does not afford absolute immunity from prosecution by state-affiliated representatives. The ruling serves as a precedent for understanding the confines of prosecutorial conduct and how perceptions of bias are adjudicated within the legal framework.
