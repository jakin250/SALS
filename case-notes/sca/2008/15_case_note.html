National Director of Public Prosecutions v Geyser (160/2007) [2008] ZASCA 15

In June 2005, the NDPP applied to the High Court for a preservation order based on the assertion that 'Ambassadors' was operating unlawfully as a brothel under section 2 of the Sexual Offences Act, 1957 ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 15

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The matter at hand concerns an appeal by the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) against Frederick Hendrik Geyser and his close corporation, Fakkel Scrap Dealers CC, regarding the forfeiture of a property believed to have been used as a brothel. Mr. Geyser and his partner opened a business named 'Ambassadors' in a property they purchased on Church Street, Pretoria, in 2000. This property underwent significant renovations aimed primarily at facilitating the operation of a brothel, masked under the pretense of being a guesthouse/bar ([1]–[2]).

In June 2005, the NDPP applied to the High Court for a preservation order based on the assertion that 'Ambassadors' was operating unlawfully as a brothel under section 2 of the Sexual Offences Act, 1957 ([2]). Following the preservation order, the NDPP sought forfeiture of the property under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act (POCA), claiming that it was an instrumentality of the offence ([2]–[3]). The initial High Court ruling found the top floor subject to forfeiture, but the NDPP's appeal contested this limitation ([3]). Geyser's cross-appeal argued against any forfeiture, claiming he was merely renting the premises ([3]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues revolve around:

  1. Whether the entire property constituted an instrumentality of the offence of keeping a brothel under POCA and hence could be subject to forfeiture ([15]).
  2. The proportionality of the forfeiture in relation to the objectives of POCA, arguing whether such action constitutes an arbitrary deprivation of property ([18]–[19]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions include:

  • Sexual Offences Act 23 of 1957: Particularly section 2, which defines brothel-keeping, and section 20(1) addressing unlawful carnal intercourse for reward ([3]–[4]).
  • Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998: Specifically sections 38(1), 48(1), and 50(1) which deal with the procedures and conditions for property forfeiture ([17]).
  • Judicial precedents which clarify the interpretation of what constitutes property being an instrumentality of an offence, including the requirement for direct causal connection ([16], [17]).

A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the court focused on two primary aspects. First, it examined the functionality and usage of the various parts of the property. The court determined that the architecture of the premises – particularly the top floor configured as nearly identical cubicles without standard guest house amenities – indicated a clear intent to facilitate commercial sex ([8], [15]). The evidence of operations conducted prior to the police intervention underscored that the property was used directly for illegal activities, thereby qualifying as an instrumentality under POCA ([16]).

Second, the court dealt with the proportionality of forfeiture. The primary aim of forfeiture under POCA is remedial rather than punitive. The court found that the forfeiture of the entire property was justified given the singularly criminal purpose for which it had been acquired and used, thus concluding it would not be disproportionate ([17], [19], [38]). The argument that income from the liquor sales could obscure the legality of the brothel activity was deemed insufficient to negate the overall illegality associated with the primary operation ([21], [22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately reversed the lower court's limited forfeiture order, establishing that the entirety of the property should be forfeited to the State ([40]). The court concluded that Mr. Geyser had engaged in a deliberate and sustained operational model that amounted to a continuous violation of the law. This decision reinforces the broad interpretative scope of POCA regarding forfeiture actions aimed at dismantling organized crime operations, alongside emphasizing the impermissibility of using properties for overwhelmingly unlawful purposes. The ruling has significant implications for the treatment of properties associated with illegal enterprises, setting a precedent that properties functioning as instruments of crime can be fully forfeited despite arguments regarding their potential legitimate use ([38]).
