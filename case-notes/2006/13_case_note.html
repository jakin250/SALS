Trevor B Giddey NO v J C Barnard and Partners (CCT 65/05)

The Johannesburg High Court, on 10 August 2005, ordered Giddey to furnish security for costs, leading to a stay of proceedings until compliance.

Constitutional Court 2006 CCT 65/05

F – Facts

The Constitutional Court of South Africa considered the application for security for costs in Trevor B Giddey NO v J C Barnard and Partners (CCT 65/05). The applicant, Trevor B Giddey, was the liquidator of Sadrema Explorations Ltd (in liquidation) and claimed US $100 million from the respondent, J C Barnard and Partners, for funds allegedly not conserved by the latter, which were to be held in trust for Sadrema. Upon initiation of the action, the respondent sought an order from the High Court for Giddey to furnish security for costs, citing the company’s liquidation which indicated potential inability to pay the respondent’s costs should they succeed ([1], [2]).

The Johannesburg High Court, on 10 August 2005, ordered Giddey to furnish security for costs, leading to a stay of proceedings until compliance. Giddey appealed this decision, claiming it violated his right to access the courts as protected under section 34 of the Constitution, asserting that a requirement for security could unjustly terminate his claim ([3], [4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question was whether requiring Giddey to furnish security for costs constituted a violation of his constitutional right to access courts as outlined in section 34 of the Constitution, which protects the right to a fair public hearing in legal disputes ([1], [4]).

Additionally, the Court needed to determine the proper exercise of judicial discretion concerning section 13 of the Companies Act, which allows for orders of security costs, particularly regarding the balancing of interests between the potential injustice to a litigant and the risks inherent to a defendant in court ([7], [8]).

R – Rules

The Court evaluated the statutory framework governing security for costs, particularly section 13 of the Companies Act, 61 of 1973, which endows courts with the discretion to require a company to furnish security if it appears unable to cover costs of the opposing party ([1], [6]). Furthermore, Rule 47 of the Uniform Rules of Court details the procedures for demanding security for costs and the implications for failing to comply ([2]).

The Constitutional principle enshrined in section 34 guarantees the right of every individual to have disputes resolved in fair hearings, necessitating the balancing of this right against the procedural safeguards for defendants ([15], [18]).

A – Application

The Court expressed that the exercise of discretion under section 13 should account for both the rights of plaintiffs to access the courts and the defendants' interests in recovering costs should they succeed. The balancing act required understanding the likelihood of a plaintiff’s inability to pay costs against potential undue hindrance to the plaintiff’s pursuit of a legitimate claim ([8], [30]).

It was found that the applicant failed to demonstrate how security would irreparably harm his case or that he lacked sufficient means to fund the litigation. His assertions were not solidly backed by evidence indicating efforts made to secure funding for the costs required ([31], [34]). The Court concluded that the High Court acted within its discretion, considering all relevant factors including the complexity of the issues at hand, the history of the parties, and the potential public interest implications of the litigation ([32], [33]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court dismissed Giddey’s appeal, affirming the High Court’s order for security. It established that the exercise of discretion regarding security for costs must weigh the interests of access to the courts against the financial feasibility of litigation, particularly where a plaintiff is in liquidation ([36]). The decision underlined that, while courts must be mindful of constitutional rights, they must also navigate the procedural rules that are designed to ensure fairness in litigation without unduly restraining access to court ([29], [30]).

The judgment reaffirmed the constitutional imperative of balancing access to justice with the prudent management of judicial resources, ensuring that potential financial burdens do not deter legitimate claims while protecting against frivolous litigation ([34]).

In summary, the ruling confirms the constitutionality of section 13 of the Companies Act regarding security for costs while articulating a coherent framework for courts to apply in balancing competing interests within the substantive law ([36]).
