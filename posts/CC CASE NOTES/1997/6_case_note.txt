Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1997

# FIRAC Case Note: Ntandazeli Fose v The Minister of Safety and Security [1996] CCT 14/96

## F – Facts
The applicant, Ntandazeli Fose, sought to appeal against a judgment delivered by Van Schalkwyk J in the Witwatersrand Local Division of the Supreme Court. The essence of the case revolved around allegations made by Fose that he was subjected to torture and other forms of ill-treatment by members of the South African Police Service during May 1994. He submitted three claims for damages: the first two (B1 and B2) mainly focused on traditional delictual claims for pain and suffering, while claim C sought "constitutional damages" in the sum of R200,000, asserting that these damages encapsulated an element of punitive damages due to the egregious nature of the violations he experienced ([paras 1–18]).

Van Schalkwyk J upheld an exception based on two grounds: 1) that the law did not recognize a claim for "constitutional damages"; and 2) that such an order did not constitute "appropriate relief" under section 7(4)(a) of the interim Constitution ([paras 14–15]). This led to the Constitutional Court being approached for leave to appeal, wherein the applicant argued the importance of asserting and vindicating constitutional rights ([paras 11–12],[20]).

## I – Issues
The primary constitutional issue at stake was whether the concept of "constitutional damages," as a specific form of relief, could be recognized within South African law, particularly in light of section 7(4)(a) of the interim Constitution, which enables courts to provide "appropriate relief" for infringements of fundamental rights ([paras 11–12],[15]). Moreover, the court had to consider whether the allegations warranted the notion that such damages could include punitive elements, thereby introducing a novel legal question in South African constitutional jurisprudence ([paras 17–22]).

## R – Rules
The court relied heavily on the provisions of sections 7(4)(a) and 35(3) of the interim Constitution. Section 7(4)(a) specifies that individuals are entitled to apply to competent courts for "appropriate relief" when their rights are infringed. The court also drew parallels with international and comparative law, such as provisions in the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms and the European Convention on Human Rights, to illustrate how other jurisdictions have addressed constitutional damages ([paras 15–22],[24–55]). Importantly, the court recalled a critical principle from previous judgments emphasizing that proper and effective relief must serve to uphold the Constitution ([paras 11–12],[19]).

## A – Application
In its analysis, the Constitutional Court examined whether the claims made for constitutional damages aligned with the functions prescribed under section 7(4)(a). Ackermann J noted that the concept of "constitutional damages" was unique to this context and was not directly supported by established legal principles in South African law. He concluded that traditional tort remedies were sufficient to address the issues raised in claims B1 and B2 without the need to establish a new category of damages ([paras 70–74]).

Further, the court held that claims for punitive damages, as part of a constitutional damages framework, were inappropriate under the specific facts of this case. Assertions were made about the necessity of deterrence and vindication of constitutional rights, but it was concluded that awarding punitive damages would be counterproductive, as they would essentially be funded by taxpayers and not serve the intended objectives of deterrence against state misconduct ([paras 87–105]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled that the exception was correctly upheld by the lower court, confirming that the applicant's claim for "constitutional damages," particularly as articulated, did not present a legally recognizable cause of action under South African law. Therefore, the appeal for leave to appeal was dismissed ([paras 75–76]). This landmark decision underscores the need for established legal frameworks in constitutional litigation while also highlighting how remedial structures need to resonate with the unique aspects of constitutional rights, paving the way for ongoing discussions on the nature of relief in similar contexts within South African law.
