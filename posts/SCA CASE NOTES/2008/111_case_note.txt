# Case Note: Asiatic Shipping Services Inc v Elgina Marine Company Limited (487/07) [2008] ZASCA 111

## F - Facts
The case revolves around an appeal following the arrest of the MV 'Orient Stride' by Elgina Marine Company Ltd (hereafter, "Elgina") in the Durban High Court, under the provisions of the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983 (the "Act") ([1]). Elgina, a Cypriot company, sought the arrest to secure its claims against Asiatic Shipping Services Inc (hereafter, "Asiatic"), a Panamanian shipping company, in arbitration proceedings in London ([2]).

On March 24, 2006, Elgina obtained an ex parte order for the arrest of the bunkers on board the 'Orient Stride' and certain freight rights held by agents for Asiatic. In response, Asiatic provided a conditional guarantee to secure the property’s release ([3]). Subsequently, Asiatic filed an application to set aside the arrest, which was dismissed by Patel J on February 5, 2007, leading to the present appeal ([4]).

Throughout the proceedings, it was established that Elgina bore the burden of justifying the arrest ([5]). The only remaining question was whether Elgina had proven a "genuine and reasonable need for security" as mandated by previous jurisprudence ([6]). 

Notably, Elgina's claim arose from the charter of the MV 'Columbine Express' and was directed against Asiatic for outstanding payments and damages caused by the late redelivery of the vessel ([8]–[9]). The legal complexities involved the relationship between Asiatic and its purported undisclosed principal, Pacific Inter-Link Sdn Bhd (“PIL”) ([15]). Tensions arose regarding the parties’ conflicting views on liability due to the undisclosed principal's status during arbitration ([12]–[13]).

## I - Issues
The primary legal issue presented before the court was:
1. Whether Elgina could demonstrate a "genuine and reasonable need for security" to justify the arrest of Asiatic’s property under section 5(3) of the Act, as interpreted in relevant case law ([6–7]).

This issue was intertwined with other concerns, including:
- The nature of the relationship between Asiatic and PIL, especially regarding duties and liabilities in arbitration ([15]).
- The sufficiency of Asiatic's assertion of its financial capabilities to satisfy any potential judgment, which Elgina contested ([16]).

## R - Rules
The courts have established several principles regarding the requirement for security in a maritime context:
- The phrase "genuine and reasonable need for security" originates from judicial interpretation rather than from the statutory wording of the Act ([7]). Specifically, Didcott J’s formulation in *Katagum Wholesale Commodities Co Ltd v The MV Paz* is key, as is the endorsement by the court in *Thalassini Avgi* and *Bocimar NV v Kotor Overseas Shipping Ltd* ([7]).
- The standard of proof for establishing the need for security is "on a balance of probabilities," rather than requiring absolute certainty regarding an inability to meet judicial awards ([7]).
- A case for security can be built upon the apprehension that a party may attempt to conceal assets or may, due to geographic or logistical factors, render any subsequent judgment difficult to enforce ([7]). 

It was recognized that while the requirement doesn’t necessitate proving that the debtor has insufficient assets, a mental state of genuine concern for an inability to satisfy a potential award must be established ([7]).

## A - Application
In applying these rules to the present case, the court first recognized Elgina’s successful establishment of a prima facie case against Asiatic, as it was common cause that the bunkers were Asiatic's property ([6–7]). 

The court then scrutinized the broader implications of the relationship between Asiatic and PIL. The facts illustrated that PIL was not only closely affiliated with Asiatic but likely held substantial assets that might affect the enforcement of obligations ([15]). Asiatic’s failures to disclose details about its financial assets exacerbated Elgina's apprehensions regarding the collectability of any arbitration award ([16]).

The court determined that Elgina had expressed reasonable apprehensions grounded in the changing dynamics of the parties’ involvement in arbitration and the historical context of the claims ([17]). Elgina's argument for security was further reinforced by its prior interactions with Asiatic about the counterclaims, which elucidated a potential reluctance from Asiatic to satisfactorily answer concerns over asset adequacy ([16]).

Thus, the court concluded that Elgina effectively demonstrated a genuine need for security based on the cumulative factual and legal context presented ([17]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the lower court’s decision to uphold the arrest. The ratio decidendi highlighted the necessity of establishing a “genuine and reasonable need for security” as a crucial legal principle governing maritime law and the enforcement of arbitration claims in South Africa ([18]). 

The implications of this decision reinforce the importance of parties’ transparency regarding asset disclosures in arbitration contexts and underscore the judiciary's commitment to ensuring that valid claims are safeguarded through appropriate security measures. Thus, the outcome serves as a significant precedent in the interpretation of the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act and the obligations of parties in maritime arbitration.
