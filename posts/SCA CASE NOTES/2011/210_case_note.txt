Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: FAWU O B O MBATHA & 11 OTHERS v PIONEER FOODS (PTY) LTD t/a SASKO MILLING & OTHERS [2011] ZASCA 210

## F - Facts
The case originated from an application for leave to appeal against a labor court judgment (Gush AJ) concerning the dismissal of certain employees (the applicants) by Pioneer Foods (Pty) Ltd for alleged misconduct relating to the company's safety regulations ([paras 1-2]). The First Applicant, FAWU (Food and Allied Workers Union), represented the dismissed employees, who were charged with serious violations of safety protocols during a gas leak incident on 25 May 2005, where a passer-by tragically lost their life due to the gas exposure ([paras 3-5]). 

During the emergency, an alarm and intercom announcement instructed all employees to evacuate to a designated assembly point; however, several employees, including a key individual, Mr. Mkhize, failed to follow these instructions ([paras 4-6]). Despite clear instructions, Mr. Mkhize was found in the change rooms, and additional employees, including the applicants, left the assembly area before the all-clear signal was given, which constituted gross insubordination ([paras 7-8]).

A disciplinary inquiry followed, and the applicants were dismissed for misconduct related to safety compliance. The applicants defended their case, arguing procedural unfairness and mishandling by the employer during the inquiry process ([paras 9-11]). Ultimately, the CCMA awarded that the dismissal was substantively unfair, leading Pioneer Foods to challenge the award before the Labor Court, resulting in the award being set aside ([paras 12-14]).

## I - Issues
The key legal issues on appeal were:
1. Whether the Labour Court (Gush AJ) misapplied the Sidumo test regarding the review of the CCMA award ([para 18]).
2. Whether the Labour Court correctly assessed the substantive unfairness of the applicants' dismissals in light of the emergency protocols established by Pioneer Foods ([para 22]).

## R - Rules
The court relied on the test for reviewing arbitrators' decisions as established in **Sidumo v Rustenburg Platinum Mines Ltd** 2008 (2) SA 24 (CC), where it states that the review must determine if the decision reached was one that a reasonable decision-maker could not arrive at ([para 18]). Furthermore, in **National Union of Metalworkers of SA v Fry’s Metals (Pty) Ltd** 2005 (5) SA 433 (SCA), the court established that for leave to appeal to be granted, it must demonstrate some additional factors beyond a mere reasonable prospect of success, particularly when there has been a full hearing in a specialized tribunal ([para 19]).

## A - Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted the rigorous application of the Sidumo test in reviewing the Labour Court's behavior. It concluded that the Labour Court adequately addressed the circumstances of the case and the nature of the misconduct under review. The court noted the gravity of the incident, where instructions pertaining to employee safety were blatantly disregarded ([paras 20-21]). 

Importantly, the court criticized the applicants' conduct during both the emergency and subsequent disciplinary proceedings. Despite acknowledging the misunderstanding surrounding instructions, it upheld the critical need for compliance with safety protocols to prevent potential risks to life ([paras 26-29]). 

The decision emphasized that the misconduct justified dismissal, given the safety implications and the applicants' failure to participate in the disciplinary process, which indicated a lack of regard for the seriousness inherent in the situation ([paras 23-24]). The court reinforced the need for reliability in safety compliance given the potential civil and criminal repercussions of the applicants' actions during a severe gas leak, asserting that even if the dismissal seemed harsh in light of the employees’ long service, it was justified under the circumstances ([paras 22, 27-28]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the application for leave to appeal was dismissed, reinforcing the Labour Court's findings and the original decision to uphold the dismissals as substantively fair. The case exemplified the critical balance between adherence to employer's safety protocols and the implications of non-compliance in emergency situations. The ruling effectively upheld the authority of the Labor Court in adjudicating the procedural and substantive issues raised, emphasizing the importance of compliance with occupational safety regulations and the risks associated with disregard thereof ([paras 28-29]). 

**Order**: The application for leave to appeal was dismissed with costs, establishing a precedent on the necessity for strict compliance with workplace safety protocols during emergencies ([para 17]).
