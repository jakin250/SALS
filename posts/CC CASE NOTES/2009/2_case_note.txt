Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2009

# Case Note: Shane van Straaten v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others

**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number:** CCT 106/08  
**Judgment Date:** 24 February 2009  
**Citation:** [2009] ZACC 2  

---

## FIRAC Analysis

### F - Facts
In this case, the applicant, Shane van Straaten, sought urgent direct access to the Constitutional Court under section 167(4)(d) of the Constitution of South Africa ([1]), claiming the invalidity of the National Prosecuting Authority Amendment Bill, 2008, and the South African Police Service Amendment Bill, 2008. These bills aimed to disband the Directorate of Special Operations, popularly referred to as the Scorpions, and create a new specialized crime-fighting unit ([1–2]).

Van Straaten alleged that certain members of the African National Congress (ANC), who voted in favor of these bills, exhibited bias against the Scorpions due to their own legal troubles, claiming they were under investigation or facing criminal charges ([2]). He asserted that this interference constituted unlawfulness, unconstitutionality, and corruption. However, the crux of his argument hinged on a misunderstanding of the jurisdictional scope under which the Constitutional Court could rule on the constitutionality of bills ([3]).

### I - Issues
The primary legal issue at stake was whether the Constitutional Court had the jurisdiction to hear and decide on the constitutional validity of the two parliamentary bills, given that they were not considered constitutional amendments as defined by the Constitution ([4]). The Court questioned the legitimacy of the applicant's claims regarding the basis for jurisdiction under section 167(4)(d) ([4–5]).

### R - Rules
The jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court is defined in section 167 of the Constitution, which outlines the Court's exclusive authority to interpret the Constitution and to determine the constitutionality of any amendment ([1]). The Court has jurisdiction to decide on the constitutionality of a Bill only under certain specific circumstances delineated in sections 79 and 121, primarily when the President or a Premier refers a Bill back for reconsideration due to constitutional concerns ([4]). In prior cases, including *President of the Republic of South Africa v United Democratic Movement* and *Doctors for Life International v Speaker of the National Assembly*, the Court has consistently held that it may not entertain challenges to the constitutionality of legislation outside of these specified avenues ([4]).

### A - Application
The Court analyzed the applicant's claims and highlighted that the bills were passed without any challenge from the President or Premier, thus indicating that the requisite conditions for its jurisdiction were absent. Specifically, it noted that the relevant constitutional provisions emphasized the time of proceedings initiation as critical for jurisdiction, rather than the timing of the Court's consideration of a matter ([5]). As such, the Court concluded that it lacked jurisdiction to entertain the application since it was both premature and not in accordance with the legislative framework governing the process ([6]).

Furthermore, although the President had signed the bills into law, the Court emphasized that the critical jurisdictional requirements under the Constitution were not met at the start of the proceedings. 

### C - Conclusion
The Court dismissed the application, ruling that it had no jurisdiction to consider the case as the prerequisites for constitutional review were not fulfilled ([6–8]). In its judgment, the Court highlighted the importance of prompt state responses to legal proceedings, reflecting on the broader implications of governmental accountability and adherence to the rule of law. It expressed concern over the failure of the State Attorney’s office to communicate responses regarding the application, emphasizing that such inaction undermines the respect for legal processes ([9–10]).

The dismissal does not only signify the end of this particular case but reiterates the procedural barriers upheld by the Constitutional Court regarding the legislative review process. Moreover, the Court indicated its intent to send a copy of the judgment to the offices of the President and the Minister for Justice, aiming for accountability and preventive measures against future violations of procedure ([11–12]).
