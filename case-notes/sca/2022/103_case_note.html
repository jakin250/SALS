Case Note: Diljan v Minister of Police ([2022] ZASCA 103)

F – Facts

The case involves the appellant, Ms. Avril Edith Diljan, who initiated a claim for unlawful arrest and detention against the Minister of Police after her arrest on 18 September 2015 ([para 2]). The arrest occurred when Constables Ntombela and Tsile responded to a complaint from a Ms. Goliath, alleging that Diljan had damaged her property by throwing stones and rubbish through her window ([para 2]). Upon assessing the scene, the officers determined that an offence of malicious damage to property had occurred and proceeded to arrest Diljan without a warrant ([para 2–3]).

Despite the officers asserting that their actions were justified under Section 40(1)(b) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA), the circumstances of the arrest prompted Diljan to contest the legality of her detention. She testified that she was not informed of the reason for her arrest, nor was she brought before a court after her detention from 18 to 21 September 2015 ([para 4]). The Magistrate’s Court ruled that the arrest was lawful based on the officers' reasonable suspicion, a decision confirmed by the Gauteng Division of the High Court on appeal ([para 5–6]).

Diljan's appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal questioned whether the peace officers properly exercised their discretion when arresting her ([para 1]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue is whether Constables Ntombela and Tsile properly exercised their discretion as peace officers under Section 40(1)(b) of the CPA when deciding to arrest Diljan ([para 1]). This encompasses two specific sub-issues:

  1. Did the officers possess a reasonable suspicion founded on facts justifying the arrest?
  2. Did they understand their discretion regarding arrest, particularly regarding warning the appellant or providing bail options ([para 8–9])?

R – Rules

Section 40(1)(b) of the CPA permits a peace officer to arrest without a warrant if they reasonably suspect that a person has committed an offence listed in Schedule 1 ([para 7]). The essential conditions needed to validate such an arrest include:

  • The individual must be a peace officer.
  • There must exist reasonable suspicion based on valid grounds that the suspect has committed an offense.
  • The officer has discretion concerning the mode of securing the suspect's attendance in court, which is not restricted to arrest ([para 7–9]).

In Minister of Safety and Security v Sekhoto, it was established that once the jurisdictional facts for an arrest are present, a discretion arises whether or not to arrest ([para 10]). Moreover, the exercise of this discretion must be informed and aware of the officer's authority ([para 12]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal underscored that both the trial court and the high court incorrectly assessed the officers' exercise of discretion. The officers mistakenly believed that they had no alternative but to arrest once a suspicion arose, which meant that they did not regard the option of issuing a warning or allowing for bail ([para 11], [para 12]). This misapprehension led to the conclusion that no proper discretion was exercised, thereby rendering the arrest and subsequent detention unlawful.

The court analyzed the implications of the unlawful arrest and explored the nature of the damages due to Diljan. Testimonies illustrated that she faced deplorable conditions during her detention, including inadequate provisions and a lack of medical attention for her heart condition ([para 22]). These factors were key in determining the quantum of damages.

Despite the trial court’s decision not addressing the damages due to its ruling on liability, the Supreme Court opted not to remit the issue of damages to the trial court, citing the sufficiency of the ventilation of the issue ([para 13]). Guided by precedents in similar cases, the court determined that R120,000 would be an appropriate and fair award for general damages given the circumstances of the unlawful arrest and the conditions of her detention ([para 22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court upheld Diljan's appeal, declaring her arrest and detention unlawful, and awarded her R120,000 in damages along with interest calculated from the date of detention ([para 24]). The decision emphasizes the necessity for peace officers to understand their discretionary powers comprehensively, as failure to do so can have severe implications on the legality of arrests and the rights of individuals. Furthermore, the ruling serves as a reminder of the importance of accountability within law enforcement and the protection of personal liberty.

This case serves as a vital precedent in reinforcing the standards required for lawful arrests and the consequences of disregarding the exercise of lawful discretion by authority figures.
