Constitutional Court 2006 CCT 48/05

Case Title: Van Der Merwe v The Road Accident Fund and Others (CCT 48/05)

Heard on: 24 November 2005
Decided on: 30 March 2006
Constitutional Court of South Africa

F – Facts

Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–13])

This case addresses the constitutional validity of certain provisions within the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984, specifically regarding the dynamics of patrimonial claims between spouses married in community of property. The applicant, Mrs Vanessa van der Merwe, was involved in a collision where her husband intentionally ran her over with a vehicle while they were still married. Following this incident, which resulted in serious bodily harm to Mrs van der Merwe, she sought damages from the Road Accident Fund (the Fund).

The statutory framework governing the claims included specific provisions that restricted recovery for delictual damages related to patrimonial loss between spouses in community of property. Referenced in the case, section 18(a) and (b) of the Act state that damages recovered by a spouse for delict committed against them do not fall into the joint estate but remain their separate property, except for claims specifically related to patrimonial loss. The High Court found that section 18(b) discriminated against spouses in this category, thus rendering it unconstitutional, prompting a confirmation application to the Constitutional Court ([paras 1–6]).

High Court Decision ([paras 16–18])

The High Court concluded that the prohibition under section 18(b) against recovering patrimonial damages between spouses married in community of property amounted to unfair discrimination on the grounds of marital status, violating sections 9(3) and 10 of the Constitution regarding non-discrimination and dignity ([paras 17–18]).

I – Issues

The Constitutional Court's inquiry focused on several pivotal questions:

  1. Does the legislation in question differentiate between categories of people (i.e., spouses married in and out of community of property) in a manner that constitutes unfair discrimination? ([para 18]).
  2. Is there a rational basis or legitimate government interest served by the differentiation between the two marital property regimes as outlined in section 18(b)? ([paras 34–48]).
  3. Does the discrimination arising from section 18(b) unjustifiably limit the applicant’s constitutional rights under the equality clause? ([paras 63–64]).

R – Rules

The following are key legal principles and precedents referenced throughout the decision:

  1. Equality Clause (Section 9): Section 9(1) affords every individual equal protection and benefit of the law, while section 9(3) prohibits discrimination on various grounds, including marital status ([paras 2, 12]).
  2. Dignity Rights (Section 10): Section 10 guarantees all individuals the right to dignity and the protection thereof ([para 3]).
  3. Property Rights (Section 25): Section 25(1) safeguards against arbitrary deprivation of property ([para 3]).
  4. Limitation of Rights (Section 36): Any limitation must be justified under section 36 of the Constitution, where the onus rests with the state to prove the legitimacy and proportionality of such a limitation ([para 36]).

A – Application

Distinction and Rationality ([paras 42–57])

The Court identified that section 18(b) creates a stark distinction between spouses in community versus those married out of community of property, restricting the former from claiming patrimonial damages arising from bodily harm inflicted by their spouses. It was determined that this distinction lacked a rational connection to a legitimate government purpose. Advocating for the necessity of equal protection under the law, the Court emphasized that the aim of matrimonial property law should not only regulate but also protect spouses from discrimination ([paras 48–56]).

Gender Discrimination ([paras 64–68])

Furthermore, the Court noted that section 18(b) has particular implications for women disproportionately affected by domestic violence and economic vulnerability. The legislative provision perpetuates an inequality that primarily disadvantages women, thus failing the equality analysis [[para 66]].

Remedial Action ([paras 70–76])

Ultimately, the Court concluded that legislative restriction under section 18(b) was inconsistent with the Constitution. A declaration of invalidity was ordered, with the necessity to sever and read in specific words to ensure that recovered patrimonial damages would no longer revert to the joint estate but instead become the separate property of the injured spouse ([para 75]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court upheld the High Court’s finding that section 18(b) of the Matrimonial Property Act was unconstitutional as it discriminated against spouses married in community of property, limiting their capacity to claim patrimonial damages arising from delictual acts against them. The court confirmed the order of constitutional invalidity while tailoring the remedy to ensure that the reformed provision would provide necessary equitable protection to injured spouses ([paras 78–80]).

Ratio Decidendi: The Court's ruling emphasizes the critical importance of upholding constitutional values of equality and dignity, particularly in the face of entrenched discriminatory practices within matrimonial law that fail to adapt to contemporary societal standards.

The ruling significantly alters the legal landscape for spouses in community of property and underscores the necessity for legislative frameworks that safeguard against disparities based on marital status, reinforcing the constitutional guarantees of equality and dignity for all individuals.
