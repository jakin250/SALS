# FIRAC Case Note: *National Director of Public Prosecutions v Zuma* (573/08) [2009] ZASCA 1

## F – Facts:

The case arises against the backdrop of a tumultuous political environment in South Africa. Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma, the respondent, served as Deputy President of South Africa before being dismissed in June 2005. On August 23, 2003, the then National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP), Bulelani Ngcuka, announced a decision not to prosecute Zuma based on an insufficient likelihood of securing a conviction concerning allegations raised in the conviction of Schabir Shaik for corruption ([1]–[4]). In December 2007, however, a new NDPP, Vusi Pikoli, decided to indict Zuma [paras 5–6]. Pikoli's decision was further revisited by the acting NDPP, Mokotedi Joseph Mpshe, who reiterated the indictment on December 27, 2007, which produced an extensive indictment comprising 18 counts [paras 7–9]. 

Zuma approached the court seeking to have the decisions of both Pikoli and Mpshe declared void due to procedural irregularities, specifically alleging that the decisions constituted a review of Ngcuka’s earlier forbearance in prosecuting him without affording him an opportunity to make representations, a procedural right he asserted under s 179(5)(d) of the Constitution [paras 9–10]. The High Court under Nicholson J ruled in favor of Zuma, nullifying the Mpshe decision [paras 11–12]. 

## I – Issues:

This appeal concentrated on two primary issues:

1. Whether the Ndpp's decisions to prosecute Zuma were invalid due to a failure to consult him as required under s 179(5)(d) of the Constitution prior to reviewing the decision not to initiate prosecution [paras 9, 53].
2. Did Zuma possess a legitimate expectation for a hearing due to the context of the decisions which mandated representation [paras 10, 76]? 

Furthermore, the appeal examined the limits of judicial authority in addressing political concerns and relevance in judicial pronouncements, particularly concerning judicial overreach in commenting on political motives or actions [paras 15–16].

## R – Rules:

The legal grounding of the appeal identified several constitutional provisions and principles relevant to prosecutorial discretion, including:

1. **Section 179(5)(d) of the Constitution**: This provision delineates the procedural requirements for the NDPP to consult the relevant Director of Public Prosecutions and consider representations when conducting a review of prosecutorial decisions [paras 9, 55–66].
2. **Section 33 of the Constitution**: This section specifies the right to just administrative action and is relevant to Zuma's argument of legitimate expectation arising from prior representations suggested by Ngcuka’s statement [para 10].
3. The ruling also referenced legal precedents that outline the limits of the judiciary concerning political issues, emphasizing the independence of prosecutors devoid of undue executive influence [paras 20, 28].

## A – Application:

The Court carefully assessed the merits of Zuma’s claims, focusing particularly on the interpretation of s 179(5)(d). It determined that this section pertains specifically to reviewing decisions made by Directors of Public Prosecutions and does not extend to the NDPP’s reconsideration of his own prior decisions [paras 70–72]. Thus, the Court held that the NDPP was not mandated to invite representations from the accused following the earlier decision of Ngcuka, given that it was a situation of a new decision rather than a review of an old one. Furthermore, while Zuma argued that he had a legitimate expectation to be heard, the Court found insufficient basis for such expectations, noting that he had not availed himself of opportunities for representation during the earlier investigative phases [paras 77–80].

The judgment also commented on the judicial role in matters touching upon political sensitivity, suggesting that Nicholson J had erred by making findings that extended beyond the procedural questions presented [paras 15–16]. The judgment underlined that speculation or deductions about political motivations were inappropriate considerations in judicial determinations [para 18].

## C – Conclusion:

The appeal was upheld, and the earlier ruling by the High Court was overturned. The NDPP maintained its right to make prosecutorial decisions without extending the procedural requisites contemplated in s 179(5)(d) to reviews of its own decisions. As a result, the prosecution against Zuma was allowed to continue unimpeded by the procedural claims he raised. The judgment reinforces the boundary between judicial review and prosecutorial discretion and clarifies the procedural scope pertaining to representation in prosecution matters. The implications of the ruling suggest a strong reaffirmation of prosecutorial independence, while also delineating the limits of judicial oversight concerning politically charged matters ([54]–[88]). 

As a broader takeaway, the decision contributes to the discourse on the relationship between political accountability, judicial integrity, and the enforcement of criminal law in a constitutional democracy.
