Supreme Court of Appeal

Plattekloof RMS Boerdery (Pty) Ltd v Dahlia Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd

Clause 10 stipulated that if the respondent received any bona fide offer to sell the property, it was obliged to inform the appellant and grant them 14 days to accept or reject the offer.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 Plattekloof RMS Boerdery (Pty) Ltd v Dahlia Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd (667/2021) [2022] ZASCA 182 Delivered 15 December 2022

Neutral Citation: Plattekloof RMS Boerdery (Pty) Ltd v Dahlia Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd (667/2021) [2022] ZASCA 182

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Coram: Van der Merwe, Gorven, Mothle JJA, Windell and Mali AJJA

Heard: 3 November 2022

Delivered: 15 December 2022

F – Facts

This case arose from a dispute regarding a pre-emptive right associated with the lease of two portions of a farm situated in Riversdale, Western Cape, owned by the respondent, Dahlia Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd. On 13 April 2018, the appellant, Plattekloof RMS Boerdery (Pty) Ltd, entered into a lease agreement with the respondent, which included a right of first refusal (clause 10).

Clause 10 stipulated that if the respondent received any bona fide offer to sell the property, it was obliged to inform the appellant and grant them 14 days to accept or reject the offer. On 7 April 2020, the respondent sold the entire farm to Swellendam Plase (Pty) Ltd for a total price of R17 million, without specifying the price of the two relevant portions. The appellant, upon learning of the sale on 16 April 2020, demanded compliance with clause 10, but the respondent neither acknowledged the claim nor provided the requisite offer. Consequently, the appellant initiated legal proceedings in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, which dismissed the application for specific performance ([paras 1–8]).

I – Issues

The primary issue for determination was whether the respondent had a contractual obligation to present an offer for the two leased portions based on the sale to Swellendam Plase and whether the appellant was entitled to enforce the enforcement of the right of first refusal under clause 10 ([paras 9–10]).

A secondary issue pertained to the interpretation of clause 10, particularly whether the pre-emptive right was activated by the sale that included the two portions as part of a global deal ([paras 11–12]).

R – Rules

The court relied on established principles of contract interpretation, recognizing that the rights granted under clause 10 must be construed in light of the ordinary meaning of the contract language. Particularly, the court cited GB Bradfield, Christie’s Law of Contract in South Africa, and referenced the precedent set in Sher v Allan for the interpretative framework ([paras 10–14]).

Additionally, the court examined the principles surrounding the enforcement of rights in contracts, specifically in cases of breach related to pre-emptive rights as discussed in earlier judgment, Brocsand (Pty) Ltd v Tip Trans Resources and Others ([paras 17–18]).

A – Application

In analyzing the relevant clauses, the court emphasized that the appellant's right was not contingent upon receiving a separate offer solely for the two portions. Instead, the sale of the entire farm activated the right of pre-emption under clause 10. The court reasoned that selling the farm as a package did not circumvent the appellant’s rights, as the contract's intention could only be understood through the lens of keeping the pre-emptive right intact ([paras 12–14]).

The court rejected the respondent's argument that by not specifying a separate price for the two portions, it could deny its obligation to make an offer to the appellant. It determined that the respondent indeed had to assess and present to the appellant what portion of the sale price was attributable to the two portions ([paras 18–24]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately varied the high court's decision by setting aside the dismissal of the appellant's application. It ordered the respondent to deliver a written offer for the two portions in accordance with clause 10 within 10 days. The judgment confirmed that the respondent was in breach of its contractual obligation by failing to make an offer corresponding to the pre-emptive right triggered by the global purchase.

The court also held that this case served as a reminder of the necessity of honoring contractual agreements and that it is within the courts' power to enforce such compliance without imposing undue burdens on either party ([paras 27–29]). The appellant was ordered to bear the costs of the appeal, including those of two counsel, establishing that compliance with contractual obligations should not disproportionately disadvantage one party ([para 28]).

This case reinforces contractual integrity and serves as a pivotal reference for future disputes regarding pre-emptive rights and their enforcement.
