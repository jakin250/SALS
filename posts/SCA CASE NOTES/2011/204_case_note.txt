Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Law Society of the Northern Provinces v Sonntag (189/2011) [2011] ZASCA 204

### F – Facts
The present appeal arises from the decision of the North Gauteng High Court that had suspended the respondent, Rachel Freda Sonntag, from practice as an attorney for one year, with a three-year suspension of that sentence conditional upon certain requirements. The appellant, Law Society of the Northern Provinces, contested this decision, arguing that Sonntag should have been struck off the roll entirely ([1]).

Sonntag, a sole practitioner in Tzaneen since her admission as an attorney in 1999, faced multiple charges related to her engagement with touts—namely, Basie van Schalkwyk and his son-in-law, Eugene Swanepoel—who solicited third-party claims on her behalf, violating the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 ([3]-[5]). An investigation initiated on complaints led to findings of unprofessional conduct, with Sonntag guilty of five charges enclosed in a disciplinary report ([5]).

The charges included: referring work exclusively reserved for attorneys to an unqualified individual, collaborating with individuals not entitled to practice, knowingly working for persons referred by such individuals, and sharing fees and office space with them ([5]-[6]). Ultimately, Sonntag pleaded guilty to these charges, attributing this decision to emotional distress during her earlier plea of not guilty and alleged promises made regarding fines by her counsel ([6]).

### I – Issues
The central legal question before the court was whether the High Court made an appropriate discretionary decision by opting for a suspension rather than striking Sonntag off the roll as an attorney. Specifically, the court had to determine if Sonntag's actions rendered her unfit for practice, thus necessitating her removal ([1]-[2], [17]).

Questions of procedural fairness and the proportionality of sanctions in disciplinary matters also arose, with particular focus on whether exceptional circumstances justified a lighter penalty than removal ([15]-[19]).

### R – Rules
The governing provision in this case is section 22(1)(d) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979, which empowers the court to remove an attorney from the roll or suspend them from practice if they are deemed unfit to continue ([2]). The court emphasized a three-stage inquiry in such cases:

1. Establishing the alleged misconduct on a preponderance of probabilities.
2. Evaluating whether the attorney is not a fit and proper person based on the proven conduct.
3. Deciding on the appropriate disciplinary measure (removal vs. suspension) considering the gravity of the misconduct ([2]).

The court also referenced the precedent set in Botha v Law Society, Northern Provinces, providing guidelines on delineating the discretion exercised in deciding punitive measures against attorneys, emphasizing that dishonesty is not a necessary precondition for striking-off ([14]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the charges against Sonntag were substantiated and indicative of serious misconduct impacting her fitness to practice law. Elements such as the systematic employment of touts for soliciting third-party claims, sharing fees, and cohabitating in professional space were critiqued as fundamentally requiring a response beyond mere suspension ([7], [11], [19]).

In reviewing the High Court’s discretionary decision, the Supreme Court identified significant misdirections in its reasoning. The High Court's reliance on mitigating factors—such as a lack of severe financial mismanagement or past conduct—did not appropriately reflect the seriousness of the misconduct. Furthermore, comparisons with prior cases were deemed inappropriate since each case's unique context should guide decisions rather than establish a hierarchy of misconduct ([15]).

The sustained denial of wrongdoing by Sonntag during the disciplinary proceedings was highlighted, revealing a lack of contrition that further warranted her removal. The voluminous evidence demonstrated ongoing, serious misconduct over several years, culminating in substantial financial dealings with touts totaling over R800,000, validating the need for the severest sanction ([19]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that it was inappropriate for the High Court to impose a mere suspension when the respondent had demonstrated a lack of integrity and fitness for practice due to her extensive involvement with touts and deceitful conduct throughout the proceedings ([21]). Therefore, the Court upheld the appeal, ordering Sonntag's removal from the roll of attorneys with costs awarded to the Law Society. This case reiterated the legal profession's demand for complete honesty and integrity, reinforcing disciplinary standards expected of legal practitioners within South Africa ([1], [21]). 

Given the gravity of Sonntag's transgressions, the decision served as a critical reminder for legal professionals regarding the significant repercussions for breaches of ethical conduct in the practice of law.
