# FIRAC Case Note: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa (Case No 543/03)

## F – Facts

The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal relates to the interpretation of the Counterfeit Goods Act 37 of 1997 ('the Act'). The first respondent, The Gap, Inc., along with its affiliated companies, holds registered trademarks for the GAP mark in multiple jurisdictions, including South Africa. The appellants, comprising several clothing manufacturers, hold certain trademark registrations for related marks but have faced legal challenges regarding their validity ([1]).

The backdrop of the dispute involves the importation and transhipment of GAP-branded clothing sourced from countries where the trademark is valid, including Lesotho, Swaziland, and Mauritius, destined for markets outside South Africa. The appellants have attempted to halt these transhipments, labeling the goods as 'counterfeit' and employing the provisions of the Act to do so. In retaliation, the respondents sought and obtained a declaratory order from the Durban High Court, affirming that the transhipments do not violate the Act or the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([2]) ([3]).

The salient point of contention was whether goods in transit through South Africa fall under the prohibitions outlined in section 2(1)(f) of the Act, which prohibits the import, export, or transit of counterfeit goods unless for private use ([3]). During the High Court proceedings, this point of law had yet to be conclusively interpreted regarding the Act's applicability to goods merely passing through South Africa without entering the local market ([4]).

## I – Issues

The primary issues before the court were:

1. Whether the transhipment of goods through South Africa constitutes an 'import' under section 2(1)(f) of the Act.
2. The implications of the definitions of 'counterfeit goods' as prescribed by the Act in the broader context of international agreements, particularly the TRIPs agreement ([8]) ([10]).

The resolution of these issues focused on the statutory interpretation of 'importing' and 'transhipment,' along with the legislative intent underlying the Act ([8]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal framework governing the case includes:

1. **Counterfeit Goods Act 37 of 1997**: Specifically, section 2(1)(f), which prohibits the importation or exportation of counterfeit goods into or from South Africa, except for private use ([3]).

2. **Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993**: This Act governs the registration and infringement of trademarks, reinforcing the legal standing of trademarks against counterfeiting claims ([1]).

3. **TRIPs Agreement**: Provides the international legal backdrop for trademark registration, enforcement, and standards. Article 51 relates to procedures for right holders to seek the suspension of the release of counterfeit goods ([4]).

4. **Precedential Case Law**: The court drew on past decisions regarding the distinction between 'importing' and 'transhipment,' as established in cases such as *Tieber v Commissioner for Customs and Excise* ([9]).

## A – Application

In applying these rules to the facts, the court conducted a detailed interpretative analysis of the term 'import.' The court noted the specific legal definitions and historical context influencing the Act. It referenced past rulings which concluded that transhipment should not be regarded as 'import' in the legislative sense used in South Africa ([8]). The court reinforced this assertion by citing prior judgments that delineated between importing goods and merely facilitating the transit of goods through South Africa without local market engagement ([9]).

Moreover, the court emphasized that the legislative intent behind the Act did not extend to criminalizing the legitimate transit of goods that are not intended for sale in South Africa. It maintained that interpreting the statute to include transhipment would construe the law too broadly, undermining the purpose of the Act to prevent trademark infringement rather than hinder legitimate trade practices ([11]).

The court also rejected the appellants' suggestions that the phrase 'imported through' could extend to include goods that arrive at a local port but are destined for markets outside the country. It concluded that if the legislature intended for such provisions, it would have articulated this clearly in the statutory language ([12]). 

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal and upheld the High Court's declaration that the respondent's actions regarding goods in transit were lawful under the Counterfeit Goods Act. The court's decision clarified that transhipment through South Africa does not constitute an 'import' in the manner prohibited by the Act, asserting that criminalizing such transitory movements would undermine legitimate economic activities and violate international trade agreements ([13]).

The implications of this ruling extend beyond this case, establishing a precedent for the treatment of goods in transit under South African law, balancing intellectual property protection with a commitment to facilitating international trade without unnecessary barriers. The case reinforces the principle that criminal statutes must be constructed with clarity and foresight, emphasizing the need for a measured approach to intellectual property rights that does not inhibit commerce ([11]). 

This case highlights the importance of statutory interpretation in aligning domestic law with international standards and protecting both local and foreign interests in an increasingly global marketplace.
