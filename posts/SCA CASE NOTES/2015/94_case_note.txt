Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Modiga v The State (20738/14) [2015] ZASCA 94

## F – Facts ([paras 1–4])

On 17 December 2007, a cash-in-transit robbery occurred near Vereeniging when a vehicle belonging to Fidelity Cash Management was ambushed by a group of men. After the robbery, wherein firearms were used and several stolen vehicles were utilized, police officer Sergeant Robert Henry Deere responded to a radio dispatch about the incident. While en route, he observed a Mazda Drifter and a Toyota Cressida speeding away from the crime scene; shots were fired at him from the Mazda Drifter during a subsequent chase, which ended with the arrest of the appellant, Simon Modiga, among others in an area called Drie Rieviere.

The appellant was later charged with multiple offenses including robbery with aggravating circumstances, theft of motor vehicles, attempted murder, and illegal possession of firearms, ultimately being convicted on various counts and sentenced to 45 years imprisonment. Following an appeal in the North Gauteng High Court, his sentence was reduced to a cumulative 22 years, yet his convictions remained intact. The partial leave to appeal led to the current appeal concerning the identity of the appellant as part of the gang involved in the robbery ([paras 1–3]).

## I – Issues ([paras 4–8])

The primary legal question on appeal involved the adequacy of the evidence against the appellant as a member of the gang that executed the robbery. Specifically, the focus was on whether the evidence of a single witness, Mr. Nxuma, who implicated the appellant, was reliable. Additionally, the court sought to determine if the circumstantial evidence was sufficient to support the convictions for counts of robbery, theft of motor vehicles, and possession of illegal firearms ([paras 4–8]).

## R – Rules ([paras 18–19]; [31–33])

Various legal standards and precedents were considered throughout the appeal. Firstly, the cautionary rule for relying on the evidence of a single witness was highlighted, especially as articulated in *R v Mokoena* 1956 (3) SA 81 (A) and subsequently qualified in *S v Sauls and Others* 1981 (3) SA 172 (A). The reliance on circumstantial evidence was governed by the principles that even slight individual pieces of evidence could amount to a strong case when considered together (as stated in *S v Reddy*).

Further, the court noted the necessity for the prosecution to establish the guilt of the appellant beyond reasonable doubt, as per *S v Ntsele* 1998 (2) SACR 178 ([paras 20–24]). The judgments stressed the requirement for credible evidence from the prosecution while simultaneously recognizing that the burden did not shift to the appellant to prove his innocence ([paras 29–30]).

## A – Application ([paras 26–34])

The court first evaluated the veracity of Nxuma’s testimony, given the trial court's direct engagement with the witness. Despite some inconsistencies in Nxuma’s demeanor during cross-examination, the trial court found him credible and reliable. This credibility was central to the assessment of the circumstantial evidence including the appellant's arrest within a house containing the stolen vehicles and firearms ([paras 25–26]).

It was further emphasized that the circumstantial evidence, when considered collectively, established a coherent narrative leading to the conclusion that the appellant was part of the gang, despite his denial. The appellant's failure to disclose Nxuma’s involvement to the police at the time of his arrest was viewed as a gap in his defense, instead supporting the prosecution's case ([paras 32–33]).

In examining the counts of unlawful possession of stolen vehicles, the court ultimately found a lack of evidence directly tying the appellant to the ownership or use of the stolen Mercedes-Benz and Mazda Drifter, even though admissions related to the vehicles were acknowledged. The essential elements of possession (detentio and animus possidendi) were not sufficiently proven, leading to the quashing of these convictions ([paras 34]).

## C – Conclusion ([paras 34])

The Supreme Court of Appeal partially upheld the appeal. While the convictions and sentences on counts 1, 2, 6, and 7 were confirmed, the convictions and sentences on counts 3 and 4 (involving possession of the stolen vehicles) were set aside due to insufficient evidence establishing the appellant's direct involvement with the vehicles in question. This case illustrates the delicate balance courts must navigate between the weight of witness testimony and the validity of circumstantial evidence in criminal proceedings ([paras 34]). 

This ruling reaffirms the legal principles surrounding the evaluation of single witness testimony while highlighting the importance of corroborative evidence within the criminal justice system. It serves as a pertinent illustration of due process considerations and the rigorous standard of proof required in the prosecution of serious criminal offenses.
