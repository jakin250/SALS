Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: Women's Legal Centre Trust v President of the Republic of South Africa

**Court**: Constitutional Court of South Africa

**Case Citation**: Case CCT 13/09 [2009] ZACC 20

**Heard**: 20 May 2009  
**Decided**: 22 July 2009

**Judges**: Cameron J, Langa CJ, Moseneke DCJ, Mokgoro J, Ngcobo J, Nkabinde J, O’Regan J, Sachs J, Skweyiya J, Yacoob J

---

## F - Facts

The case involves the **Women’s Legal Centre Trust** (applicant) seeking direct access to the Constitutional Court, arguing that the **President** and **Parliament** have failed to fulfil their constitutional obligations regarding the recognition of Muslim marriages. The applicant requested an order directing these entities to prepare, initiate, enact, and implement legislation to recognize all Muslim marriages as valid within South African law ([1]).

The legal background includes a July 2003 report by the **South African Law Reform Commission**, which included a draft **Muslim Marriages Bill** intended to provide legal recognition of Muslim marriages. However, progress on the bill had stalled, and the Women’s Legal Centre contended that this inaction violated various constitutional rights including equality, dignity, and freedom of religion, as it resulted in the non-recognition of marriages solemnized by imams who were not registered ([8], [9]).

The case was heard in light of two preliminary issues decided by the Chief Justice: 
1. Whether the obligations claimed by the applicant fall within the exclusive jurisdiction outlined in Section 167(4)(e) of the Constitution.
2. If not, whether direct access should be granted under Section 167(6)(a) ([2]).

The respondents, led by the President and various ministers, disputed both the merits of the claims and the appropriateness of direct access, arguing that the application was premature ([10], [6]).

## I - Issues

The case presented two primary issues for determination:

1. **Jurisdictional Issue**: Are the obligations claimed by the Women’s Legal Centre obligations within the exclusive jurisdiction described in Section 167(4)(e) of the Constitution? This provision states that "only the Constitutional Court may decide that Parliament or the President has failed to fulfil a constitutional obligation" ([2]).

2. **Direct Access Issue**: If the obligations do not fall within the aforementioned jurisdiction, is it nonetheless appropriate for the Constitutional Court to entertain this application as a court of first and last instance under Section 167(6)(a)? ([3], [26]).

## R - Rules

The key legal provisions at play include:

- **Section 167(4)(e)** of the South African Constitution, which delineates the exclusive jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court regarding constitutional obligations of the President and Parliament.
- **Section 167(6)(a)** provides that the Constitutional Court may allow direct access in interests of justice ([2], [3]).
- Relevant decisions include *President of the Republic of South Africa and Others v South African Rugby Football Union* [1998] ZACC 21, where the court interpreted the narrow meaning of "fulfil a constitutional obligation" in section 167(4)(e) ([11]).

Additionally, the duties imposed by the Bill of Rights and the obligations derived from sections pertaining to the rights of individuals, such as sections 9, 10, 15, 30, and 31 of the Constitution, were noted as providing a context for assessing the duties of the state ([9]).

## A - Application

### Jurisdictional Analysis

The Court, led by Cameron J, discerned that the obligations claimed do not meet the threshold for exclusive jurisdiction under Section 167(4)(e). The court noted that while the applicant’s claims relate to the obligation to legislate and recognize Muslim marriages, this obligation cannot be strictly identified as resting solely upon the President or Parliament; rather, it extends to all organs of state collectively, thus precluding exclusive jurisdiction ([21], [22]).

Cameron J reasoned that Section 167(4)(e) is not intended to encompass all responsibilities of the executive or legislative branches and must be construed narrowly to avoid undermining the jurisdiction conferred to lower courts by Section 172(2)(a), which permits other courts to review the constitutionality of actions by the President and Parliament ([11], [21]).

### Direct Access Consideration

In evaluating whether to grant direct access, the Court underlined the infrequency with which it exercises this power, preferring to operate as a court of last resort and not first instance. The Court expressed a preference for allowing lower courts to clarify issues arising from disputes before them, which would permit the benefits of a multi-stage litigation process. The presence of conflicting theories and expert testimony about Muslim marriage issues reinforced the need for a lower court to thoroughly address such complexities ([26], [28]).

## C - Conclusion

The **Constitutional Court** dismissed the application for direct access because it was mistakenly directed solely at the Court’s exclusive jurisdiction, which, in light of the analysis, did not apply here. The judgment established a critical precedent that underlines the necessity of interpreting the constitutional provisions regarding jurisdiction restrictively to avoid undermining the broader legal framework governing state obligations.

Additionally, the ruling underscores the importance of a layered judicial process in handling complex constitutional issues, thereby contributing to a richer dialogue within the courts regarding sensitive matters of social and legal relevance.

The Court’s decision did not engage with the substantive merits of the claims made by the Women’s Legal Centre, thereby leaving the door open for future claims to be made in the appropriate forums ([33]).

As for costs, the Court ordered that there should be no costs orders against the unsuccessful litigant, aligning with the principle that individual litigants asserting constitutional rights against the state should not face adverse costs consequences, even when unsuccessful ([32]).

**Judgment**: The application for direct access is dismissed, with no order as to costs.
