Supreme Court of Appeal

Pretoria Society of Advocates v Van Zyl (517/18) [2019] ZASCA 13

Events unfolded between 2009 and 2011 during which Van Zyl was entrusted with the floor fund's finances.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 13

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts:

The appeal came before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) concerning the removal of Advocate Margaret van Zyl from the roll of advocates due to serious misconduct. The Gauteng Division of the High Court had initially imposed an 18-month suspension rather than removal ([1]). The respondent was part of the Pretoria Society of Advocates and managed a floor fund alongside a senior advocate, Mr M D du Preez SC, which included financial responsibilities such as reporting on expenditures ([2]–[3]).

Events unfolded between 2009 and 2011 during which Van Zyl was entrusted with the floor fund's finances. She was required to make several financial transactions, including a once-off contribution from fund members for kitchen equipment ([5]). However, Van Zyl withdrew R6,850 from the fund for personal use, a fact later contested when discrepancies were identified in financial records ([8]–[10]). Her attempts to justify the withdrawals relied on claims that funds had been transferred into her own personal accounts, which later proved to be misleading as she admitted to perjury and forgery regarding documents and statements ([12]–[16]).

The High Court, while recognizing the respondent's misconduct as serious, chose not to remove her from the roll but rather opted for a suspension based on her stated remorse and the repayment of the funds ($17$). This decision was appealed by the Pretoria Society of Advocates, leading to the current case, which sought to overturn the High Court's decision and remove Van Zyl from practice ([1], [17]).

I – Issues:

  1. What constitutes serious misconduct for the purposes of removing an advocate from the roll?
  2. Did the High Court exercise its discretion appropriately when it opted for suspension over removal?
  3. What are the implications of the respondent’s admissions of perjury and forgery in relation to her fitness to practice?

These issues necessitate an analysis of whether the respondent's actions reflected a fundamental unfitness to continue practicing law, overshadowing any claims to contrition or ability to abide by professional ethical standards.

R – Rules:

The legal framework governing the conduct of advocates in South Africa is derived primarily from the Advocates Act 74 of 1964, along with established case law that outlines the responsibilities and expectations of advocates. Key guidelines dictated by previous cases include:

  • Fitness to practice: As established in Malan & another v The Law Society of the Northern Provinces ([18]), a three-stage inquiry is essential:
  1. Determine if the alleged misconduct is proven on a balance of probabilities.
  2. Assess whether the individual is fit to continue as an advocate.
  3. Decide on the appropriate sanction based on established facts ([18]).

When addressing allegations of dishonesty, the courts have consistently placed the onus on maintaining public trust in the legal profession, deeming acts of theft, forgery, and perjury as significant enough to warrant removal. Previous rulings, such as Kekana v Society of Advocates of SA and Matyityi, further illustrate the non-negotiable ethical standards expected of legal practitioners, linking lapses in character to the unacceptability of retaining professional status ([16], [22]).

A – Application:

In applying these legal principles, the SCA found several critical misdirections in the High Court's evaluation. Firstly, the SCA emphasized that Van Zyl’s actions constituted clear and intentional acts of dishonesty, evident in her repeated fabrications to conceal both her misappropriation of funds and misrepresentations in her affidavit ([19], [20]). The SCA rejected the High Court’s conclusion that her statements and conduct could merely “amount” to perjury, asserting that the evidence established her guilt beyond reasonable doubt ([21]).

Moreover, the High Court's reliance on Van Zyl’s repayment of funds and claims of remorse was deemed inappropriate. The SCA underscored that merely repaying stolen funds does not mitigate actions that reflect a profound character defect, leading them to rule that she was not a fit and proper person to continue practicing law ([25], [28]). The findings of the SCA highlighted that true remorse must entail a genuine acknowledgment of wrongdoing, which was absent in Van Zyl’s conduct leading up to the proceedings. Her apologies did not indicate a recognition of the severity of her actions, but rather a strategic maneuver prompted by legal repercussions ([26], [28]).

C – Conclusion:

The SCA ultimately concluded that the High Court had committed significant errors in its assessment and application of the law. The cumulative effect of Van Zyl's misconduct – including serious financial improprieties, forgery, and perjury – warranted removal from the roll of advocates ([29]). The court nuanced the importance of public protection and the integrity of the legal profession, emphasizing the necessity of upholding legal standards ([27]–[30]).

The order explicitly struck Van Zyl from the roll and underscored the critical judiciary obligation to ensure that only fit and proper individuals are allowed to engage in legal practice, thus delivering a stark message regarding the consequences of ethical breaches within the profession ([32]). Costs were also awarded against Van Zyl, which is customary in such serious cases ([31]).
