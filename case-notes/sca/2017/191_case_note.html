Supreme Court of Appeal

Merchant Commercial Finance (Pty) Ltd v Katana Foods CC

The case involves Merchant Commercial Finance (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) and Katana Foods CC (the respondent) regarding a scheme of fraud that resulted in the appellant making payments for fictitious sales.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: [1–13]

The case involves Merchant Commercial Finance (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) and Katana Foods CC (the respondent) regarding a scheme of fraud that resulted in the appellant making payments for fictitious sales. The appellant operates a factoring business where it purchases claims against customers for goods sold. In this instance, it had entered into a factoring agreement with Black Ginger 81 (Pty) Ltd (its client) and commenced factoring invoices for goods purportedly sold by Black Ginger to Katana.

On 15 May 2006, the appellant began factoring invoices after receiving confirmation from Katana's director, Mr David Rahman, that goods had been sold and delivered. Over time, 13 invoices—resulting in payments exceeding R3 million—were factored, of which the last three (invoices numbered 105156, 105331, and 105332) were unpaid. These three invoices were factored in May 2007 following similar confirmations by Mr Rahman.

However, it was later revealed that all invoices were fictitious and that Mr Rahman had collaborated with Black Ginger to create false records. When the appellant attempted to recover the payments related to these invoices, Katana’s managing member disclosed the fraudulent arrangement. Consequently, the appellant sought to wind up Katana, leading to a claim for damages totalling R404,557.26 for the last three invoices, which was denied at trial and by the court a quo, forcing the appellant to appeal.

I – Issues: [13–19]

The primary issue pertains to whether the appellant could establish causation—both factual and legal—between the fraudulent misrepresentations made by Mr Rahman and the loss associated with the last three invoices. Specifically, the court needed to address:

  1. Did the previous fraudulent confirmations by Mr Rahman induce the appellant to factor the last three invoices?
  2. Was there an adequate causal link between the appellant's loss and the conduct of Mr Rahman, especially considering the timing of the misleading confirmations?
  3. How do legal principles regarding causation apply to this specific context of fraudulent misrepresentation?

R – Rules: [20–31]

The court examined established principles of causation in delictual or tortious liability, particularly the "but-for" test as articulated in prior cases such as International Shipping Co (Pty) Ltd v Bentley [1990] and Minister of Safety and Security v Van Duivenboden [2002]. The but-for test requires a hypothesis of what would have occurred absent the defendant's wrongful act, determining if the loss would still ensue.

Additionally, the court considered legal causation concerning foreseeability and proximate cause, emphasizing that liability should stem from actions sufficiently linked to the harm suffered. This principle ensures a reasonable connection between the wrongful conduct and the resulting damage.

A – Application: [22–30]

In applying these rules, the court determined that both factual and legal causation were satisfied. It noted that the ongoing fraudulent conduct created an expectation of reliability and trust in Mr Rahman's confirmations. This context is crucial; the three invoices did not stand in isolation—they were part of a broader pattern of deceit that lulled the appellant into vulnerability.

The court rejected the lower courts’ view that the absence of specific misrepresentations immediately prior to the payments negated causation. Instead, it stated that the persistent fraudulent scheme by Mr Rahman made it substantially probable that had he not engaged in earlier misrepresentations, the appellant would not have factored the last three invoices nor sustained the resultant loss.

Moreover, the court criticized the lower courts for amalgamating the inquiries into factual and legal causation. They had erroneously concluded that a previous lack of misrepresentation precluded the establishment of either type of causation, thus failing to recognize the inherent link between Mr Rahman's overall fraudulent activity and the appellant's loss.

C – Conclusion: [31–32]

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the appellant successfully demonstrated both factual and legal causation, overturning the lower courts’ decisions. The court ruled in favor of the appellant, ordering Katana to pay R404,557.26 along with interest and costs.

This ruling underscores the principle that patterns of fraudulent behavior can substantiate claims of causation, reinforcing the notion that fraudulent misrepresentations that lead to reliance create liability for resulting losses. The decision and its reasoning have significant implications for factors and other businesses that rely on representations made by clients in financial transactions, reinforcing protections against systemic fraud.
