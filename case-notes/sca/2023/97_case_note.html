Democratic Alliance v The Minister of Home Affairs (67/2022) [2023] ZASCA 97

Introduction The South African Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) addressed the constitutional validity of section 6(1)(a) of the South African Citizenship Act 88 of 1995 (the Act) in this case.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 97

Introduction
The South African Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) addressed the constitutional validity of section 6(1)(a) of the South African Citizenship Act 88 of 1995 (the Act) in this case. The court ruled that the provision, which automatically deprives South African citizens of their citizenship upon acquiring the citizenship of another country, contravenes the Constitution, particularly the right to citizenship.

F – Facts

The Democratic Alliance (DA), as the appellant, contested the constitutionality of section 6(1)(a) of the Act, which stipulates that adult citizens automatically lose their citizenship upon the voluntary acquisition of foreign nationality, unless they have sought permission to retain their South African citizenship from the Minister of Home Affairs ([1]-[5]).

Mr. Phillip James Plaatjes, a South African citizen who acquired British citizenship while living in the UK, discovered in 2014 that he had lost his South African citizenship under section 6(1)(a) after his South African passport was cancelled upon his attempt to renew it ([3]-[4]). The DA initiated proceedings in the Gauteng Division of the High Court to declare that section 6(1)(a) is unconstitutional and to restore citizenship to South Africans who lost their citizenship under this section since the Act's promulgation on 6 October 1995 ([1]-[2]).

The High Court dismissed the DA's application, refusing to accept the claim that section 6(1)(a) was irrational or violated constitutional rights, a decision that the DA appealed ([2]-[6]).

I – Issues

The two central legal questions for consideration were:

  1. Is section 6(1)(a) of the South African Citizenship Act irrational, constituting a violation of the principle of legality or the rights in the Bill of Rights? ([7])
  2. Does the provision unjustifiably infringe the rights protected by the Constitution, specifically the right not to be deprived of citizenship under section 20? ([7], [34])

R – Rules

The court based its decision on the following constitutional principles and statutory provisions:

  • Section 3(3) of the Constitution of South Africa mandates national legislation to govern the loss and restoration of citizenship ([8]).
  • Section 20 of the Constitution asserts that no citizen may be deprived of citizenship, which the court interpreted in the context of establishing protection for citizenship as a fundamental right ([9]-[35]).
  • Section 36 of the Constitution allows for limitations of rights if such limitations are reasonable and justifiable within an open and democratic society ([35]).

The court also referenced judicial precedent, including the Chisuse case, illustrating the importance of citizenship in securing various constitutional rights ([9]-[10]).

A – Application

Rationality Analysis

The SCA found that section 6(1)(a) failed to demonstrate a legitimate government purpose justifying the automatic forfeiture of citizenship when an individual acquires foreign nationality. The court emphasized that merely stating the provision regulates citizenship is insufficient to establish a legitimate aim ([23]-[25]).

The court criticized the reliance on section 6(2), which allows retention of citizenship upon ministerial approval, arguing that this added arbitrary nature to the provision and compounded its irrationality ([26]-[31]). The insistence on ministerial permission created a situation where citizens could lose their rights without notice or due process, contradicting the foundational values of rationality and legality in governance ([32]).

Constitutional Rights

The High Court's view that the loss of citizenship differs from deprivation under section 20 was scrutinized. The SCA clarified that all laws affecting citizenship must conform to the Bill of Rights principles, thus unless justified under section 36, any loss of citizenship is, in effect, a deprivation ([35]-[36]).

The court established a clear link between the automatic loss of citizenship and the infringement of political rights and the rights to freedom of movement and occupation guaranteed by the Constitution ([37]).

Remedy

The SCA ruled that section 6(1)(a) is both irrational and inconsistent with the Constitution, thus it declared it null and void from the date of the Act's promulgation on 6 October 1995 ([38]). It directed that those who lost their citizenship under this provision be deemed to have never lost their citizenship, ensuing an automatic restoration without necessitating a further application process ([43]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, declaring section 6(1)(a) unconstitutional and invalid from its inception ([47]). The judgment has significant implications for South African citizens who previously lost their citizenship under this section, ensuring their rights to citizenship are respected and restoring their status without additional legal hurdles. The ruling not only affirms the right to citizenship as paramount but also underscores the necessity for legislative provisions to align with constitutional standards, reinforcing the importance of clear and rational lawmaking in a democratic society.

Implications

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the immediate restoration of citizenship. It highlights the need for legislative reform to address citizenship laws in alignment with constitutional principles, effectively ensuring that no South African citizen is subjected to arbitrary loss of citizenship without due process ([46]).

This case serves as a critical reminder of the protection of individual rights against state actions, advocating for fair and transparent governance in matters of citizenship and national identity.
