Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

### Case Note: Strydom v Engen Petroleum Limited (184/2012) [2012] ZASCA 187

#### FIRAC Method Case Note

**F - Facts**  
[1] The appellant, Ockie Strydom, was a director of Soutpansberg Petroleum (Pty) Ltd (Soutpansberg), which distributed petroleum products on behalf of the respondent, Engen Petroleum Limited (Engen). Soutpansberg was provisionally wound up on 13 November 2006, leading to a final winding-up order on 12 November 2007, at which time it owed approximately R25 million to Engen. On 15 December 2004, Strydom executed an unlimited deed of suretyship in favor of Engen, binding himself as surety for Soutpansberg’s obligations. Engen subsequently initiated recovery proceedings against Strydom and another surety in the North Gauteng High Court. The court ruled in favor of Engen, awarding R25,311,432.21 along with interest and costs against Strydom.

[2] Strydom contested the application on two grounds: first, he asserted that Engen failed to substantiate the debt amount; second, he claimed that he was married in community of property and lacked his spouse’s written consent for the suretyship as required under s 15(2)(h) of the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984 (the Act). The court, presided over by Southwood J, dismissed both defenses but granted leave to appeal regarding the second defense.

**I - Issues**  
[3] The primary legal issue was whether the deed of suretyship entered into by Strydom was invalid due to his lack of spousal consent under the provisions of s 15(2)(h) of the Act. The case also raised questions regarding the interpretation of statutory provisions that govern spousal consent, particularly how these provisions relate to actions undertaken in the ordinary course of business.

**R - Rules**  
[4] The relevant provisions from the Matrimonial Property Act include:

- **s 15(1)**: Specifies that a spouse in a marriage in community of property can perform any juristic act concerning the joint estate without consent from the other spouse.
- **s 15(2)(h)**: Clarifies that a spouse must not bind themselves as surety without the written consent of the other spouse.
- **s 15(6)**: States that the consent requirement does not apply where the action is performed in the ordinary course of a spouse’s profession, trade, or business.

The court also made reference to precedent cases, notably *Amalgamated Banks of South Africa Bpk v De Goede* and its interpretation of these sections, particularly regarding acts performed in the ordinary course of business.

**A - Application**  
[5] The court examined Strydom's position as a director of Soutpansberg, evaluating the nature of his involvement in the company’s operations. Strydom claimed he was primarily responsible for marketing, distancing himself from daily operational duties. Nevertheless, the evidence showed that he played a pivotal role in the company's primary business of distributing Engen’s products. 

[6] The court emphasized that the characterization of an act as being in the ordinary course of business should consider the individual's business involvement rather than the company’s operation as a whole. It was determined that Strydom was engaged in the day-to-day activities essential to the business's operations and that the execution of the suretyship was indeed in the ordinary course of that business.

[7] Furthermore, the court highlighted the necessity for Strydom to provide affirmative evidence to support his claim that the suretyship was not executed in the ordinary course of business. His failure to present evidence effectively shifted the burden back to him, undermining his argument regarding spousal consent.

**C - Conclusion**  
[8] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming that Strydom had failed to demonstrate that the suretyship was invalid due to the lack of spousal consent. The court reiterated that Strydom, as a director engaged directly in the business operations, acted within the ordinary course of that business when executing the deed of suretyship; thus, the protections of s 15(2)(h) did not apply.

[9] The judgment elucidated the interpretation of statutory provisions concerning marriage and financial obligations, emphasizing that the changes made by the Matrimonial Property Act aimed to facilitate equality between spouses. The court's decision reinforced the understanding that while spousal consent is crucial for certain transactions, exceptions exist, particularly when the actions align with the normal professional undertakings of the spouse involved.

This judgment serves as an important precedent in clarifying the application of the Matrimonial Property Act concerning suretyships and the responsibilities of spouses within a marriage in community of property. The case illustrates the balance between protecting the joint estate and allowing spouses to conduct business efficiently without unnecessary impediments.
