Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2003

# FIRAC Case Note: The Law Society of the Northern Provinces v Mutshutshu Wilson Mamatho

### Case Citation
**The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa**  
Case No: 435/02  
Date Delivered: 16 September 2003  
Coram: Scott, Farlam, Conradie, Van Heerden, Motata JJA

---

### F - Facts
The appellant, formerly known as the Law Society of the Transvaal, initiated motion proceedings in the Transvaal Provincial Division, seeking an order to strike the respondent, Mamatho, off the roll of attorneys. The primary grounds for this action were twofold: (1) the respondent had failed to submit an audit certificate for the financial year ending February 1999; and (2) the respondent was practicing law without a fidelity fund certificate, contravening section 42(3) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 (hereafter "the Act"). Practicing without this certificate constitutes a criminal offense and represents a serious infringement of an attorney's professional responsibilities ([1]).

In earlier proceedings, on 2 July 1996, an urgent application led by the appellant resulted in a rule nisi being issued, temporarily suspending the respondent from practicing law. This suspension specifically excluded the jurisdiction area of Venda, where the respondent continued to practice ([2]–[3]). Ultimately, the application to strike him off was dismissed by the court a quo on jurisdictional grounds, which the appeal now contests ([4]).

The key legal issue revolves around whether the Transvaal Provincial Division has jurisdiction to strike an attorney off the roll, considering that the respondent was practicing outside its jurisdiction ([5]). The court a quo's judgment was predominantly based on the interpretation of section 22(1)(d) of the Act, which stipulates that a society may seek to strike off an attorney only in the jurisdiction where they currently practice ([4]).

---

### I - Issues
1. **Jurisdiction**: Does the Transvaal Provincial Division have the legal authority to adjudicate matters concerning the striking off of an attorney who has ceased practicing in its area but remains registered as an attorney? ([4]–[6])
   
2. **Interpretation of Section 22(1)(d)**: How should the term "practices" in section 22(1)(d) of the Act be interpreted? Does it encompass attorneys who have previously practiced in the jurisdiction but currently practice elsewhere? ([6]–[10])

---

### R - Rules
- **Attorneys Act 53 of 1979**: This Act governs the professional conduct and practice of attorneys in South Africa, specifically Section 22, which allows law societies to apply for the striking off or suspension of attorneys for failing to maintain the requisite standards of propriety ([6]).
  
  - **Section 22(1)(d)** states that any attorney may be struck off the roll if deemed not fit and proper by the court within the jurisdiction where they practice.
  
  - **Section 22(2)** further states that the court may permit service of process by publication if the attorney has left the actual jurisdiction.

- **Case Precedents**: Relevant decisions, including *Vassen v Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope* (1998) and others, illustrate judicial interpretations concerning the jurisdiction and inherent power of courts to regulate their practitioners ([6]–[10]).

---

### A - Application
The court analyzed whether the previous or current practice location of the respondent impacts the jurisdiction of the Transvaal Provincial Division to entertain the application to strike him off the roll. Notably, although the respondent was practicing in a different area (Venda), no statutory provision expressly barred the initial division from exercising jurisdiction over former practitioners. The inquiry into the wording of section 22(1)(d) was crucial; the court held that "practices" should also be understood to include "has practiced," thereby allowing the court to maintain its jurisdiction over attorneys regardless of their current practicing location ([8]–[10]). 

The court overturned the reasoning applied in the court a quo, where it was concluded that the Transvaal Provincial Division lacked jurisdiction. Citing the inherent power of the court, it was determined that the intention of the legislature was to allow for the transfer of jurisdiction notwithstanding geographical changes, especially concerning essential professional standards such as submitting an audit certificate, which is crucial for maintaining public trust in the legal profession ([5]–[10]).

---

### C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Transvaal Provincial Division indeed possesses the jurisdiction to adjudicate the matter regarding striking off the respondent from the roll of attorneys, despite his current practice location ([10]). The appeal was thus upheld, the judgment of the court a quo set aside, and the case referred back for examination of its merits. This decision affirms the court’s inherent authority to regulate the standards and conduct of attorneys, regardless of their current geographical practice location, thereby reinforcing the professional integrity of the legal system in South Africa ([11]).

**Implications**: This judgment underscores the importance of a comprehensive interpretation of legislation governing professional conduct and emphasizes the judiciary's responsibility to maintain standards in the legal profession. The ruling also clarifies the jurisdictional limits for law societies and courts when dealing with attorneys who have previously practiced in different jurisdictions, thus ensuring accountability and adherence to professional standards.
