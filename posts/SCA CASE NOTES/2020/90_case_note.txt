Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa v Sbahle Fire Services CC

## Facts

[1] This case concerns the Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa (PRASA) and Sbahle Fire Services CC (Sbahle), involving a contractual dispute regarding payment for fire and safety consultancy services rendered to PRASA over a period from June 2010 to August 2013. Sbahle initiated a claim demanding payments of R1,227,999.21 and R9,095,968.47 related to these consultancy services after PRASA had initially compensated it with R2,034,938.19 for fire consultancy services up until November 2012. The claim arose from the Mabopane Bridge Development Project, for which Sbahle was contracted as a fire and safety consultant.

[2] Following PRASA's partial payment, it instituted a claim in reconvention arguing that the earlier payment was made in error and sought the return of the R2,034,938.19. Although Sbahle dropped its first claim, it continued with the second claim related to safety consultancy fees, asserting that a contractual clause obligating PRASA to pay for any material or additional services performed was applicable due to project delays and changes initiated by PRASA.

[3] The context of the dispute involved allegations from Sbahle regarding the significant changes and additional requirements mandated by PRASA throughout the project, which allegedly necessitated additional consultancy work. PRASA, on the other hand, contended that the services provided by Sbahle were continuous extensions of previously contracted work, asserting that no new material services were required or instructed.

## Issues

[4] The primary legal issues for determination arise from the interpretation of the contractual agreement between the parties, particularly regarding:

- Whether the project alterations constituted substantial changes that would trigger an obligation for PRASA to pay additional fees for consultancy services ([para 41]).
- The legitimacy of PRASA's claim for repayment of the previously paid R2,034,938.19 based on principles of enrichment and the nature of the agreement established for consultancy services ([para 21]).

## Rules

[5] The court relied on principles of contract law, particularly concerning the interpretation of contracts as established in **Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality** [2012] ZASCA 13; 2012 (4) SA 593 (SCA), which holds that the language of the contract must be interpreted with regard to its context, purpose, and the mutual intention of the parties.

[6] Relevant legal principles concerning the **condictio indebiti** (unjustified enrichment) require the claimant to prove that a payment was made under a mistake of fact that was both reasonable and excusable ([para 23]). 

## Application

[7] In reviewing the case, the court assessed the contractual language concerning payment obligations. The clause stipulating that PRASA would incur additional costs only when Sbahle provided material additional services because of project changes was pivotal. The court noted that while Sbahle clearly rendered services post the original completion date, it failed to adequately substantiate its claim that these were material additional services, as the amendments made to the project could be construed merely as adjustments rather than expansions of the scope of work ([paras 49–64]).

[8] PRASA's argument rested upon their assertion that the consultancy work remained the same and that they were not liable for additional payments based on continued project delays alone. The court scrutinized testimonies, particularly from Sbahle's witness, Mr. Khuzwayo, whose claims about additional requirements were insufficient to construct a robust and persuasive argument for claiming further remuneration ([para 31]).

[9] Additionally, the court noted PRASA's lack of contrary evidence during the proceedings, which could have supported their argument regarding the claimed erroneously paid fee being classified as a mistake. The absence of written instructions regarding the requirement for additional services was highlighted, as Sbahle's claim needed to satisfy that contractual condition to invoke additional payments legally ([para 56]).

## Conclusion

[10] Ultimately, the court ruled in favor of PRASA concerning the claim in reconvention, finding that PRASA's payment was not made in error that would justify recovery on grounds of unjust enrichment, as Sbahle had already invoiced these payments based on valid contractual obligations ([para 38]). Conversely, the court upheld that Sbahle failed to substantiate its claim for additional payments, leading to the dismissal of its second claim.

[11] The significance of this ruling underscores the necessity for parties engaging in contractual relationships to ensure clarity in written agreements and establish mechanisms for managing additional requests, further illustrating the critical nature of adhering to prescribed contractual terms and conditions when claiming further remuneration in business dealings.

[12] The order reflected the complexities surrounding contractual obligations in construction and consultancy work, including the implications of project management and resource allocation, especially in public sector undertakings. The court's decision not only affirmed contractual terms but also highlighted the vital importance of clear communication regarding project scope and changes.
