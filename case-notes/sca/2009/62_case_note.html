Du Plessis & Smith NNO v Goldco Motor & Cycle Supplies (372/2008) [2009] ZASCA 62

Goldco occupied the premises, but the process for drafting the sale contract was delayed due to the trustees' inaction and issues relating to the sectional title registration.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 62

F – Facts

In this case, the appellants, Jan Abraham du Plessis and Nicolaas Johannes Smith, are trustees for the Prosperitas Trust, which owns immovable property in Welkom, South Africa. The respondent, Goldco Motor & Cycle Supplies (Pty) Ltd ("Goldco"), entered into a lease agreement for premises constructed by the trust. The lease agreement dated 10 March 2005 included an option clause allowing Goldco to purchase the premises for R4,000,000 plus VAT, subject to specific conditions that included the opening of a sectional title register and the signing of a written contract.

Goldco occupied the premises, but the process for drafting the sale contract was delayed due to the trustees' inaction and issues relating to the sectional title registration. Goldco claimed to have expressed its intent to exercise the option but faced resistance from the trust, which refused to proceed with the sale after the lease expired in March 2007. Goldco ultimately sought relief from the Free State High Court, which ordered that the option be deemed exercised, prompting an appeal from the trustees ([paras 1–5]).

I – Issues

The central issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

  1. Was the option to purchase enforceable given that the written contract was not executed within the stipulated period?
  2. Could the doctrine of "fictional fulfilment" be applied to deem the option exercised despite the lack of formal acceptance in writing?
  3. Did the trustees' alleged obstruction of the execution of the necessary documents waive their right to rely on non-compliance with the option's formalities?

These issues are framed in terms of contract law principles, particularly regarding options and conditions precedent in contracts for the sale of immovable property ([paras 6–12]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal principles can be summarized as follows:

  1. Option Agreements: An option to purchase property is a binding offer that cannot be revoked before the expiry of the period set within the option clause ([para 15]). The grantor of an option is under an enforceable obligation until the option is either exercised or expires ([para 16]).
  2. Fictional Fulfilment: This doctrine applies when a party intentionally frustrates the execution of a contract. Under certain circumstances, performance may be deemed to have occurred, preventing a party from escaping contractual obligations based on technical grounds ([paras 22–27]).
  3. Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981: This act requires that contracts for the sale of land be in writing and contain all material terms to be enforceable (s 2(1)). This includes ensuring the property is sufficiently described ([paras 36 – 39]).
  4. Agency Principles: The attorney drafting the option agreement is generally considered the agent of the party that commissioned the document. This principle upholds the expectation that the agent will act faithfully in the best interests of their principal ([para 21]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the enforceability of the contract under the established rules.

  • Enforceability of the Option: The court concluded that the option clause effectively conferred a right upon Goldco to compel the trustees to sell the property at the agreed price, reflecting the essential elements of a sale contract ([para 18]). The trust's contention that the option was merely an agreement to agree was dismissed as lacking merit ([para 14]).
  • Fictional Fulfilment: The court acknowledged that the trustees, through their attorney, had consistently delayed the procedural steps necessary for Goldco to formalize the contract. It concluded that this constituted a deliberate frustration of the option exercise, thus applying the doctrine of fictional fulfilment to deem the option exercised despite the lack of a signed contract ([paras 29–30]).
  • Description of the Property: The court addressed arguments regarding whether the property was sufficiently described. The provided details were deemed adequate, as Goldco had occupied the premises, thereby confirming mutual knowledge of the property in question ([paras 19–20]).
  • Agency: The court found that the attorney acted as an agent of the trustees and that any delays in drafting the sale contract should not be held against Goldco, affirming that the trustees were bound by the actions of their appointed agent ([para 21]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal and upheld the High Court's order, affirming that Goldco's right to purchase the property existed by virtue of the option clause and that the trustees had obstructed its performance. The court emphasized that the originally required formality (the signed written contract) was effectively moot due to the trustee's decisive inaction and obstruction.

The order was modified to expressly deem that the option was exercised and directed the trustees to transfer the property to Goldco upon payment of the escalated purchase price. The case underscores the importance of adhering to contractual obligations and the principle that one should not benefit from their own wrongdoing in a contractual context ([paras 32–43]).

Order Confirmation: The adjusted order included provisions for the transfer of property and the costs associated with the application, highlighting the judiciary's role in enforcing equitable outcomes even amid procedural complexities ([para 32]).
