# FIRAC Case Note: RAPPORTEERBAAR

**Case Citation:** Rapporeteerbaar (Case No: 89/99, Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa).

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal

**Judges:** Smalberger, Vivier, Plewman, Farlem, Chetty

**Hearing Date:** 22 November 2000

**Judgment Date:** 29 November 2000

## F – Facts

The appellant, a cooperative society duly incorporated under the provisions of the Co-operatives Act 91 of 1981 ("the Act"), sought to establish the legitimacy of the membership termination of the respondent, H.W. Louw, a former milk supplier. The dispute stemmed from a letter dated 4 December 1991, written on behalf of the respondent, which was intended to communicate the termination of his membership from the appellant ([1]).

The respondent filed an application in the Cape of Good Hope Provincial Division for a declaratory order stating he was a member of the appellant from 20 April 1987 until 28 February 1993. The lower court upheld this application based on the interpretation of the termination letter and granted the requested relief ([2]).

The appellant's articles of association, specifically Article 33(1), stipulated that a member's resignation would only take effect at the end of a financial year and only if notice was provided at least three months prior to the year-end ([3], [5]). The parties agreed that if a member submitted a resignation notice before December 1 of a financial year, the resignation would take effect at the year-end, while notices submitted between December 1 and the end of the financial year would be effective at the end of the following financial year ([5]).

The respondent had stopped delivering milk to the appellant as early as April 1991 and had begun supplying it to a different entity, Simonsberg cheese factory, with the appellant's knowledge ([11]). The ongoing correspondence indicated a misunderstanding about the respondent’s membership status and the timing of his resignation ([13]–[18]).

## I – Issues

The primary issues for determination by the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

1. Whether the letter dated 4 December 1991 constituted a unilateral resignation under Article 33(1) of the articles of association of the appellant ([19]).
2. Whether the respondent's membership was, in fact, validly terminated, and if so, the effective date of such termination ([25]).
3. Whether the appellant's acceptance of the resignation was timely and in accordance with the statutory requirements ([22]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework pertinent to this case is primarily derived from the provisions of the Co-operatives Act 91 of 1981 and the appellant's own articles of association:

- **Article 33(1)** of the articles provides that a member's resignation becomes effective at the end of the financial year, provided that notice has been given at least three months prior ([3]).
- **Article 33(2)** allows for the cancellation of shares of a member who has resigned, and **Article 89** outlines the financial year-end ([5], [9]).
- The courts have interpreted that, to be binding, a resignation must clearly express the member's intention to resign ([19]).

Precedents such as *Cape Dairy Co-operative Ltd v Ferreira* (1997) have established that resignations can occur both unilaterally under the statutes or by mutual agreement ([4]).

## A – Application

The court examined the content and the context of the resignation letter. It found that despite the initial interpretation provided by the appellant's board, which treated it as an application to resign outside of the statutory terms, the wording of the letter indicated a clear and unambiguous intention to resign ([7], [19]). 

Following the acceptance of resignation by the board on 11 February 1992, it was determined that this was in alignment with the request for early termination ([22]). The delay in formally notifying the respondent of this acceptance did not invalidate the acceptance. The correspondence from June 1992 implied that the respondent's resignation was acknowledged, indicating the member’s position had changed to that of a non-member following acceptance of the resignation ([23], [24]).

The court ultimately recognized that although resignations typically take effect only at financial year-end, the appellant had the discretion to allow for earlier termination ([20], [25]). Each piece of correspondence was weighed to ascertain whether there existed a mutual agreement that constituted an early termination of the membership based on the respondent's indications and the appellant's responses ([26]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the respondent's effective resignation commenced in late August or early September 1992, when the appellant accepted the resignation and paid out distributions associated with his member shares ([25], [27]). 

As such, the court overturned the lower court's ruling due to the erroneous finding that the respondent's membership remained intact until February 1993. The implications of this ruling extend beyond the individual case, highlighting the importance of clear communication in corporate governance and the necessity of mutual agreements concerning membership termination in cooperative entities.

The final order reflected the success of the appellant's appeal, resulting in the lower court's decision being set aside, and a new order dismissing the respondent's application was put in place, along with cost orders ([27]). 

This case underscores the precarious nature of membership interpretations within cooperatives and the pivotal role of statutory compliance in organizing member relations.
