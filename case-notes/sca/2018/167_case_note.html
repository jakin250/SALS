Supreme Court of Appeal

Swifambo Rail Leasing v PRASA (1030/2017) [2018] ZASCA 167

On discovering fraud related to the procurement process, PRASA's newly reconstituted board applied to the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court to have the contract declared invalid.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 167

F – Facts

The case involves a dispute between Swifambo Rail Leasing (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) and the Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa (PRASA) (the Respondent). PRASA awarded a tender for the supply of locomotives to Swifambo in 2013, following a flawed procurement process rife with corruption and irregularities, primarily orchestrated by PRASA officials under the leadership of Mr. Lucky Montana ([1]–[2]).

On discovering fraud related to the procurement process, PRASA's newly reconstituted board applied to the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court to have the contract declared invalid. PRASA argued that the tender was obtained through corrupt practices and non-compliance with statutory procurement requirements, notably the Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment (BBBEE) Act 53 of 2003 ([1]–[2], [5]).

Swifambo contended it was an innocent tenderer, asserting that it had acted in good faith unaware of the corrupt circumstances surrounding its bid. The high court, however, found significant irregularities in Swifambo's tender, including lack of necessary tax and BBBEE compliance, and deemed the entire tender process corrupt, thus invalidating the contract and permitting PRASA's application to set it aside ([2]–[8], [17]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case were:

  1. Whether PRASA's delay in seeking to set aside the contract was unreasonable and whether condonation for such delay should be granted ([16], [31]).
  2. The status of Swifambo as an innocent tenderer versus its alleged involvement in ‘fronting’ as defined under the BBBEE Act ([24]–[28]).
  3. The admissibility of hearsay evidence presented by PRASA and its impact on the findings of fact made by the High Court ([18]–[20]).
  4. Whether the high court's findings concerning the tailoring of specifications and resultant corruption in the tender process were justified ([23], [24]).

R – Rules

In addressing these issues, the court relied on various legal principles and statutory frameworks, including:

  • The Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act 53 of 2003: This act defines and prohibits fronting, setting out provisions that warrant punitive measures against parties found to engage in such practices ([26]).
  • The Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988: This legislation regulated the admissibility and consideration of hearsay evidence in judicial proceedings. The court found that under s 3(1) of the Act, hearsay evidence can be admissible if it is deemed in the interests of justice ([20]).
  • The Promotion of Access to Administrative Justice Act 2 of 2000 (PAJA): While initially assumed to apply in determining the reasonableness of PRASA's delay, the court clarified that a state organ's review under common law principles of legality would govern such matters ([32]).
  • Relevant precedent including Esorfranki Pipelines (Pty) Ltd v Mopani District Municipality [2014] ZASCA 21 elaborated on the definition of fronting and its implications ([30]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the court scrutinized the findings of the high court and the defenses raised by Swifambo. It upheld the high court's conclusion that Swifambo was involved in fronting, thereby undermining its claim as an innocent tenderer. Notably, the court highlighted the dubious timing of Swifambo's incorporation and the lack of operational substance behind the company at the time of the bid ([24]–[26]).

Regarding hearsay evidence, the court noted that while typically inadmissible, the specific circumstances—namely PRASA's newly reconstituted board's inability to rely on direct evidence—justified its inclusion in accordance with the interests of justice ([20]). The court reinforced that evidence supporting claims of irregularities should not be taken lightly, and Swifambo's failure to dispute the allegations further solidified the case against it.

On the issue of delay, the court reasoned that while the duration between the tender award and the application was considerable, various extenuating circumstances, including internal corruption and obstruction within PRASA, justified the delay. The board’s reconstitution and efforts to comprehend the past malfeasance were critical in determining the reasonableness of extending the application timeline ([34]–[39]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the high court's ruling to set aside the contract between PRASA and Swifambo on the grounds of substantial procurement irregularities and corruption ([49]). The court emphasized that condoning the delay in reviewing the contract was warranted in light of public interest and the significant taxpayer money implicated in the matter. The decision signifies a robust stance against corruption in public procurement processes and underscores the critical need for regulatory compliance in such dealings. By firmly establishing that both parties were not innocent in the context of the tender process, the court reinforced the need for accountability within state-owned enterprises and public service contracts, promoting the values of transparency and integrity essential for proper governance in South Africa.
