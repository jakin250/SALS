# FIRAC Case Note: Scott-Crossley v The State [2007] SCA 46 (RSA)

## F – Facts
In this case, Mark Scott-Crossley (the appellant) was convicted of the murder of Simon Chisale and sentenced to life imprisonment ([1]). After unsuccessfully applying for leave to appeal from the trial court, the Supreme Court of Appeal granted leave to appeal ([1]). This led the appellant to seek bail pending the outcome of his appeal. However, his application for bail was dismissed by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]).

The murder was classified as a premeditated murder under Part 1 of Schedule 2, read with section 51(1)(a) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, which prescribes a minimum sentence of life imprisonment ([2]). According to section 60(11)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, an accused charged with a Schedule 6 offence must show exceptional circumstances justifying release from custody ([2]).

To substantiate his claim for bail, the appellant presented no viva voce evidence but relied on affidavits from himself, his wife, and his attorney, arguing primarily that the mere granting of leave to appeal suggested he had for his conviction, thus establishing "exceptional circumstances" ([5]). 

## I – Issues
The core legal issues presented in this appeal revolve around:
1. Whether the mere fact that the Supreme Court of Appeal granted leave to appeal constitutes "exceptional circumstances" as required by section 60(11)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act ([1], [5]).
2. Whether the appellant's personal circumstances and the prospects of success on appeal were sufficient to meet the threshold for bail ([12]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal framework involves:
1. **Section 60(11)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**: This provision mandates that an accused must show exceptional circumstances permitting release when charged with a Schedule 6 offence ([2]).
2. **S v Bruintjies 2003 (2) SACR 575 (SCA)**: The court iterated that those convicted of Schedule 6 offences are held to a stringent burden and cannot claim a lighter standard in seeking bail ([3], [7]). 
3. **Constitutional principles on bail**: Established by S v Dlamini; S v Dladla and others; S v Joubert; S v Schietekat 1999 (2) SACR 51 (CC), which confirmed the constitutionality of stricter bail provisions under the amended law ([6]).

The case law indicated that the existence of "reasonable prospects of success" does not automatically equate to "exceptional circumstances" sufficient for bail ([7]).

## A – Application
In evaluating the appellant's case, the court considered:
1. The assertion that the appellant had reasonable prospects of success due to the Supreme Court's grant of leave to appeal. However, the court clarified that this alone did not satisfy the threshold of "exceptional circumstances" ([5], [12]).
2. The court refrained from conducting an exhaustive analysis of the evidence from the trial court, consistent with its role at the bail stage ([7]). Instead, it kept its assessment at a high level. The appellant’s claims of being an accessory after the fact were undercut by the nature of his own admission within his affidavit, reflecting a serious crime regardless of the potential for a lesser conviction ([10]).
3. Overall, while the court took into account the appellant’s various arguments regarding the merits of his appeal, including alleged procedural irregularities, it ultimately recognized that these would likely not prevent an eventual custodial sentence matching or exceeding the time already served by the appellant ([11]).

The court determined that the personal circumstances presented were not out of the ordinary and did not amount to the "exceptional circumstances" necessary to merit bail under section 60(11) ([12]).

## C – Conclusion
The court dismissed the appeal, confirming the lower court's decision to deny bail pending the appellant's appeal against conviction. The dismissal underscored the burden placed upon individuals convicted of serious crimes to demonstrate exceptional circumstances for bail ([14]). 

In conclusion, this case reaffirms the strict interpretation of bail provisions for serious offences in South Africa, illustrating the balancing act between individual rights and public safety where serious criminal convictions are concerned. The court's rationale stresses that mere prospects of success on appeal, absent any extraordinary facts, cannot by themselves meet the criteria set for bail under the law ([13]).
