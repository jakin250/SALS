Supreme Court of Appeal

Comshipco Schiffahrtsagentur GmbH v The Commissioner for South African Revenue Service

The appellant argued that these address commissions constituted marketing expenditure, qualifying for a marketing allowance under section 11 bis(4)(f).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2001 1992 ([para 1–4]). The relevant legal framework for this case is based on the Income Tax Act 58

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The appellant, Comshipco Schiffahrtsagentur GmbH, operates as a ship charterer based in Durban, South Africa. It was involved in legal proceedings concerning the disallowance of certain tax deductions relating to address commissions paid to charterers as part of its operations from 1 October 1988 to 30 September 1992 ([para 1–4]). The relevant legal framework for this case is based on the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 as amended, specifically section 11 bis ([para 12]).

The appellant argued that these address commissions constituted marketing expenditure, qualifying for a marketing allowance under section 11 bis(4)(f). It claimed that such commissions were necessary for its business operations and should be deductible from its income ([para 2, 6]). However, the South African Revenue Service (SARS) disallowed this deduction, leading to an appeal which was initially dismissed by the Natal Income Tax Special Court ([para 1, 12]).

The nature of the address commission, as introduced by the appellant during the trial, indicated that it was a payment made to the charterers for services rendered in respect of port operations, which the owner would typically undertake, including navigational services, port charges, and other expenses ([para 4, 5]). The appellant asserted that these payments were commissions related to obtaining orders for chartering vessels ([para 8, 10]).

I – Issues

The primary issue at hand was whether the address commissions paid by the appellant to the charterers qualified as "marketing expenditure" under section 11 bis(4)(f) of the Income Tax Act, allowing the appellant to deduct these amounts from its taxable income ([para 1, 12]).

A secondary question arose concerning whether the payments constituted remuneration for services or mere facilitation, thereby impacting their deductibility under the specified section of the tax legislation ([para 29–30]).

R – Rules

The legal principle that governs the case is enshrined in section 11 bis of the Income Tax Act, which stipulates specific qualifications for marketing expenditure. According to subsection (4), marketing expenditure must be incurred directly in relation to commissions for orders for goods or services, particularly from export service industries ([para 2, 12–14]).

In terms of earlier case law, particularly the precedent set in Commissioner for Inland Revenue v Wandrag Asbestos (Pty) Ltd 1995 (2) SA 197 (A), the court had previously interpreted the term "commission" as remuneration for services rendered in securing orders ([paras 8, 21]). This judgment affirmed the need for an explicit link between the payment of commission and services or goods provided to substantiate tax deductions ([paras 18–20]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the nature of the address commissions within the context of Comshipco's business operations. The judgment emphasized that address commissions are not payable for securing orders but rather for facilitating services post-order confirmation, including loading and unloading operations ([paras 11, 23, 29]).

The court highlighted that a true commission must relate to the procurement of orders, distinguishing between an agency relationship required for marketing purposes and a service relationship linked to operational expenses ([para 28]). The judgment did not support the appellant's position, as it established that the address commissions were not for procuring orders but for services rendered, thus failing to meet the criteria stipulated under section 11 bis(4)(f) for marketing expenditure ([para 30]).

In applying this reasoning, the justices maintained that although the charterers performed essential operational functions on behalf of the disponent owner, the commissions were not incurred for obtaining the charterers’ agreement to charter the vessels. Thus, the character of the payments was not aligned with the anticipated scope of deductions as outlined in the Income Tax Act ([paras 29–30]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, concluding that the address commissions paid by Comshipco Schiffahrtsagentur GmbH to the charterers did not qualify as marketing expenditure under section 11 bis(4)(f) of the Income Tax Act ([paras 13, 31]). The rationale was that these payments were made for services rendered related to port operations, rather than for the procurement of orders for the charter of ships.

The court's decision reaffirms the critical distinctions in tax law regarding what constitutes deductible expenditures and reflects the legislative intent to support genuine marketing activities aimed at promoting exports. This case sets a precedent in delineating operational payments from those that can be characterized as marketing expenses, which can be pivotal for companies operating within the export service industry.
