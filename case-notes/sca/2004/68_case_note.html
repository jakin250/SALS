Supreme Court of Appeal

The Minister of Safety and Security v Rudman (2004)

[2] The plaintiffs, Pieter Nicolaas Rudman and Petrus Botha Schabort, initiated a delictual claim against Becker and the Minister, alleging that Becker's actions and decisions after arriving at the scene constituted a breach of his duty of care.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2004

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

[1] On 6 October 1997, young Roald John Rudman, aged two years and eight months, suffered a near-drowning incident in an unsecured swimming pool at his home in Pretoria, resulting in severe hypoxic brain damage that rendered him a spastic tetraplegic with an epileptic tendency. The South African Police Service (SAPS) was summoned, and Sergeant Johan Jacobus Becker, an officer on duty with the Minister of Safety and Security, arrived at the scene ([1]).

[2] The plaintiffs, Pieter Nicolaas Rudman and Petrus Botha Schabort, initiated a delictual claim against Becker and the Minister, alleging that Becker's actions and decisions after arriving at the scene constituted a breach of his duty of care. Specifically, they contended that Becker incorrectly assessed Roald as deceased without proper examination and stopped the CPR being performed by a lay rescuer, Kobus Pienaar ([2]).

[3] The plaintiffs sought damages for the brain injury sustained by Roald due to alleged negligence by Becker. In their particulars of claim, they outlined multiple alleged failings of Becker, including (7.1) hindering CPR already in progress, (7.2) failing to assist with CPR, (7.3) incorrectly declaring Roald dead without a proper examination, and (7.4) allowing a significant lapse in CPR ([3]).

[4] The appellants contested any liability, claiming contributory negligence from the child's stepbrother, Petrus Botha Schabort (Bo), who had removed the pool's safety net and left Roald unattended ([4]).

[5] The trial court, following a separation of issues, found Becker and the Minister jointly and severally liable for the damages sustained by Roald and allocated 20% of the liability to Bo ([6]).

I – Issues

[6] The primary legal issues for consideration were whether Becker's (a) actions constituted negligence, (b) breached a legal duty of care to Roald, and (c) whether any negligence by Becker was causally connected to Roald's brain damage. Additionally, the applicability of the Apportionment of Damages Act 34 of 1956 regarding concurrent wrongdoers was also at issue ([2], [6]).

R – Rules

[7] The applicable legal framework draws primarily from the principles of delict in South African law. Central to the determination of negligence is the "reasonable person" standard, as articulated in Kruger v Coetzee, which assesses if a person in Becker's position would foresee the possibility of harm and take reasonable steps to prevent it ([65]).

[8] Furthermore, the existence of a duty of care is predicated on a legal premise, assessed through a balance of competing interests as defined in Van Eeden v Minister of Safety and Security and subsequently elaborated in Martínez cases, concerning the public policy implications of imposing liability on state officials ([56]).

[9] The Apportionment of Damages Act stipulates that joint wrongdoers may be liable for the same damage, and the essence of the Act focuses on whether damages caused can be attributed to concurrent wrongful acts ([86]).

A – Application

[10] The court assessed Becker's actions primarily against the backdrop of the immediate chaotic circumstances upon his arrival at the scene. The finding highlighted that Becker, believing Roald was dead based on apparent signs (cold body, dilated pupils), took the decision to cease CPR. This action was evaluated in light of his lack of specialized medical training ([75]).

[11] The court determined that Becker's cessation of CPR, as well as his directive to Kobus to stand down, constituted an unlawful act given that he disrupted an ongoing rescue effort despite having no basis for medically declaring Roald dead. Thus, his decision not to perform CPR or alternatively allow Kobus to continue was viewed as negligent ([72]).

[12] Meanwhile, the argument about contributory negligence from Bo was acknowledged, affirming he played a role in creating the perilous situation by removing the safety net ([4], [79]). However, the assessment of Becker's negligence underlined that his actions directly led to the exacerbation of Roald's injuries, giving rise to a presumption of wrongfulness due to the intervention ([56]).

C – Conclusion

[13] Ultimately, the court held that Becker's actions constituted a significant breach of duty resulting in causal negligence connected to the brain injuries suffered by Roald, affirming the trial court's initial ruling of vicarious liability on the Minister's part ([77]). However, it adjusted Becker's liability in light of contributory negligence from Bo, which led to a recalculation per the Apportionment of Damages Act, finding both Becker and Bo liable at different percentages for the damage sustained ([90]).

[14] This conclusion highlights the ongoing tension in the scope of police duties and access to competent medical assistance in emergency situations, alongside the need for nuanced application of liability principles in cases involving concurrent wrongdoers. The implications extend beyond immediate liability, potentially informing legislative reforms relating to emergency response protocols by law enforcement agencies in South Africa.
