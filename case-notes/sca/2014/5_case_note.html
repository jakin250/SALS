Supreme Court of Appeal

The City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v The Chairman of the Valuation Appeal Board for the City of Johannesburg (282/2013) [2014] ZASCA 5

The valuation roll reflected a total market value of R3,379,000 without apportioning this value between the different categories of usage.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 5

F - Facts

The case revolves around a dispute between the City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality (the appellant) and the Chairman of the Valuation Appeal Board for the City of Johannesburg alongside Connaught Properties (Pty) Ltd (the second respondent) concerning property rates levied on a ten-story building known as 'Park Mews', located on erven 3563 and 3564 in Hillbrow, Johannesburg ([1]–[3]). The property is zoned as 'Residential A', permitting uses that include shops and offices on the ground floor. However, the appellant's rates policy categorized the property as 'multiple purposes' under the municipal valuation roll, applying a higher business rate universally across the property's total value without distinguishing between its business and residential uses ([4]–[8]).

The valuation roll reflected a total market value of R3,379,000 without apportioning this value between the different categories of usage. The second respondent objected to this valuation during the inspection period, arguing for an apportionment of the market value based on actual use, contending that 79.44% of the market value should correlate with residential use ([9]–[10]). The appeal to the Valuation Appeal Board was upheld, leading the appellant to seek a review in the South Gauteng High Court, which dismissed the application, prompting the current appeal ([11]–[12]).

I - Issues

The core legal issue in this case is whether the appellant properly applied the provisions of the Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004, specifically concerning the categorization and apportionment of property taxes for properties with multiple uses ([4]–[12]). The dispute focuses on the interpretation of sections 8 and 9 of the Act, particularly whether the municipal valuer was required to reflect the apportionment of market value based on the dual classifications (business and residential) of the property ([1]–[12]).

R - Rules

The relevant legislation includes:

  1. Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004:
  • Section 2: Grants municipalities the power to levy rates on property within their area.
  • Section 8(1): Allows differentiation of rates based on the use and permitted use of the property.
  • Section 9: Mandates that properties used for multiple purposes must be assigned to a category reflecting their various uses and requires apportionment of their market values accordingly ([4]–[5]).

The Act requires that where a property is used for multiple purposes, there must be a clear demarcation of the different uses and a corresponding apportionment of the market value of the property amongst these uses for rates purposes ([5]–[6]).

A - Application

The court analyzed whether the appellant's method of valuation and categorization adhered to the statutory requirements established in the Municipal Property Rates Act. The arguments proposed by the appellant, asserting that the rates policy relied on permitted use, not actual use, were found unpersuasive ([13]–[14]). The court took a pragmatic approach, emphasizing that multiple use properties must reflect all categories of use on the valuation roll.

The court underscored that the municipal valuer’s responsibilities include applying the statutory provisions governing property value determinations, particularly with respect to properties categorized as having multiple uses. The failure to do so not only disregards the legislation's express intent but undermines the objectivity required of a municipal valuer, potentially allowing municipal councils to inappropriately leverage their authority without regard for expert valuation ([25]–[34]).

Emphasizing a comprehensive interpretation of all relevant sections of the Act (particularly sections 48 and 39), the court determined that the municipal valuer must assess and reflect apportionments, thereby preventing municipal authorities from exerting undue influence over property valuations without a sound evidentiary basis ([19]–[31]).

C - Conclusion

The appeal was ultimately dismissed with costs, upholding the decision of the Valuation Appeal Board. The court concluded that the municipal valuer erred in dismissing the second respondent's objection without considering the necessary apportionment of market value between the property’s various designated uses ([32]–[35]). This ruling has significant implications for municipal policies and practices regarding property valuations, suggesting that municipalities must adhere strictly to legislative requirements, ensuring that property taxes reflect actual usage rather than merely permitted use, thus protecting the rights of property owners against potentially disproportionate tax levies.

In doing so, the court reinforced the longstanding principle that the statutory interpretation must prioritize fairness and transparency in municipal taxation practices ([28]–[29]).
