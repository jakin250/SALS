Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Schoombee and Another v The State [2016] ZACC 50

## F – Facts
The case involves applicants Phillip Daniël Schoombee and Frederick Johannes Massyn, who were convicted of murder in the North West High Court on 2 March 2007 and were subsequently sentenced to life imprisonment. Following their initial refusal for leave to appeal by the trial judge, the Supreme Court of Appeal granted them leave to appeal to the Full Court of the High Court on 1 March 2012 ([para 1]). During the appeal process, the applicants discovered that the original trial record was lost. Instead of an official transcript, they received a reconstructed record prepared by the trial judge, based on his notes ([para 2]). After some deliberation, they proceeded to appeal using this reconstructed record, with Schoombee contesting only his life sentence, while Massyn appealed against both his murder conviction and sentence. The Full Court dismissed their appeals ([para 2]), and the SCA subsequently refused further leave to appeal on 17 May 2013 ([para 2]).

On 6 July 2016, the applicants sought direct access to the Constitutional Court, not to challenge their prior convictions or sentences directly but to contest the adequacy of the reconstruction of the trial record, arguing that it violated their constitutional right to a fair trial as per section 35(3) of the Constitution ([para 3]). The Court requested further information from both the applicants' attorneys and the Director of Public Prosecutions regarding the reconstruction process ([para 4]).

## I – Issues
The central legal issue is whether the reconstructed trial record was adequate for a fair consideration of the applicants' appeals, particularly concerning their constitutional right to a fair trial and the implications of the inadequacies in that record. Specifically, the questions were whether the applicants had adequately participated in the reconstruction process and whether the reconstruction itself complied with fair trial standards ([para 39]).

## R – Rules
The ruling rests on established jurisprudence regarding the right to a fair trial as embodied in section 35(3)(o) of the Constitution, which encompasses the right to adequate legal representation and appeal ([para 19]). The judgment references several cases indicating that when a record is inadequate, it generally warrants setting aside sentencing or convictions ([para 19]). Key cases cited include *Davids v S* [2013] ZAWCHC 72, which emphasizes the necessity of a good record for the exercise of an appeal, and *Chabedi v S* [2005] ZASCA 5, which states that a defective record must still be adequate for appeal consideration ([para 20]). Furthermore, the necessity of engaging both the accused and the State in a record reconstruction process is affirmed in the jurisdiction ([para 20]).

## A – Application
The Court conducted a detailed assessment of the reconstructed record, arguing that while the record was improperly prepared, it remained adequate for a fair assessment of the appeal. The trial judge's notes were comprehensive, providing a full account of the evidence elicited during the trial ([para 27]). The applicants initially expressed intent to participate in the reconstruction but ultimately decided to proceed with the available reconstruction due to delays in their appeals. This expressed intention, given the legal advice they followed, raised questions about whether they effectively waived their rights regarding participation in the reconstruction process ([para 22]). The Court concluded that, despite the reconstruction's imperfections, the reconstructed record provided sufficient details regarding the proceedings, enough for substantive consideration of the issues raised during appeal. It was determined that the Full Court had sufficient information to assess the applicants' claims, including crucial elements from the applicants' guilty pleas ([para 34]).

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Constitutional Court dismissed the application for leave to appeal, emphasizing that the applicants had received a fair trial and appeal under the present circumstances. The applicants' argument highlighting inadequacies in the reconstructed record failed to substantially demonstrate a denial of their right to appeal. Therefore, both the failure of the applicants to successfully raise concerns regarding their trial record in their earlier appeals and the sufficiency of the reconstructed record resulted in the conclusion that there had not been a breach of their constitutional rights. The order was that the application for leave to appeal was dismissed ([para 43]). This case illustrates the complexities surrounding the adequacy of trial records in maintaining the integrity of the right to a fair trial.
