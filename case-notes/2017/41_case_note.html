FIRAC Analysis of Ramuhovhi and Others v. President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [2017] ZACC 41

The High Court found that the customary law applicable to the deceased's polygamous marriages did not afford wives any proprietary rights, thus infringing their rights to equality and dignity as guaranteed under the Constitution of South Africa.

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 41

F - Facts

The case arises from a challenge to the constitutionality of section 7(1) of the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 (Recognition Act) by the applicants, Matodzi Ramuhovhi and Thinamaano Edson Netshituka, who are children of the deceased likely subject to polygamous customary marriages. The deceased, Musenwa Joseph Netshituka, had entered into several marriages, both civil and customary, which created complex ownership claims concerning his estate ([paras 5–8]).

The High Court found that the customary law applicable to the deceased's polygamous marriages did not afford wives any proprietary rights, thus infringing their rights to equality and dignity as guaranteed under the Constitution of South Africa. This led to a declaration of constitutional invalidity of section 7(1) of the Recognition Act, which stipulated that the proprietary consequences of customary marriages prior to the Act remained governed by customary law, effectively entrenching gender discrimination ([paras 2–9]).

Additionally, the court explored the interests of Ms. Thokozani Thembekile Maphumulo, an intervening party, who sought intervention to assert her rights as a wife in a pre-Act polygamous marriage. This intervention highlighted the broader implications of discrimination faced by women in these marriages ([paras 14–22]).

I - Issues

The core legal issues in this case are:

  1. Constitutionality of Section 7(1): Does this provision, which perpetuates gender inequality in the context of proprietary rights in pre-Act polygamous marriages, contravene the constitutional rights to equality and human dignity?
  2. Remedy: If section 7(1) is found constitutionally invalid, what form should the remedial action take? Should the declaration of invalidity take effect retrospectively or prospectively?
  3. Intervention: Should Ms. Maphumulo's application to intervene be granted, given the rising concerns regarding retrospective justice for affected parties ([paras 22–30]).

R - Rules

The rules governing this case include:

  • Constitution of South Africa: Sections 9 (Equality) and 10 (Human Dignity) form the constitutional bedrock for assessing the validity of section 7(1) of the Recognition Act ([para 36]).
  • Recognition of Customary Marriages Act: Specifically, section 7(1), which mandates that the proprietary consequences of pre-Act customary marriages are governed exclusively by customary law.
  • Precedent: The judgement in Gumede v. President of the Republic of South Africa ([2008] ZACC 23) constituted a significant precedent that declared similar provisions unconstitutional regarding monogamous customary marriages, thereby prompting scrutiny of section 7(1)'s constitutionality in polygamous contexts ([paras 2–4]).

A - Application

The court applied the constitutional framework, examining the inherent contradictions between the Recognition Act and the values enshrined in the South African Constitution. The court found that section 7(1) perpetuates significant gender inequality by denying wives in pre-Act polygamous marriages equal rights to ownership and control of property ([para 39]).

Discrimination and Lack of Justification

The court reiterated that discrimination based on gender and marital status is unconstitutional unless justified under section 9(5) of the Constitution. The government did not provide any justification for this discrimination, rendering section 7(1) unconstitutional ([paras 34–40]).

Remedial Considerations

The court highlighted that while previous rulings allowed for prospective remedies, in this case, the ongoing discrimination against wives necessitated an immediate and just response. As such, the court determined that the declaration of invalidity should be suspended for 24 months, allowing the legislature to address the issues while concurrently instituting interim rights during this period ([paras 44–50]).

Intervention

The decision to grant Ms. Maphumulo's application to intervene was anchored on the interests of justice. The court acknowledged that her contributions were relevant and significant to the broader narrative and implications of socio-economic realities faced by women in similar situations ([para 26]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's declaration of invalidity of section 7(1) of the Recognition Act, emphasizing the urgent need for compliance with the constitutional mandates of equality and dignity. The suspension of invalidity for 24 months allows Parliament to redress the identified legislative gap, while interim rights for joint ownership were instituted to protect the rights of wives in existing polygamous marriages ([paras 71–70]).

This case reinforces the court's commitment to rectify historical injustices perpetuated by customary law. Its implications extend beyond individual cases, asserting a necessity for legislative reform to align customary laws with constitutional imperatives of equality, thereby ensuring that women in customary marriages are afforded the dignity and respect they have long been denied.
