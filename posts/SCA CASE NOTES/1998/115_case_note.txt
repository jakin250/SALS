Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH
Source Folder: 1998

### FIRAC Case Note: **Durban's Water Wonderland (Pty) Ltd v Botha**

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa   
**Judges**: VAN HEERDEN DCJ, HOWIE, HARMS, SCOTT JJA, MELUNSKY AJA  
**Heard**: 16 November 1998  
**Delivered**: 27 November 1998  
**Judgment**: SCOTT JA

---

### **F - Facts** ([paras 2–23])

On 25 November 1988, Mrs Botha and her 2½-year-old daughter, Mariska, sustained injuries on a ride called the 'jet ride' at the appellant's amusement park, managed by Durban’s Water Wonderland (Pty) Ltd. During their ride, an unexpected hydraulic failure caused the car to violently jerk, resulting in Mrs Botha and Mariska being flung from the car. The Bothas initiated a claim for damages in the Durban Magistrate's Court. The appellant denied negligence and cited a disclaimer posted at the ticket offices that purported to exempt it from liability for any injuries sustained during rides.

The trial involved three key issues:  
1. Whether the disclaimer on the ticket office windows was incorporated into the contract governing the use of the amusement park amenities.  
2. The interpretation of the disclaimer's language regarding negligence liability.  
3. Whether the appellant was negligent in the operation of the amusement park ride.

The magistrate ruled against the appellant on all three counts. The case was then appealed to the Natal Provincial Division, which also sided with the respondents on two of the three issues. The present appeal, with the leave of the lower court, pertains primarily to the legal interpretation of the disclaimer and the implications of liability.

---

### **I - Issues** ([paras 3, 4, 11])

The primary legal issues under consideration were:  
1. **Incorporation of the Disclaimer**: Did the disclaimer term become part of the contract between Mrs Botha and the appellant when she purchased her ticket?  
2. **Interpretation of the Disclaimer**: Did the language of the disclaimer effectively exempt the appellant from liability for negligence?  
3. **Negligence**: Was the appellant negligent in the design, construction, or operation of the amusement ride, and how does that intersect with the disclaimer?

---

### **R - Rules** ([paras 10, 15–18, 20, 21, 22])

Legal principles guiding this case include:

1. **Incorporation of Terms**: A disclaimer must be clearly communicated and reasonably brought to the customer's attention to be considered part of a contract. This follows from case law regarding display notices and ticketing agreements, as articulated in *Central South African Railways v McLaren* 1903 TS 727.

2. **Interpretation of Disclaimers**: Disclaimers must be interpreted in clear and unambiguous terms. If ambiguity arises, it should be construed against the proferens (the party seeking to enforce the disclaimer), as established in *Government of the Republic of South Africa v Fibre Spinners & Weavers (Pty) Ltd* 1978 (2) SA 794 (A).

3. **Negligence Standard**: The standard of negligence requires a demonstration of an unreasonable failure to act concerning safety and risks, as defined in tort law principles.

4. **Final Judgments and Appealability**: An interlocutory order is not appealable unless it settles a substantive issue, as seen in various cases including *Santam Bpk v van Niekerk* and *Raubex Construction (Pty) Ltd v Armist Wholesalers (Pty) Ltd*.

---

### **A - Application** ([paras 10–23])

**1. Incorporation of the Disclaimer**:  
The court found that the notices with the disclaimer were prominently displayed in a reasonable location where patrons would expect terms of use to be presented. Given that Mrs Botha was aware such notices existed in amusement parks, the court determined that the appellant had adequately indicated that there were terms attached to the ticket purchase, thus satisfying the contractual obligation to inform patrons.

**2. Interpretation of the Disclaimer**:  
The wording of the disclaimer explicitly disallowed liability for injuries arising from negligence, which the Court confirmed as unambiguous. The argument presented by the respondents that the disclaimer merely shifted the burden of proof rather than exempting liability was dismissed. The court reinforced that the use of definitive language indicated a complete exclusion of liability.

**3. Negligence and the Appellant's Conduct**:  
The magistrate initially concluded negligence on the grounds of improper bolting of the seat to the ride, which was found to fall within the scope of the disclaimer. The court affirmed this conclusion, determining that the claim was grounded in the design and operation issues that the disclaimer effectively covered. Hence, any argument around operational negligence lost weight against the explicitly stated terms in the agreement.

**4. Appealability of the Judgment**:  
The court clarified that the finding of liability held significant weight as it represented a final resolution of the substantive defense raised distinctly from the respondents' claims. Therefore, it reiterated that such a finding was appealable, thus distinguishing itself from previous legal interpretations of what constitutes a final judgment.

---

### **C - Conclusion** ([paras 22–23])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appellant's appeal, overturning the lower court's decision which had found the appellant liable. The court concluded that the disclaimer effectively exempted the appellant from liability for negligence and that it was properly incorporated into the contract at the point of ticket purchase.

**Ratio Decidendi**: The critical takeaway is that properly communicated disclaimers, displayed where a reasonable patron could be expected to see them, can validly limit liability for negligence in a contractual context. The findings reinforce the necessity for both clear communication of terms and an understanding of the surrounding contractual implications.

**Implications**: This case establishes a precedent on the enforceability of disclaimers in the context of amusement parks and similar venues, demonstrating that liability can be effectively limited if patrons are adequately informed of the terms beforehand. It underscores the importance of clarity and visibility in contractual disclaimers.
