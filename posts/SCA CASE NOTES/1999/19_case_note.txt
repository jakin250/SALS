# Case Note: W G Davey (Pty) Ltd v National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts ([paras 1–29])

The appellant, W G Davey (Pty) Ltd, a manufacturer in the engineering field, faced legal action from the National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa (NUMSA), the registered trade union representing its striking employees. In August 1992, nineteen employees joined a national strike initiated by NUMSA after failed wage negotiations in a collective bargaining forum, despite an interim court order declaring the strike illegal ([paras 1–3]). As the strike progressed, W G Davey communicated a "Final Ultimatum" to its employees, demanding they return to work by 7 am on 28 August, failing which they would face dismissal ([paras 5–6]). 

Following minimal compliance with the ultimatum—only twelve workers returned on time—the appellant dismissed the remaining employees on 31 August after they failed to comply with the ultimatum ([paras 5–7]). NUMSA contended that these dismissals constituted an unfair labour practice, seeking redress from the industrial court, which initially ruled against NUMSA ([para 1]). This decision was subsequently overturned by the Labour Appeal Court (LAC), which determined the dismissals were indeed unfair ([paras 2–3]).

### I – Issues ([paras 2–4, 16–20])

The primary legal question was whether the dismissals constituted an unfair labour practice by W G Davey following the issuance of the ultimatum. A secondary issue pertained to the appropriateness of the reinstatement order made by the LAC after overturning the industrial court's determination ([para 4]). 

### R – Rules ([paras 1, 11, 15, 20])

The legal foundation governing the case was rooted in the Labour Relations Act 28 of 1956 (LRA), which defines unfair labour practices, including undue dismissal of employees engaged in lawful strike action ([para 1]). The LAC's analysis aligned with precedents such as *National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa v Vetsak Co-operative Limited and Others* ([1996] 4 SA 577 at 583) where courts emphasized the need for fairness in employment relations ([para 11]). The concept of fairness requires consideration of both the employer's position and the employees' conduct and circumstances ([paras 17-19]).

### A – Application ([paras 11–24])

The LAC held that while the initial ultimatum issued by W G Davey was deemed fair at the outset, the employer's rigid adherence to the ultimatum without considering evolving circumstances constituted unfairness ([paras 16–24]). Critically, the appellant had not taken into account the fact that NUMSA was actively guiding its members about returning to work based on the NSC's recommendations, which were communicated broadly following the court ruling ([paras 17–19]). 

The court emphasized the importance of an employer's responsiveness to new facts after issuing ultimatums, suggesting that a reasonable employer would have considered ongoing communication from NUMSA regarding its members' return to work ([para 21]). The failure to engage in negotiations or provide an extension for the deadline indicated an "inflexible and intransigent" approach by the appellant, undermining claims of fairness in executing mass dismissals ([paras 20–22]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 23–29])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the LAC's decision, confirming that the dismissals were indeed unfair due to the employer's rigid refusal to reconsider the ultimatum in light of changing developments ([para 26]). Additionally, the court modified the LAC's order concerning reinstatement, stipulating conditions for compensation that would account for any earnings during the period of dismissal ([para 28]). 

This case reaffirms essential principles regarding fair labour practices, highlighting the obligation of employers to exhibit flexibility and awareness of evolving employee circumstances post-ultimatum, while also emphasizing the necessity for continued dialogue during disputes in the workplace. The decision serves as an important precedent in cases involving dismissal of employees during strike action, setting a standard for future employer-employee engagements in similar contexts.
