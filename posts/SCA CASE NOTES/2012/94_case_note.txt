Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

### FIRAC Case Note: Ngwenyama v Mayelane & another (474/11) [2012] ZASCA 94

#### F: Facts
[1] This appeal originated from the North Gauteng High Court, where the first respondent, Ms M F M, sought an order to declare her customary marriage to Hlengani Dyson Moyana (the deceased) valid and to dismiss an application regarding a subsequent marriage claimed by the appellant, Ms M M N, which she insisted was entered into without due legal processes as required by the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 (the Act). The appellants were married under customary law, with their union having been celebrated on 1 January 1984, leading to the birth of three children. The marriage went unregistered prior to the deceased's death on 28 February 2009. 

[3] Upon the deceased’s passing, when Ms M F M sought to register her customary marriage, she discovered that Ms M M N claimed to have married the deceased on 26 January 2008 without her consultation. Ms M F M contended that this subsequent marriage was null and void due to the deceased's failure to comply with section 7(6) of the Act, which mandates that a husband intending to enter a further marriage must first obtain court approval for a written contract governing the proprietary consequences of multiple marriages.

[4] The deceased's elder brother corroborated the existence of the first marriage and the customary requirement for consultation prior to any subsequent marriage. Despite the lack of compliance with the statutory mandate of section 7(6) by the deceased, the first court found the second marriage invalid.

#### I: Issues
[8] The principal issue for determination was whether the non-compliance by the husband with section 7(6) of the Act, which requires prior court approval before entering into a further customary marriage, renders that marriage null and void from the outset, particularly in the absence of an express provision in the Act outlining such a consequence.

#### R: Rules
[12] Section 7(6) of the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act states that a husband in a customary marriage who wishes to enter into a further customary marriage must apply for court approval of a written contract regulating the future matrimonial property system associated with both marriages. The Act aims to ensure the equality and rights of spouses in customary marriages and the complexities surrounding polygamous relationships.

[14] The court below emphasized the peremptory nature of section 7(6) in maintaining the proprietary rights and interests of existing spouses, inferring that a failure to comply with this section necessitates the invalidation of any subsequent customary marriage. Precedents, such as *Cronje and Heaton* in *South African Family Law*, supported this view, holding that failure to observe statutory requirements results in the voiding of marriages as such failure undermines legislative intent.

#### A: Application
[17] The Supreme Court of Appeal sought to avoid an interpretation that would render a marriage valid under customary law void ab initio based solely on non-compliance with section 7(6). The majority opinion reasoned that while compliance with section 7(6) is certainly advisable and pertinent to protect the rights of both existing and prospective wives, the ultimate determination of marital validity rests with the provisions enhancing legal recognition of customary marriages outlined in sections 2 and 3 of the Act.

[18] The court focused on how the legislative intent behind section 7 was to provide equitable treatment and protection to women in polygamous circumstances. This is crucial considering the substantial adverse implications for any children born into a marriage deemed invalid due to technicalities. The dissenting rationale would perpetuate existing patterns of disadvantage and discrimination against women in subsequent marriages.

[20] Moreover, it was emphasized that the failure to interpret section 7(6) in line with the objectives of the Act, particularly its alignment with constitutional norms of dignity and equality, would undermine the overall intentions of the legislature to reform customary law and protect women’s rights. The view postulated by the amicus curiae highlighted the adverse effects discriminatory interpretations have on women married under customary law.

#### C: Conclusion
[28] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately reversed the order of the North Gauteng High Court, holding that the failure to comply with section 7(6) does not invalidate the subsequent customary marriage entered into - it merely affects the matrimonial property regime. This finding served to reinforce the Act’s purpose of accommodating and recognizing the rights of all spouses in customary marriages, thereby ensuring that the dignity and equality of vulnerable individuals, such as women in these circumstances, are upheld ([para 28]).

The implications of this judgment reformulate the understanding of marital validity within the framework of customary law in South Africa, pointing towards a more inclusive approach that emphasizes equal protection under the law for women in polygamous marriages. This is essential not only for the women directly involved but also for the children born of such marriages, thereby safeguarding their legal standing within the societal context under customary law.
