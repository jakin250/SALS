Abore v Minister of Home Affairs and Another [2021] ZACC 50

The applicant, Desta Abore, an Ethiopian national, entered South Africa illegally from Zimbabwe after fleeing political persecution [1].

Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 50

F – Facts

The applicant, Desta Abore, an Ethiopian national, entered South Africa illegally from Zimbabwe after fleeing political persecution [1]. Abore was arrested on 8 June 2020 in Eshowe, KwaZulu-Natal, for unlawful entry under the Immigration Act [3]. He was convicted on 7 July 2020 and sentenced to 50 days' imprisonment, which he finished on 25 August 2020. Despite the fine for his imprisonment being paid, he remained in detention at Lindela Repatriation Centre, as the respondents sought to deport him [4].

On 21 July 2020, the High Court of Durban had granted an interim order preventing his deportation pending the determination of his legal standing [4]. However, this order lapsed, and by February 2021, a warrant for further detention was issued to facilitate his deportation [5]. In March 2021, Abore applied to the High Court, Johannesburg, for an interdict against his deportation and detention, claiming unlawful detention and the right to apply for asylum. The High Court dismissed his application on 29 March 2021, concluding that his detention was lawful due to the provisions of the Immigration Act, citing a lack of timely asylum application [7-8].

I – Issues

  1. Whether Abore, as an illegal foreigner, was entitled under the Refugees Act and its amendments to apply for asylum despite not doing so upon entry into the country.
  2. Whether the delay in Abore's expression of intention to apply for asylum barred him from such an application.
  3. Whether Abore's prolonged detention was lawful under the Constitution and statutory framework.
  4. Whether the amendments to the Refugees Act affect the principles established in prior case law concerning asylum seekers [20].

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework includes:

  • The Refugees Act 130 of 1998, particularly section 2, which articulates the principle of non-refoulement, ensuring no refugee is returned to their home country where they face persecution [44].
  • The Refugees Amendment Act 11 of 2017, outlining new requirements for asylum applications, including provisions related to the need for asylum transit visas and interviews by immigration officers [21][27].
  • For past precedents, Ruta v Minister of Home Affairs [2018] ZACC 52, which established that an illegal foreigner who expresses a desire to apply for asylum is entitled to do so without being barred by previous delays [21][48].

A – Application

Applicability of Legal Framework

The court had to determine whether the amendments to the Refugees Act enacted in January 2020 affected Abore's entitlement to asylum, given he expressed the intention to apply for asylum post-amendment. It was established that prior decisions still applied, as section 2 of the Refugees Act was unchanged and upheld the principle of non-refoulement, thus protecting Abore from deportation until his asylum status had been resolved [41][44].

Although the High Court concluded that Abore's delay in applying for asylum indicated he did not intend to pursue legal status, the Constitutional Court noted that while delay may impact the credibility of an asylum claim, it does not serve as a perpetual bar to claiming asylum [21][48]. This perspective mirrors international law principles that support enabling asylum seekers the right to apply [43].

Lawfulness of Detention

Moreover, the court held that Abore's continued detention from 26 August 2020 until 7 February 2021 was unlawful since his custodial sentence had expired and there were no legal grounds for his continued detention following the lapse of the interim order [50]. The absence of a subsequent legal basis for his detention was emphasized as contravening his constitutional rights under section 12 [50].

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court upheld Abore's appeal, overturning the High Court’s ruling [53]. The ruling confirmed that Abore was entitled to remain in South Africa under the Refugees Act and its amendments until his asylum status was determined. The court ordered the respondents to facilitate Abore's asylum application within 14 days. Furthermore, it recognized the unlawful nature of his prolonged detention and directed the respondents to bear the costs of both the High Court and the Constitutional Court proceedings.

This judgment clarifies the ongoing applicability of non-refoulement principles under South African law and provides significant precedent regarding the rights of illegal foreigners to apply for asylum amidst legislative amendments, reinforcing robust protection for asylum seekers against potential unlawful deportation [41][46].
