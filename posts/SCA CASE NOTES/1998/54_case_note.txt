Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APP
Source Folder: 1998

# Case Note: Kleinbooi Kekana v Society of Advocates of South Africa

### FIRAC Analysis

**F - Facts: [paras 2–16]**

The case revolves around the applicant, Kleinbooi Kekana, who sought the reinstatement of an appeal regarding an order that removed his name from the roll of advocates due to misconduct. The proceedings leading to his removal originated from a report received by the Society of Advocates, which investigated allegations of dishonesty against Kekana and his colleague, Mr. Mabena, during their representation of pro deo clients in Tzaneen. Charges included the submission of false claims for meals and accommodation, including claims for expenses incurred by two female companions, which were not permissible under the guidelines for advocates.

The case progressed through various procedural stages, including a hearing where oral evidence was considered. Initially, the Bar Council had conducted an internal enquiry but decided to pursue removal based on material disputes of fact that emerged during the preliminary investigation. Significant delays in prosecuting the appeal were noted, mostly attributed to the applicant's representation and procedural negligence. Ultimately, the trial court ruled against the applicant, finding that he and Mabena were unfit to practice law based on their dishonest conduct. The appeal to reinstate the case was made after considerable time lapsed without meaningful progress.

**I - Issues: [paras 7–10]**

The primary legal question was whether the Supreme Court had grounds to reinstate the applicant's appeal, which had lapsed due to gross non-compliance with procedural rules. Specifically, the court was tasked with assessing factors such as the prospecs of success on appeal, the degree of non-compliance, and the prejudicial implications for the applicant following the order of removal. One significant sub-issue pertained to whether the original court had exercised its discretion capriciously or based on improper principles when determining that Kekana was unfit to continue practicing law.

**R - Rules: [paras 11–10]**

The court referenced several legal precedents regarding the obligations of advocates and the serious nature of dishonest conduct in the legal profession. Section 7(1) of the Admission of Advocates Act 74 of 1964 permits the removal of an advocate from the roll if they are deemed unfit and improper. The court explained that the exercise of discretion in such matters is limited; interference at the appellate level is warranted only where it is established that the trial court exercised its discretion capriciously, or not grounded in substantial reasoning (Beyers v Pretoria Balieraad 1966(2) SA 593 (A) at 605 F-H).

The judgment also emphasized maintaining high ethical standards within the legal profession, detailing that honesty is paramount for legal practitioners, who must both advocate for their clients and uphold justice.

**A - Application: [paras 11–16]**

In its deliberation, the Supreme Court analyzed whether the applicant had demonstrated sufficient prospects of success to warrant reinstatement of the appeal. The court acknowledged the applicant's background and efforts to rectify his procedural faults after becoming aware of his earlier attorneys' negligence. However, it concluded that the blatant nature of the non-compliance undermined any active effort to advance the appeal. Furthermore, the court engaged with the original ruling, which convicted Kekana of misconduct involving dishonesty and perjury, calling into question his character and fitness to practice.

The ruling confirmed that such dishonesty was particularly egregious, emphasizing that the nature of the offense reflecting a persistent and serious breach of ethical conduct was of greater significance than the trivial amounts involved in the financial claims. The court also examined the mitigating factors presented by the applicant's counsel, ultimately deeming them insufficient to persuade the court to reinstate the appeal, given the serious implications of his dishonesty.

**C - Conclusion: [paras 16–16]**

The Supreme Court ultimately dismissed the application for reinstatement of the appeal, citing inadequate prospects of success and the grave nature of Kekana's conduct. The reasoning highlighted the serious implications of dishonesty and the necessity for the legal profession to maintain integrity. The judgment reinforced the principle that integrity and honesty are non-negotiable attributes for legal practitioners and established the court's commitment to upholding high ethical standards in the profession. The court's decision served to reiterate the importance of personal integrity within the legal field, ensuring that members who exhibit untrustworthiness, particularly through perjury, cannot retain their standing as advocates. The persuasive nature of the underlying misconduct ensured that the appeal could not be revived, emphasizing the necessity for compliance with procedural norms in legal practice.
