Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

### FIRAC Case Note: LD v Central Authority (RSA) and Another 

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–21])

In the matter of *LD v Central Authority (RSA) and Another*, the appellant LD, the father of a minor child named E, sought the return of his daughter from South Africa to Luxembourg after the child's mother, PH, unlawfully removed E from Luxembourg on 5 October 2018. PH had previously been involved in legal proceedings in Luxembourg, where she applied for permission to relocate to South Africa, which had been denied. Despite this, PH relocated to South Africa and established her residence with her new husband and E’s half-brother S.

On 19 December 2018, the Luxembourg Juvenile Court ordered a social enquiry into E's circumstances in South Africa and granted LD rights of access. However, PH failed to comply with the court orders, leading to LD filing an application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, under the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction. Initially, the High Court ordered E's return to Luxembourg, but PH's appeal against this decision led to the Full Court dismissing the application, prompting LD to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

The case’s core fact was the evaluation of whether the return of E would expose her to grave psychological harm, justifying an exception under Article 13(b) of the Hague Convention ([paras 1–21]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 22–22])

The primary issue for determination was: whether PH had successfully proven a defense under Article 13(b) of the Hague Convention, arguing that E’s return to Luxembourg would expose her to grave risk of physical or psychological harm or otherwise place her in an intolerable situation. 

This required consideration of the balance between the best interests of the child and the implications of the mother's unlawful actions ([paras 22–22]).

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 23–29])

The *Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction* outlines the legal framework governing cross-border child custody disputes. Notably, Article 3 establishes the wrongful nature of a child’s removal if it contravenes custody rights and Article 12 mandates the prompt return of a child wrongfully removed from their habitual residence. Moreover, Article 13 provides exceptions to this rule, stipulating that a child need not be returned if the person opposing the return establishes a grave risk of harm.

Substantial jurisprudential interpretation emerged from various cases, including *Pennello v Pennello* and *Sonderup v Tondelli*, that clarified the burden of proof on the person resisting a return order to demonstrate a grave risk. The courts highlighted that the harm must be severe and not merely the normal disruption accompanying an unwelcome return. Courts also acknowledged the child’s best interests as central to determinations under the Convention, without allowing for custody disputes to overshadow the statutory principles ([paras 23–29]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 30–39])

The court scrutinized the evidence presented regarding the potential psychological impact on E if returned to Luxembourg. Both a social worker, Professor Spies, and an educational psychologist, Ms. De Vos, provided reports indicating that E had formed a secure bond with her mother and half-brother and had adapted well in South Africa. They suggested that uprooting E could lead to severe emotional trauma, particularly given her attachment to her family unit, which included her mother's new husband and half-brother in South Africa.

The court emphasized that while the mother’s conduct was unlawful and unjustifiable, the ultimate focus should remain on E’s best interests. It found that separating E from her family in South Africa, particularly her strong bond with her mother and half-brother S, would predictably lead to psychological harm amounting to an intolerable situation as outlined in Article 13(b). 

The court acknowledged that this would not merely be the anticipated stress of a return but rather a profound emotional dislocation, taking into account the holistic impact on E’s psychological well-being if separated from her established support structure ([paras 30–39]).

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 40–40])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal be dismissed, affirming that PH successfully established her defense under Article 13(b) of the Hague Convention. The court underscored that E’s immediate relationships and wellbeing were paramount, emphasizing that her return to Luxembourg would disrupt her familial ties in South Africa, thus placing her in a situation of grave risk.

The ruling reinforced the application of the Convention’s principles while balancing the implications of prior unlawful conduct against a child's welfare, asserting that the best interests of E took precedence in this context. The judgment also highlighted the necessity for courts to weigh the psychological consequences of enforced relocations in child abduction cases, shaping future considerations where the welfare of children in similar disputes could be effectively protected ([paras 40–40]).
