# Case Note: Minister of Police v Van der Watt and Another (1009/2021) [2022] ZASCA 114

### FIRAC Structure

**F – Facts:**  
This appeal arises from the unsuccessful application for the rescission of two consent court orders granted on 22 October 2015 and 10 February 2017 by the Gauteng Division of the High Court ([1]). The first respondent, Mr. Pierre Christo Van der Watt, initiated legal action against the Minister of Police for damages related to unlawful arrest, detention, and assault that occurred during a domestic disturbance on 3 March 2013 ([3]–[6]). Despite explicit instructions from Colonel Mahube of the South African Police Service to resist Van der Watt's claim, the State Attorney, through Ms. Nangomoso Qongqo and counsel Mduduzi Thabethe, settled the issue of liability ([8]–[9]). This settlement resulted in the Minister being held liable for 50% of Van der Watt’s damages, leading to the issuing of the court orders that the Minister later sought to rescind ([10]–[12]).

**I – Issues:**  
The principal legal question pertains to the authority of the State Attorney and counsel to enter into the settlement agreements that became the basis for the consent orders ([14]). Specifically, whether the Minister's contention that the agreements were void due to lack of actual authority (i.e., the State Attorney acted against express instructions) warranted rescission under Rule 42(1) of the Uniform Rules of Court ([11]).

**R – Rules:**  
The court predominantly referenced Rule 42(1)(a) of the Uniform Rules of Court, which allows rescission of an order erroneously sought or granted in the absence of any affected party ([15]). Furthermore, the concept of apparent authority, derived from the common law principle articulated in *Makate v Vodacom (Pty) Ltd*, was pivotal to the analysis of whether the Minister should be bound by the settlement agreements made by his representatives ([18]). Among case law references, *Eke v Parsons* and *Moraitis Investments (Pty) Ltd* were instrumental in considering the implications of the court orders and the finality they imparted to the outstanding legal questions ([22], [23]).

**A – Application:**  
The court found that the Minister and his legal representation were present and involved in the settlement process, thus were not absent when the orders were made, negating the application of Rule 42(1)(a) ([16]). In examining whether the State Attorney and counsel possessed apparent authority, the court concluded that the Minister had created an impression for Mr. Van der Watt and his counsel that the State Attorney had authority to act, leading to the conclusion that the Minister was liable under the settlement agreements despite the express instructions to resist the claims ([19], [20]). The court further dismissed the Minister's argument based on *iustus error*, stating that such reliance is only applicable when discussing the consequences of valid settlement agreements, not when the authority to settle is questioned ([25]).

Furthermore, the court scrutinized the distinction between the current appeal and previous cases such as *Kunene and Others v Minister of Police*, clarifying that no fraudulent activity or bad faith conduct was present that would undermine the integrity of the settlements ([26]–[29]). The lack of fraud and illegality was essential, as the court sought to uphold the legitimacy of the orders once granted, promoting stability in judicial proceedings ([23]–[24]).

**C – Conclusion:**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, reinforcing the validity of the consent orders and highlighting the Minister's liability as a consequence of the apparent authority exercised by the State Attorney ([33]). The court maintained that since there was no evidence of fraudulent conduct from Mr. Van der Watt or his legal team, the orders remained enforceable and were correctly upheld by the full court ([30]–[32]). The ruling underlines the importance of clear communication and authority within legal proceedings, particularly where state representation is concerned, indicating the potential for further investigation into breaches of duty by the State Attorney’s office ([31]). The implications suggest a need for procedural reforms or disciplinary actions to prevent similar occurrences in future governmental representations.
