Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1998

# Case Note: Republic of South Africa v Ivor Niselow (Case No. 450/96)

## F - Facts

Ivor Niselow (the Appellant) was appointed as an "agent" by Liberty Life Association of Africa Limited (the Respondent) under a written agreement. The arrangement involved Niselow conducting business on behalf of Liberty Life by canvassing for applications for insurance contracts exclusively for the Respondent ([para 2]). Following disciplinary inquiries, the Respondent terminated this agreement. Niselow subsequently filed a claim in the Industrial Court, asserting that the termination of his agreement constituted an unfair labour practice under the now-repealed Labour Relations Act 1956 (the "Act") ([para 3]).

In response, the Respondent raised a preliminary point (in limine) regarding jurisdiction, contending that Niselow did not qualify as an "employee" under the Act, which was accepted by the Labour Appeal Court after initially being dismissed by the Industrial Court ([paras 2–3]). The appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal arose from the Labour Appeal Court's ruling that the Appellant was not an employee, thus barring any jurisdiction for the Industrial Court to adjudicate the matter ([para 3]). The only contentious issue before the court was whether Nikelow could be classified as an "employee" according to the definition provided in the Act ([para 4]).

## I - Issues

The legal issue presented before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether Ivor Niselow was an "employee" as defined in Section 1(1) of the Labour Relations Act 1956. The resolution of this question determined the jurisdiction of the Industrial Court over the claim presented by Niselow for unfair dismissal ([para 4]).

## R - Rules

The Labour Relations Act of 1956 defines an "employee" as “any person who is employed by or working for an employer and receiving or entitled to receive any remuneration” and includes “any other person whomsoever who in any manner assists in the carrying on or conducting of the business of an employer” ([para 5]). The distinction drawn between an independent contractor and an employee at common law is pertinent; the former contracts to produce specified results while the latter undertakes to provide personal services ([para 4]).

Judicial precedents such as *Smit v Workmen's Compensation Commissioner* and *South African Master Dental Technicians Association v Dental Association of South Africa* were cited to clarify that an employee, in common law, must render personal services, while independent contractors focus on results ([paras 4–5]). Moreover, a significant aspect of employment relationships is the level of supervision and control exercised by the employer ([para 11]).

## A - Application

The Court analyzed the written agreement between the parties to discern the nature of the relationship established. The agreement specified that Niselow was to work full-time and exclusively for the Respondent, with remuneration being commission-based on contracts obtained ([para 5]). It was crucial to assess whether the contractual terms indicated a relationship of personal service or merely a contractual result.

The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that several conditions of the agreement underscored an independent contractor relationship rather than that of employment. Notably, Niselow had discretion over how to achieve the contractual objectives, employed other assistants, and could lose the contract if production standards were not met ([paras 8–10]). While the Respondent did enforce some standards and practices (like attendance at training), these did not rise to the level of control typical in employer-employee relationships ([para 12]).

The Labour Appeal Court's conclusion that Niselow retained control over his productive capacity reinforced the classification as an independent contractor. Furthermore, the specificity of remuneration based on results rather than time invested signified a contract focused on outcomes rather than the personal service aspect of employment. Thus, the Court found no grounds to overturn the finding of the Labour Appeal Court that Niselow was not an employee as envisioned by the Act ([paras 10–12]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Labour Appeal Court’s decision and concluded that Ivor Niselow was not an employee under the Labour Relations Act, thus the Industrial Court lacked jurisdiction over the unfair dismissal claim. The court highlighted that costs should follow the result, awarding the Respondent costs, including the expenses incurred for two counsel’s representation ([para 14]). 

Ultimately, the ruling clarified the distinctions between an employee and an independent contractor within South African employment law, emphasizing the importance of contractual language in determining the nature of employment relationships, with significant implications for similar future disputes regarding classification under labour law.
