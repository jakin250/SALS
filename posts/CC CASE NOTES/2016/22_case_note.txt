Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: **Land Access Movement of South Africa and Others v Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces and Others** [2016] ZACC 22

### FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–5])

In this landmark case, the Constitutional Court of South Africa addresses the complex issue of land restitution, which remains an emotive issue stemming from historical dispossession during colonial and apartheid eras ([1]). The applicants include several organizations and communal property associations vested in land reform, challenging the constitutional validity of the Restitution of Land Rights Amendment Act 15 of 2014 (the Amendment Act). The primary basis for the challenge is the claim that the National Council of Provinces (NCOP) failed to adequately facilitate public participation during the legislative process as mandated by sections 72(1)(a) and 118(1)(a) of the Constitution ([2]).

Initially, the Restitution Act of 1994 allowed land claims to be lodged until 31 December 1998. However, the Amendment Act sought to reopen the claims window, which led to concerns from the applicants regarding the potential prejudice to existing claimants due to the lack of “ring-fencing” existing claims against newly lodged ones. The applicants assert that the failure to ensure proper public consultation constitutes a breach of constitutional duty ([3]). They seek an order of constitutional invalidity and, alternatively, a declaration deeming a specific provision in the Amendment Act, section 6(1)(g), unconstitutional and invalid.

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 6–7])

The central issue is whether the NCOP failed to comply with its constitutional obligation to facilitate public involvement during the legislative process leading to the enactment of the Amendment Act ([6]). Specifically, a determination is needed on whether the inadequacies of public participation, particularly the truncated timelines imposed for legislative consideration, amount to a breach of the constitutional mandate ([7]).

Additionally, the court must consider whether the alternative challenge regarding the validity of section 6(1)(g) holds merit, emphasizing the vagueness of the provision and its implications for existing land claimants ([4]) ([5]).

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 56–62])

The court primarily relies on sections 72(1)(a), 59(1)(a), and 118(1)(a) of the Constitution, which impose obligations on the NCOP and the National Assembly to facilitate public involvement in the legislative process ([56]). The judgment also refers to previous case law, including *Doctors for Life International v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others* [2006] ZACC 11, which emphasized the necessity of reasonable public participation and consultation within a legislative context ([61]).

These rules establish a foundational legal framework that ensures the democratic principle of public participation, requiring that legislative processes not only include public input but that such input be meaningful and adequately considered ([59]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 63–82])

In analyzing the constitutional obligations of the NCOP, the court highlights the critical nature of land restitution rights in South Africa, recognizing these rights as instrumental to dignity and socio-economic stability ([63]). It critiques the urgency of the legislative timeline adopted by the NCOP, arguing that the self-imposed deadline compromised the quality and scope of public participation ([66]).

The court meticulously assesses the processes undertaken by both the NCOP and the Provincial Legislatures, finding significant shortcomings in facilitating adequate public hearings. It found that most public hearings were conducted with insufficient notice and that many participants were unable to voice their concerns adequately due to a lack of accessible information and time ([77]) ([80]).

Crucially, the court concludes that the inadequacies in public participation fundamentally tainted the legislative process, determining that the failure of one house (the NCOP) to satisfy its constitutional obligations ultimately constitutes a failure of Parliament as a whole ([82]).

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 82–93])

The court issued a declaration that Parliament, specifically the NCOP, failed to satisfy its constitutional duty to facilitate public involvement as required by section 72(1)(a) of the Constitution, categorizing the Amendment Act as invalid ([82]). The judgment emphasized that invalidating the Amendment Act would apply prospectively, taking effect from the date of judgment to avoid disruption of land claims lodged in good faith under the now-invalid Act ([86]). 

The implications of this holding are profound, reinforcing the significance of public participation in legislative processes affecting fundamental rights, particularly concerning land restitution. The decision obligates Parliament to reconsider and re-enact legislation that adheres to constitutional mandates for public involvement, thereby upholding democratic principles and ensuring that affected communities have a voice in decisions that impinge on their rights ([93]).

In awarding costs against the NCOP alone, the court further delineates accountability in legislative processes and underlines the necessity for conscientious procedural adherence to constitutional standards ([92]).

This case sets a significant precedent regarding the interplay between legislative processes and constitutional rights, particularly in the context of restitution of land rights in South Africa, reinforcing that public involvement is not merely procedural but fundamental to the legitimacy of legislative action.
