# Case Note: Oppressed A C S A Minority 1 (Pty) Ltd and Another v Government of the Republic of South Africa and Others

## FIRAC Case Note

**Neutral Citation:** Oppressed A C S A Minority 1 (Pty) Ltd and Another v Government of the Republic of South Africa and Others (case no 898/2020) [2022] ZASCA 50 (11 April 2022)

### F – Facts ([paras 1–37])

The appeal revolves around a consent order granted by the Gauteng Division of the High Court. The first appellants, Oppressed ACSA Minority 1 (Pty) Ltd, and Up-Front Investments 65 (Pty) Ltd, represent a minority shareholder segment holding 4.21% of the shares in Airports Company of South Africa (ACSA), the third respondent. This percentage had been accumulated when the appellants acquired their shares for R172 million in 1998. The Government of South Africa, as the majority shareholder, maintains a 74.6% interest in ACSA, established under the Airports Company Act 44 of 1993.

Following concerns regarding ACSA’s deviation from planned business practices and failure to declare dividends, the appellants filed an application under section 163 of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, which pertains to oppressive conduct towards minority shareholders ([paras 2-4]). The application culminated in a consent order on August 1, 2017, wherein ACSA was to repurchase the appellants' shares at a valuation determined by a referee ([paras 8-10]).

However, the consent order was contested by the Government, which argued that it lacked authority over the terms of the settlement based on alleged contraventions of several statutory provisions, including the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) and corporate governance rules ([paras 10-11]).  The government asserted that the consent order was unlawful, leading to its application for rescission approximately one year later, claiming no resolution had been passed by ACSA's Board to authorize the buy-back ([paras 12-14]).

### I – Issues ([paras 16–23])

The primary issues of law that arose from this case include:

1. **Standing of the Government**: Whether the Government had the standing to seek rescission of the consent order when it was not a party to the underlying settlement agreement.
  
2. **Authority to conclude the settlement agreement**: Whether ACSA's representatives had the requisite authority to enter into the settlement agreement that led to the consent order.
  
3. **Grounds for rescission**: The legal basis for rescission under rule 42(1) of the Uniform Rules of Court, common law grounds of justus error, and whether the consent order contravened statutory provisions.

### R – Rules ([paras 24–30])

The applicable legal rules and precedents revolved around principles of corporate law, particularly as encapsulated within the Companies Act, the PFMA, and rules of court pertaining to rescission of judgments:

1. **Companies Act, Section 163**: Provides a mechanism for minority shareholders to seek relief from oppressive conduct.

2. **Public Finance Management Act**: Sets out governance mechanisms requiring board resolutions and notifications to the National Treasury for significant shareholding transactions ([para 11]).

3. **Rule 42(1) Uniform Rules of Court**: Allows for rescission of judgments when they were erroneously granted or are otherwise unrecognized by law.

4. **Principles of Justus Error**: Generally allow a party to seek rescission of a judgment on the grounds of a mistake regarding the facts or law leading to the judgment ([para 24]).

5. **Interplay of Authority**: The principle that an unauthorized act cannot be ratified or validated by subsequent judicial approval ([para 37]).

### A – Application ([paras 24–36])

In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal ("SCA") emphasized that the starting point for assessing rescission must be the order itself rather than the underlying agreement. Here, it recognized the lack of authority on ACSA's part to execute the terms of the settlement agreement by noting that the Board had never passed a resolution approving it ([paras 28-29]).

The Government’s standing was found valid, as it had a direct interest in ACSA's operations as the majority shareholder. This relationship necessitated its involvement in matters concerning significant corporate transactions such as share buy-backs ([paras 19–21]).

The court rejected the appellants’ claims regarding the validity of the 'ratification' of the agreement due to a lack of formal approval from ACSA's Board or the Minister of Transport ([paras 34-36]). The appellants' arguments regarding compliance with order terms—citing ACSA's actions following the consent order—did not suffice to override the procedural requirements stipulated in the PFMA and Companies Act ([paras 25–27]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 37–37])

The SCA ultimately dismissed the appeal, upholding the high court's rescission of the consent order. The judgment underscored the judicial recognition that courts must scrutinize consent orders for their legal compliance and authority. The implications of this decision reinforce the necessity for strict adherence to statutory mandates when state entities engage in transactions involving public assets. The requirement for transparency and accountability in managing public interests for entities like ACSA remains a critical aspect of corporate governance in South Africa ([para 37]). 

The court declared that proceeding without legitimate authorization could not be endorsed, ensuring that similar future transactions adhere to governance standards, thus promoting the integrity of corporate and public financial management in South Africa.
