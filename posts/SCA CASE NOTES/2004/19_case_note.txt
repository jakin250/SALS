# Case Note: Pretoria East Builders CC and Infogold Investments CC v Edriaan Stéphan Basson

**FIRAC Case Note**

**F – Facts:** 
In the case of *Pretoria East Builders CC and Infogold Investments CC v Edriaan Stéphan Basson* ([1]), the Supreme Court of Appeal dealt with a contract for the sale of immovable property. On 10 May 2002, the court granted a final interdict against the appellants, Pretoria East Builders CC (“Pretoria East Builders”) and Infogold Investments CC (“Infogold”), ordering them to fulfil a written agreement with the respondent, Basson. Pretoria East Builders was responsible for developing erf No 6733, owned by Infogold, whose sole member was F van Schalkwyk, who also managed Pretoria East Builders ([2]).

The dispute arose when Basson made an offer for the property at R890,000, which was conditionally accepted by Ms G Badenhorst on behalf of Pretoria East Builders. The offer included a clause (paragraph 18) concerning a building contract, added without Basson’s initials or signature, thereby becoming a counter-offer ([2]–[3]). As construction progressed, further alterations were made at Basson’s request, but the necessary building contract was never executed, nor was the mortgage application presented in Basson’s name as originally intended ([3]–[4]).

The situation changed when it became clear that Pretoria East Builders intended to cancel the agreement despite previous assurance of compliance. Their attorneys clarified that Van Schalkwyk had not authorized the sale, contending there was no enforceable contract, leading Basson to seek urgent relief ([4]).

**I – Issues:** 
The primary legal issues before the court were:
1. Whether there existed a binding contractual agreement between Basson and the appellants, particularly in light of the amendments made to the original purchase offer.
2. The implications of the lack of authorization from Infogold to act on behalf of Infogold in the transaction ([5]–[6]).
3. Whether the relief of specific performance was justified, given the owners' refusal to sell to Basson and the impossibility of enforcing the contract ([10]).

**R – Rules:** 
The rules guiding the court's decision included:
1. Section 2(1) of the Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981, which dictates the necessity of a written and signed contract for the sale of land ([9]).
2. Principles surrounding the doctrine of the undisclosed principal, as expressed in *Grossman v Baruch* ([6]), which highlights the requirement for explicit authorization in property transactions.
3. Jurisprudence regarding specific performance, wherein the court may refuse it if the obligation is impossible to perform or if the property has been disposed of to a third party ([10]).

**A – Application:** 
The court meticulously analyzed the facts to determine whether a binding contract existed. It concluded that the insertion of paragraph 18 transformed the initial acceptance into a counter-offer, which was not acknowledged by Basson. As such, the requirements of the Alienation of Land Act were not satisfied for a valid contract to exist ([9]). 

The arguments regarding Infogold's agency were found to be unpersuasive, as no evidence substantiated the claim that Badenhorst acted for Infogold during the sale ([6]–[8]). The court applied the Plascon-Evans rule, affirming that it could not accept inferences that contradicted the evidence on record, reaffirming that Ms Badenhorst was never authorized to sell on behalf of Infogold ([7]). 

Furthermore, the court assessed the impracticality of specific performance, determining that since Infogold had stated its refusal to sell, compliance from Pretoria East Builders was impossible. This finding aligned with established legal precedent, where a court would not enforce specific performance under such circumstances ([10]).

**C – Conclusion:** 
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of the appellants, allowing their appeal against the lower court’s order for specific performance and costs ([11]). The court set aside the previous orders, concluding that no enforceable contract existed between Basson and the appellants, and specific performance was not viable given the circumstances. The implications of this ruling underscore the necessity for clear authorization and compliance with statutory requirements in real estate transactions, reinforcing the legal principles governing contracts for the sale of immovable property.
