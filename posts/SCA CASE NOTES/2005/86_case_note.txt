Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: Clive Thomas Buttner v. Cheryl Ann Buttner

## F – Facts

The case emanates from divorce proceedings initiated by the appellant, Clive Thomas Buttner, against his wife, Cheryl Ann Buttner, in April 2003 ([paras 2-3]). The parties married on July 2, 1977, under an antenuptial contract excluding any community of property. The couple, after years of joint financial management and living in several locations, including New Zealand and Australia, became embroiled in a contentious divorce ([paras 2-4][9-15]). Notably, the appellant, during the marriage, operated a successful business, T & B Log Homes, while the respondent raised their children and managed household affairs ([paras 6-10]). 

The couple acquired various assets, including a house in Brisbane, Australia. Upon the dissolution of their marriage, disputes arose regarding the distribution of these assets and claims made by both parties, particularly concerning the net proceeds from the sale of their former matrimonial home and the respondent's interest in Wanderer Night 20 CC. The appellant claimed redistribution under section 7(3) of the Divorce Act 70 of 1979, while the respondent sought half of the matrimonial home proceeds and maintenance ([paras 3-4][9-10][12-18]).

The trial court ruled in favor of the respondent, resulting in an award of R360,000.00 and token maintenance of R10.00 per month until her death or remarriage ([paras 19-20]). The appellant, dissatisfied with the trial court's decision, appealed ([paras 4, 20]). 

## I – Issues

The key legal questions revolved around:
1. Whether the trial court appropriately applied section 7(3) of the Divorce Act concerning asset redistribution ([paras 20-21]).
2. The correctness of the trial court’s maintenance order and the applicability of the factors outlined in section 7(2) of the Divorce Act ([paras 1, 33-34]).
3. The potential interference of the appellate court in the exercise of discretion by the trial court ([paras 20, 24-25]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal framework includes:

1. **Divorce Act 70 of 1979**: Section 7 deals with the redistribution of assets upon divorce and outlines the criteria for granting relief under subsections (2) and (3). Notably, subsection (3) grants courts the discretion to order asset transfers based on the contributions of a party to the marital estate ([paras 19-20]).
   
2. **Judicial Discretion**: Courts are granted wide discretion in asset redistribution cases. According to *Beaumont v. Beaumont*, the appeal court can interfere only if the trial court misdirected itself with respect to law or fact ([paras 19-20]).

3. **Token Maintenance**: Section 7(2) outlines factors to consider for maintenance orders, including earning capacities and financial needs. The trial court must ascertain the existence of a need for maintenance and the ability to pay by the other party ([paras 33-34]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the trial court had misdirected itself regarding both the claims for redistribution and the maintenance order. 

- **Asset Redistribution**: The trial court's consideration of the respondent's contributions to the estate was flawed. It emphasized the absence of a direct contribution from the respondent to the appellant’s assets without recognizing the joint nature of their financial management throughout the marriage ([paras 21-23]). The appellate court emphasized the evidence indicating that the couple had historically pooled their finances and assets, aligning with their understanding of marital partnerships ([paras 25-27]). The Supreme Court replaced the trial court's focus on misconduct and misdirected acceptance of limited contributions with a broader perspective on equity and shared contributions ([paras 26-30]).

- **Token Maintenance Order**: The Supreme Court scrutinized the token maintenance award, asserting that while courts can award token maintenance, it should reflect the recipient’s financial needs and the circumstances of both parties ([paras 34-36]). Given the respondent's age and lack of employment history, the maintenance order needed reevaluation, considering the substantial figure of R218,000 secured by the respondent from the appellant for equitable distribution while maintaining a reasonable support measure.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the initial ruling of the trial court was fundamentally flawed and upheld the appeal ([para 43]). The court substituted the maintenance order and ordered the appellant to pay R218,000.00 to the respondent, maintaining the token maintenance of R10.00 per month. Importantly, the judgment reaffirmed that equitable principles take precedence over any focus on marital misconduct regarding asset redistribution ([paras 42-43]). 

Overall, the ruling emphasizes the importance of equal sharing in marriages, no matter the form of financial contribution, and sets a significant precedent regarding adjudicating divorce settlements under South African law.
