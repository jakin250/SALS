Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

### Case Note: RH v DE (594/2013) [2014] ZASCA 133

---

#### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts**  
The case originated from an action for damages instituted by the respondent (plaintiff) against the appellant (defendant) in the North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria. The plaintiff's claim rested on the allegation that the defendant had engaged in adultery with the plaintiff's wife, referred to here as Ms H. The plaintiff and Ms H were married on 30 April 2005 and had two children. Ms H left the marital home on 23 March 2010, and a divorce was granted in September 2011 ([1]–[3]).

The plaintiff contended that the adulterous relationship commenced before Ms H left the marital home. In contrast, the defendant and Ms H argued that their relationship developed after she had removed herself from the home and officially after her divorce action had been initiated ([3]–[4]). The trial included testimonies from both parties and extensive evidence was recorded, comprising records exceeding 1,200 pages ([3]).

In the High Court, damages were awarded under two headings: "contumelia" (insult or injury to self-esteem) and "loss of consortium" (loss of spouse's companionship). The court awarded a total of R75,000 in damages ([2]).

**I – Issues**  
The key legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal revolved around the validity and continuance of the delictual claim for adultery, specifically addressing:
1. Whether contumelia was rightly awarded.
2. Whether the claim for loss of consortium was justified.
3. The relevance of the historical context and modern societal norms regarding adultery claims in South Africa ([15]–[16]).

**R – Rules**  
This case drew extensively from principles of delict and common law, examining:
- The actio iniuriarum as it relates to adultery and its applicability for damages.
- The historic grounding and evolution of the claim for adultery from English common law, notably the principles established in **Viviers v Kilian** [1927] AD 449 and **Wiese v Moolman** [2009] 3 SA 122.
- The legal principles regarding wrongfulness under the South African Constitution (s 39(2)) and public policy considerations ([17]–[26]).

In terms of the legal status of the action for adultery, historic context was considered relevant, including judgments that highlighted the action's origins in perceptions of marriage as a proprietary relationship for husbands and the inherent implications for both men and women ([20]–[21]).

**A – Application**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the factual findings of the High Court while expressing general reluctance to interfere with such findings unless there's a definitive misdirection. The appeal court assessed the merits of the adultery claim purely on the basis of contumelia and loss of consortium, concluding that:
- The claim for contumelia could stand as the domestic sphere allowed for emotional distress claims ([10]–[15]).
- However, the claim for loss of consortium did not hold weight since, by the time the alleged adultery occurred, the plaintiff had indicated his marriage was irretrievably broken ([14]).

The court assessed the public interest in maintaining the action for adultery, which would protect marriage institutions versus the historic precedent that has been gradually reconsidered in light of modern societal norms. The court reflected on comparative law approaches from different jurisdictions (such as England) which had long since abolished similar claims.

Ultimately, the court concluded that the rationale for the action no longer aligned with the current societal context, leading to the assertion that the action for adultery—specifically as it supported claims for curtailing personal freedoms and escalating litigation—is no longer justifiable ([40]–[41]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, emphasizing that:
1. The previous award for loss of consortium was inappropriate and therefore set aside, concluding  it had no basis under the current marital circumstances.
2. The action for adultery, which once had a significant position in South African law, has been found outdated in the context of contemporary principles of marriage and human relationships ([41]).

The court declared the action based on adultery no longer wrongful and eliminated its availability under South African common law. This determination contributes to ongoing discourse concerning the evolving nature of legal structures in response to societal change and aligns with broader movements towards the reform of family law principles ([42]).

The outcome emphasized an emerging necessity for legal frameworks that resonate with present-day societal values rather than ones anchored in obsolete traditions, affirming a prospective approach to marital relations in contemporary South Africa. This decision signals possible reforms that may extend to other areas of familial law, reprioritizing the emphasis on personal agency and rights over traditional property notions in intimate relationships.
