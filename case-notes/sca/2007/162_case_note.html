FIRAC Case Note: Be Bop A Lula Manufacturing & Printing CC v Kingtex Marketing (Pty) Ltd [2007] SCA 162 RSA

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007 2002 (4) SA 397

FIRAC Case Note: Be Bop A Lula Manufacturing & Printing CC v Kingtex Marketing (Pty) Ltd [2007] SCA 162 RSA

F - Facts ([1]–[8])

This case is an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal concerning a rejection of a defence based on a claimed compromise. Be Bop A Lula Manufacturing & Printing CC ("the appellant") placed an order for 60,000 T-shirts with Kingtex Marketing (Pty) Ltd ("the respondent"). After partial delivery, a dispute arose regarding the quality and timely delivery of the T-shirts. The appellant sent a letter and a cheque marked "full and final settlement of account" amounting to R107,196.89, along with a demand for a credit of R122,649.18 for the defective products.

The cheque was sent on 19 February 2002 but was post-dated to 28 February 2002. The respondent's employee deposited the cheque for special clearance without the director’s knowledge, and the cheque was cleared on 28 February 2002. On 1 March 2002, the respondent’s attorneys communicated their rejection of the offer of compromise, advising the appellant to stop payment, but the payment could not be stopped due to its clearance. The respondent directed that the proceeds be held in trust pending further actions.

I - Issues ([9])

The primary legal issue concerns whether the cheque sent by the appellant constituted an offer of compromise, and if the deposit and retention of the cheque's proceeds by the respondent constituted acceptance of that compromise. This raises questions regarding offer and acceptance in contract law, specifically whether an agreement was reached between the parties.

R - Rules ([9]-[13])

The applicable legal rules revolve around the principles of contract formation, particularly regarding offers and acceptances. According to South African contract law principles, an offer must be clear and unequivocal, and acceptance of an offer can be inferred from conduct, including retention of payment. The court noted that for a compromise to be valid, the parties must have a consensus on all contractual requirements, as seen in established precedents (e.g., ABSA Bank Ltd v Van de Vyver NO 2002 (4) SA 397 (SCA); Sonap Petroleum SA (Pty) Ltd v Pappadogianis 1992 (3) SA 234 (A)).

The inscription "full and final settlement of account" on the cheque was scrutinized in relation to the surrounding correspondence to ascertain if it was indicative of an intention to compromise. According to case law, acceptance of an offer cannot be conditional, which plays a crucial role in determining whether the appellant's offer was accepted upon deposit of the cheque ([10]–[14]).

A - Application ([11]-[14])

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) focused on the intention discernible from the documents exchanged and the parties' actions. The court determined that the letters accompanying the cheque—Credit Request and Final Reconciliation—were framed in a way that indicated an offer which the respondent could either accept or reject. The use of "full and final settlement of account" suggested an offer of compromise.

Moreover, while the credit request indicated that the appellant acknowledged a specific financial liability, it was also perceived as a proposal to settle the overall dispute, which would create binding legal relations if accepted.

The court concluded that by depositing the cheque, the respondent had accepted the offer of compromise to settle the outstanding balance. Retaining the funds while attempting to add conditions to that acceptance was inappropriate; therefore, the court found that the respondent should have returned the funds sorted as being bound by the terms of the cheque's deposit.

Opposing actions by the appellant after being informed that stopping the cheque was impossible pointed to a reasonable reliance on the notion that the compromise was accepted through the cheque's clearance. Thus, despite the claim of non-acceptance from the respondent, the court found that acceptance could be reasonably inferred based on the circumstances ([12]-[14]).

C - Conclusion ([15]-[16])

The SCA upheld the appeal, overruling the previous decisions of the Cape High Court and the trial court, which had dismissed the appellant's defence of compromise. The ruling underscored the importance of interpreting contractual terms and recognizing offers of compromise even when the parties involved had not explicitly agreed to all terms. The court recognized that while the respondent did not intend to settle fully, their actions indicated acceptance, thereby forming a legally binding agreement.

The SCA's order was to dismiss the respondent's claims against the appellant, clarifying the implications for similar contract disputes and ensuring that companies recognize the significance of their payment terms and actions regarding compromise offers. The appeal was upheld with costs, highlighting the legal precedent set regarding the interpretation of cheque inscriptions and acceptance in the context of contractual disputes ([16]).
