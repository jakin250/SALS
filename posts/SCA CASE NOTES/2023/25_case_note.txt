# FIRAC Case Note: The Thaba Chweu Rural Forum & Others v The Thaba Chweu Local Municipality and others

**Neutral Citation:** The Thaba Chweu Rural Forum & Others v The Thaba Chweu Local Municipality and others [2023] ZASCA 25 (14 March 2023)  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case Number:** 737/2021  
**Judges:** Molemela, Nicholls, and Mothle JJA, and Mali and Siwendu AJJA  
**Date Heard:** 15 November 2022  
**Date Delivered:** 14 March 2023

---

## F – Facts

The core of this litigation arises from a challenge by the Thaba Chweu Rural Forum (the appellants) against the Thaba Chweu Local Municipality (the first respondent) regarding the validity of municipal rates applied to agricultural properties within the municipality's jurisdiction. The municipality had levied rates on farm properties from 1 July 2009 to 1 June 2017, despite a historical understanding that farm properties were excluded from municipal ratings pre-1994 ([1]–[2]).

In anticipation of these rates, the appellants established the Thaba Chweu Rural Forum in 2008, aimed at representing farmers in engagements with the municipality over municipal rates levied on agricultural land. Many farmers began contesting these charges from their inception in July 2009 ([3]). 

The legal framework supporting municipal rates is primarily the Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004. This Act allows municipalities to impose rates on property but mandates compliance with specific procedures, including public consultation and adherence to established percentage caps ([4]–[5]).

The appellants argued that between 2009 and 2017, the municipality failed to comply with the provisions of the Rates Act. Specifically, they cited the failure to consult the community as prescribed, excessive rates levied above the statutory cap of 25% compared to residential properties, and the absence of proper documentation concerning the valuation of properties ([6]–[12]).

Following an application for relief in the High Court in 2017, the appellants successfully received some documentation but could not secure clear validation concerning specific agricultural classifications. After disputes and a lengthy process, the High Court ruled against the appellants' challenge, leading to their appeal before the Full Court on grounds of constitutional invalidity and failure of process on the part of the municipality ([13]–[14]).

---

## I – Issues

The appeal raised several critical legal questions surrounding the validity of the municipal rate notices issued by the Thaba Chweu Local Municipality:

1. **Legality of Rate Notices:** Whether the municipal rate notices issued between July 2009 and June 2017 should be declared invalid due to non-compliance with the Rates Act ([2]).
   
2. **Remedies under Section 172 of the Constitution:** What just and equitable order should be made pursuant to section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution, especially in light of the delay in the appellants’ action ([17]).
   
3. **Entitlement to Interdictory Relief:** Whether the appellants were entitled to the interdictory relief they sought against the municipality, given the circumstances surrounding the imposition of the rates ([2]).

---

## R – Rules

The two primary sources of law providing the framework for this case are:

1. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996:** Particularly, section 151(1), which mandates the establishment of municipalities and section 172 regarding the power of courts to declare any law or conduct inconsistent with the Constitution as invalid ([2], [17]). 

2. **Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004:** This Act governs the procedures and limitations related to the levying of property rates, including specific provisions for agricultural properties, such as the 25% cap on rates in relation to residential properties ([4], [5]).

The judgments of the Constitutional Court and other precedent cases, particularly *South African Property Owners Association v Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality* ([20]), provided additional guidance on handling invalidity and the implications of retrospective judgments.

---

## A – Application

In applying these rules to the facts of the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal assessed the municipality's compliance with the Rates Act. The court acknowledged the respondents' admissions that their actions regarding levying rates were unlawful, thereby supporting the appellants' claims ([12], [18]).

The court then evaluated the just and equitable remedies available under section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution. The approach taken considered the undue delay in launching the litigation but recognized the repeated unlawful conduct of the municipality and the public interest in food security. The court ultimately concluded that the illegal overcharging of agricultural properties could not be ignored simply due to procedural delay, and thus, the interests of justice demanded that the delay be overlooked, as there were compelling factors necessitating vindication of the legality principle ([31], [35]).

The court scrutinized each financial year's budgets to determine the lawful amounts due, noting the principle that unlawful actions of state entities must have consequences, reinforcing the constitutional mandate that adherence to law is critical for governance ([37]).

---

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal was successful, and the previous decision of the High Court was set aside. The court declared the rate notices issued by the Thaba Chweu Local Municipality invalid regarding agricultural properties and clarified the conditions under which the municipality could recover rates going forward, emphasizing the legal caps on rate imposition ([40]).

This ruling reinforced the importance of compliance with statutory requirements regarding public participation in the establishment of local government rates, affirming the principle that governmental actions must align with legal standards to promote equity and justice in local governance. Furthermore, it upholds the principle that delay alone, in the absence of other substantial justification, should not impede the rectification of unlawful conduct by a municipal authority ([36]).

The case signifies a crucial moment for accountability within local municipalities, underscoring the judiciary's role in ensuring legal adherence in the governance of public resources and services.
