**Case Note: Cape Explosive Works Ltd & AECI Ltd v Denel (Pty) Ltd & Others (Case No: 60/99)**

---

**FIRAC Case Note**

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])**  
The case revolves around an appeal from a judgment of the Transvaal Provincial Division, which centered on the binding effect of certain conditions registered in a title deed concerning immovable properties, which were subsequently omitted in later title deeds. The properties in question were sold by Cape Explosive Works Ltd ("Capex") to the Armaments Development and Production Corporation—now known as Armscor—under specific contractual conditions. These conditions included a restriction on the use of the properties for armament development and a right of repurchase by Capex should the properties no longer be needed for those purposes ([para 2]).

Armscor transferred the properties to Denel (Pty) Ltd, during which the critical conditions attached to the properties were omitted in all subsequent transfers and title deeds. A dispute arose between Capex and Denel regarding Capex's alleged entitlement to repurchase if the properties were no longer used as stipulated. It led Capex to seek rectification of the title deeds to reinstate the omitted conditions ([paras 6]).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1, 7])**  
The primary legal issue concerns whether conditions embedded in the original deed of transfer but omitted in subsequent deeds have legal effect and are binding on the new owner of the properties, Denel. Additionally, the case presents a question on whether the conditions initially created real rights or personal rights that may or may not be enforceable due to their omission from registered deeds ([para 6], [para 7]).

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 8–12])**  
The court referenced the Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937, particularly Section 63(1), which states that only conditions that restrict ownership rights can be registered ([para 8]). The court also examines the nature of real rights versus personal rights, drawing on prior cases such as **Erlax Properties (Pty) Ltd v Registrar of Deeds** and **Schwedhelm v Hauman** to delineate the requirements for a binding real right, which includes the intention to bind successors and the imposition of a substantive limitation on ownership rights. 

Furthermore, the legal framework established by **Cunningham v Karim** is significant in understanding the implications of registration errors in the context of property law ([paras 11–12]).

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 13–26])**  
The court found that Capex had executed the original transfer intending to create binding conditions that affect successors in title, thus establishing these conditions as real rights protectable by registration ([paras 15–16]). Despite later omissions in subsequent deeds, these rights were deemed not extinguished. 

In examining Denel's claims, the court concluded that the relationship between conditions 1 and 2, and their linkage to each property transfer, indicated an inseparability that must be sustained despite the errors of the conveyancer ([paras 13–14], [para 18]).

The court also addressed Denel's arguments related to the personal nature of condition 2, clarifying that while condition 2 does involve obligations, it concurrently operates as a right for Capex that arises when the properties are no longer utilized as specified, thereby not undermining its character as a real right ([paras 19–20]).

In resolving the case, the court emphasized the principle that registrable real rights provide security against third-party claims, affirming Capex's right to remedy the registration error to restore the original restrictive conditions ([paras 20–26]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 27–28])**  
The court ultimately held that the omission of conditions 1 and 2 from subsequent title deeds did not extinguish Capex's real rights. Therefore, the appeal was upheld, and the court ordered the rectification of the title deeds to reinstate the conditions, affirming their binding character on Denel and subsequent owners. The judgment reinforces the doctrine of negative registration systems in property law, emphasizing that bona fide purchasers may not derive absolute security from unregistered interests under such a system ([para 27]).

In summary, this case illustrates critical aspects of property law, particularly concerning the recognition and enforcement of real rights against the backdrop of registration errors, demonstrating the enduring nature of contractual obligations embodied within real rights despite administrative oversights. The ruling has significant implications for property transactions and the responsibilities of conveyancers in ensuring that all contractual elements are accurately reflected in official registries.
