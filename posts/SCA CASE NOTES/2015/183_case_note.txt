Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: The Isibaya Fund v Visser & another (20278/14) [2015] ZASCA 183

## F – Facts
The appellant, The Isibaya Private Equity Fund (the Fund), initiated litigation against the first and second respondents, Ernstus Jacobus Visser and Vaughn Coetzee, asserting that they derelicted their fiduciary duties as directors of the holding company, Lesiba Healthcare Holdings (Pty) Ltd, later known as the Carewell Group of South Africa (Pty) Ltd. The Fund had invested R35 million for a 25% equity stake in the holding company and provided an additional R35 million as a loan. By 2001, the holding company was dormant, failing to repay the loan and lacking any operational activity. The company was eventually wound up on January 14, 2005, after which the Fund sought personal liability from the respondents under section 424 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 ([para 1]–[6]).

The respondents filed a special plea, claiming that the Fund’s action was time-barred under section 11(d) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969. The Gauteng Division of the High Court (Preller J) upheld this plea, prompting the Fund to appeal ([para 3]).

The Fund argued initially on three grounds: (a) the prescriptive period applicable was 15 years, as the debt was owed to the State; (b) it only gained knowledge necessary to formulate a claim post a section 417 inquiry in May 2006; (c) a written acknowledgment of debt interrupted the running of prescription. Eventually, the Fund conceded its second and third arguments, focusing solely on whether it qualified as 'the State' under section 11(b) of the Prescription Act, impacting the prescriptive period applicable to its claim ([para 7]–[9]).

## I – Issues
The core legal issue before the court was whether the Isibaya Fund could be classified as 'the State' as contemplated in section 11(b) of the Prescription Act, which would grant it a 15-year prescriptive period instead of the standard three years prescribed under section 11(d) of the Act. This determination pivoted on the interpretation of the term 'State' within the context of the Prescription Act ([paras 1, 10]).

## R – Rules
Section 11(d) of the Prescription Act stipulates a three-year prescription period for claims that are not explicitly extended to 15 years under section 11(b) for debts owed to the State. For reference, section 424 of the Companies Act outlines the responsibility of individuals who carry on a company's business recklessly or with intent to defraud creditors ([para 1]). 

The most pertinent judicial precedent in this case was the determination made by the Supreme Court of Appeal in **Holeni v Land and Agricultural Development Bank of South Africa** 2009 (4) SA 437 (SCA) ([para 10]), wherein it was held that the Land Bank did not fall under the definition of 'the State’ despite its public entity status.

## A – Application
The court applied statutory interpretation principles, focusing on the context surrounding the definition and application of 'the State' in the relevant Act. It noted that while the Isibaya Fund is governed by the Public Investment Corporation Act 23 of 2004 and engaged in financial activities, it was still a juristic person operating independently and distinct from government. The Fund had the authority to conduct its own business, separate from the public service sphere, lacking many of the characteristics typically associated with state entities.

The court highlighted that the Fund’s operational framework and independence mirrored that of the Land Bank in the **Holeni** case, ruling that the Fund does not qualify as the State under section 11(b) of the Prescription Act. Consequently, the shorter prescriptive period of three years applied. As the Fund's claim was initiated more than three years after the relevant cause of action arose, it was therefore prescribed ([paras 11, 12]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Fund's appeal on the basis that it did not meet the statutory criteria to be considered 'the State' for purposes of the Prescription Act. The court's ruling upheld the Gauteng Division's finding on the applicability of the three-year prescription period, ultimately concluding that the Fund's claims had lapsed. The implications of this decision reaffirmed the necessity for claimants to be vigilant and act within the time limits established by law, highlighting the clear demarcations between public entities and private juristic persons in the context of legal prescriptions ([para 12]). 

The order included a dismissal of the appeal with costs, reflecting the court's position on the merits of the case ([Order]).
