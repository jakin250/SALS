# FIRAC Case Note: Unitrans Passenger (Pty) Ltd t/a Greyhound Coach Lines v The Chairman of the National Transport Commission

## F – Facts

The appeals before the Supreme Court of Appeal stem from two different applicants—Unitrans Passenger (Pty) Ltd, trading as Greyhound Coach Lines (the "Appellant" in Case No: 403/97), and Transnet Ltd (the "Appellant" in Case No: 415/97). They challenge decisions linked to their objections against an application for a road carrier permit submitted by Interkaap Ferreira Busdien (“Interkaap”) to the National Transport Commission (NTC) ([1]).

Prior to 1 May 1996, both the NTC and Local Road Transportation Boards had concurrent jurisdiction over the awarding of road carrier permits. Following Proclamation R22 on 1 May 1996, the NTC was divested of its authority to award the relevant type of permit, thereby centralizing the permit issuance to Local Road Transportation Boards ([2]).

In particular, on 18 March 1996, Interkaap lodged its application with the NTC according to § 12 of the Road Transportation Act 74 of 1977 (the "Act"). The NTC acknowledged this application on 3 May 1996 by publishing it in the Government Gazette ([3]). Greyhound opposed the application, formally objecting on 23 May 1996 ([5]). The scheduled hearing for Interkaap’s application was postponed multiple times, with Greyhound being actively involved in the proceedings. Eventually, Greyhound sought an interdict against the NTC from making a decision regarding Interkaap's application, asserting that the NTC lacked the jurisdiction necessary to continue hearings due to the changes in law brought by Proclamation R22 ([7][9]).

The NTC, however, did not cease its actions concerning Interkaap's application, leading to Greyhound's appeal against the decisions made by Mynhardt J in the court below, which dismissed Greyhound's review application ([9]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented in the appeals revolve around:

1. Whether a pending application for a road carrier permit is affected by newly introduced legislative amendments that terminate the NTC's authority to adjudicate such applications and, if so, the impact on ongoing applications ([1]).
  
2. The determination of whether Interkaap's application was indeed "pending" at the effective date of the new legislation ([2]).

## R – Rules

The applicable legal principles concern the interpretation of amending statutes, particularly their retroactive effect:

1. The established principle holds that a statute is not to be construed as having retrospective application unless it explicitly states so or such intention arises unequivocally from legislative intent ([11]).
  
2. The distinction between true retrospectivity and a weaker form of retrospective effect is critical, specifically relating to whether existing rights are affected by amendments to the law ([12]).
  
3. The principle stemming from *Bell v Voorsitter van die Rasseklassifikasieraad* 1968 (2) SA 678 (A) elucidates that amendments do not typically apply to actions initiated before the statute took effect unless the legislation expressly states otherwise ([18]).

Furthermore, the principle derived from *Minister of Public Works v Haffejee NO* 1996 (3) SA 745 (A) asserts that procedural changes should not nullify previously initiated procedures unless indicated by the legislature ([21]).

## A – Application

In the application of these rules to the facts of the case, Olivier JA emphasized that there was no clear indication from the legislature's amendments that pending applications should be terminated. The absence of provisions for the reassignment of pending applications, the lack of fee repayment mechanisms, and the impracticality of applying new procedural standards to ongoing matters affirmed the notion that the amending legislation did not apply retroactively to nullify the NTC's adjudication of Interkaap’s application ([23]).

The court further rejected the argument that an application only becomes pending upon formal acknowledgement by the NTC. Instead, it ascertained that the application, upon lodging, constituted a pending application as per the provisions of the Act ([25]). Consequently, the amending statute's application to Interkaap’s application was deemed inappropriate and unjust.

## C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed both appeals. It held that the amending legislation was not applicable to pending applications before the NTC, allowing the NTC to proceed as if the amendment had not been enacted. This decision reaffirms the principle that legislation affecting procedural rights must be evaluated contextually, upholding the integrity of applications that were validly submitted prior to legislative changes. The implications of this ruling underscore the need for clarity in legislative drafting, particularly concerning the retroactive effect of amendments on ongoing legal processes ([24][26]).

---

This case establishes an important precedent regarding the treatment of pending applications in the face of legislative amendments and highlights the significance of legislative intent in determining the viability of administrative processes.
