# FIRAC Case Note: Sappi Cape Kraft (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner for Inland Revenue Case No. 348/94

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 2–4])

The appellant, Sappi Cape Kraft (Proprietary) Limited, operates a paper manufacturing business in Milnerton, Cape Province. During the relevant period, Sappi was registered as a vendor under the Sales Tax Act 103 of 1978, which was later repealed. The company imported and purchased various materials, notably "screen plates," "under and top wires," and "wet and dry felts" for its manufacturing operations. Initially, there were several disputed items regarding tax assessments raised by the respondent, the Commissioner for Inland Revenue. However, by this appeal, the focus had narrowed to whether the assessments for sales tax for the years 1983 to 1987 for specific goods were valid.

Following a tax inspection, the respondent issued assessments, which Sappi objected to; these objections were subsequently disallowed. The case was escalated to the Income Tax Special Court, which upheld Sappi's objections regarding certain transactions involving top and under wires while affirming the assessments concerning screen plates and wet felts. Sappi's appeal to the Cape Provincial Division resulted in a partial victory, as the court upheld the appeal for dry felts but dismissed it for wet felts and agreed with the ruling against top and under wires. Sappi now sought a final determination from the Supreme Court, specifically regarding whether the purchases of wet felts, screen plates, and under and top wires were exempt from sales tax.

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 4–5])

The central legal issue in this appeal is whether the purchases of wet felts, screen plates, and under and top wires by Sappi were exempt from sales tax under the provisions of the Sales Tax Act. This involves determining whether these items constitute "parts or materials" that qualify for exemption under section 6 of the Act and whether they fall under any specified non-qualifying goods categories.

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 6–10])

The legal framework governing this case is primarily derived from the now-repealed Sales Tax Act 103 of 1978, particularly section 6, which delineated circumstances under which sales tax would not be applicable. Schedule 2 of the Act listed specific exempt categories for manufacturing enterprises, with particular emphasis on paragraphs pertinent to parts and materials.

One critical rule discussed is the burden of proof established in section 23 of the Act, which requires the claimant to demonstrate entitlement to any exemption from sales tax. Additionally, the court was informed by previous case law, most notably *CIR v Dunlop South Africa Limited*, where the classification of items as "parts" or "tools" was scrutinized concerning taxation.

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 11–22])

The court engaged in a detailed examination of each category of disputed items—wet felts, screen plates, and under and top wires—in the light of the legislative provisions. It was noted that the sales tax exemptions hinged primarily on whether the items were deemed integral to the manufacturing process.

1. **Screen Plates**: The court determined that screen plates, which were affixed to vibrating troughs and directly affected the manufacturing process, clearly fell within the classifications described in section 6(1) and Schedule 2 of the Act. It noted that the Special Court had incorrectly classified them solely as "materials" when they clearly functioned as tools or ancillary equipment, reinforcing their taxable nature under item (3) of the non-qualifying goods.

2. **Wet Felts**: Similar reasoning applied to the wet felts, which were integral to the drying process, acting to absorb moisture. The court found that they, too, constituted non-qualifying goods as they met the criteria of being attached to machinery and playing a vital role in altering the goods.

3. **Wires**: Finally, regarding the under and top wires, the court concluded that these materials were necessary components that facilitated the dewatering process in paper formation. The evidence presented made clear that the wires came into direct contact with the slurry and affected the product's characteristics.

In all three cases, the court emphasized that the appellants’ arguments conflated terminology concerning "essential" and "integral" parts, stating that once an item was classified as a "part," further examination into its purported necessity was unwarranted.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 23–22])

The Supreme Court concluded that all contested items—wet felts, screen plates, and under and top wires—were appropriately subjected to sales tax, confirming the assessments made by the Commissioner. The court held that these items do not satisfy the criteria for exemption under the Sales Tax Act, as they fall under non-qualifying goods.

Sappi's appeal was dismissed, with costs awarded in favor of the Commissioner. The implications of this decision underscore the judiciary's position on the definitions of taxable goods within fiscal legislation, reinforcing the importance of statutory classifications in tax assessments.

The case enhances jurisprudential clarity on the interpretation of materials in manufacturing contexts and the applicability of tax exemptions, serving as a reference for future disputes in similar fiscal cases. The decision also reiterates the burdens associated with proving the entitlement to exemptions, thereby influencing how manufacturers contract transactions relating to their operational supplies.
