# FIRAC Case Notes: **Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd v Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd** [2010] ZASCA 50

### F – Facts
The case revolves around the appeals concerning the grant of a prospecting right under the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 ("the Act"). On 8 February 2006, *Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd* (hereafter "Genorah") lodged its application for a prospecting right over five farms, including *Eerstegeluk 322 KT* and *Nooitverwacht 324 KT* in Limpopo Province. The Regional Manager accepted this application on 20 February 2006, and required Genorah to submit an Environmental Management Plan (EMP) by 21 April 2006, which was duly submitted ([paras 1–3]).

Subsequently, on 14 July 2006, *Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd* ("Bengwenyama") applied for the same prospecting rights over the same properties. The Regional Manager also accepted this application ([para 3]). However, on 28 August 2006, the Deputy Director-General of the Department of Minerals and Energy granted the prospecting right to Genorah. This right was formally executed on 12 September 2006 ([paras 4–5]).

After being notified of the grant to Genorah, Bengwenyama, represented by legal counsel, sought to have Genorah's prospecting rights cancelled or suspended, arguing non-compliance with various provisions of the Act, including improper consultation with the Bromwenyama community ([para 5]). The first appellant's (Bengwenyama) application was said to be a community application, reliant on Section 104 of the Act, although this claim was presented only after the fact ([paras 6–7]). The High Court dismissed their application for review, which led to the current appeal ([paras 8–9]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:
1. **Whether the grant of the prospecting right to Genorah was ultra vires** under the provisions of the Act.
2. **Whether the appellants were entitled to appeal** the original decision granting the prospecting right given that they failed to obtain condonation for a late appeal, thus abandoning their internal remedy.
3. **Whether the appellants' application was indeed a community application** under Section 104, thus entitling them to a preferent right ([paras 9–30]).

### R – Rules
The decision relied upon several statutory provisions and legal principles:
- **Section 16 of the Act** pertains to the application process for prospecting rights and requires sufficient compliance from applicants ([para 3]).
- **Section 104** governs community applications for prospecting rights, mandating that such applications must demonstrate community benefit ([paras 6–7]).
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)** provides mechanisms for review and imposes a 180-day time limit within which administrative action can be contested ([para 21]).
- **Section 96 of the Act** outlines the appeals process for those aggrieved by decisions of administrative officials ([para 20]).

### A – Application
The Court found that:
1. The prospecting right, as granted to Genorah, was validly issued. The delegation of authority from the Minister to the Deputy Director-General was not improperly exercised as the latter's decision was recognized as that of the Minister ([paras 9–10]).
2. Bengwenyama’s claim that their application constituted a community application was rejected, noting that it was only raised retrospectively after their original application was processed as a standard application under Section 16 ([paras 17–19]).
3. Critical issues included compliance with procedural fairness and notice protocols. The appellants argued that the Department had not given adequate notice of Genorah's application, failing the consultation requirements imposed by law. This contention was dismissed as no meaningful consultation occurred pre-application ([paras 11–14]).
4. The High Court's ruling demonstrated prudent administrative justice, recognizing that the appeal brought forward by the appellants was invalid as they had abandoned internal remedies by failing to seek condonation properly. This reasoning was supported by their lack of diligence in adhering to statutory timelines ([paras 24–28]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's decision, denying the appeal and affirming that the appellants had abandoned their internal remedies. The judgment emphasized the importance of adhering to procedural rules established under the relevant statutory framework and validated the actions taken by the Deputy Director-General in granting the prospecting right to Genorah. The Court further noted that the refusal to grant the prospecting right to Bengwenyama was justified as they had not established a prior community application nor followed proper legal channels to contest the initial decision ([para 32]). The implications of this ruling reinforce the need for prospective applicants to rigorously comply with the legal requirements set out in the Act, especially regarding community rights and procedural fairness.
