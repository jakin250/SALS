Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

### FIRAC Case Note: Tatiana Malachi v Cape Dance Academy International (Pty) Ltd and Others [2010] ZACC 13

#### Facts
The case revolves around the legal validity of certain provisions of the Magistrates’ Courts Act 32 of 1944, specifically sections 30(1) and (3), which authorize the arrest of a debtor under suspicions of flight to evade payment. The applicant, Tatiana Malachi, a Moldovan national, was employed as an exotic dancer in South Africa. Upon arrival, her passport was seized by her employers, Cape Dance Academy International (Pty) Ltd and House of Rasputin Properties (Pty) Ltd, who later sought her arrest when they feared she would leave the country to avoid repaying an alleged debt of R100,000 ([paras 1–9]).

Malachi was arrested on July 9, 2009, and detained until July 24, 2009. Following her arrest, she challenged the constitutional validity of the arrest provisions, arguing that they infringed upon her rights to equality (section 9), dignity (section 10), freedom and security of the person (section 12), and freedom of movement (section 21) pursuant to the South African Constitution ([paras 10–14]). The High Court ruled in her favor, declaring the provisions unconstitutional and invalid ([paras 12–42]).

#### Issues
The primary legal issues presented in this case are:
1. **Constitutional Validity**: Whether the arrest tanquam suspectus de fuga as authorized by sections 30(1) and (3) of the Magistrates’ Courts Act infringes Malachi's rights under the Constitution ([paras 13–15]).
2. **Justification of Limitation**: If the arrest is found to limit her rights, is such a limitation justifiable under section 36 of the Constitution, which outlines the conditions for limiting rights in a free and democratic society ([para 36])?
3. **Remedies and Retrospective Effect**: What remedy is appropriate for the constitutional invalidity found, and should the effects of the declaration be retrospective ([paras 44–49])?

#### Rules
The judgment considers numerous constitutional provisions and judicial precedents, including:
1. **Section 12 of the Constitution**: Right to freedom and security of person, specifically prohibiting arbitrary detention ([paras 14–15]).
2. **Section 36 of the Constitution**: Conditions for justifiable limitations on rights, which include the necessity of fairness and proportionality ([para 36]).
3. **Judicial Precedent**: The case draws on historical and contemporary definitions of "just cause" regarding the deprivation of liberty, particularly referencing **De Lange v Smuts NO**, **S v Coetzee**, and **Bid Industrial Holdings** to support the principle that a lawful basis for arrest must exist ([paras 26–32]).

#### Application
In assessing the application of the law, the court examined the nature of the arrest authorized by sections 30(1) and (3). It found that these provisions allowed for the arrest and detention of a debtor based merely on suspicion without establishing any proven liability, thus infringing upon the right to freedom as articulated in section 12(1)(a) of the Constitution ([paras 19-34]). 

The court highlighted that the arrest aims to prevent flight, yet it was ineffective in guaranteeing repayment of the debt due to the absence of a legal mechanism to enforce payment once a civil judgment was granted. Consequently, the arrest was deemed arbitrary and unnecessary. 

Moreover, the court argued that other less invasive remedies, such as applying for an interdict preventing a debtor from leaving the jurisdiction, were available but not considered in the application for arrest ([paras 35–41]).

#### Conclusion
The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's declaration of invalidity, asserting that the provisions of the Magistrates’ Courts Act allowing for arrest tanquam suspectus de fuga are unconstitutional. The court held there was no just cause for the deprivation of Malachi's liberty, and the limitation imposed was unreasonable in a democratic society. The judgment underscored the importance of protecting individual rights against arbitrary state action, echoing the findings of historical case law concerning civil liberties.

The judgment also included orders regarding costs, emphasizing the state’s responsibility to ensure constitutional compliance, resulting in the Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development being ordered to pay costs ([paras 50–54]).
