# FIRAC Case Note: BG Bojosinyane & Associates v Sheriff: Smith and Another (1072/22) [2023] ZASCA 174

## F – Facts
The case arises from a dispute between **BG Bojosinyane & Associates (the appellant)**, a law firm, and **Sheriff Michael Smith (the first respondent)**, who serves the Vryburg Magistrates’ Court. The appellant filed an urgent application in the North-West Division of the High Court, Mahikeng, seeking relief related to the sheriff's practice of demanding upfront payment of fees before serving or executing court processes ([para 2]).

In the founding affidavit, Mr. Bojominyane alleged that Sheriff Smith continuously required advance payment, resulting in delays and hindrances to the administration of justice. He described instances where the sheriff demanded fees before serving documents, which led to unnecessary disputes and delays in legal proceedings ([para 3]). The particular cases cited by the appellant illustrated that each demand for upfront payment caused delays, contrary to the legal principles requiring timely service of court processes ([paras 4–5]).

The high court initially dismissed the application, primarily ruling that the applicant had not demonstrated an urgent need for relief and that it lacked a satisfactory alternative remedy ([para 14]). The court also found that it was unnecessary to address the sheriff's entitlement to demand upfront payments, as the appellant's argument for an interdict was not sufficiently established ([para 14]).

## I – Issues
The principal issues presented before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) included:

1. **Is the sheriff entitled to refuse to serve or execute a court process unless fees and charges are paid upfront, absent magistrate authorization as per s 14(7) of the Magistrates’ Court Act 32 of 1944?**
   
2. **Did the high court properly assess whether the appellant had a clear right and an imminent threat of harm to warrant an interdict?**
   
3. **Was the appellant entitled to interdictory relief, and did the high court err in its assessment of available remedies and the urgency of the matter?** ([para 1, 32])

## R – Rules
The relevant legal framework consists of:

1. **Magistrates’ Court Act 32 of 1944.** Particularly sections 14(7) and (8), which provide conditions under which a sheriff may refuse service based on outstanding fees owed to him ([para 11]).

2. **Magistrates’ Courts Rules**, specifically Rule 8(2), which mandates sheriffs to serve processes without unreasonable delay. It also requires sheriffs to delineate their charges only after service has been effectuated ([para 6]).

3. **Sheriff's Act,** which governs the conduct of sheriffs and delineates their powers and obligations ([para 12]). 

4. **Judicial precedents,** which establish that the law does not allow for sheriffs to withhold service based on speculative future fees, thus promoting access to justice ([para 23]).

## A – Application
The SCA applied the relevant rules, emphasizing that sheriffs, being statutory creatures, possess only those powers explicitly conferred by statute ([para 20]). It found that the legislation does not afford a sheriff the right to demand upfront payments before fulfilling the statutory obligation to serve court processes.

In evaluating the appellant’s claims for an interdict, the court established that:

1. **Clear Right:** The appellant had a legitimate expectation founded on statutory provisions, particularly those mandating timely service of processes ([paras 31–32]).

2. **Imminent Harm:** The ongoing nature of the sheriff's demands posed a real threat of causing further delays, thus constituting ongoing harm which justified the interdictory relief sought ([para 32]).

3. **Lack of Alternative Remedy:** The court rejected the high court's claim that taxation of fees served as an adequate remedy, determining that it did not address the immediate issue of upfront payment and service ([para 33]).

The SCA ultimately concurred that the first respondent’s conduct of insisting on upfront payments violated statutory mandates aimed at ensuring expeditious access to judicial processes. By delaying service and execution of court processes until payment was received, the sheriff not only contravened the principle of timely service but also engaged in self-help, which is inconsistent with the rule of law established in judicial precedent ([paras 24–32]).

## C – Conclusion
The SCA upheld the appeal on the grounds that the sheriff is not entitled to demand advance payment as a condition for serving or executing court processes, reaffirming the principles set forth in statutory law and prior case law ([para 35]). The judgment established clear legal boundaries around the conduct of sheriffs, reinforcing the importance of timely service in the interest of justice. The SCA’s explicit directions frustrate the practices that undermine access to justice and reinforce the integrity of the legal process. Consequently, the appeal was successful, with the respondents directed to share the costs ([para 36]).
