Supreme Court of Appeal

The DPP Western Cape v Bongo (990/2022) [2024] ZASCA 70

Bongo was initially arraigned on one count of corrupt activities regarding public officers and two alternative charges.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 70

F – Facts

In the case of Director of Public Prosecutions, Western Cape v Bongo ([1]–[10]), the appellant, the Director of Public Prosecutions, sought to appeal against a refusal by the Western Cape Division of the High Court to reserve questions of law under Section 319 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA). The respondent, Bongo, was charged with corruption-related offenses under the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004 (the PRECCA). The essence of the charge was that Bongo attempted to induce Mr. Vanara, a public officer, to feign illness to obstruct an inquiry into Eskom.

Bongo was initially arraigned on one count of corrupt activities regarding public officers and two alternative charges. Following the state’s presentation of its case, Bongo sought discharge under Section 174 of the CPA. The trial court discharged him, finding insufficient evidence to support a conviction ([6]–[8]). Subsequently, the state applied to reserve six questions of law for appellate review, which the trial court denied without reasons. Upon petitioning, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) heard the application for leave to appeal ([29]).

I – Issues

The key issues for the SCA included:

  1. What constitutes a "question of law" in the context of reserving questions under Section 319 of the CPA? ([29])
  2. Did the trial court misdirect itself regarding the credibility of witness testimony? ([26])
  3. Were the findings of the trial court in relation to the elements of corruption consistent with the applicable legal standards? ([35])
  4. How should previous consistent statements of the accused be evaluated in relation to evidence presented? ([54])

R – Rules

The legal framework pertinent to this case primarily stems from:

  • Section 319 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which governs the reservation of questions of law from a trial court to the appellate court.
  • Section 174 of the CPA, which permits a court to discharge an accused if there is no evidence upon which a reasonable court could convict ([37]).
  • The Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004, notably Sections 1, 3(b), and 4, which outline the definitions and requirements for establishing the offense of corruption ([5]).

The SCA noted that questions of law must be clearly framed, based on established facts to evaluate their implications accurately ([35]), and that the principles governing the evaluation of evidence dictate that a trial judge should not overly scrutinize witness credibility at the discharge stage unless such credibility is "utterly destroyed" ([38]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the SCA concluded that the trial court made multiple legal errors. It found the trial court's dismissal of Mr. Vanara's testimony flawed, focusing unduly on the absence of a specific payment offer rather than recognizing that the mere attempt to induce corrupt behavior constituted a completed offense of corruption ([42]–[53]).

Specifically:

  • Question 3: The SCA determined that the trial court misinterpreted the law by demanding proof of an explicit arrangement for payment. The court established that an offer of gratuity does not require a formal agreement to be constituted as a crime ([41]–[42]).
  • Question 6: The SCA criticized the trial court’s reliance on the respondent's previous consistent statement, arguing it mischaracterized the statement's evidentiary value and thus erred in accepting its relevance in corroborating the defense position without proper evidential backing ([56]).

Thus, the SCA found that the misunderstanding of the criminal elements at play materially affected the trial's outcome, warranting appellate intervention ([60]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the state regarding the critical questions of law reserved for its determination. The Court ordered that the lower court's discharge of the respondent under Section 174 be set aside and that the matter be remitted for trial de novo before a different court ([61]–[62]).

The implications of this ruling reinforce the necessity for trial courts to apply statutory definitions accurately and to avoid unwarranted credibility assessments that could undermine the prosecution’s case at preliminary stages. This case serves as a precedent on the interpretation of corruption laws and the appropriate assessment of witness credibility in South African jurisprudence.
