Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Citation:** MEC for Health: Eastern Cape v Mbodla (449/2013) [2014] ZASCA 60 (6 May 2014)

### F – Facts

The respondent, Feza Mbodla, sustained injuries in a motor vehicle accident on June 25, 2006, allegedly due to negligent treatment at Nelson Mandela Hospital. He initiated legal proceedings against the Member of the Executive Council for Health in the Eastern Cape Province (the MEC) on November 1, 2011. The MEC raised two special pleas in response: firstly, failure to adhere to the notice requirement within six months as mandated by section 3(1)(a) and section 3(2)(a) of the Institution of Legal Proceedings against certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002 (Legal Proceedings Act), and secondly that the claim had prescribed under section 10(1) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([paras 1–2]).

Following the MEC's plea, Mbodla sought condonation for the late submission of his notice, asserting that actual knowledge of his claim only surfaced after a consultation with an attorney in April 2011, contrary to the MEC's claim that he was aware of the identity of the debtor and the factual basis for his claim from the date of the accident ([para 1]). The High Court granted a declaratory order in favor of Mbodla, leading to the MEC's appeal ([paras 2, 9]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

1. Whether the court below erred in concluding that the requirements for the notice as per the Legal Proceedings Act had been satisfied without the need for oral evidence ([paras 5, 9]).
2. Whether the issue of prescription could be evaluated adequately on the documentary evidence submitted, given the ambiguities and inconsistencies presented in Mbodla's affidavit ([paras 5, 6]).

### R – Rules

The court relied on several legal principles and rules, primarily:

1. **Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002 (Legal Proceedings Act)** - Particularly sections 3(1)(a) and 3(2)(a) concerning the notice of claim and section 3(4) concerning condonation for non-compliance ([paras 1, 2]).
2. **Prescription Act 68 of 1969** - Notably, section 10(1) related to the prescriptive period for claims and the implications of knowledge relating to the claim ([paras 1, 2]).
3. **Uniform Rules of Court**, specifically Rule 6(5)(d)(iii) concerning notices in motion and Rule 6(5)(g), which allows courts to refer matters for oral evidence when necessary to ensure a just decision ([paras 7, 9]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the lower court had inadequately assessed the issues of prescription and statutory compliance purely on affidavit evidence, which was insufficient ([para 6]). The discrepancies in Mbodla's affidavit—particularly in relation to the timing of his knowledge of the claim—required clarification through oral evidence ([para 5]). 

The argument hinged on the fact that if the plaintiff was unsuccessful concerning prescription, his claim would be effectively dismissed. Conversely, if the plea failed, the MEC would need to confront the complexities of exhuming records and identifying witnesses after an extensive period ([para 8]). The court emphasized the need for comprehensive evidence, noting that the documentary evidence did not satisfactorily clarify the context or details surrounding the claim's timeliness ([para 6]).

In light of these considerations, the Court determined that it was appropriate to refer the matter for the hearing of oral evidence to resolve the questions of fact that underpinned the legal issues ([para 9]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the MEC's appeal, setting aside the decision of the High Court and ordering that the matter be referred for oral evidence regarding the issue of prescription and the adequacy of the notice ([para 9]). The court concluded that both the urgency of the matter and the complexities inherent in establishing whether prescription had occurred warranted a hearing where both parties could present their evidence and arguments clearly and thoroughly ([para 9]). This ruling emphasizes the importance of thorough factual evidence in legal proceedings, particularly in instances involving the prescription of claims, thereby ensuring that justice can be effectively administered.
