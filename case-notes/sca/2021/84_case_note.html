Supreme Court of Appeal

The Black Eagle Project Roodekrans v The MEC: Department of Agriculture, Conservation and Environment, Gauteng Provincial Government and Others (Case No. 542/2019) [2021] ZASCA 84

[1] The appellant, The Black Eagle Project Roodekrans (Black Eagle), is a non-profit organization incorporated under Section 21 of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, involved in environmental protection.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 84

F – Facts

[1] The appellant, The Black Eagle Project Roodekrans (Black Eagle), is a non-profit organization incorporated under Section 21 of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, involved in environmental protection. The first respondent, The MEC: Department of Agriculture, Conservation and Environment, Gauteng Provincial Government (MEC), and the second respondent, The Head of Department: Department of Agriculture, Conservation and Environment, are governmental entities responsible for environmental regulation. The third respondent, Landev (Pty) Ltd, is a property developer.

[2] The conflict originated in February 2007 when Black Eagle applied for an urgent interdict against Landev to prevent development on a specific property in Mogale Municipal District, pending a review of the MEC's decision dismissing its appeal against another of the HOD's decisions. The interdict was granted by consent. Over the subsequent years, Black Eagle made several amendments to its founding affidavit and notice of motion but did not challenge the HOD’s original decision until September 2017.

[3] The judicial saga extended for more than 14 years and involved various applications that were subject to principle decisions made by both the HOD and the MEC. Notably, the MEC dismissed Landev’s appeal in 2006 and later upheld an amendment to increase the density of Landev’s proposed development when the HOD’s application was contested by Black Eagle.

[4] The high court finally reviewed the matter in November 2018, affirming the MEC's decision (order a), while also setting aside a specific order by the MEC related to the initial appeal process (order b), and ordering cost apportionment (order c). On 29 April 2019, the high court granted Black Eagle leave to appeal against orders a and c.

I – Issues

[5] The central legal issues in this case stem from administrative law, specifically:

  • Whether Black Eagle's failure to timeously file a review against the HOD's decision constitutes grounds for dismissing the appeal against the MEC’s ruling.
  • The implications of administrative delay as articulated under sections 7 and 9 of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) and the necessity for compliance with statutory timelines.
  • Whether the principle established in Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town and subsequent cases are applicable, thereby affecting the validity of the HOD’s original decision standing until overturned by a court.

R – Rules

[6] The statutory framework that governs this case is primarily encapsulated within the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 2000. Section 7 emphasizes the need for timely judicial review applications (180 days post the impugned decision), while Section 9 allows for the extension of this period if deemed just by a court.

[7] The Oudekraal principle stipulates that administrative actions remain valid unless set aside by a judicial authority. This principle has been reiterated in subsequent cases such as Wings Park v Port Elizabeth (Pty) Ltd and Sewpersadh v The Minister of Finance, which clarifies that applicants must generally challenge both the original decision and any subsequent appellate decision to effectively seek relief.

A – Application

[8] The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) held that Black Eagle's failure to independently challenge the HOD’s decision fundamentally invalidated its appeal against the MEC’s ruling. Black Eagle sought to review the MEC's appellate decision without addressing the validity of the HOD's prior decision. The SCA reasoned that, in line with Oudekraal, the HOD's decision remains enforceable unless judicially annulled.

[9] Black Eagle had several opportunities over the years to challenge the HOD's decision but did not do so in a timely manner. Highlighting the significance of procedural compliance, the court referred to Section 7(1) of PAJA, noting that Black Eagle failed to submit its review application within the stipulated 180-day timeframe. Without justifiable cause for such a delay or a condonation application made to the court to extend this timeframe, the appeal could not succeed.

[10] The SCA further distinguished the precedent cited by Black Eagle from Sewpersadh, emphasizing that this case involved different circumstances where all facts were available to facilitate a remittal, which was not applicable here due to the significant lapse of time and changes in the regulatory landscape. Consequently, the court found that granting relief to Black Eagle would not remedy the underlying administrative governance precluded by the absence of the HOD's decision being set aside.

C – Conclusion

[11] The SCA concluded that given the procedural deficiencies in Black Eagle's challenge, the appeal against order (a) of the high court was dismissed, as the MEC's decision stood unchecked due to the failure to contest the HOD's ruling. Thus, the court upheld the reasoning that administrative actions remain valid until successfully contested in court, illustrating the importance of compliance with statutory procedures in administrative law.

[12] The court ordered that the dismissal of the appeal includes costs, notably considering the involvement of two counsel for the third respondent, Landev. This outcome underscores the legal obligation for parties wishing to contest administrative decisions to act within designated time limits and procedural frameworks to preserve their rights for judicial review.
