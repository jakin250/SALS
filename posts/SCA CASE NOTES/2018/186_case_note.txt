Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: **Stow v Regional Magistrate PE NO & Others; Meyer v Cooney NO & Others** [2018] ZASCA 186

### F - Facts

In this matter, represented in the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, two separate appeals were consolidated for consideration. The first appellant, Andrew Walter Stow, and the second appellant, Jacobo Stephanus Meyer, were respectively convicted and sentenced in previous criminal cases involving financial misconduct termed 'white collar crime' ([1]). Both appellants were sentenced to five years' imprisonment, wholly suspended under certain conditions pursuant to section 297(1)(b) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the CPA). These conditions included the prohibition against committing similar offenses and the requirement to repay certain amounts to their respective complainants ([1]).

Subsequent to their convictions, both appellants failed to comply with the repayment conditions. Stow had an outstanding debt to the South African Revenue Service amounting to R513,060.77 ([5]), while Meyer was involved in a micro-lending scheme with outstanding repayments totaling R5,278,569 to various investors ([14]). Consequently, the regional court activated their suspended sentences due to non-compliance, leading both men to seek a review of this decision through the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court on the grounds that the magistrate had misused their discretion ([2], [12]). Ultimately, both review applications were unsuccessful, prompting the appellants to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

### I - Issues

The fundamental legal questions presented to the court included:

1. Whether the decision of the regional magistrate to activate the suspended sentences was subject to review or appeal ([22]).
2. Was the court a quo correct in dismissing the review application based on the grounds presented by the appellants ([22])?

Furthermore, the appeals necessitated an examination of the constitutionality of the review process applicable to the activation of suspended sentences, particularly the interpretation of section 309 of the CPA ([22], [32]).

### R - Rules

Essential legal principles guiding the court's analysis included:

- **Section 297 of the CPA**: Providing for the conditional suspension of sentences. In particular, subsections (1)(b) and (7) elaborate on conditions under which a sentence may be suspended and the powers of review regarding the activation of such sentences ([3]).
- **Section 309 of the CPA**: Asserting that any person convicted by a lower court may appeal against such conviction and resultant sentences ([26]).
- **Judicial Precedents**: The court cited previous rulings that established the nature of appeals vs. reviews, noting that earlier rulings maintained that decisions to activate suspended sentences were generally subject to review, not appeal ([28], [39]). This rationale derived from the interpretation of ‘resultant order’ in the CPA, suggesting that the activation does not constitute a new sentence but rather a consequence of the original sentencing ([30]).

Additionally, decisions from earlier cases such as *S v Helm* and *Gasa v Magistrate for the Regional Division of Natal* provided context on the discussion regarding appealable versus reviewable decisions in the realm of suspended sentences ([26], [40]).

### A - Application

In applying these legal principles, the court examined the process by which the magistrate activated the suspended sentences:

- **Discretionary Power**: The court observed that the regional magistrate exercised the discretionary power conferred by section 297(7) judiciously. The magistrate considered evidence presented by both Stow and Meyer detailing their inability to comply with the payment order, ultimately determining that neither presented sufficient grounds for further suspension, particularly given the appellants had previously assured the court of their capacity to comply ([11], [21]).
  
- **Non-compliance Justifications**: The court highlighted that while Mr. Stow provided reasons for non-payment—ranging from personal financial difficulties to business disruptions—these were not collectively seen as compelling enough to warrant the decision to further suspend the sentence ([10]). Mr. Meyer's explanation regarding the sale of his business was scrutinized, revealing a lack of disclosure and a failure to provide legally sound justifications for non-compliance with suspension conditions ([19]).

- **Role of the Court**: The court determined that the regional court's decision reflected proper judicial discretion, reaffirming that it was not bound to accept the appellants' justifications. It emphasized that the principle of justice must consider the overall fairness to all parties involved, including the complainants ([12], [21]).

The court effectively reinforced the notion that judicial determinations regarding the activation of suspended sentences must balance the rights of the accused against the need for accountability and justice for victims.

### C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that both appeals by Mr. Stow and Mr. Meyer lacked merit and upheld the decision of the court a quo to dismiss their applications for review. The court confirmed that the activation of a suspended sentence is a non-appealable order, and the regional court's decision reflected a proper exercise of discretion respecting the conditions set forth under the CPA. As such, the order of the court a quo was upheld, and the appeals were dismissed ([35]).

The implications of this decision are significant: it clarifies that decisions activating suspended sentences are not appealable and reinforces the necessity for compliance with sentencing conditions. Additionally, it establishes that the judiciary maintains broad discretion under the CPA to ascertain compliance while upholding the principles of justice and accountability within the criminal justice framework ([47]).
