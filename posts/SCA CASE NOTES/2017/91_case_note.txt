Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Fourie v Ronald Bobroff & Partners Inc (653/2016) [2017] ZASCA 91

### FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts
The appellant, Ms. Ursha Yvonne Fourie, retained the services of the respondent, Ronald Bobroff & Partners Inc., to pursue claims for damages arising from a tragic motor vehicle accident that occurred on 27 June 2005 ([1]). The collision resulted in the demise of Mr. Fourie and their daughter, Cayleigh, along with significant injuries to Ms. Fourie and their son, Lincoln ([2]). In August 2005, Ms. Fourie instructed the respondent to pursue claims against the Road Accident Fund (RAF) for loss of support, loss of income, and general damages ([3]).

Legal proceedings commenced with the issuance of summons in August 2007, and following several medico-legal assessments, the case was set down for trial on 1 August 2011 ([4]). Leading up to the trial, the attorney for the appellant, Ms. Philippa Jane Farraj, decided not to pursue the claim for loss of earning capacity, opting instead to accept a settlement offer of approximately R1.777 million from the RAF on 29 July 2011 ([8][9]).

Dissatisfied with the settlement, Ms. Fourie initiated a suit against the respondent on 27 September 2012, claiming breach of mandate for failing to pursue the claim for loss of earning capacity and accepting inadequate compensation for general damages ([10][11]). The Gauteng Local Division, under Weiner J, rejected Ms. Fourie’s claim, prompting her appeal ([12]).

#### I – Issues
The primary legal issue revolved around whether the respondent breached its mandate by failing to adequately pursue the appellant's claim for loss of earning capacity and by improperly settling her claims for general damages ([1][10]). In particular, the court needed to ascertain if the appellant could prove that the breach resulted in quantifiable damages, as mandated under South African common law, which upholds that an attorney must exercise requisite skill and diligence ([13]).

#### R – Rules
The court relied on established legal precedents concerning the obligations of attorneys under contract law, particularly regarding the role of a legal representative in personal injury claims. It was highlighted that an attorney, when acting under a mandate, must apply the knowledge, skill, and diligence expected of an average specialist in the field ([13]).

Case law such as *Mouton v Die Mynwerkersunie* ([13]) was discussed, alongside principles of damages assessment, noting that in instances of breach, a plaintiff must show they would have prevailed had the breach not occurred and that appropriate damages can be quantified based on expert evidence ([23]).

#### A – Application
The court undertook a thorough evaluation of the evidence to determine if Ms. Farraj's actions amounted to a breach of the mandate. The evidence presented showed that although the respondent had a duty to assess and calculate the appellant's potential loss of earnings accurately, Ms. Farraj's decision not to pursue the claim for loss of earning capacity stemmed from approximated assumptions rather than expert evaluations ([21][22]).

Notably, the court stressed that an attorney specialized in personal injury claims, upon reviewing available medico-legal reports, would likely recognize the appellant's employability post-accident ([20]). The medically documented impairment of her earning capacity was not explored, leading to a significant gap in the respondent's duty of care and professional diligence ([22]).

However, despite identifying these failures, when analyzing the overall damages resulting from the alleged breach, the court noted that the appellant had failed to establish a solid mathematical basis for her claims. This failure was exacerbated by her contradictory assertions regarding her capacity to earn, which shifted throughout the litigation ([25][27]). The expert calculations presented by Mr. Whittaker indicated that any increase in potential recovery for loss of earning capacity would not surpass the amount already settled for loss of support ([28]).

Consequently, the court affirmed that the appellant had not sufficiently proven that the settlements accepted were significantly lower than what could have been achieved had the mandate been executed properly ([29]).

#### C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the court varied the initial order from the dismissal of the appellant's claims to one of absolution from the instance, effectively recognizing the breach of mandate by the respondent but further absolving them from liability for damages as the appellant did not prove that she had suffered quantifiable harm as a result of that breach ([30]). The court's ultimate ruling also dismissed the appeal with costs, emphasizing the significance of presenting clear, expert-backed evidence in claims for damages stemming from breaches of legal mandates. The decision underlines the intricate balance between an attorney's duty to act in the client’s best interest and the client's burden of proof concerning consequential damages ([30]).
