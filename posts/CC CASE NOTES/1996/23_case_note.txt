**Case Note: J T Publishing (Pty) Ltd and Eugene Marais v Minister of Safety and Security and Others (1996)**

---

**FIRAC Case Note**

**F – Facts:**

The matter under consideration involves an appeal against the refusal by the Transvaal Provincial Division to refer constitutional questions concerning the legality of certain sections of the Publications Act (42 of 1974) and the Indecent or Obscene Photographic Matter Act (37 of 1967) to the Constitutional Court ([2–5]). The first applicant, J T Publishing, is a publisher of the magazine *Hustler* and other materials which came under scrutiny due to alleged contraventions of the Publications Act ([4]). The second applicant, Eugene Marais, is a franchisee who distributes these publications. Following a police raid that seized merchandise, the applicants sought legal recourse, arguing that the statutes infringed their constitutional rights ([4]).

The applicants initially brought their concerns before the Transvaal Provincial Division, which dismissed their referral application on the grounds that it found no existing case involving the validity of the Acts in question, leading them to seek leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([5–6]). The legal battle highlighted the tension between statutory censorship and constitutional protections, prompting the court's examination of the issue of whether the referral for constitutional interpretation was appropriate ([3–4]).

**I – Issues:**

1. **Competence of Referral**: Whether the issues raised regarding the constitutional validity of the Publications Act and the Indecent or Obscene Photographic Matter Act were within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court, as stipulated by section 102(1) of the Constitution ([6–9]).

2. **Decisiveness of the Issue**: Whether the matters in question were indeed pivotal to the case before the Provincial Division, impacting the applicability of referral provisions ([10–11]).

3. **Separation of Issues**: Whether the court could distinguish between the primary constitutional issues and any ancillary matters present in the lower court's proceedings ([12–13]).

**R – Rules:**

The relevant legal principles are drawn from sections of the South African Constitution, particularly section 102 (1) which allows for the referral of constitutional issues to the Constitutional Court when these issues may decisively influence the case ([6–8]). The court also cited previous cases such as *S v Vermaas* and *Brink v Kitshoff NO*, which illuminate the proper context for such referrals and the interpretation of “issues” as distinct from the broader “matter” ([10–11]). Additionally, the discretion to refuse declaratory relief based on the existence of merely abstract or academic questions has been established within broader legal precedent ([17–19]).

**A – Application:**

The court examined the interpretation of section 102(1), concluding that the refusal to refer was predicated upon a misreading of this section. Didcott J emphasized the need for the court to ensure that all pertinent constitutional issues, regardless of their singular prominence in a case, could be referred if they serve the interests of justice ([9–11]). The court’s analysis highlighted that the issues at stake were directly significant to the applicants’ claims and therefore were pivotal for consideration by the Constitutional Court ([11–13]).

The Applicants were found to fall within the categories able to claim constitutional relief, thus substantiating their right to seek referral under section 7(4) of the Constitution, which protects them against infringements of their rights by binding laws ([7]). 

Furthermore, the court noted that the refusal to acknowledge the referral request had effectively barred the applicants from the constitutional remedy they sought, necessitating correction on appeal to maintain justice and address the ongoing implications of the disputed legislation ([14–18]).

**C – Conclusion:**

The Constitutional Court ultimately held that the referral should have been granted and reversed the decision of the lower court. Consequently, the court allowed the appeal, mandating the referral of constitutional questions regarding the validity of the relevant statutes ([19]). The judgment emphasized the necessity of the Constitutional Court addressing significant constitutional issues that impact public and individual interests, especially in cases of censorship where constitutional rights may be at risk. Notably, due to the recent legislative changes with the enactment of the Films and Publications Act (65 of 1996), the court refrained from issuing a ruling on the constitutional issues since they had become largely academic ([16–18]).

The ruling affirms the critical role of the Constitutional Court in adjudicating matters inherent to upholding constitutional rights against statutory infringements, providing valuable precedent for future cases involving freedom of expression and statutory censorship.
