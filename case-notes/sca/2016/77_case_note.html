Supreme Court of Appeal

Lucky Star Ltd v Lucky Brands (Pty) Ltd (164/2015) [2016] ZASCA 77

The appellant sought an interdict to prevent the respondents from infringing its trademark and requested the first respondent’s company name to be declared confusingly similar to its own registered trademarks ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 77

F – Facts

The appeal concerns Lucky Star Ltd (formerly Oceana Brands Ltd), the appellant, against Lucky Brands (Pty) Ltd and various entities operating restaurants with similar names, who are the respondents (the first to the sixteenth respondents). The appellant is known for selling canned fish under the registered trademark "Lucky Star," while the respondents operate restaurants serving fish and chips under the names "Lucky Fish," "Lucky Fish and Chips," and similar variations ([1]–[2]).

The appellant sought an interdict to prevent the respondents from infringing its trademark and requested the first respondent’s company name to be declared confusingly similar to its own registered trademarks ([2]). The case was precedently addressed by the Western Cape Division of the High Court but was dismissed, prompting the appellant's appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]).

The Court was tasked with interpreting sections 34(1)(a), (b), and (c) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 and Section 11(2) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 regarding the alleged trademark infringements [1].

I – Issues

The primary legal questions presented to the Supreme Court were:

  1. Did the appellant establish that the respondents infringed its trademark under section 34 of the Trade Marks Act by using similar marks likely to deceive or cause confusion?
  2. Did the name "Lucky Brands (Pty) Ltd" and associated names of the other respondents contravene the provisions of section 11(2) of the Companies Act, which prohibits confusingly similar names to registered trademarks? ([2], [14]–[15]).

R – Rules

The legal framework considered by the Court primarily involved:

  1. Section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act, which provides rights to a registered trademark owner against the unauthorized use of an identical or nearly resembling trademark that is likely to deceive or cause confusion ([3]).
  2. Section 34(1)(b), which governs the infringement when marks are used in relation to similar goods or services, focusing on the likelihood of deception or confusion ([11]).
  3. Section 34(1)(c), which prohibits the dilution of a trademark through unauthorized use, irrespective of confusion ([13]).
  4. Section 11(2) of the Companies Act stipulating that company names must not be identical or confusingly similar to registered trademarks without permission ([14]).

The Court referred to key precedents, including:

  • Plascon-Evans Paints (Pty) Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints for principles on the likelihood of confusion ([6]).
  • Bata Ltd v Face Fashions CC, which underscored how common words can impact trademark protection ([10]).

A – Application

The Court began by confirming that the appellant needed to prove both the registration of its trademarks and the unauthorized use of a mark that resembled its trademarks. The argument centered on the common element "Lucky," which both parties shared in their names. However, the Court concluded that the respondents' use of the word "Fish" provided significant differentiation from the appellant’s trademark "Star" ([4]).

The Court conducted an objective comparison of the marks under the criteria established in prior judgments, asserting that confusion would not likely arise among average consumers when distinguishing between "Lucky Star" and "Lucky Fish" due to the distinctive nature of the words "Star" and "Fish" ([10], [12]).

For section 34(1)(b), the Court reiterated the interdependence of mark similarity and similarity of goods. The goods were found to be sufficiently dissimilar, further supporting the rejection of the claim ([11]).

In considering section 34(1)(c), the Court noted that "similar" must not be overly broadened to avoid monopolistic implications. It established that there was a marked distinction between the marks involved, thereby nullifying the risk of dilution ([13]).

With respect to the Companies Act, the judgment maintained that there was no evidence supporting a claim that the respondents' names were confusingly similar or misleading regarding the appellant’s brand ([15]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's decision that the appellant did not satisfy the necessary legal thresholds under sections 34(1)(a), (b), and (c) of the Trade Marks Act, nor under section 11(2) of the Companies Act ([17]). The ratio decidendi indicated that trademark confusion must be assessed based not only on the similarity of names but also on perceptions influenced by the distinctiveness and context of the goods or services provided. The decision underscores the necessity for clear distinctions in developing brand identity within competitive markets and highlights the safeguarding of traders against unsubstantiated claims of trademark infringement in a contextually varied commercial landscape.

This case serves as important jurisprudence on trademark law in South Africa, clarifying the thresholds for establishing infringement and the relevant judicial interpretations of statutory provisions concerning trademark and company name regulations ([1]–[17]).
