# Case Note: The Voice of the Unborn Baby NPC v Minister of Home Affairs & Another [2022] ZACC 20

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
In the matter of *The Voice of the Unborn Baby NPC and Another v Minister of Home Affairs and Another* [2022] ZACC 20, the applicants sought the confirmation of a High Court order that declared certain sections of the Births and Deaths Registration Act 51 of 1992 (BADRA) constitutionally invalid ([1]). The contested provisions included sections 20(1) regarding burial orders for stillbirths, along with sections 18(1) to (3) concerning the issuance of stillbirth certificates. The High Court had ruled that these provisions infringed on the constitutional rights of prospective parents to privacy, dignity, religion, and equality by prohibiting burial for foetal remains other than in cases of stillbirth ([4]). The applicants, The Voice of the Unborn Baby NPC and the Catholic Archdiocese of Durban, contended that the law unjustly distinguished between foetuses lost through miscarriage and those born stillborn, with no legitimate state objective governing this distinction ([5]).

Both applicants were joined by amici curiae including the Women’s Legal Centre Trust and the Sexual and Reproductive Justice Coalition, who emphasized the implications of potential burial rights on reproductive health services and public resources ([2]). The government respondents, namely the Ministers of Home Affairs and Health, argued against the proposed constitutional invalidity, suggesting it would impose administrative burdens and interfere with legislative authority ([12]-[13]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues arose from:
1. Whether the High Court was correct in declaring certain provisions of BADRA invalid based on constitutional grounds ([17]).
2. Whether BADRA, in its current form, explicitly prohibited the burial of pre-viable foetal remains ([17]).
3. Whether the rights to dignity and privacy were infringed by the contested provisions of BADRA and the associated regulations ([17]-[18]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework relied upon in this case mainly includes:
- **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**: Sections evaluating the rights to dignity (Section 10), privacy (Section 14), and equality (Section 9).
- **Births and Deaths Registration Act 51 of 1992 (BADRA)**: Particularly sections 1 (definitions including 'stillborn'), 18 (the issuance of stillbirth certificates), and 20 (regulations regarding burial orders) ([19]-[20]).
- **Regulations Relating to the Management of Human Remains**, which were also challenged under the scrutiny of constitutional validity ([1]).

### A – Application
The Constitutional Court, led by Tlaletsi AJ, assessed whether the provisions of BADRA indeed prohibited the burial of non-stillborn foetal remains. The Court noted that while the High Court had found these provisions inconsistent with the Constitution, a thorough examination revealed that BADRA did not specifically restrict burial rights to only stillbirths, particularly, a pre-viable foetus did not fall within the definition of a 'corpse' as prescribed in the Act ([20]-[22]). As such, it indicated a misinterpretation by the High Court regarding the provisions governing burial.

The Court further remarked on the importance of interpreting the law in line with the Constitution, specifically referencing Section 39(2) which mandates promoting the Bill of Rights during legal interpretation ([24]). The judgment concluded that since the provisions did not contain an explicit prohibition against the burial of foetal remains, the High Court's declaration of invalidity was unwarranted. Thus, there was no justification to limit the rights claimed by the applicants ([25]-[26]).

Furthermore, the Court addressed the potential implications of allowing such burial rights and recognized that significant, practical issues would arise for healthcare providers in managing requests to dispose of foetal remains ([27]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Constitutional Court did not confirm the High Court's declaration of the constitutional invalidity of the contested provisions of BADRA. Instead, it found that:
1. **BADRA does not explicitly prohibit the burial of pre-viable or terminated foetal remains**, thus rendering the High Court's conclusions flawed ([26]).
2. The High Court's orders were set aside, and the application was dismissed, mandating each party to bear its own costs.

This decision reinforces the significance of precise legal interpretation within statutory frameworks and highlights the importance of ensuring that legislative provisions are closely aligned with constitutional imperatives while considering broader implications on public health administration and reproductive rights ([35]). 

Overall, the ruling clarifies the legal standing of foetal remains concerning burial rights, reflecting the delicate balance between humanitarian concerns and legislative authority.
