Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: Delphisure Group Insurance Brokers Cape (Pty) Ltd v Dippenaar and Others 

## Facts
The case revolves around a delictual claim for pure economic loss allegedly suffered by the first and second respondents due to the negligent misrepresentation of an insurance product called "Farmsure" ([1]). The first and second respondents, involved in wheat farming in the Piketburg district of the Western Cape, sought assurances from the appellant (Delphisure), an insurance brokerage, regarding the availability of the Farmsure product. Notably, despite marketing efforts, this insurance product was never formally underwritten by any insurer, specifically Lloyds of London ([1]–[3]).

Delphisure marketed the Farmsure product in conjunction with Bexsure, an affiliated entity. The product was designed to offer coverage superior to other available options. However, due to various negotiations and misunderstandings, it was revealed that Delphisure had not secured the necessary underwriting from Lloyds for the 2004 growing season ([2]–[4]). 

The respondents were misled by representations made at a marketing meeting held on May 5, 2004, where it was asserted that Farmsure was a fully underwritten policy by Lloyds. This statement prompted the first and second respondents to cancel their prior applications with Mutual & Federal Insurance Company Ltd and subsequently apply for Farmsure, leaving them uninsured when their crops ultimately failed due to adverse conditions ([12]–[13]). The Western Cape High Court initially ruled in favour of the respondents against Delphisure, leading to an appeal by the appellant concerning the second respondent's claim ([1]–[14]).

## Issues
The core legal issues considered by the Court were:
1. **Negligence:** Whether Delphisure and its representatives acted negligently in misrepresenting the status of the Farmsure product ([16]).
2. **Causation:** Whether the first and second respondents established a direct causal link between their economic losses and the misrepresentation issued by Delphisure ([28]–[30]).
3. **Wrongfulness:** Whether public policy considerations would support the imposition of liability on Delphisure for the alleged misrepresentation leading to pure economic loss sustained by the farmers ([23]).

## Rules
The legal principles applicable to this case include:
1. **Negligence:** The standard for establishing negligence encompasses the foreseeability of harm, the existence of a duty of care, and the breach of that duty ([16][20]).
2. **Causation:** The so-called 'but-for' test determines if the loss would not have occurred but for the defendant's actions. Legal causation also considers the closeness of the connection between the wrongful act and the resultant loss ([28][29]).
3. **Wrongfulness and Pure Economic Loss:** Liability for pure economic loss due to negligent misrepresentation is dependent on the existence of a legal duty, foreseeability, and policy considerations which determine when liability should be recognized ([23][24][27]).

## Application
The Court determined that Kolovos of Delphisure was negligent in marketing Farmsure as if it were fully underwritten when, in fact, it was not ([21]). The misrepresentation was deemed a proximate cause of the respondents’ decision to cancel their existing insurance and apply for Farmsure, a decision which ultimately led to their uninsured status when their crops failed. This was established through direct testimonies and evidence from various witnesses who attended critical meetings ([8][12]).

### Foreseeability and Reasonableness
The Court concluded that it was reasonable to foresee that farmers, upon hearing guarantees of coverage from a newly marketed product, would cancel existing insurance applications ([19]). The argument that farmers would not foreseeably be at risk without insurance was rejected; the unique selling points of the Farmsure coverage would likely entice insured farmers to transition to Farmsure despite existing policies ([18]–[19]).

### Causation Analysis
The analysis of causation revealed that the first respondent's loss was directly linked to the misrepresentation; he would have been compensated by Mutual & Federal had the misrepresentation not occurred ([29]). Conversely, the second respondent's claim failed due to insufficient evidence of direct causation; he did not convincingly establish that he would have opted for Mutual & Federal's coverage had the Farmsure product not been advertised ([30][35]).

### Wrongfulness and Policy Implications
The Court addressed the question of wrongfulness, noting that public policy considerations did favor recognizing liability in this instance. The misrepresentation was made in the course of business and a substantial relationship existed between the parties that warranted a duty of care. Furthermore, the limited number of likely claimants mitigated concerns around boundless liability ([23][27]).

## Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Respondents’ claim against Delphisure regarding the first respondent while dismissing the second respondent’s claim due to insufficient proof of causation ([36]). The Court ruled that Delphisure’s misrepresentation of Farmsure being underwritten by Lloyds constituted a negligent act resulting in identifiable economic harm. The decision emphasized the responsibilities of insurance providers to accurately present their offerings, particularly when those offerings significantly influence stakeholders' financial decisions in high-risk industries such as agriculture. The order provided that Delphisure is liable for the first respondent’s damages while setting aside the claim of the second respondent, substantiating the legal frameworks surrounding negligence and economic loss ([36][40]).
