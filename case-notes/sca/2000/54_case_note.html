Supreme Court of Appeal

Tecmed (Pty) Ltd v Eastern Cape Provincial Tender Board [2000] ZASCA 63

In September 1996, the Eastern Cape Provincial Tender Board (the Board) invited tenders for a Bi-Plane Catheterisation Laboratory for the Provincial Hospital in Port Elizabeth.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2000 [2000] ZASCA 63

F – Facts

In September 1996, the Eastern Cape Provincial Tender Board (the Board) invited tenders for a Bi-Plane Catheterisation Laboratory for the Provincial Hospital in Port Elizabeth. The tender was awarded to Tecmed (Pty) Ltd (Tecmed), the appellant, which submitted its proposal on time but delivered it late due to unforeseen industrial action affecting transport ([paras 1–8]). Philips (the fourth respondent) subsequently sought a review in the High Court, aiming to set aside the award. The High Court ruled in favor of Philips, granting the orders sought and denying Tecmed leave to appeal, although this leave was later granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 1–2]).

The Board operates under the Provincial Tender Board Act 2 of 1994 (Eastern Cape) and its regulations, which mandate a specific procedure for the tendering process. Notably, the tender notice itself failed to comply with regulatory requirements, stating that tender submissions were subject to outdated regulations ([paras 2–5]). Tecmed's tender was treated as a late submission, and crucially, the Board was never informed of the circumstances around this lateness, thus impacting its decision-making process ([para 9]).

I – Issues

  1. Did the Board have the authority to consider Tecmed’s late tender despite the delivery issues?
  2. Was the High Court justified in its orders to set aside the operation of the Board's decision and to exclude Tecmed from further consideration in the tender process?
  3. What are the implications of procedural oversight in the tender process on the validity of the award ([paras 1–12])?

R – Rules

The legal rules governing the case stem largely from the Provincial Tender Board Act of 1994 and the associated regulations. Key provisions include:

  • Regulation 7(6)(d): This rule stipulates that tenders received after the closing time shall generally be returned to the tenderer, but allows the secretariat to refer late tenders to the Board for consideration if delayed through no fault of the tenderer ([para 9]).
  • Discretion of the Board: The Board, vested with the sole power to procure supplies, is required to exercise discretion in line with legislative provisions ([para 4]). If the Board did not know about the tardiness of a tender, it could not exercise its discretion effectively.
  • Judicial Review Principles: The court upheld the principle that any action taken by the Board contrary to the procedural requirements as prescribed by the Act and regulations is susceptible to judicial review ([para 2]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal found significant procedural flaws in the tender process, notably that the Board had not followed the required procedures stipulated in the regulations ([paras 5, 6]). Furthermore, the court concluded that because the Board did not exercise its discretion—due to being unaware of the circumstances surrounding Tecmed’s late submission—it had acted unlawfully in awarding the tender ([para 9]).

The court highlighted that the erroneous assumption that Tecmed's tender was a facsimile (not delivering a timely physical document) was not a matter of law but rather a procedural oversight by the Board ([para 8]). It determined that the High Court had correctly set aside the Board's award but improperly excluded Tecmed from further participation, as the regulations allowed the Board the discretion to consider the late tender if they were properly informed of the circumstances affecting its timing ([paras 11–14]).

The judgment underscored the importance of procedural fairness and transparency in administrative actions, emphasizing that the lack of information available to the Board concerning Tecmed’s late submission constituted a significant oversight ([para 10]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court dismissed the appeal in part and upheld the setting aside of the tender award based on procedural flaws. However, it reversed the High Court's orders that sought to preclude Tecmed from any future participation in the tender process, reasoning that the Board retained the authority to reconsider Tecmed’s late submission ([paras 21–23]). The implications of this judgment reaffirm the necessity for adherence to procedural fairness and legislative requirements within public procurement processes and highlight the importance of safeguarding all parties' opportunities to engage in the tender process without undue disadvantage.

The case illustrates the complexities and legal consequences inherent in the regulation of public tenders, particularly in the context of timing and adherence to procedural requirements. The judgment serves as a pivotal reference point for understanding the nuances of administrative discretion and judicial review in the tendering process in South African law.
