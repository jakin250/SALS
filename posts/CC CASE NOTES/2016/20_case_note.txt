Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Pheko and Others v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality and Others (No 3) [2016] ZACC 20

**FIRAC Method Case Note**

### F – Facts
The case revolves around the applicants, former residents of the Bapsfontein Informal Settlement, who were forcibly removed from their homes following the municipality's declaration of the settlement as a disaster area under the Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002. Subsequent to their removal, the applicants sought relief from the High Court regarding the legality of their eviction but were unsuccessful. They escalated the matter to the Constitutional Court, which, in **Pheko I**, declared the removal unlawful and mandated the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (hereafter the Municipality) to provide adequate housing alternatives and engage with the affected communities ([4]–[5]).

This case (referred to as Pheko II) is an interlocutory application for supervision over the municipality’s compliance with the earlier order. Disputes arose from the municipality’s report concerning unsuitable land and the alleged lack of meaningful engagement with the applicants about relocation ([12]–[13]). The Municipality identified land but faced challenges, including technical constraints and planning issues ([6]–[10]). The applicants, backed by the Socio-Economic Rights Institute of South Africa (SERI), opposed the municipality's submissions ([3]).

### I – Issues
The legal questions for determination included:
1. Should the Constitutional Court discharge its supervisory jurisdiction over the municipality's execution of the order from Pheko I?
2. What should be the terms of referral to the High Court concerning the matter?
3. How should costs be allocated, including the qualifying fees of experts involved?
([22]–[24]).

### R – Rules
The court’s ruling relied heavily on precedents regarding the enforceability of socio-economic rights provided in the Constitution, particularly Section 26(3), which protects individuals from being evicted without just cause ([23]). 

Additionally, the court highlighted the necessity for municipalities to comply with their constitutional obligations to provide adequate housing as mandated in the Housing Act 107 of 1997, which requires municipalities to take all reasonable steps to ensure housing access ([23]–[24]). The court also invoked Section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution, which grants the court broad remedial powers in constitutional matters ([32]).

### A – Application
In its analysis, the court deliberated on the necessity of retaining oversight over the implementation of its prior order versus referring the matter to the High Court. The court underscored that maintaining its supervisory role could lead to inefficiencies and overlap with the High Court's proceedings, thus proposing that such technical issues and disputes be resolved by the High Court, which could conduct proper evaluations and manage hearings ([27]–[29]).

Crucially, the court considered that the disputes regarding the suitability of the identified lands and the nature of the community's engagement required substantive oral evidence from experts. The complexities in resolving the disputes indicated that a collaborative approach involving both the High Court and this Constitutional Court would be optimal, allowing for full ventilation of the issues ([36]).

Furthermore, the court asserted that discharging the supervisory jurisdiction while referring certain structural aspects for High Court determination allows for a detailed examination of fact-sensitive issues without compromising the applicants' rights under Section 26(3) of the Constitution ([30]–[31]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court, concluding that the discharge of supervisory jurisdiction would best serve the interests of justice, ordered several actions: the discharge of certain paragraphs from the previous order, a referral of the matter to the High Court for resolutions regarding land identification and relocation projects, and an explicit allocation of costs to the Municipality for their non-compliance ([46]). Ultimately, the ruling emphasizes the balance between enforcing socio-economic rights and the practical challenges associated with municipal administration, reinforcing the commitment to adequate housing while allowing technical disputes to be thoroughly addressed in a competent forum ([41]). 

This decision establishes a precedent for similar future engagements, where the interplay between municipal obligations and constitutional rights can lead to more structured resolutions, ensuring accountability and fostering community-engaged governance ([31]–[37]).
