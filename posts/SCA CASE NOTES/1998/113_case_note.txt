Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1998

### FIRAC Case Note: Republic of South Africa, Case No. 627/97

#### F – Facts
The case involves a dispute over the appointment and election process of trustees for the Grey Street Mosque in Durban, an institution governed by a trust deed. The appellants—Abubaker Mohamed No., Aboobaker Ismail No., Ahmed Asruff Essay No., Mehmood Khan No., Habib Gani No., Haroun Gani No., Farouk Rooknoodeen No., and Ismail Ally No.—claim to be the duly appointed trustees. However, only the first four appellants' appointments have been ratified at an annual general meeting (AGM) of the mosque's congregation; the latter four were appointed temporarily. Since 1987, no AGM has been held, leaving their positions unvalidated by congregational approval ([paras 2–3]).

The respondent, Faizal Ally, who is an active member of the congregation, initiated this litigation seeking an order for the appellants to convene an AGM. The conflict reveals two factions: supporters of the existing trustees and those aligned with the respondent, the latter accusing the trustees of acting as a self-perpetuating oligarchy. The appellants counter that the respondent engages in populist tactics detrimental to the organization ([paras 2–4]).

The trust deed, originally executed in 1916 and amended in 1957, stipulates the composition and authority of the board of trustees, including provisions for the filling of vacancies. The appellants argue for the need for separate electoral processes for different “sections” within the community, while the respondent contends for a unified electoral process at the AGM ([paras 4–6]).

#### I – Issues
The principal issues before the court are:

1. **Interpretation of the Trust Deed**: Whether the trust deed requires separate meetings for different sections of the mosque community to nominate and elect trustees, or if such elections must occur during a general meeting inclusive of all members ([paras 5–6]).

2. **Filling Vacancies**: The appropriate procedure for filling vacancies on the board of trustees, particularly whether this requires adherence to sectional representation or if a congregational vote at the AGM suffices ([paras 6–12]).

#### R – Rules
The legal rules applicable to this case are primarily derived from the provisions in the Juma Musjid Trust Deed itself, particularly:

- **Clause 4(a)**: Outlines the sectional representation of trustees on the board.
  
- **Clause 4(e)**: States that all vacancies in the Board of Trustees shall be filled at the AGM, mandating selection from the relevant sectional class without contradicting the stipulations of Clause 4(a).
  
- **Clause 4(b)**: Requires that interim trustees be appointed by the remaining trustees and must comply with Clause 4(a).
  
- **Clause 7(d)**: Specifies the requirement for a quorum and voting procedures at AGMs, stipulating that votes at general meetings will be taken on a show of hands unless otherwise stated ([paras 8–12]).

Additionally, past case law, including **Cassim & Others v Meman Mosque Trustees**, 1917 AD 154, serves as a precedent concerning the importance of community consent in governance structures ([paras 5–6]).

#### A – Application
The court's analysis began by examining the precise wording of the trust deed. The appellant's interpretation—that vacancies should be filled by separate meetings of each “section”—was critically assessed against the clear requirements laid out in the deed. The presiding judge emphasized the need to honor the trust's broader objectives, which focus on serving the congregation's collective interests, rather than sectional interests alone ([paras 10–14]).

The court highlighted the absence of provisions for separate sectional meetings, indicating that Clause 4(e) explicitly requires that all vacancies be filled at the AGM. This interpretation was reinforced by examining the historical context and intended functioning of the trust and its trustees, focusing on unity and shared governance among all congregants ([paras 12–18]).

The appellants’ claim that a trust representative must act solely in the interests of a particular section was found to conflict with the deed's overall purpose. The judge pointed out that electing trustees through a generalized process at an AGM would better serve the trust's intent and maintain congregational harmony, rather than fostering divisive sectarianism. The court noted the dangers of fragmenting the congregation into classes with competing interests, which could lead to internal conflict ([paras 17–18]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, firmly concluding that the trust deed mandates all vacancies on the trustee board be filled by election at the AGM, without the necessity for prior nomination in separate sectional meetings. The court upheld the interpretation presented by the respondent, confirming that the appellants' approach could undermine the communal function of the trust ([paras 18–19]). 

The ruling reinforces the importance of congregational participation in governance, encouraging broader engagement rather than allowing a small group to dictate terms. The implications extend beyond this case, setting a precedent for how religious and community trusts might operate in a manner that promotes inclusivity and democratic governance ([paras 18–19]). The costs were to be borne by the trust, reflecting the court's support for the respondent's initiative to restore proper governance practices within the mosque ([paras 18–19]).
