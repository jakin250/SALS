Shembe v Shembe N.O. [2021] ZACC 17

In proceedings initiated by Vela Shembe, the High Court made critical factual findings including the authenticity of a Deed of Nomination which allegedly appointed him as the successor.

Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 17

F - Facts

The case revolves around a dispute concerning leadership succession within the Nazareth Baptist Church (the Church), established by the Prophet Isaiah Shembe in 1910, primarily affecting the Ebuhleni congregation ([paras 1–2]). Following the death of the last Titular Head, Mr Vimbeni Shembe, in 2011, two individuals emerged with conflicting claims to succeed him. The first applicant, Mduduzi Shembe, claimed an oral nomination by the late leader, while the now deceased Vela Shembe claimed he had a written Deed of Nomination naming him as the successor. The respondent, Ntombifikile Primrose Shembe, represents the estate of the late Vela Shembe and contested the first applicant's claims ([para 2]).

In proceedings initiated by Vela Shembe, the High Court made critical factual findings including the authenticity of a Deed of Nomination which allegedly appointed him as the successor. It also concluded that the succession process was governed not by the church's Trust Deed established in 1935 but rather by a constitution purportedly adopted by the Ebuhleni congregation in 1999 ([paras 9–10]).

Upon appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's findings, asserting consistency with trial findings, leading to the current appeal being lodged to the Constitutional Court ([para 12]).

I - Issues

The principal legal issues raised in this case include:

  1. Whether a court can vary a Trust Deed through the adoption of a separate document (the Constitution), in contravention of the Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988.
  2. Whether this matter engages the Constitutional Court's jurisdiction based on general public importance as articulated in section 167(3)(b)(ii) of the Constitution.

Further, the underlying factual disputes about succession legitimacy highlighted questions concerning the doctrines of law, trust property control, and ecclesiastical authority ([paras 15–18]).

R - Rules

The legal framework touches upon several statutes and principles, including:

  • Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988: Governs amendments to trust deeds and their operational legality.
  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa: Particularly sections 167(3)(b)(i) and (ii), governing the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court.
  • Common Law Trust Principles: Establishes the need for adherence to established procedures in trust administration and succession ([para 14]).

Precedent from previous cases, especially regarding the separation of powers and the interpretations of trust documents, also played a pivotal role ([para 15]).

A - Application

The Court dismissed the appeal based on two critical points:

  1. Jurisdictional Grounds: The application was deemed not to raise sufficient legal issues of general public importance. The Court identified that the matter primarily involved factual disputes concerning the applicability of the two governing documents (Trust Deed versus Constitution). It concluded that the arguments presented were either factual determinations, not requiring constitutional scrutiny, or academic in nature ([paras 19–20]).
  2. Legal Interpretation of Documents: The Court analyzed the applicants' contention that the Supreme Court of Appeal's remarks on how the Constitution varied the Trust Deed introduced a dangerous legal precedent. However, it was observed that the Supreme Court's conclusions were based on factual findings regarding the recognition of the Constitution as the operational document of the Church, which did not necessitate a challenge to the core trust norms under South African jurisprudence ([para 20–21]).

The Court also noted that the validity of the succession claims tied back significantly to factual determinations already made, including that the first applicant's claims lacked a firm foundation when juxtaposed against the documented nomination of Vela Shembe ([para 24]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court unanimously decided to refuse leave to appeal, thereby upholding the findings of the Supreme Court of Appeal. The applicants were ordered to bear the costs, including those of two counsel, reinforcing the principle that unsuccessful parties in litigation typically are responsible for costs incurred ([paras 26–27]).

Ultimately, the ruling clarified that the applicants' quest to assert the primacy of the Trust Deed was overtaken by earlier factual determinations regarding the succession, limiting significant implications for constitutional jurisprudence or trust law doctrine. Thus, while the documented processes of church governance were scrutinized, the decision reaffirmed adherence to factual findings over abstract legal challenges or hypotheticals.
