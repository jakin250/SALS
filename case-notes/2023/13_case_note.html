Arena Holdings (Pty) Ltd t/a Financial Mail and Others v South African Revenue Service and Others [2023] ZACC 13

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 13

F - Facts

The case centers on a request made by the applicants, Arena Holdings (Pty) Ltd (first applicant), AmaBhungane Centre for Investigative Journalism NPC (second applicant), and Warren Thompson (third applicant), aimed at accessing the tax records of former President Jacob Zuma from the South African Revenue Service (SARS) ([1]-[4]).

In early 2019, Thompson applied to SARS under the Promotion of Access to Information Act 2 of 2000 (PAIA) for Mr. Zuma's tax records, citing suspicions of tax evasion based on claims made in Jacques Pauw's book, The President’s Keepers ([6]-[8]). SARS denied the request on the grounds that the records were confidential under sections 35(1) and 69(1) of the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 (TAA) ([8]). Subsequent appeals and applications culminated in the High Court declaring the relevant provisions unconstitutional. The applicants sought confirmation of this order from the Constitutional Court ([14]-[15]).

I - Issues

The central legal questions addressed by the court included:

  1. Whether the impugned provisions of sections 35 and 46 of the PAIA and sections 67 and 69 of the TAA, which prohibit access to taxpayer information, infringe on the constitutional rights to access information and freedom of expression ([16], [42]).
  2. Whether the limitations imposed by these sections are justified under section 36 of the Constitution ([42]).

R - Rules

The Constitutional Court employed sections 32 and 36 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, which respectively establish the right of access to information held by the state and the criteria for justifiable limitations of rights. The applicants argued against the constitutionality of sections 35 and 46 of PAIA and sections 67 and 69 of the TAA, emphasizing the need to balance taxpayer confidentiality with public interest and accountability ([49]-[50]).

Legal Precedents

The Court considered previous rulings, notably Johncom Media Investments Ltd v M and Mail and Guardian Media Ltd v Chipu N.O., which invalidated absolute prohibitions on information access. These cases established the principle that such limitations must be necessary and proportionate ([60]-[62]).

A - Application

The Court analyzed both the constitutional validity of the prohibitory provisions and the necessity of striking a balance between the right to privacy and the rights to access information and freedom of expression. Mhlantla J, in dissent, upheld the argument that taxpayer confidentiality is vital for ensuring taxpayer compliance with tax laws ([180]-[185]). In contrast, Kollapen J's majority ruling argued that an absolute prohibition on taxpayer records conflicts with democratic principles and public accountability ([123]-[196]).

The implications discussed were significant, considering how various jurisdictions treat taxpayer confidentiality, but also emphasizing South Africa's specific constitutional framework which endorses transparency as essential for accountability ([175]-[176]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's ruling declaring sections 35 and 46 of PAIA, and sections 67 and 69 of the TAA unconstitutional, holding that they unjustifiably limited the rights of access to information and freedom of expression ([202]). The order of constitutional invalidity was suspended for 24 months, allowing Parliament to address the found unconstitutionality ([204]).

This case underscores the tension between the right to privacy and the public's right to access information, reinforcing the necessity for legislative frameworks to evolve in compliance with constitutional mandates after judicial review. The Court's decision thus marks a significant step in advancing democratic values of transparency and accountability in South Africa ([198]-[200]).
