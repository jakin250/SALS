Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: CSARS v Big G Restaurants (Pty) Ltd (157/18) [2018] ZASCA 179

### FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case involved the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (the Appellant) appealing against the decision of the Tax Court of South Africa, which ruled in favor of Big G Restaurants (Pty) Ltd (the Respondent) regarding income tax assessments for the tax years 2011 to 2014 ([paras 1–2]). Big G Restaurants operates as a franchisee under agreements with Spur Group (Pty) Ltd. Notably, clause Q.1.1 of the franchise agreement stipulated that the sole business of Big G would be the operation of Spur Steak Ranch Restaurants and operations specializing in pizza and pasta ([paras 2–3]). The franchise agreement also mandated that Big G pay a fee to the franchisor, calculated as a percentage of gross sales ([para 3]).

Big G Restaurants claimed allowances under Section 24C of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 (the Act) for future expenditures related to refurbishments as required by the franchise agreements ([paras 4–5]). The Tax Court was called upon to rule on two main legal questions: whether the income from operations constituted amounts "received or accrued" in terms of the franchise agreements, and whether the refurbishment expenditures were incurred "in the performance of obligations under such contract” ([paras 6–7]).

The Tax Court ruled in favor of Big G Restaurants, stating that the income was earned under the same contractual framework as the obligations arising from the franchise agreement. As a result, the court set aside the additional assessments raised by the Commissioner ([para 7]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal questions before the court were:
1. **Did the income received by Big G Restaurants from operating its franchise qualify as income received or accrued in terms of the franchise agreement as mandated by Section 24C of the Act?**
2. **Were expenditures to refurbish or upgrade the restaurants incurred in the performance of taxpayer’s obligations under the franchise agreements?** ([paras 6–7]).

### R – Rules
The pivotal legal authority in question was Section 24C of the Income Tax Act, which allows for deductions on future expenditures in relation to income “received or accrued” from contracts, provided that such expenditures are incurred in the performance of obligations under the same contract ([para 8]). The court referenced previous case law to explore the interpretations of “in terms of” and related statutory language, emphasizing that this phrase could take both narrow and wide meanings depending on the context ([paras 10–13]).

In particular, the court noted the legislative intent behind Section 24C, which aimed to provide tax relief for taxpayers who receive advance payments intending to use them in fulfilling contractual obligations, suggesting that a close connection between the contract governing the income and the contract governing the expenses was critical for the application of the provision ([paras 16–21]).

### A – Application
The court applied the narrow interpretation of the phrase “in terms of” to establish whether the income generated by Big G Restaurants originated from the franchise agreement or from separate patron contracts that arose when they sold food and beverages to customers ([paras 14, 15, 19]). 

The court concluded that the obligations that Big G Restaurants owed under the franchise agreement did not create any rights to actual income but rather facilitated the earning of income through contracts with individual patrons. Therefore, the income was derived from sales to customers and not from the franchise agreement itself ([paras 17–18]). 

Despite counsel for the taxpayer arguing that the franchise agreement and patron contracts were inextricably linked, the court rejected this view, emphasizing that facilitative or preparatory connections do not suffice to establish that income was earned “in terms of” the franchise agreement. Instead, a direct connection was necessary, dictated by the requirements of Section 24C ([paras 20–22]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service, setting aside the prior decisions of the Tax Court. The court ruled that Big G Restaurants did not receive income “in terms of” the franchise agreement, meaning they were not eligible for the tax relief under the provisions of Section 24C for future refurbishment expenditures ([paras 22–23]). This decision clarified the requirement that both the income and the obligations for future expenditures must stem from the same contract to qualify for tax deductions, emphasizing the importance of a direct contractual relationship in tax law. 

Consequently, the ruling has broader implications for franchisees and other businesses operating under multiple contractual frameworks, affirming the need for clear delineation of income sources in relation to tax obligations.
