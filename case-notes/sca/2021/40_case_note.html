FIRAC Case Note: Motus Corporation (Pty) Ltd and Another v Wentzel [2021] ZASCA 40

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 40 ### F – Facts On 16 May 2018, Abigail Wentzel

FIRAC Case Note: Motus Corporation (Pty) Ltd and Another v Wentzel [2021] ZASCA 40

F – Facts

On 16 May 2018, Abigail Wentzel (the respondent) filed an application against Motus Corporation (Pty) Ltd and Renault South Africa (Pty) Ltd (the appellants) in the Gauteng Division of the High Court. She sought to cancel a credit agreement regarding a Renault Kwid vehicle and requested a refund of the purchase price amounting to R256,965.84, asserting that the vehicle was defective in violation of the Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008 (the Act) ([paras 1-2]).

Wentzel alleged that defects manifested shortly after the purchase on 7 December 2017, including noise issues and faulty air conditioning. Despite several communications with Renault and attempts at repairs, she expressed dissatisfaction with the vehicle's condition and subsequently approached the Motor Industry Ombudsman of South Africa (MIOSA) for assistance ([paras 7-15]). Following MIOSA’s refusal to handle her complaint, citing jurisdictional issues, Wentzel turned to the High Court ([paras 16-17]).

The High Court found in favor of Wentzel and ordered the appellants to refund her, which they contested on appeal, primarily arguing that Wentzel had not exhausted all internal remedies required by s 69 of the Act before bringing her case to court ([para 4], [paras 19-20]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues in this appeal were:

  1. Whether Wentzel had exhausted the internal remedies as mandated by the Consumer Protection Act before approaching the court.
  2. Whether Wentzel could demonstrate sufficient grounds under s 56(3) of the Act for a refund of the purchase price due to unresolved defects.
  3. Whether the High Court erred in awarding a complete refund, including amounts not directly paid to Renault, contrary to statutory stipulations ([paras 4, 22-23], [para 45]).

R – Rules

The relevant provisions of law include:

  • Section 69 of the Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008: outlines the procedures for a consumer to seek redress, emphasizing the necessity for exhausting internal remedies including approaches to the Tribunal or relevant ombudsmen before resorting to courts.
  • Section 55: guarantees consumers the right to goods that are of good quality and free from defects ([para 36]).
  • Section 56: provides an implied warranty of quality where consumers can return defective goods within six months and seek repairs, replacements, or refunds, specifying conditions under which refunds can be claimed ([paras 37-44]).

The court's interpretation of these provisions was critical, especially in relation to whether a hierarchy of remedies existed and if Wentzel's claim aligned with statutory requirements ([paras 25-27]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal examined whether Wentzel’s application to the High Court was premature and considered Renault’s arguments against the admissibility of her claims, including the alleged failure to exhaust internal remedies ([paras 19-24]). The Court critiqued earlier High Court judgments that interpreted s 69's remedy process too rigidly, emphasizing the constitutional right of access to courts as paramount ([para 26]).

In assessing the merits of Wentzel’s claim under s 56(3), the Court found that she did not satisfy the statutory requirements to justify a refund since defects did not re-occur post-repair within the specified timeline, nor did she provide evidence of new defects to support her claims ([paras 43-45]).

The judgement also emphasized that the amount awarded by the High Court included finance charges not payable to Renault, contravening the Act's definitions of "price" and the requirements outlined in s 20 regarding refunds ([paras 46-48]). This miscalculation led to the conclusion that the High Court had materially misdirected itself and consequently erred in the final order ([para 45]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court’s order should be set aside since Wentzel failed to exhaust all internal remedies as mandated by the Consumer Protection Act and could not demonstrate her claim for a refund met the statutory criteria outlined in s 56(3) of the Act. Moreover, the calculation of the amount to be refunded was incorrect, further substantiating the decision’s reversal. The appeal was allowed with no order as to costs, underlining that Renault’s responses during the proceedings did not merit punitive costs ([paras 52-52]).

This case underscores the necessity for consumers to navigate the statutory remedy frameworks established under the Consumer Protection Act carefully, confirming that proper application of the Act’s provisions is essential for asserting claims effectively and emphasizes the protective purpose of consumer legislation as intended by the drafters ([paras 32-34]).
