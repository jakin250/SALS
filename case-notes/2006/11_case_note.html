Constitutional Court 2006 [2006] ZACC 11

FIRAC Method

F - Facts

The Constitutional Court of South Africa adjudicated a direct constitutional complaint brought by Doctors for Life International, contesting the legislative process followed by the National Council of Provinces (NCOP) and various provincial legislatures in passing four health statutes: the Choice on Termination of Pregnancy Amendment Act 38 of 2004, the Sterilisation Amendment Act 3 of 2005, the Traditional Health Practitioners Act 35 of 2004, and the Dental Technicians Amendment Act 24 of 2004 ([1]-[4]).

The applicant argued that during the legislative process, the NCOP failed to comply with its constitutional obligations to facilitate public involvement, as mandated by sections 72(1)(a) and 118(1)(a) of the Constitution, which stipulate that legislatures must ensure public participation in their processes ([2]-[5]). Only the National Assembly was acknowledged by the applicant to have fulfilled its obligation to facilitate public involvement ([6]-[7]).

The respondents contended that the NCOP complied with its duty by providing opportunities for public involvement at varying stages of the legislative process, arguing that what is required is merely the opportunity for public submissions ([7]-[9]). Notably, the health legislation had been enacted by the time the complaint was lodged, raising questions about the timing of potential judicial interventions ([10]-[11]).

I - Issues

The Court was required to address several key issues:

  1. Exclusive Jurisdiction: Does the Constitutional Court have exclusive jurisdiction under section 167(4)(e) to rule on whether Parliament has failed to fulfill statutory obligations ([11])?
  2. Judicial Relief: Is it competent for the Court to grant declaratory relief regarding legislative processes that have not yet concluded ([11])?
  3. Scope of Obligation: What constitutes the nature and scope of the duty to facilitate public involvement as set forth in the Constitution ([11])?
  4. Compliance: Did the NCOP and provincial legislatures indeed comply with their constitutional obligations ([11])?
  5. Remedial Action: If non-compliance is established, what appropriate relief can be granted ([11])?

R - Rules

The key constitutional provisions relevant to this case include:

  • Section 72(1)(a): Requires the NCOP to facilitate public involvement in its legislative processes ([14]).
  • Section 118(1)(a): Contains a similar requirement for provincial legislatures ([14]).
  • Section 167(4)(e): Grants exclusive jurisdiction to the Constitutional Court to determine whether Parliament has failed to fulfill its constitutional obligations ([10]).
  • Section 172(1)(a): Stipulates that the Court must declare any law inconsistent with the Constitution to be invalid ([202]).

The principles from cases such as King v Attorneys Fidelity Fund Board of Control ([14]) clarifying the interpretation of "constitutional obligations" particularly in relation to legislative processes also inform the Court's analysis.

A - Application

In its analysis, the Court first established its jurisdiction under section 167(4)(e) to review whether the NCOP had failed to fulfill its obligations under sections 72(1)(a) and 118(1)(a) ([16]-[23]). The Court emphasized that public involvement is a central component of legislative processes in a democracy and that the legislative bodies must not only facilitate but actively ensure participation.

The NCOP and provincial legislatures’ actions were scrutinized against the standard of reasonableness to determine whether the public was afforded proper opportunities to participate, particularly through public hearings and submissions ([125]-[145]). Evidence showed that while the NCOP intended for public hearings to be held, the execution was inconsistent: only a minority of provinces actually conducted hearings, undermining the overall public involvement process ([150]-[175]).

The failure to hold promised public hearings, especially for Bills of significant public interest, was deemed unreasonable, thus leading to the conclusion that both the Choice on Termination of Pregnancy Amendment Act and the Traditional Health Practitioners Act were passed without the necessary public involvement and were therefore invalid ([203]-[207]).

Referring to the nature of the legislation and the public interest in the respective health laws, the Court determined that the requirements for public participation were not met and that the failure to act accordingly led to a breach of constitutional imperatives ([178]-[201]).

C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the Court ruled unequivocally that the NCOP had failed to facilitate public involvement in the legislative processes regarding both the Choice on Termination of Pregnancy Amendment Act and the Traditional Health Practitioners Act. As a result, these statutes were declared invalid based on the failure of the NCOP to adhere to constitutional obligations ([204]-[206]). However, the invalidity declaration was suspended for 18 months to allow Parliament sufficient time to rectify the procedural shortcomings and enact the laws in compliance with the Constitution ([206]-[207]).

The Court's decision underscored the importance of public participation in South Africa’s legislative framework as a core democratic principle, and the ruling has significant implications for how legislative processes are conducted by the NCOP and provincial legislatures in future ([239]-[241]). The judgment not only affirms the role of the Constitutional Court as a guardian of democratic principles but also reinforces the necessity for legislatures to engage the public actively in their law-making processes.
