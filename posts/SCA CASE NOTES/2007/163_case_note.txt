# FIRAC Case Note: Hosmed Medical Aid Scheme v Thebe Ya Bophelo Healthcare [2007] SCA 163 (RSA)

## F – Facts

The case involves an appeal by the **Hos+Med Medical Aid Scheme** (the Appellant) against an order of the **Pretoria High Court**, which set aside an award made by an arbitration appeal tribunal ([para 1]). The dispute arose from an agreement under which the **Thebe Ya Bophelo Health Care Marketing and Consulting (Pty) Ltd** (the 1st Respondent) acted as a broker for Hosmed, leading to claims regarding fees payable to Thebe ([para 1]).

In a series of events, Hosmed initially entered into a broker agreement in November 1999, which was later amended to adjust fee structures in light of regulatory changes associated with the **Medical Schemes Act** and the associated regulations ([paras 4-7]). After sending invoices for fees relating to ongoing services that had not been claimed since the 2001 amendments, Thebe's CEO discovered no fees had been paid, leading to litigation ([paras 8, 8.1]).

The arbitration agreement mandated that disputes be resolved through arbitration and allowed appeals to a designated tribunal ([para 10]). During arbitration, both parties raised several issues, including whether Thebe was precluded from claiming fees based on a misunderstanding of the regulatory amendments and compliance with **Section 228** of the **Companies Act** [61 of 1973] ([para 14]).

The arbitrator ruled in favor of Thebe, but Hosmed appealed the decision to a tribunal, which upheld the arbitrator's findings while introducing a discussion on the issue of "unanimous assent" related to company business disposal ([para 20]). Following the tribunal's ruling, Thebe sought to set aside the award, asserting that the tribunal exceeded its powers by considering the issue of unanimous assent, which had not been expressly pleaded ([para 21]).

## I – Issues

The core legal question in this appeal revolved around whether the arbitration appeal tribunal had exceeded its powers or committed a gross irregularity in considering the issue of unanimous assent, which had not been specifically pleaded ([para 3, 28]). The relevant statutory provisions under scrutiny included:

1. Whether the tribunal’s finding on unanimous assent contravened the **Arbitration Act** [42 of 1965] (particularly s 33).
2. The implication of the tribunal's decision in relation to the defined pleadings and the consequences that followed ([paras 27-29]).

## R – Rules

The judgment primarily deals with the interpretation and application of the **Arbitration Act** [42 of 1965], specifically Section 33, which allows for setting aside arbitration awards under certain conditions, including misconduct or exceeding powers ([para 27]). The court also referred to relevant case law, such as *Shill v Milner* [1937] AD 101, which addresses the latitude in pleadings in civil proceedings, and interpreted its application in an arbitration context ([para 31]).

The court emphasized that the powers of arbitrators are confined to the stipulations within the arbitration agreement, asserting that any decision-making outside of established pleadings is impermissible and can lead to the setting aside of the tribunal's decisions ([para 30]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the arbitration appeal tribunal's use of evidence presented by Hosmed, particularly regarding the issue of unanimous assent. It found that the tribunal appraised this evidence while neglecting that the specific issue of unanimous assent had not been pleaded, thereby arguably exceeding its jurisdiction ([para 32, 35]).

Furthermore, the SCA highlighted that even if the tribunal’s assessment of the evidence suggested that unanimous assent existed, the manner in which it had been raised was far from exhaustive or explicit enough throughout the proceedings ([paras 33-34]). The court concluded that the tribunal had failed to adhere to the procedural rules governing the arbitration agreement, which correctly mandated adherence to presented pleadings ([para 35]).

The court ultimately affirmed that the tribunal had exceeded its powers by delving into an unpleaded issue. Given that the inquiry into unanimous assent did not fall within the ambit of the arbitrator’s authority, the appeal tribunal lacked the jurisdiction to make a finding on that matter ([para 36]).

## C – Conclusion

The SCA set aside the tribunal’s award based on its finding that it had exceeded its authority under the **Arbitration Act**, marking a significant ruling on the procedural boundaries of arbitration ([para 46]). The court reiterated that the arbitration agreement explicitly delineated the issues to be adjudicated and that deviations from that construct undermine the integrity of the arbitration process ([para 30]).

As a consequence, the matter was remitted to a new arbitration tribunal, as prescribed under s 33(4) of the Arbitration Act, to explore the issues afresh, ensuring adherence to the formal procedural requirements laid forth in the original arbitration agreement ([paras 42-44]). The judgment illustrates the critical balance between the need for procedural precision in arbitration and the parties' agreed-upon terms, influencing future arbitration proceedings in South Africa. 

In conclusion, while Hosmed's appeal partly succeeded in seeking a new tribunal, it highlighted the paramount importance of clarity in arbitration agreements and strict adherence by arbitrators to the terms set forth by the parties involved ([para 46]).
