Supreme Court of Appeal

National Credit Regulator v Dacqup Finances CC trading as ABC Financial Services – Pinetown and Another (382/21) [2022] ZASCA 104

[2] The NCR applied to the National Consumer Tribunal (the Tribunal), which found that Dacqup had contravened various sections of the NCA, leading to a fine and the order for appointing an independent auditor to assess the extent of Dacqup’s overcharging practices.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 104

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

[1] The appellant, the National Credit Regulator (the NCR), initiated a complaint against Dacqup Finances CC, trading as ABC Financial Services – Pinetown (Dacqup). This complaint was based on alleged contraventions of the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (the NCA), which was enacted to protect consumers in the credit market. The NCR's investigation following a 'scouting exercise' revealed that Dacqup was apparently overcharging on interest rates and failing to conduct proper affordability assessments for customers. Specifically, an inspector noticed a sign advertising 'instant loans' and posed as a potential customer, discovering that Dacqup charged an interest rate of 30% per month, exceeding the statutory maximum permissible rate.

[2] The NCR applied to the National Consumer Tribunal (the Tribunal), which found that Dacqup had contravened various sections of the NCA, leading to a fine and the order for appointing an independent auditor to assess the extent of Dacqup’s overcharging practices. Dacqup appealed to the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which upheld Dacqup's point in limine regarding the lack of reasonable suspicion to initiate the NCR’s investigation, leading to this appeal.

[3] The relevant statutory provisions of the NCA include sections 136, 80, and 81, mandating certain requirements for credit providers to avoid engaging in reckless credit practices. The regulatory backdrop of this case is notable, considering the NCA's purpose to curtail high costs of credit and protect vulnerable consumers from potentially deleterious lending practices.

I – Issues

[4] The core legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the high court correctly held that the NCR had failed to establish reasonable suspicion to initiate an investigation into Dacqup’s conduct ([para 10]). A further issue concerned the Tribunal's competence to order Dacqup to appoint an auditor to assess its credit practices and reimburse consumers ([paras 29-30]).

R – Rules

[5] The legal framework underpinning this case is found primarily in the NCA, particularly sections 80 and 81, concerning reckless credit practices, and section 136, permitting the NCR to initiate complaints based on reasonable suspicion of contraventions of the Act ([para 4]). The defined standard for reasonable suspicion, as characterized in South African law, is a low bar that exists when there is a factual basis to support the suspicion — it must be more than mere conjecture or a hunch ([paras 19-20]). This concept is crucial in administrative oversight, especially against the backdrop of protecting consumer rights within the credit industry.

A – Application

[6] The Supreme Court of Appeal examined the facts surrounding the NCR's initiation of the investigation. The court emphasized that reasonable suspicion, as articulated in previous legal precedents, does not demand prima facie proof; rather, it necessitates a factual foundation that could reasonably justify the regulator's concerns regarding Dacqup’s conduct ([paras 21-27]). The court critiqued the high court’s reliance solely on the wording of an advertisement, stating that it overly conflated reasonable suspicion with the necessity for prima facie evidence. The appearance of 'instant loans' could reasonably infer a lack of stringent compliance with lending regulations ([para 26]).

[7] The court reinforced the necessity for a regulatory body like the NCR to operate within a framework that allows it to take proactive steps based on reasonable grounds, particularly in contexts where consumer protection is paramount, and where the potential harms of noncompliance can affect vulnerable populations ([para 23]). Hence, the court concluded that Ms Mbedzi’s observations regarding both the interest rate and the nature of the advertisements provided sufficient grounds for the initiation of an investigation ([paras 25-28]).

C – Conclusion

[8] The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the high court's decision, thereby reinstating the Tribunal's orders. The court ruled that there was indeed reasonable suspicion for the NCR to initiate the investigation into Dacqup's activities and that it was competent for the Tribunal to order the appointment of an independent auditor ([paras 31-32]). This decision underscores the SNC's mandate to protect consumers, illustrating the judiciary's support for robust regulatory frameworks aimed at ensuring compliance within the credit industry. Furthermore, the court mandated that costs followed the result, reinforcing that statutory bodies fulfilling their duties should not be penalized financially unless acting in bad faith ([para 31]).

This thorough examination of the case, structured accordingly, presents a cohesive understanding of the legal principles and practical implications stemming from the judgment. The emphasis on consumer protection and adequate regulatory oversight strengthens the legal precedent while addressing issues pertinent to financial practices within South Africa’s micro-lending sector.
