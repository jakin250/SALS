Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note: Solidarity v The Public Health & Welfare Sectoral Bargaining Council (442/13) [2014] ZASCA 70

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

In this case, Jacobus Adriaan Hendrik Kotze (the employee, second appellant) was employed by the Department of Health: Free State (the employer, third respondent) for 17 years before being placed on a 'precautionary suspension' on 4 July 2007 pending an investigation into misconduct allegations ([1]). On 23 July 2007, during his suspension, he accepted employment without permission with a firm called Compu Africa, owned by a relative ([1]). 

On 19 October 2007, the employer issued a letter stating that the employee was deemed discharged from the public service effective 3 July 2007 under Section 17(5)(a) of the Public Service Act (PSA) 103 of 1994 due to his acceptance of alternative employment while still in service ([2]). Following unsuccessful attempts to challenge this decision internally, the matter was referred to the Public Health and Welfare Sectoral Bargaining Council (the first respondent) for arbitration ([3]). The commissioner, CL Dickens NO (the second respondent), ruled that the Bargaining Council lacked jurisdiction to hear the case as the termination was not classified as a dismissal under the Labour Relations Act ([4]).

After a dismissal of the matter in the Labour Court ([5]), the appellants appealed to the Labour Appeal Court (LAC), which upheld the lower court's findings ([6]). This appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) followed with special leave ([7]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case concerned whether:

1. Jacobus Kotze's acceptance of alternative employment while on suspension constituted an absence without permission, leading to a deemed discharge under the PSA ([6]).
2. The Bargaining Council had jurisdiction to adjudicate the matter as the employee argued there had been a dismissal ([4][6]).
3. The interpretation of Section 17(5) of the PSA was correctly applied by the lower courts ([10][12]).

### R – Rules

The case was primarily grounded in the interpretation and application of several key provisions of the Public Service Act 103 of 1994:
- **Section 17(5)**: Provides conditions under which an officer who absents themselves without permission or assumes other employment without permission shall be deemed discharged ([9]).
- **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995**: Outlines the rights of employees concerning dismissal and unfair dismissal, including processes for arbitration and burden of proof ([8]).

Relevant case law included:
- *Minister van Onderwys en Kultuur v Louw* 1995 (4) SA 383 (A)
- *Grootboom v National Prosecution Authority* 2014 (1) BCLR 65 (CC)
- *Masinga v Minister of Justice, Kwazulu Government* [1995] 2 All SA 350 (A)

### A – Application

The SCA critically analyzed the application of Section 17(5). It clarified that the employee's absence as a suspended individual did not equate to being absent without permission because the suspension itself was an action taken by the employer ([10][11]). The court highlighted the distinction between a discharge that occurs by operation of law versus one initiated by an employer's decision ([10]). 

It noted that Kotze’s actions should not be viewed as an absence without permission since he was not required to report for duty due to his suspension and thus could not have “absented himself” as contemplated by the PSA ([10][11]). The interpretation by the LAC that his accepting alternative employment acted as a resignation was found untenable; the court emphasized the temporary nature of his new employment being compatible with his suspended status as it did not inherently conflict with his duties to the Department ([11]).

Furthermore, the SCA noted that merely assuming other employment does not automatically trigger a deemed discharge unless it satisfies the specific statutory requirements articulated in Section 17(5)(a)(i) and (ii) ([13]). The actual communication from the employer, which did not substantively identify a dismissal but rather a statutory consequence of the employee's actions, played a key role in the interpretation ([10][13]).

### C – Conclusion

The SCA concluded that the Labour Appeal Court's decision was based on a misinterpretation of the law regarding the deemed discharge provisions. Consequently, the Court held that the Bargaining Council does indeed have jurisdiction to adjudicate the matter as it constitutes a dismissal under the ambit of the applicable laws. Therefore, the SCA upheld the appeal, set aside the orders of the LAC, and remitted the dispute back to the Bargaining Council for proper arbitration of the matter ([17]).

This case elucidates the subtle nuances in the interpretation of statutory provisions related to employment status during disciplinary procedures and reinforces the notion that the framework surrounding public employment is intricately tied to the obligations of both the employee and employer, particularly during periods of suspension. The implications of this ruling could foster a more structured approach to handling similar disputes in the public sector while emphasizing the necessity for clear procedural adherence by employers in discharging employees.
