Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: **The Head of Department: Department of Education, Free State Province v Welkom High School & Harmony High School (766 & 767/2011) [2012] ZASCA 150**

## F – Facts

The case involves two appeals which stemmed from directives issued by the Head of the Department (HOD) of Education in the Free State regarding the treatment of learner pregnancies at Welkom High School and Harmony High School ([2]–[3]). 

The governing body of Welkom High School adopted a pregnancy management policy on November 20, 2008, and it took effect on January 1, 2009. Similarly, Harmony High School implemented its pregnancy policy to regulate the educational engagement of pregnant learners ([3]). A significant dispute arose when both schools informed students Ms D and Ms M, who had faced pregnancy-related exclusions, of their policies that led to their temporary removal from school. 

In Ms D's case, the HOD issued a directive on October 28, 2010, instructing the school principal to allow her immediate return. Conversely, Harmony High School decided not to readmit Ms M, who had given birth, asserting their policy prohibited resuming studies within the same academic year ([4]–[5]). 

Both schools subsequently sought urgent remedies in the Free State High Court, leading to an order that the HOD could not compel the schools to act contrary to their governing policies. The HOD’s appeal challenged these declarations and sought to assert authority over issues related to learner pregnancies ([6]–[7]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case include:

1. Whether the HOD had the authority under the South African Schools Act 84 of 1996 to override the governing bodies of the schools regarding their adopted pregnancy policies ([6]–[7]);
2. The distinction between a direct challenge and a collateral challenge to administrative decisions, specifically whether the HOD could raise such challenges regarding the schools’ pregnancy policies ([12]–[13]);
3. Whether the High Court was obliged to address the constitutionality of the schools' pregnancy policies invoked by the HOD ([17]–[20]);
4. The implications of the principle of legality in the exercise of public power by the HOD ([25]–[26]).

## R – Rules

The central legal framework relies on the South African Schools Act 84 of 1996, which delineates the powers and responsibilities of school governing bodies, particularly in relation to their autonomy in educational governance ([8]–[10]). 

Key provisions include:

- Section 20(1)(d), which mandates governing bodies to adopt codes of conduct, implying that policies regarding learner management—such as pregnancy—rest with the governing body.
- Principles articulated in *Oudekraal Estates Pty Ltd v City of Cape Town* that invalid administrative actions, unless set aside, maintain their legal consequences ([23]).
- Case law from *Head of Department, Mpumalanga Department of Education v Hoërskool Ermelo* establishing that the governance of schools, including policy adoption, primarily falls under the autonomous decision-making of the governing bodies ([8]–[10]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) engaged significantly with the nuances of administrative power and the principle of legality. The Court rejected the HOD’s claim of authority to override school governance, asserting that even if the governing bodies’ policies were potentially unlawful, the HOD lacked the jurisdiction to compel adherence to his directives contrary to said policies ([21]–[22]).

1. **Authority of the HOD**: The court reinforced that the governance matters, including pregnancy policies, rest solely with the governing bodies. The HOD’s directives that aimed to counteract these policies were deemed unauthorized, leading to an unlawful exercise of power ([23]–[24]).

2. **Direct vs. Collateral Challenge**: The SCA elucidated that the HOD's actions constituted a direct challenge to the validity of the governing bodies' decisions rather than a collateral challenge since he sought to alter the effects of the administrative decisions without a counter-application or proper judicial review, violating the principle of administrative law ([12]–[14]).

3. **Constitutional Arguments**: The court found the HOD's argument regarding the constitutionality of the policies irrelevant as he failed to initiate legitimate proceedings to challenge their legality. The procedural issue of the HOD's authority and the governing body's powers overshadowed any substantive constitutional debate regarding the policies themselves ([19]–[20]).

## C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the SCA dismissed the appeals lodged by the HOD, asserting his unlawful overreach into the governing bodies' domains. The court reaffirmed the integrity of the governing bodies’ authority to enact and maintain policies concerning learner management, including those related to pregnancy, and emphasized the requisite legal processes for challenging such policies. 

The implications of this ruling underline the necessity for administrative bodies to operate within the parameters of their legal authority while reinforcing the independence of educational governance structures in South Africa. The correct procedural avenues for challenging potentially unconstitutional policies were affirmed, providing clarity on the distinction between direct and collateral challenges in public administrative law ([26]–[29]).
