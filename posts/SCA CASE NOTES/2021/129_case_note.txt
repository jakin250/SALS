Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Nimble Investments (Pty) Ltd v Johanna Malan and Others (556/2020) [2021] ZASCA 129

### F – Facts
The appeal in question concerns the eviction of the first to eighth respondents from a property owned by the appellant, Nimble Investments (Pty) Ltd. The appellant purchased the farm known as Topshell Park in 2008, inheriting an existing lease with the first respondent, Johanna Malan, who had been living there since 1974 due to her late husband’s employment ([1]-[3]). Following a series of negotiations regarding relocation, the respondents ultimately moved from cottage 1 to cottage 5 on November 28, 2016. However, during this relocation, members of the Malan family unlawfully removed building materials from cottage 1 and began erecting an illegal structure ([7]). 

The appellant, viewing this as a fundamental breach of their relationship under s 10(1)(c) of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA), subsequently terminated the respondents' right of residence without affording them the opportunity to make representations as required under s 8(1)(e) of ESTA ([8]-[10]). The eviction order was first granted by the Stellenbosch Magistrates' Court but was later set aside by the Land Claims Court (LCC), which found the first respondent to be a long-term occupier under the protection of ESTA and cited the lack of opportunity for representations ([10]-[12]).

### I – Issues
The central issues presented for determination in this appeal were:

1. **Whether the termination of the respondents' right of residence was just and equitable**, both in terms of substance and procedure as prescribed by ESTA ([12]).
   
2. **Whether the eviction itself was justified under the same standards**, particularly concerning a fundamental breach of trust as outlined in s 10(1)(c) of ESTA ([11]-[12]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework guiding the case includes:

1. **Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA)**: Specifically, s 8(1) regarding the conditions under which an occupier’s right of residence may be terminated and s 10(1)(c) concerning fundamental breaches of the occupier-owner relationship, which state that the owner may seek eviction if such a breach renders the relationship impossible to restore.

2. **Case Law Precedents**: The Court referred to *Snyders v De Jager* ([2016] ZACC 55), which underscores the necessity for procedural fairness in termination of residence rights, as well as *Klaase and Another v Van Der Merwe* ([2016] ZACC 17) to establish the metrics around fundamental breaches and their implications for potential eviction orders ([20]-[21]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) undertook a comprehensive assessment of the evidence and procedural adherence throughout the eviction process. In doing so, it emphasized two main considerations: 

1. **Procedural Fairness**: The Court upheld that the failure to invite the first to ninth respondents to make representations before ousting them constituted a serious procedural violation, rendering the termination of their occupancy rights unlawful ([24]). This echoed the requirements outlined in *Snyders*, reaffirming that any termination must be both just and equitable, taking into account not only individual circumstances but also the rights of the occupiers to due process.

2. **Fundamental Breach**: The evidence indicated that Mrs Malan and her family had indeed committed conduct amounting to a fundamental breach of trust by unlawfully removing materials and constructing an unauthorized structure. The SCA found that the seriousness of this breach meant that it was not practically possible to restore the relationship of trust between the parties ([59]-[62]). However, due to the preceding failure to provide procedural fairness prior to the eviction’s initiation, the Court decided first on the unlawful termination of occupancy rights, thus making the subsequent eviction proceedings illegitimate ([60]). 

### C – Conclusion
The SCA ultimately upheld the appeal. It reversed the Land Claims Court's decision and reinstated the eviction order. The legal findings highlighted the importance of procedural fairness in property law, particularly in evictions under ESTA. The Court directed that the respondents vacate the farm by March 31, 2022, and required that the local municipality provide them with emergency housing ([76]). This case reinforces the application of procedural protections and continues to elucidate the balance between the rights of landowners and those of long-term occupiers, consequently establishing a significant precedent in South African land reform and tenancy law.

**Order**: The appeal is upheld; eviction order reinstated with specific directives for municipal housing provision ([76]).
