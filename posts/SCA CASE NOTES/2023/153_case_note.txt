Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Forestry South Africa v Minister of Human Settlements, Water and Sanitation and Others (777/2022) & Minister of Human Settlements, Water and Sanitation and Others v Forestry South Africa (824/2022)

**Court**: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Date Delivered**: 15 November 2023  
**Judges**: Unterhalter AJA, Mothle JA, Mocumie JA, Weiner AJA, Windell AJJA  
**Neutral Citation**: [2023] ZASCA 153  

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts ([1]–[34])

The appeals concern the interpretation of provisions within the **National Water Act 36 of 1998**. The first appellant, **Forestry South Africa (Forestry SA)**, is a non-profit organization advocating for the timber industry, which sought declaratory relief regarding water use rights. The respondents include various statutory authorities involved in water management, namely the **Minister of Human Settlements, Water and Sanitation**, and catchment management agencies.

Forestry SA's application originated in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, where it argued for an authoritative interpretation of existing lawful water uses, particularly concerning stream flow reduction activities stipulated under s 32 of the National Water Act. These activities pertain to afforestation intended for commercial use, as defined in s 36 of the Act. The high court granted partial relief to Forestry SA but dismissed aspects of its claim regarding species limitations and the necessity for lawful water use under prior regulations.

Both parties sought leave to appeal: Forestry SA over the refusal for greater declaratory relief, and the statutory authorities concerning points of law and interpretations that were unfavorable to them. The appeals were consolidated for hearing before the Supreme Court of Appeal.

### I – Issues ([32]–[34])

1. **Recognition Issue**: Whether an existing lawful water use under s 32(1)(a)(ii) pertaining to stream flow reduction activities is subject to prior lawful use requirement.
   
2. **Species Issue**: Whether obligations under s 34(1)(a) restrict existing lawful water use of stream flow reduction activities to the planting of specific tree species.

3. **Regulatory Competence Appeal**: The extent of the regulatory authority's powers under s 35 concerning the verification of existing lawful water use, particularly the impact of changing tree species on the verification process.

### R – Rules ([35]–[48])

The court primarily interprets the following statutes:  
1. **National Water Act 36 of 1998**  
   - **Section 32**: Defines existing lawful water use and outlines categories, including flow activities and controlled activities.
   - **Section 34**: Conditions for continued use of existing lawful water use.
   - **Section 35**: Procedures for verifying existing lawful water use, including lawfulness and extent.

2. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa**  
   - Sections emphasizing property rights and the right to participate in the judicial process (Section 38) and the right to access to water (Section 27).

The court also considers prior legislation, namely the **Forest Act** and the interplay of old order law regarding land use and water rights, which guide judicial interpretations on lawfulness and authorisation requirements for water use.

### A – Application ([49]–[82])

The court applied an interpretative framework to address the recognition issue. It ruled that existing lawful water use under s 32(1)(a)(ii) does not necessitate prior authorisation under old law. The court contended that distinct categories of existing lawful water use must remain separate, and each category, including flow activity, should be interpreted on its merits.

For the species issue, it was argued that the obligations outlined in s 34(1)(a) do not impose limitations on the types of species that can be planted, except where specific conditions are derived from law prior to the commencement of the Act. The court maintained that any limitations imposed by the previous legislation that affect the rights of actors under the new Act should be explicitly noted to avoid misunderstanding.

Notably, in the regulatory competence appeal, the court concluded that verification under s 35 should solely assess the extent of land used for afforestation activities rather than water consumption. The court rejected the statutory authorities’ argument that genus exchange affects the verification of lawful use.

### C – Conclusion ([83]–[101])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled largely in favor of Forestry SA, holding that:

1. **Recognition Issue**: Existing lawful water use for stream flow reduction activities is not subject toauthorisation as stipulated in prior law.
   
2. **Species Issue**: The obligations under s 34(1)(a) do not enforce species limitations unless such limitations existed at the commencement of the Act.

3. **Regulatory Competence Appeal**: Verification processes under s 35 are restricted to assessing land use without consideration of the specific genus or species planted unless previous legal restrictions apply.

The court's interpretations not only clarified the rights of Forestry SA but also established a precedent governing how regulatory agencies can approach water use verification in the future. Both appeals were ordered concerning the costs, solidifying the implications of the Act concerning both existing lawful use rights and the regulatory powers vested in statutory authorities. 

### Implications
This judgment has far-reaching consequences for the timber industry in South Africa, determining the thresholds and standards for water use and resource management under current legislation. It underscores the importance of statutory clarity in areas where private interests intersect with environmental regulations.
