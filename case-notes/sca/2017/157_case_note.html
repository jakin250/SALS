Minister of Defence and Military Veterans v Mamasedi (622/2017) [2017] ZASCA 157

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 157

F – Facts

The appellant, the Minister of Defence and Military Veterans, alongside the Chief of the South African National Defence Force (SANDF), contested a decision affecting Mr. Jonas Molefi Mamasedi, who, as a sergeant in the SANDF, was implicated in a procedural neglect leading to his dismissal. Mamasedi was deemed dismissed under section 59(3) of the Defence Act 42 of 2002 after failing to report for duty from 29 November 2011 to 18 January 2012 ([paras 1-2]). Upon discovery of his dismissal, Mamasedi sought recourse through a grievance procedure, asserting that his absence was due to abduction and initiation ([paras 4-6]).

On 18 January 2012, a board of enquiry was convened to investigate his absence, recommending against his re-instatement in a report received by Mamasedi on 11 July 2013 ([paras 8, 10]). Mamasedi contested this recommendation, advocating for a review in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which ruled in his favor, setting aside the Chief of the SANDF's decision and ordering his re-instatement along with retroactive salary from 15 December 2011 ([paras 1, 2]).

The appellants sought to overturn this ruling, claiming procedural unfairness and misapplication of statutory powers in the High Court's order ([paras 24-27]).

I – Issues

The case raises two principal issues:

  1. Whether the Chief of the SANDF's decision not to re-instate Mamasedi was rendered procedurally unfair due to his exclusion from the enquiry proceedings, violating principles of natural justice and applicable legislative provisions under the Defence Act and the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([paras 12, 22]).
  2. If the Chief's decision was indeed procedurally unfair, whether the re-instatement order made by the High Court was appropriate within the context of the powers allocated to courts versus administrators ([para 23]).

R – Rules

The relevant legislation and case law considerations in this decision include:

  • Defence Act 42 of 2002, specifically Section 59(3), which provides that a member absent from duty without permission for over 30 days is deemed dismissed ([para 2]).
  • Section 102 of the Defence Act, which details the procedural regulations governing board enquiries, emphasizing that individuals adversely affected by recommendations have the right to present their case and be represented ([paras 19-21]).
  • PAJA, particularly Section 3, which ensures that administrative actions materially affecting rights must comply with procedural fairness standards ([para 16]).
  • Relevant case law highlighting the importance of procedural fairness in administrative matters, including Minister of Health & another NO v New Clicks South Africa (Pty) Ltd ([para 14]) and the PAJA guidelines concerning procedural rights ([para 18]).

A – Application

In addressing the first issue, the court analyzed whether Mamasedi's exclusion from the board of enquiry contravened his rights to procedural fairness. It affirmed that his right to be heard was not just a formal necessity but a substantive right that aligned with both the Defence Act’s provisions and the principles established under the PAJA. The court concluded Mamasedi’s exclusion from hearing or challenging evidence against him violated statutory rights established in Section 102, rendering the Chief's decision procedurally unfair ([paras 22-23]).

Regarding the second issue, the court articulated that when a reviewing court sets aside an administrative decision, it typically does not substitute its judgment unless exceptional circumstances exist, as prescribed by Section 8(1) of the PAJA. The ruling underscored that the court lacked the requisite expertise to directly re-instate Mamasedi, and a factual dispute existed regarding his actions during the questioned period ([paras 24-27]). This necessitated that the Chief of the SANDF reassess Mamasedi's case upon proper evidence examination ([para 25]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of the appellants with partial success, holding that while the Chief of SANDF’s dismissal decision was procedurally unfair and correctly set aside, the High Court's order for re-instatement was inappropriate given the lack of discretion possessed by the judiciary to usurp administrative powers. The initial dismissal by operation of law under Section 59(3) was reaffirmed, explaining the necessity for administrative powers to be exercised properly following a comprehensive hearing ([paras 28-30]).

The court ordered the High Court’s re-instatement directive set aside, amending the order to maintain the Chief of the SANDF’s discretion to reconsider Mamasedi's application while ordering the appellants to bear costs for the initial proceedings ([para 30]).

The findings underscore the vital interplay between administrative justice and employment law within military institutions, reinforcing procedural fairness’ importance in administrative actions, even in a military context ([paras 12, 22]).
