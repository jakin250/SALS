General Index - CERTIFICATION OF THE CONSTITUTION OF THE REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA, 1996 (CCT 23/96).

The objectors, comprising political parties and civil groups, submitted numerous written and oral representations highlighting perceived inadequacies in the new text (NT).

Constitutional Court 1996 CCT 23/96

F - Facts

The Constitutional Court of South Africa was tasked with certifying whether all provisions of the proposed new constitution complied with specific Constitutional Principles as outlined in the Interim Constitution. This certification process was a unique task, necessitated by the need for legitimacy amid South Africa’s transition from apartheid to a democratic regime ([1–4]).

The objectors, comprising political parties and civil groups, submitted numerous written and oral representations highlighting perceived inadequacies in the new text (NT). The Constitutional Assembly adopted the NT on 8 May 1996, and it was submitted for certification ([20–21]). The Court's certification focused on assessing compliance with the 34 Constitutional Principles ([15]).

I - Issues

The court needed to address several constitutional questions:

  1. Whether the NT contained sufficient provisions for the protection of human rights as expressed in the CPs ([5–12]).
  2. How effectively the NT established a clear separation of powers between the legislative, executive, and judiciary as outlined in CP VI ([106–111]).
  3. The adequacy of provisions related to provincial autonomy under CPs XVIII and XX ([306–316]).
  4. Consistency with international human rights standards particularly regarding socio-economic rights and the Bill of Rights ([49–79]).

The overarching legal question was whether the NT was compliant with the Constitutional Principles and thus could be certified as the supreme law of South Africa.

R - Rules

The judicial review was guided by the following key rules and constitutional provisions:

  1. Constitutional Principles (CPs): Established foundational tenets for the drafting of the NT, particularly emphasizing democracy, human rights, and the rule of law ([11]).
  2. Separation of Powers: The requirement for an appropriate separation between the three branches of government was articulated in CP VI, which stipulates the existence of checks and balances ([106–111]).
  3. Provincial Autonomy: CPs XVIII and XX ensured provincial powers and functions must not be substantially inferior to those outlined in the Interim Constitution, ensuring effective governance at the provincial level ([230–236]).
  4. Justiciability of Rights: CP II required the rights be justiciable and embedded within the constitutional framework, thereby providing citizens the ability to seek redress ([48–49]).

The Judicial Service Commission’s mandate and the role of the Public Protector were also examined as relevant to maintaining judicial integrity ([119–124], [161–167]).

A - Application

The Court meticulously examined the objections raised against the NT provisions and applied the Constitutional Principles to the facts of the case. In its analysis, the Court found several areas where the NT failed to comply with the CPs:

  1. Bill of Rights: It determined that the Bill of Rights, particularly in terms of its horizontal application and the rights of individuals to engage in collective bargaining, did not cover necessary rights adequately ([23–24], [8(2), 23]).
  2. Powers of the Executive: The Court found provisions allowing the President considerable powers without sufficient checks raised concerns under CP IV regarding the supremacy of the constitution and the separation of powers ([114–122]).
  3. Provincial Autonomy: It identified that aspects guiding provincial legislative frameworks were insufficiently defined, recognizing that the powers were “substantially less” than those provided for in the Interim Constitution ([472–473]).
  4. Public Institutions: The provisions for the independence of essential institutions such as the Public Protector and the Auditor-General were inadequate, thus failing to comply with CP XXIX ([484]).

Where certain sections indicated potential diminutions of the provinces’ power (especially regarding policing and public service commissions), the Court weighed these against the powers that remained intact or were augmented ([457–460], [470–474]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that while the NT materially fulfills many requirements of the CPs, it does not fully comply, particularly in critical areas affecting rights, provincial autonomy, and institutional independence ([482]).

The final judgment was as follows:

  • Provisions NT 241(1) which shielded the Labour Relations Act from constitutional scrutiny could not be certified as compliant with the CPs.
  • The powers and functions of the provinces were found to be significantly inferior in several respects compared to their previous rights under the Interim Constitution.
  • The NT contained notable achievements but ultimately, the Court could not certify the provisions as aligned with the Constitution's requirements.

The Court's order denied the certification of the NT, emphasizing the need for revisions to ensure compliance with the CPs. This pivotal decision underlined the importance of a constitution that genuinely represents the democratic aspirations of all South Africans.
