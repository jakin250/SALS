Constitutional Court

Twee Jonge Gezellen (Pty) Ltd and Others v Land and Agricultural Development Bank of South Africa

This case concerns an application for leave to appeal against a judgment from the Western Cape High Court concerning the provisional sentence procedure.

Constitutional Court 2011

F – Facts

This case concerns an application for leave to appeal against a judgment from the Western Cape High Court concerning the provisional sentence procedure. The applicants, Twee Jonge Gezellen (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter referred to as “Twee Jonge”) and Nicolas Charles Krone, allege that provisional sentence, as established by common law and governed by Rule 8 of the Uniform Rules of Court, is inconsistent with their constitutional rights (specifically, the rights to fair hearing and equality) under sections 34 and 9(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa 1996 ([1]–[2]).

Twee Jonge operates a wine farm, and Krone is its director. In 2005, both applicants signed an acknowledgment of debt in favor of the first respondent, the Land and Agricultural Development Bank of South Africa (the “Land Bank”), confirming a liability of R39 714 027.01 and agreeing to pay via installments. Their failure to adhere to the payment schedule led to the Bank’s initiation of provisional sentence proceedings ([5]–[6]).

Following issuance of the provisional sentence summons by the Land Bank on 26 November 2008, the applicants claimed that an oral agreement reduced their debt to R20 million and that the Land Bank had promised to extend time for payment, which the Bank denied. The High Court ruled against the applicants, finding their defenses unmeritorious, and ultimately the Supreme Court of Appeal declined to hear their appeal ([7]–[10]).

In response to their constitutional challenge, which they later refined, the applicants sought to have the provisional sentence procedure declared unconstitutional, asserting that it unfairly barred defendants from delivering their case when they could not provide immediate payment of the judgment ([24]–[30]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues arise from the applicants' argument that the existing provisional sentence procedure is incompatible with the constitutional guarantees of a fair hearing and equality under sections 34 and 9(1) of the Constitution. The Court thus needed to determine:

  1. Whether the common law remedy of provisional sentence constitutes an unconstitutional limitation on a defendant’s right to a fair hearing.
  2. Whether the standard requirements of provisional sentence unduly favor particular classes or types of litigants, consequently breaching the right to equality before the law ([24]–[28]).

R – Rules

The legal framework for provisional sentence is rooted in common law and further articulated through Rule 8 of the Uniform Rules of Court, which mandates that such a sentence can be granted only on the basis of a liquid document, requiring the plaintiff to establish their claim ([12]–[13]):

  1. Section 34 of the Constitution - Guarantees the right to a fair public hearing before a competent court.
  2. Section 9(1) of the Constitution - Mandates that everyone is equal before the law and entitled to equal protection and benefit of the law.

In assessing the constitutionality of the provisional sentence mechanism, the Court considered precedents establishing that a fair hearing includes not just the right to appear in court but the opportunity to adequately present one’s case ([24], [40]).

Additionally, the Court analogized provisional sentence to summary judgments and other fast-track procedures, acknowledging that while they are designed to expedite court processes, they must also uphold fundamental rights of the defendant ([62]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the Court evaluated the constitutional challenge to provisional sentence. A crucial finding was that the existing mechanism could impose a barring effect on defendants unable to satisfy a provisional order, effectively precluding their entry into substantive proceedings to present defenses that could be valid under normal trial conditions ([43]).

The Court outlined circumstances where the limitation of rights exists: when defendants cannot show a defense on affidavit (thus needing oral evidence), when they lack the ability to pay the judgment, and when courts have no discretion to refuse provisional sentence outside defined ‘special circumstances’ ([50]–[52]).

The Court further recognized that the absence of discretion to consider the merits of a defense could lead to unjust outcomes. It held that the current provisional sentence system unjustly limited access to legal recourse, especially disadvantaging those without resources to immediately satisfy a court judgment, thereby failing to meet the constitutional standards of fairness and equality under the law ([66], [70]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, ultimately declaring the provisional sentence procedure inconsistent with the Constitution and invalid as it pertains to the absence of judicial discretion under specified circumstances. The judgment established that courts must have the discretion to refuse provisional sentence in cases where (1) the defendant demonstrates inability to pay the debt, (2) the likelihood of success in the main case is evenly balanced, and (3) there is a reasonable likelihood that oral evidence could sway the outcome ([78]).

This ruling signals a significant development in South African law, emphasizing the necessity of balancing procedural efficiency with the constitutional guarantees of fair access to justice. The decision implies that the courts are now required to ensure a fair procedural framework that allows for equitable consideration of defendants’ rights, marking a pivotal shift in adjudicating provisional sentence matters. Consequentially, this may foster a more equitable legal environment in civil proceedings, reinforcing the principles of justice and fairness embedded in the South African Constitution ([70], [77]).

The appeal was upheld with costs awarded against the applicants, affirming the Land Bank’s entitlement to recover its claim whilst concurrently reshaping procedural norms within the region’s legal landscape ([78]).
