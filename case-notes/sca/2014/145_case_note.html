Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 145 (26 September 2014) ## F – Facts ([pars 1-5]

Case Title:

Calvin v The State (962/2013) [2014] ZASCA 145 (26 September 2014)

F – Facts ([pars 1-5]):

The appellant, Netshivhodza Ndivhuwo Calvin, was charged with the rape of a six-year-old girl in Thohoyandou, Limpopo, on 10 March 2005. The crime took place after the appellant lured the complainant with sweets. When she refused, he forcibly took her to a nearby orchard and raped her. His apprehension followed as a young man, drawn by the victim's cries, intervened, causing Calvin to flee the scene. He turned himself in to the authorities the next day.

Initially, the appellant pleaded not guilty and was convicted on 5 October 2005, receiving a life imprisonment sentence the same day, under section 51 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, which mandated this sentence due to the victim's age. Calvin appealed his sentence based on claims that it was shockingly inappropriate and that substantial and compelling circumstances warranted a lesser sentence.

I – Issues ([pars 3-4]):

The primary legal questions involved in this appeal were:

  1. Whether the life imprisonment sentence was shockingly inappropriate in light of the circumstances surrounding the case.
  2. Whether substantial and compelling circumstances existed to justify a deviation from the mandatory minimum sentence for rape, particularly in consideration of the appellant's youthfulness and lack of relevant previous convictions.

R – Rules ([pars 7-9]):

The court relied on several key legal principles:

  1. Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, s 274(1) - A court can consider additional evidence before sentencing to inform its decision adequately.
  2. Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, s 51 - This provision prescribes life imprisonment for certain sexual offenses against minors but allows for deviations if the court finds substantial and compelling circumstances.
  3. S v Malgas ([2001] 2 SA 1222 (SCA), para 25) - Established that courts must assess whether a prescribed sentence is disproportionate to the individual case based on all relevant circumstances.
  4. S v Vilakazi ([2012] 6 SA 353 (SCA), para 15) - Affirmed that a sentencing court has a duty to determine the proportionality of the prescribed sentence concerning the seriousness of the offence and the offender's personal circumstances.

A – Application ([pars 10-22]):

In this case, the court found that the trial court failed to consider all relevant circumstances surrounding the appellant, including his age, lack of prior convictions, and the time spent in pre-trial custody. The initial judgment neglected to mention critical facts which were presented informally, such as his age (20 at the time of the crime) and the absence of pre-sentence reports that could have substantiated the appellant's claims of youthfulness and potential for rehabilitation.

The court emphasized the importance of evaluating whether the prescribed life sentence was proportionate. It noted that while the crime of raping a six-year-old is of utmost seriousness and warrants severe repercussions, the case's specific circumstances indicated that life imprisonment might be disproportionately harsh. Factors such as the absence of violent aggravation had to be considered, coupled with the acknowledgment of the psychological impact on the victim, albeit without physical injuries beyond a bruise and minor tearing.

The court concurred with precedent suggesting that youth can mitigate culpability, particularly when combined with other factors, such as remorse or family responsibilities. It thus leaned towards recognizing that a severe custodial sentence was warranted, but that the life imprisonment prescribed was not proportional given the appellant's youthfulness and the circumstances leading to the crime's commission.

C – Conclusion ([pars 23]):

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Calvin's appeal, concluding that the life imprisonment sentence was indeed shockingly inappropriate and disproportionate to the crime. The court substituted the sentence with a more fitting term of 20 years’ imprisonment, antedated to 5 October 2005. This decision reinforces the principles of proportionality in sentencing and underscores the need for courts to adequately consider the personal circumstances of offenders, especially youths, when determining appropriate sentences. The implications of this ruling are significant, reflecting the balance between upholding justice for the victim and recognizing the potential for rehabilitation of young offenders.
