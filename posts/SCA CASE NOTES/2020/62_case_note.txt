Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

### FIRAC Case Note for *Valor IT v Premier, North West Province and Others* ([2020] ZASCA 62)

#### F – Facts
The case concerns the appellant, Valor IT CC (VIT), which was engaged by the Department of Sports, Arts and Culture in the North West Province (the Department) to provide an Enterprise Content Management (ECM) system for record management. Initially, VIT submitted an unsolicited proposal, which led to a service delivery agreement (SDA) in October 2011 for a project worth R498,000, excluding VAT ([1]–[11]). The project’s scope expanded dramatically, and by 2014, the provincial government had paid VIT over R41 million, raising concern over adherence to public procurement regulations ([1], [19], [21]).

Following a series of agreements that purported to extend the scope of VIT's services, significant issues regarding the legality of the contracts arose. Notably, the agreements were executed without a proper tender process as mandated by section 217 of the Constitution, which requires procurement to be fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([1], [40]). The provincial government later sought to cancel the agreements, citing irregularities and compliance failures, leading to VIT filing for a declaratory order that the cancellation and associated claims for damages were unlawful ([2], [19]).

#### I – Issues
The case raises several legal issues, granting importance to the adherence to public procurement laws:
1. Whether the attorney for the provincial government had the authority to represent it in the appeal ([23]).
2. The legitimacy of the provincial government’s late filing of affidavits and whether condonation should be granted ([24]–[26]).
3. Whether the provincial government's delay in bringing its counter-application required condonation ([27]).
4. Whether the contracts awarded to VIT, including the SDA and subsequent extensions, were lawful ([28]).
5. The validity and implications of the settlement agreement made with VIT and its subsequent court order ([28], [44]).

#### R – Rules
The Supreme Court’s judgment heavily relies on provisions from the Constitution and related statutory frameworks. Section 217 of the Constitution stipulates that all procurement processes must be conducted fairly and transparently ([1], [40]). The Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 (PFMA) and the accompanying Treasury Regulations further stipulate the necessary procurement processes, which include mandatory competitive bidding for contracts exceeding R500,000 ([41], [43]).

The precedent case of *Allpay Consolidated Investment Holdings v Chief Executive Officer, South African Social Security Agency* underscores that compliance with established procurement law is essential for valid contract formation ([41]). Additionally, the case of *Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Asla Construction* reinforces that a settlement agreement involving an unlawful contract cannot be legitimized through judicial endorsement ([54]).

#### A – Application
The Court found that the contracts awarded to VIT did not comply with lawful procurement processes as specified in the Constitution and relevant legislation. The SDA and subsequent extensions were deemed invalid due to the absence of a competitive bidding process, a fact acknowledged through the pattern of increased spending without lawful authorization ([42], [43]).

Regarding the settlement agreement, the Court highlighted that the attempt to reclassify the contract as a “transversal term contract” was insufficient to remedy its inherent unlawfulness. The Court drew from *Gibson v Van der Walt* to assert that legal principles disallow repackaging unlawful contracts ([50]).

Ultimately, the Court upheld the decision of the lower court, which had found that the provincial government’s initial settlement with VIT was unlawful and thus not enforceable. The designation of the settlement agreement as a court order was also deemed improper since the underlying contractual framework it sought to validate was invalid ([56]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Court dismissed the appeal with costs, reinforcing the need for strict adherence to public procurement laws to preserve fairness and accountability in government contracts ([56]). The ruling underscores critical implications for public procurement practices, emphasizing that non-compliance not only invalidates agreements but also prevents judicial endorsement of unlawful contracts. This case is a reminder of the importance of adhering to established legal frameworks to protect public resources and uphold the integrity of government procurement processes. 

In conclusion, the decision demonstrates a rigorous interpretation of constitutional mandates regarding procurement, significantly impacting how public entities must conduct their contracting processes moving forward ([56]).
