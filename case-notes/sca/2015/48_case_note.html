Supreme Court of Appeal

Terblanche v The Minister of Safety and Security and Another

Citation: Terblanche v The Minister of Safety and Security and another (20006/2013) [2015] 48 ZASCA (27 March 2015)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] 48 ZASCA (27 March 2015) ## FIRAC Case Note ### F – Fact

Citation: Terblanche v The Minister of Safety and Security and another (20006/2013) [2015] 48 ZASCA (27 March 2015)

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

Ettienne Terblanche (the appellant), a farmer, sustained severe injuries on 20 July 2005 while in police custody, due to the negligent driving of police officers. This incident resulted in long-term damage to his earning capacity, prompting Terblanche to file a claim against the Minister of Safety and Security and Inspector M I Mogale (the first and second respondents, respectively). The trial court, led by Visser AJ, ruled that Terblanche failed to establish a loss of earning capacity sufficient for compensation. This decision was upheld by the full court. The proceedings before the Supreme Court of Appeal were limited to the issue of Terblanche's future loss of earnings ([paras 1–2]).

Terblanche's farming experience spanned nearly 20 years, and he derived his income solely from this occupation. His injuries rendered him unable to perform all required physical tasks on the farm. He had previously employed seven permanent workers and contemplated hiring an additional skilled individual to assist him post-injury, as he could no longer manage certain physically demanding activities ([paras 3–5]). Expert testimonies confirmed that Terblanche required a substitute for the physical aspects of farming that he could not handle. Actuarial calculations indicated that the costs of employing such a substitute amounted to R1,557,136.69 ([paras 8–10]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–2, 14–16])

The vital issue in this appeal was whether Terblanche adequately proved a claim for future loss of earning capacity against the respondents. The Supreme Court of Appeal was tasked with evaluating whether the lower courts had erred in their assessments regarding Terblanche’s loss of earning capacity and ability to substantiate such a claim ([paras 1, 14]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 15–17])

In addressing the quantification of loss of earning capacity, the court referred to pertinent legal standards and precedents. The case Rudman v Road Accident Fund 2003 (2) SA 234 (SCA) was cited to underscore the burden placed on the claimant to demonstrate a reduction in earning capacity that yields a pecuniary loss ([paras 15, 16]). Additionally, the court referenced President Insurance Company Ltd v Mathews 1992 (1) All SA 179; SA 1992 (1) (A) to affirm that the cost of employing a substitute could form the basis of damages claims arising from a plaintiff’s inability to perform their prior work ([paras 16, 17]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 6–10, 11–18])

The court evaluated Terblanche's evidence in light of the expert testimonies provided. Terblanche had demonstrated that his injuries significantly impaired his ability to perform the extensive range of physical tasks associated with managing his farm. The trial court originally concluded that existing employees could manage these tasks on Terblanche's behalf, effectively questioning the substantiation of his claim. However, the Supreme Court found that Terblanche provided uncontested expert evidence indicating the necessity of hiring additional skilled labor for specific tasks he could no longer perform due to his injuries.

In making its determination, the Supreme Court distinguished between the trial court’s reliance on the judgment in Rudman and Terblanche’s situation, where it was conclusively proven that additional labor costs were necessary to substitute for Terblanche’s diminished capacity. The actuarial computation was deemed appropriate, and the provided figures substantiated the claim for damages ([paras 17, 18]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([para 19, 20])

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that both the trial court and full court erred in ruling that Terblanche had not established a claim for loss of earning capacity. The appeal was upheld, and the court awarded Terblanche the quantified amount of R1,557,136.69 for future loss of earnings, while dismissing claims for past losses. This ruling emphasized the principle that a claimant can indeed recover damages based on the costs of substituted labor necessitated by an inability to perform prior capacities, thus providing crucial clarification on how future earning capacity claims should be substantiated in tort actions arising from personal injury ([paras 19, 20]).

Implications

This decision notably reinforces the evidentiary burden on claimants to provide comprehensive data regarding future earning capacity and substantiates the utility of actuarial evidence in such assessments. The ruling creates a precedent affirming that the costs associated with hiring skilled labor as a means of mitigation are compensable, providing critical legal clarity for similar personal injury cases in South Africa.

Overall, the court's judgment contributes to the broader discourse on tort law and the appropriate measures to ensure victims of negligence are fairly compensated, particularly when traditional means of income are compromised due to injury-related impairments.
