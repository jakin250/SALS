Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Jiba & another v The General Council of the Bar of South Africa and Mrwebi v The General Council of the Bar of South Africa (141/17 and 180/17) [2018] ZASCA 103

### FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])

The appellants, Nomgcobo Jiba and Lawrence Sithembiso Mrwebi, both officials of the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA), were subjected to an application by the General Council of the Bar (GCB) to strike their names from the roll of advocates. This action was initiated in April 2015, following various complaints regarding the appellants’ fitness to practice as advocates. The core issues stemmed from administrative misconduct during a high-profile prosecution involving Lieutenant-General Richard Mdluli, who faced multiple charges, including fraud and corruption.

Initially, the application sought to strike off both Jiba and Mrwebi based on their management of cases and alleged misconduct that reflected a lack of integrity required for practicing professionals ([paras 7–12]). The GCB asserted that both appellants were not fit and proper persons to retain their status as advocates due to their respective roles in withdrawing critical charges against Mdluli and failing to adhere to procedural requirements in legal proceedings ([para 22]).

The court a quo ruled in favor of the GCB, striking both Jiba and Mrwebi from the roll of advocates, while a separate application against a third respondent, Sibongile Mzinyathi, was dismissed. Both Jiba and Mrwebi subsequently appealed the ruling, contesting that the alleged misconduct was not substantiated ([paras 2–4]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 5–6])

The primary legal issues before the court concerned whether the actions of Jiba and Mrwebi constituted an adequate basis to declare them unfit to continue practicing as advocates under Section 7(1)(d) of the Admission of Advocates Act 74 of 1964. More specifically, the court needed to consider:

1. Whether the alleged misconduct had been established on a preponderance of probabilities ([para 6]);
2. Whether Jiba and Mrwebi's conduct warranted their classifications as unfit and improper persons to practice as advocates ([paras 11–12]);
3. The appropriate sanctions to apply if misconduct was found, particularly whether striking them off the roll was justified in light of the circumstances.

#### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 4–6])

The court's deliberation was mazed through statutory provisions governing the conduct of advocates. Section 7(1)(d) of the Admission of Advocates Act stipulates that a court could strike an advocate from the roll if it determines that the individual is not fit and proper to continue to practice. The court referenced established case law, including **Jasat v Natal Law Society** [2000] 3 SA 44 (SCA) and **General Council of the Bar of South Africa v Matthys** 2002 (5) SA 1 (E), outlining principles that should guide such inquiries:

1. Establishing alleged offending conduct must be on a factual basis and assessed on a preponderance of probabilities.
2. Reputation and integrity are critical, and considerations of public interest and the honor of the profession must be weighed.
3. If misconduct is established, the court must decide whether to remove the individual or impose a suspension.

#### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 12–28])

**Analysis Relating to Jiba:** 

The court scrutinized complaints presented against Jiba, particularly regarding her handling of the Mdluli case and complications during the powerful review proceedings initiated by Freedom Under Law (FUL). The concerns included a failure to file complete court records timely and misleading statements about compliance with procedural rules ([paras 9–10]). 

The court noted a significant lack of evidence proving Jiba's intention to mislead or that her failures resulted in substantial misconduct; rather, it pointed out that the explanations provided showed a lack of clarity regarding her responsibilities within the NPA’s legal framework ([paras 14–17]). Importantly, the handling of her responses illustrated that her actions could not be categorized as deceitful or indicative of incompetence severe enough to warrant her removal ([paras 12–16]).

**Analysis Relating to Mrwebi:** 

In Mrwebi's case, the court noted inconsistencies in his handling of the prosecution decisions, particularly surrounding his consultations. Although evidence indicated a misapprehension of "in consultation" as required by Section 24(3) of the NPA Act, the court was hesitant to label his actions as intentionally deceptive. Mrwebi's misunderstanding was extensively critiqued, especially when he failed to clarify intricate distinctions that ultimately led to the improper withdrawal of charges ([paras 20–28]).

Despite the finding that Mrwebi’s conduct merited disapproval, the court contested the severity of a permanent removal, deeming a six-month suspension as a more fitting consequence given that no personal gain or malicious intent was ascribed to his conduct.

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 29–74])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the GCB failed to present sufficient evidence that Jiba and Mrwebi were unfit to retain their advocate status. Both were not struck from the roll; Jiba's appeal was upheld based on insufficient evidence of misconduct, while Mrwebi was suspended for six months due to acknowledged but not extensively harmful irregularities in his conduct ([paras 31–32]).

The implications of this ruling emphasize the necessity for clear standards of evidence in disciplinary actions against practitioners, safeguarding against the unjust removal due merely to unproven failures or procedural mishaps. Additionally, this case highlights the tension between upholding the integrity of legal professions while ensuring that punitive measures are proportionate to misconduct. The findings pointed towards an emphasis on rehabilitative measures, sparking discussions on the appropriate balance between punishment and the protection of the public interest in prosecutorial conduct.
