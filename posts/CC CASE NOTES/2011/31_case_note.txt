# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of *Aviation Union of South Africa v South African Airways (Pty) Ltd* [2011] ZACC 31

## F – Facts

The case, *Aviation Union of South Africa v South African Airways (Pty) Ltd*, stemmed from a series of legal proceedings initiated by the Aviation Union of South Africa (the First Applicant) and the South African Transport and Allied Workers’ Union (the Second Applicant) against South African Airways (Pty) Ltd (SAA) and LGM South Africa Facility Managers and Engineers (Pty) Ltd (LGM). 

The core issue involved the termination of an outsourcing agreement that had been in place for the provision of certain services by LGM to SAA. The outsourcing agreement, which had commenced in April 2000 for a ten-year term, was terminated by SAA in June 2007 due to a breach of contract by LGM, effective from 30 September 2007 ([paras 6-10]). 

After the termination, employees of LGM faced redundancy, prompting the Aviation Union to seek assurances from SAA regarding the re-employment of these workers ([paras 12-14]). However, SAA maintained that it had no legal obligation to re-employ the workers once the agreement ended. The union subsequently filed applications in the Labour Court, alleging that the termination constituted a "transfer of business" under section 197 of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) ([paras 14-15]).

Initially, the Labour Court ruled that section 197 did not apply to second or subsequent outsourcing arrangements, and thus denied the relief sought by the unions ([para 16]). The Labour Appeal Court overturned this interpretation, positing that a broader understanding of "transfer" was necessary to protect employees' rights. However, the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of SAA, thereby reinstating the Labour Court's decision ([para 26]) and creating a legal dispute over the wording of section 197 concerning the nature of the "transfer."

## I – Issues

The primary legal question presented to the Constitutional Court concerned the interpretation of section 197 of the LRA. Specifically, the Court needed to address whether the termination of the outsourcing agreement led to a transfer of LGM's employees back to SAA or any relevant third party under the LRA, which aimed to protect workers' rights ([paras 28-32]).

The secondary issues included:
1. The meaning of "transfer" in the context of the LRA, particularly whether the term "by" indicated a requirement for an active role by an old employer (LGM) in facilitating the transfer ([para 26]).
2. The implications of a ruling limiting the application of section 197 to first-generation contracting out, leaving employees unprotected in subsequent arrangements ([paras 30-31]).

## R – Rules

The interpretation of section 197 was central to the case. According to section 197(1), "transfer" refers to the transfer of a business as a going concern from one employer (the old employer) to another (the new employer) ([para 4]). The section is designed to alter the common law position where the transfer of business ordinarily resulted in the automatic termination of employment contracts. The interpretation must comply with constitutional rights, particularly the right to fair labor practices as provided in section 23 of the Constitution of South Africa ([paras 34-36]).

In earlier case law, particularly in *National Education Health and Allied Workers Union v University of Cape Town* ([2002] ZACC 27), it was established that the purpose of section 197 includes facilitating the transfer of businesses while simultaneously protecting employment ([paras 36-37]). The ruling emphasized the necessity of interpreting laws against the constitutional backdrop to ensure employee protection ([para 34]).

Furthermore, the decision in *COSAWU v Zikhethele Trade (Pty) Ltd* ([2005] 26 ILJ 1056) highlighted potential abuses that could arise from rigid interpretations that limit worker protections, advocating for a broader reading of "transfer" ([para 38]).

## A – Application

The Constitutional Court undertook a detailed analysis of how section 197 applied in the case, disagreeing with the Supreme Court of Appeal's interpretation which limited the application of section 197 to first-generation outsourcing arrangements. The Court held that a strict reading of the word "by" failed to acknowledge the section's legislative intent to safeguard employees involved in any transfer of business, whether initiated by the old or new employer ([para 39]).

Through a purposive interpretation of section 197, the Court concluded that the provision aimed to protect employees during all outsourcing scenarios, including subsequent arrangements ([para 54]). The Court underscored that the focus should be on whether a transfer of business as a going concern occurred, rather than exclusively on the transactional dynamics of the employers ([paras 71-72]). 

The evidence presented indicated that despite the termination of the outsourcing contract, there was an intention within the agreement for LGM to transfer assets and employees back to SAA, thereby potentially meeting the criteria for a transfer under section 197 ([paras 120-123]). Thus, the lack of conclusive evidence on whether a transfer of business occurred necessitated further inquiry in the Labour Court ([para 124-125]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court's decision to grant leave to appeal and ultimately uphold the Labour Appeal Court's judgment reflected a significant endorsement of protecting workers' rights in outsourcing agreements and reiterated the importance of interpreting labor laws in light of constitutional principles ([para 128]). 

The implications of this ruling extended beyond the immediate parties, as it set a precedent for future cases involving outsourcing and the application of section 197. The Court directed the matter back to the Labour Court to investigate the factual circumstances surrounding the termination of the outsourcing agreement, further reinforcing the need for courts to ensure the fair treatment of employees within the framework of business operations ([para 127]). 

This ruling thus highlights the ongoing relevance of section 197 not only in safeguarding employee rights but also in promoting fair labor practices within the dynamic landscape of contract outsourcing in South Africa.
