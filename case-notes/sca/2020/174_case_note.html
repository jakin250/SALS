Bester and Others NNO v Gouws and Others [2020] ZASCA 174

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 174

F – Facts

The case originates from the collapse of the RVAF Trust, an investment scheme operated by Herman Pretorius from the early 2000s, which promised unsustainable returns to investors through a fraudulent pyramid scheme ([para 1-6]). By March 2004, the Trust was officially created as part of the Abante Group, with its operations dominated by Pretorius despite a trust deed requiring at least three trustees ([paras 5-6]). Following several scandals, including Pretorius’ chronic mismanagement, the Trust was provisionally sequestrated on August 1, 2012, leading to the appellants being appointed as provisional trustees ([paras 7-8]).

The appellants filed summons against several brokers, alleging unjust enrichment and also seeking recovery under sections of the Insolvency Act, claiming that commissions paid were invalid due to the Trust’s illegal operations ([paras 9-10]). The claims against the brokers were instituted on November 9, 2015, after a delay that was contested on the basis of prescription, with the respondents asserting that the claims had already prescribed ([para 8]).

The first plea of prescription asserted that the trustees had knowledge of their claims as early as October 23, 2012, while the second plea claimed that all debts arising from payments made prior to August 1, 2009, were already prescribed ([paras 11-12]). The High Court sided with the respondents, ruling that the appellants could have (and should have) initiated their claims earlier ([para 15]).

I – Issues

The appeal poses two primary legal questions:

  1. Did the appellants possess actual or deemed knowledge sufficient to commence action against the respondents by October 23, 2012?
  2. If not, did the claims concerning payments made before August 1, 2009, prescribe ([para 16])?

Both issues pivot on the interpretation of the relevant sections of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly sections 12(1) and 12(3) regarding the commencement of prescription ([para 40]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework is predominantly governed by the Prescription Act, which stipulates that a debt becomes due when the creditor is aware of both the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises ([para 40]). Section 12(3) of the Act indicates that a creditor is deemed to have knowledge of a debt if that information could have been acquired with reasonable care ([para 40]).

The court referenced previous cases, including Gore NO, to expound on the concept of reasonable knowledge—indicating that mere ignorance or lack of complete understanding of legal implications does not prevent the accrual of prescription ([para 41-42]). Knowledge should be interpreted broadly, incorporating beliefs inferred from available circumstances and information ([para 41]).

A – Application

The court examined the appellants' claims to establish whether they could have reasonably ascertained the necessary facts by October 23, 2012. The appellants contended that they lacked full knowledge of the payments made to the brokers and their nature until late 2013 ([para 19]). However, the court noted that critical information about the fraudulent scheme and insolvent status of the Trust was already within the appellants' knowledge ([paras 17-20]).

The majority of the factual information relating to the brokers and commission payments was present at the Group’s premises and accessible to the appellants, revealing that their failure to act on this knowledge constituted a neglect of their responsibilities as trustees ([paras 45-48]). The court indicated that prioritization of investigating other issues could not serve as a justification for failing to act on claims that were within reasonable reach ([para 51]).

In evaluating the credibility of testimonies, the court pointed out there were no adverse credibility findings against the appellants’ witnesses; rather the evidence suggested they neglected to inquire into the brokers’ claims thoroughly ([para 46]). The court ultimately reinforced that the appellants had the knowledge and access to necessary documentation to investigate claims against the brokers much earlier than they did ([paras 48-50]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court’s decision that the claims had indeed prescribed. The court emphasized that the appellants, as trustees, were required to exercise diligence and could not postpone the commencement of prescription based on their inaction or misguided prioritization of tasks ([paras 54-57]). The implications of this ruling underscore the obligations of trustees to act promptly on claims within the parameters set forth by the Prescription Act, reinforcing accountability in the administration of insolvent estates.
