Supreme Court of Appeal

Tau v Mashaba and Others (335/2019) [2020] ZASCA 26

This case arose from a contentious defamation claim involving political figures Parks Tau, the appellant, and Herman Mashaba, the respondent, against the backdrop of South African party politics.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 26

F - Facts

This case arose from a contentious defamation claim involving political figures Parks Tau, the appellant, and Herman Mashaba, the respondent, against the backdrop of South African party politics. Tau, then a member of the African National Congress (ANC) and former Mayor of Johannesburg, made disparaging remarks about his political rival Mashaba, who belonged to the Democratic Alliance (DA) and succeeded Tau in the mayoral position ([1]–[8]).

In an address at a funeral on 28 August 2016, Tau accused Mashaba of believing that senior female executives in Johannesburg had "prostituted themselves" for their positions and made similar comments implying Mashaba's anti-black sentiments. These remarks ignited significant public backlash, leading to communications from several organizations, including the Congress of South African Trade Unions (COSATU) and the African National Congress Women’s League (ANCWL), urging Mashaba to address the alleged sexism in his comments ([2]–[3], [6]–[8]).

Following these statements, Mashaba initiated legal proceedings on 1 November 2016, seeking an interdict against Tau, requiring him to retract the statements, refrain from making further defamatory remarks, and issue a public apology. Mashaba claimed that Tau's statements were false, defamatory, and intended to damage his reputation ([4]–[5]). Tau opposed the application, arguing that his statements were justified critiques stemming from Mashaba's own remarks, which he perceived as sexist and racially insensitive ([6]–[10]).

The initial ruling by Van der Linde J in the Gauteng Division of the High Court declared Tau's remarks defamatory and issued an order restricting Tau from repeating them, while deferring other relief sought for a later trial. However, the judge later acknowledged an error in separating the issues for trial ([12]–[14]).

Decision-making involved examining whether the High Court's declarations were justified, considering the implications for both Tau's freedom of expression and Mashaba's right to protect his reputation.

I - Issues

The principal legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the High Court erred in granting a declaratory order that Tau's comments were defamatory and whether it was appropriate to issue an interdict against him, thereby potentially infringing his constitutional right to freedom of speech ([16]–[18], [21]).

Additionally, the court addressed whether Mashaba had established a sufficient basis for the claim of irreparable harm to warrant such an interdict and whether an action for damages alone could serve as an adequate remedy for the alleged defamation ([27]–[29]).

R - Rules

The legal principles pertinent to this case resonate with authorities on defamation, the justification of freedom of speech, and the circumstances under which interdicts may be granted. Central to South African defamation law are the requirements for establishing a prima facie case of defamation, including the need for reputation harm and a false statement, as elucidated in Herbal Zone (Pty) Limited v Infitech Technologies (Pty) Limited and other relevant cases ([24], [29]).

Further, an interdict may require proof of a prima facie right, irreparable harm, balance of convenience, and absence of a satisfactory alternative remedy ([21], [27]–[29]). The court must exercise caution when issuing interdicts in defamation cases, ensuring that defendants are not denied their constitutional rights to free speech ([26]–[27]).

A - Application

In scrutinizing the High Court's decision, the SCA highlighted fundamental errors in applying the law regarding interim interdicts and the definition of actionable defamation. The Court emphasized that the relief sought by Mashaba through interdicts was inseparable from the broader defamation claim pending before the court. It criticized the High Court for making a premature determination of the statements' defamatory nature without a full evidentiary basis to support such a broad conclusion ([16]–[19]).

The SCA reaffirmed that both Tau’s statements and Mashaba’s responses must be considered within the context of political discourse, underscoring that in the realm of politics, heightened scrutiny and robust debate are commonplace ([1]–[10]).

The Court also found that Mashaba had not demonstrated a well-founded apprehension of irreparable harm necessitating urgent interdicts, pointing out that Tau had not repeated the alleged statements in a significant period since their initial delivery. Furthermore, the SCA ruled that damages would be an adequate remedy as they could sufficiently compensate Mashaba should defamation be established at trial ([19]–[26]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld Tau's appeal, concluding that the High Court had erred in its assessment and in granting relief not explicitly sought. The order of the High Court was set aside, with the SCA dismissing Mashaba’s application for the interdict and affirming Tau's right to political expression, aligning with constitutional protections of free speech ([29]).

This decision underscores the delicate balance that courts must maintain between protecting reputations and upholding freedom of expression, especially in the politically charged atmosphere characteristic of South African politics. The ruling reiterates that while defamation provides a basis for action, it does not automatically curtail freedom of speech without thorough legal scrutiny and contextual analysis.
