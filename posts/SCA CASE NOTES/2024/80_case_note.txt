Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** George Hlaudi Motsoeneng v South African Broadcasting Corporation Soc Ltd and Others [2024] ZASCA 80 (27 May 2024) 

**Coram:** Ponnan, Hughes and Meyer JJA and Tlaletsi and Mbhele AJJA  

---

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1–11])

The appellant, George Hlaudi Motsoeneng, formerly served as the Chief Operating Officer (COO) of the South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC), the first respondent ([1]). Under his employment, he was also a member of the South African Broadcasting Corporation Pension Fund, the third respondent ([1]). On 12 June 2017, Motsoeneng's employment was terminated, entitling him to a withdrawal benefit from the Fund due to the employment termination.

Subsequently, the SABC discovered that Motsoeneng had received a success fee of R11,508,549.12, which was allegedly unauthorized ([2]). This fee was approved by the Governance and Nominations Committee (GNC) based on claims that Motsoeneng had raised R1.19 billion for the benefit of the SABC, a claim that later came under scrutiny ([2]). Upon realizing the discrepancies surrounding the payment, the SABC sought to halt the payout of Motsoeneng’s pension benefits on the basis of the alleged misconduct ([3]).

To secure the withholding of these funds, the SABC initiated legal proceedings against the Fund, ultimately resulting in an interim interdict preventing the payment of the disputed funds ([6]). An investigation by the Special Investigating Unit (SIU) further substantiated allegations of misconduct against Motsoeneng, leading to the SIU’s involvement as a co-applicant in the case ([8]).

Despite the findings, the process continued through various stages in the Gauteng Division of the High Court. The High Court found in favor of the SABC, declaring the payment of the success fee illegal and ordering Motsoeneng to repay these funds with interest, but Motsoeneng sought to appeal this finding ([10-11]). His application for leave to appeal was originally dismissed by two judges, prompting Motsoeneng to apply to the President of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) for reconsideration under s 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, claiming exceptional circumstances ([11]).

---

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([11–21])

1. **Exceptional Circumstances:** The central legal issue was whether the appellant could demonstrate exceptional circumstances under s 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act, which would justify the reconsideration of the refusal of leave to appeal ([11-14]).
   
2. **Condonation for Delay:** Another issue was whether Motsoeneng's default in prosecuting his appeal could be condoned given the procedural missteps that led to the lapse of the appeal ([12-13]).

3. **Legal Merit of Appeal:** Implicit in the inquiry was the examination of whether the merits of Motsoeneng’s case posed reasonable prospects of success upon appeal ([20-21]).

---

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([12–20])

### Statutory Framework
- **Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013:** Particularly s 17, which governs applications for leave to appeal and the conditions under which such appeals can be reconsidered. 

### Precedents
- The court referenced established principles from prior cases concerning the interpretation of “exceptional circumstances,” notably from *Liesching and Others v S (Liesching II)* and *Ntlanyeni v S*, where it was noted that a party must demonstrate factors that are not merely repetitive of arguments already considered and rejected ([14-19]).
- Relevant case law, such as *Norwich Union Life Insurance Society v Dobbs* and *Avnit v First Rand Trading*, underscored the necessity for a stringent interpretation of what constitutes exceptional circumstances ([19]).

---

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([11–20])

In evaluating the application for reconsideration, the court systematically articulated the burden on Motsoeneng to delineate exceptional circumstances. The judges assessed whether the failure to file heads of argument constituted a significant hindrance to the administration of justice and whether any reasons presented sufficiently indicated the likelihood of a grave injustice if the appeal were not allowed.

While acknowledging that the lapse in filing was relatively minor—only eight days late—the judges weighed this against the merits of Motsoeneng’s claims. They concluded that the substantive legal challenges put forth by Motsoeneng did not sufficiently rise to the level that would demonstrate exceptional circumstances conducive to overturning the previous dismissal ([12-14], [19-20]).

The court identified that the arguments presented by Motsoeneng and his legal team resembled rehearings of previously rejected assertions rather than presenting new insights or grounds, which did not meet the established threshold for exceptional circumstances ([19-20]). This reinforced the view that simply having a dissatisfactory outcome in prior litigation does not in itself warrant a second trajectory of appeal.

---

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([21])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Motsoeneng's application for condonation, finding that he failed to establish the exceptional circumstances required under s 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act ([21]). The Court emphasized the need for strict adherence to procedural rules and underscored the dangers of allowing repeated appeals based on previously litigated issues without substantively demonstrating new, compelling arguments.

The ruling reinforces the principle of finality in legal proceedings and discourages frivolous attempts at prolonging litigation without just cause, thus promoting judicial efficiency ([21]). The costs order against Motsoeneng, including costs for two counsel, further signifies the court's stance on combating what it sees as strategic litigious maneuvering in the public interest ([21]).

This case highlights the imperative for litigants to not only understand the procedural aspects of appeal processes but also to substantively frame their arguments to meet the higher barriers imposed by law, especially when invoking special procedures intended for exceptional cases.
