# FIRAC Case Note: **Moodley and Another v The State (475/2023)**

**Neutral Citation:** Moodley and Another v The State (475/2023) [2024] ZASCA 102 (20 June 2024)

**Coram:** Hughes, Mabindla-Boqwana, and Smith JJA

---

### F – Facts
In this appeal, Dinesh Moodley and Ugresen Perumal (the appellants) were convicted of the murder of Avinash Manjanu and sentenced to 25 years' imprisonment ([1]). The conviction stemmed from events that transpired on the evening of November 4, 2017, when an altercation occurred at a party hosted by Vinay Choonie in Lenasia South. Moodley and the deceased were in attendance, leading to a physical confrontation involving the deceased and Moodley's brother ([3]).

Following the altercation, the deceased returned to the party to confront the appellants. At this point, Perumal arrived in a Hyundai i20, exited the vehicle with a firearm, and shot the deceased while being encouraged by Moodley ([3]-[5]). Witnesses included family members and friends of both the appellants and the deceased, who provided testimony about the events leading up to and following the shooting, demonstrating prior knowledge of the appellants ([6]-[10]).

The trial court relied on eyewitness testimony, concluding that despite challenges to their credibility, the witnesses were reliable due to prior acquaintance with the appellants ([14]-[19]). During the trial, the appellants presented alibi defenses that were ultimately rejected by the court, which found compelling evidence linking them to the crime ([12]-[22]).

### I – Issues
The central legal question in this case was whether the State proved the identity of the appellants as the assailants beyond a reasonable doubt. This included evaluating the credibility and reliability of eyewitness testimony given the witnesses' previous knowledge of the appellants ([2]).

Key issues also included:
- The extent to which eyewitness identification can be trusted given prior acquaintance ([16]-[18]).
- The treatment of alibi evidence and its rejection in light of circumstantial proofs, including cellphone data and vehicle identification ([22]-[24]).
- The admissibility and relevance of ballistic evidence as indicative of the guilt or innocence of the appellants ([23]).

### R – Rules
The judgment referred to several key legal principles regarding identification:

1. **Eyewitness Identification**: The court reaffirmed the principles articulated in *S v Mthetwa*, emphasizing caution with eyewitness identification due to the fallibility of human observation ([16]). The court noted that prior knowledge of the accused increases the likelihood of accurate identification ([19]).

2. **Burden of Proof**: The State bears the onus to establish the identity of the accused beyond a reasonable doubt and to effectively counter any alibi defense ([15]).

3. **Evaluation of Witness Credibility**: The trial court must evaluate testimonial credibility while considering the opportunity and conditions under which identification was made, even if discrepancies are present ([25]).

4. **Role of Expert Evidence**: Courts are not bound to accept expert testimony if it lacks corroboration from the surrounding evidence ([24]).

### A – Application
The court applied these principles to the case as follows:

- The eyewitnesses had multiple opportunities to observe the events during the incident and were known to the appellants which lent credibility to their identifications. The court found that the well-lit environment facilitated their identification ([14], [18]).

- The trial court's rejection of the appellants' alibi defenses was largely supported by cellphone records placing both appellants near the crime scene at the relevant time. This evidence was crucial in corroborating the State's case ([22]).

- Despite recognizing some discrepancies in witness testimony, the trial court concluded that the witnesses' accounts regarding the identification of the appellants as assailants remained consistent and credible. The evidence of murder was thus found overwhelming ([25]).

- Ballistics evidence introduced by the State was treated critically, with the court acknowledging its inconclusive nature but affirming that it did not undermine the eyewitness evidence indicating that the appellants were directly involved in the shooting ([23]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the conviction and sentence. The court held that the State had successfully established the identity of the appellants beyond a reasonable doubt, as the eyewitness accounts were credible and corroborated by substantial circumstantial evidence, including cellphone data and vehicle identification ([26]).

The case underscores the critical importance of identifying witnesses' prior knowledge in assessing the reliability of identification testimony and reiterates the principle that courts must balance discrepancies in witness accounts against a totality of evidence when reaching a verdict. The implications for future cases involve careful consideration of identification procedures and the evidence necessary to support claims of mistaken identity or alibi.
