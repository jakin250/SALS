# Case Note: Matamela v Mulaudzi (475/2021) [2022] ZASCA 71

## FIRAC Method Case Analysis

### F – Facts
The appeal arises from a judgment delivered by the Thohoyandou Magistrate’s Court, which ordered the eviction of the appellant, Tshioma Matamela, from property owned by the respondent, David Masilo Mulaudzi ([1], [2]). The case was escalated to the High Court of Limpopo, which on September 18, 2020, removed the matter from the roll. On the same day, the appellant sought leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([2], [4]). A tumultuous scenario developed in the High Court, where it was disclosed by the presiding judges that there were serious allegations of judicial capture against attorneys linked to the appeal. Consequently, the court felt unable to hear the appeal, determining that an attorney representing the appellant had connections to these allegations ([3], [5]).

After several procedural delays, the High Court granted leave to appeal to the SCA on March 18, 2021. However, the SCA questioned the validity of this leave, noting that as per Section 16(1)(b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, special leave from the SCA, not the High Court, is required for such appeals ([6], [11]). It was specifically noted that the High Court lacked jurisdiction to grant leave to appeal and that therefore the leave was a nullity ([12]).

### I – Issues
The central legal issue revolves around whether the SCA has the jurisdiction to hear the appeal, given that the High Court incorrectly granted leave to appeal, which contravened the statutory requirements set out in the Superior Courts Act ([1], [12]). The implications of judicial capture and whether the SCA could exercise any jurisdiction despite the apparent procedural irregularities were also underlying considerations ([12]).

### R – Rules
The SCA's jurisdiction is explicitly governed by the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, specifically Section 16 and Section 19. Section 16(1)(b) establishes that any appeal against a High Court's decision on appeal to it requires special leave from the SCA ([9], [10]). This was reiterated in previous cases, including *Tadvest Industrial (Pty) Ltd v Anthea Hanekom & others*, which highlighted that the High Court, sitting as an appeal court, does not possess the authority to grant such leave ([8], [12]).

### A – Application
The Court analyzed whether the High Court indeed had the authority to grant leave for the appeal. The SCA concluded that it did not. Highlighting the statutory framework, the judgment reiterated that the High Court's erroneous grant of leave constituted a lack of jurisdiction, rendering the order null and void ([11], [12]). The SCA’s strict adherence to the jurisdictional requirements illustrates the division's commitment to procedural integrity, emphasizing that an insufficient grant of leave from the High Court extinguishes review under its purview.

Furthermore, the SCA addressed arguments related to judicial independence raised during the High Court's hearings, noting that while the allegations against the attorneys were serious, they did not alter the procedural jurisdiction that is strictly governed by statute ([5], [13]).

### C – Conclusion
The SCA struck the appeal from the roll with costs, underscoring that the appeal was not properly before it due to the failure to obtain the necessary special leave ([13]). The ruling not only highlights the stringent jurisdictional constraints applicable to appeals but also sends a clear message concerning the necessity of adhering to the legislative framework governing judicial processes. Thus, the case serves as a pivotal reference for potential future litigants and legal practitioners on the importance of understanding statutory qualifications for appeals in the South African legal system.

### Implications
This ruling reinforces the necessity for rigorous adherence to procedural law in appeals. It underscores the significant, albeit often overlooked, jurisdictional prerequisites which must be satisfied and sets a precedent on the role of statutory provisions in shaping judicial authority. Moreover, the situation surrounding judicial capture remains a focal point for safeguarding judicial independence, although it did not affect the procedural outcome in this instance.
