Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: The Body Corporate of Savannah Park v Brainwave Projects 1147 CC (735/2010)

**Neutral Citation**: The Body Corporate of Savannah Park v Brainwave Projects 1147 CC (735/2010) [2011] ZASCA 239 (1 December 2011).

**Coram**: Mthiyane, Cachalia, Mhlantla, Leach, and Majiedt JJA

## F – Facts
The appeal emanated from the **KwaZulu-Natal High Court, Durban** (Griffiths AJ). This case involved a dispute between the developer (**first respondent**) and the body corporate of a sectional title scheme (**appellant**) over income derived from a lease agreement concerning a cellular telephone mast erected on common property ([paras 1-2]).

In September 2005, the developer leased land to a cellular company (**second respondent**), permitting it to erect a cellular mast on a specified portion of the property, in exchange for rental payments ([para 2]). Subsequently, the developer initiated the establishment of a sectional title scheme on the property and reserved a right of extension under **s 25(1)** of the **Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986** ([para 3]). A 'Certificate of Real Right' was later issued, confirming the developer’s right to erect and complete a further building on the common property within ten years ([para 4]).

Despite this, the body corporate alleged that the developer had no right to receive income from the lease as it pertained to a section of the common property. The crux of the case was whether the developer's reserved rights included the right to commercially exploit the leased portion, or whether it merely allowed for development without usufructuary rights ([paras 6-7]). 

## I – Issues
The central legal issue to be determined was whether the developer's right of extension under **s 25(4)** conferred the right to derive income from the leasing of the mast erected on the common property, or merely permitted the developer to develop that property without any rights to the benefits of commercialization ([para 9]).

## R – Rules
This case primarily revolved around the interpretation of **s 25(4)** of the Sectional Titles Act, which states:
1. A right reserved in terms of subsection (1) is deemed to be a right to immovable property capable of being mortgaged.
2. The right may be transferred via a notarial deed of cession, which indicates it is a real right, but does not inherently provide the right to lease or derive income ([para 9]).

The court also referenced prior case law, including **Erlax Properties (Pty) Ltd v Registrar of Deeds & others**, where it was established that although the right is a real right, it does not equate to a usufruct that allows the developer to exploit the property commercially ([para 10]). Furthermore, commentators noted that the character of the extension right is more akin to a statutory real right, distinct from personal servitudes which typically allow for enjoyment and use of property ([para 11]).

## A – Application
In its analysis, the court clarified that the reserved extension right under **s 25(4)** does not convey any usufructuary rights allowing the developer to lease the property and obtain profits from it. The court emphasized that the intent of the extension right was to enable the developer to create a mortgageable asset to finance future developments, not to personally exploit any part of the common property ([para 12]). 

The appeal was informed by the legislative amendments and prior interpretations that delineated the rights of developers versus the rights of the body corporate. The court held that the right did not encompass a latitude for commercialization or leasing, reinforcing the principle that the developer remained subject to the governance and management of the body corporate regarding common property ([para 14]).

In rejecting the developer’s assertion of entitlement to income based on its reserved rights, the court maintained that such rights must be strictly construed and exercised in line with the provisions of the Act ([para 16]). The developer's concession during the appeal further illustrated an understanding that the claim of income from leasing was untenable if the court sided against its interpretation of the extension right ([para 17]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the body corporate, holding that the developer's right of extension under **s 25(4)** of the Sectional Titles Act does not confer the right to lease or receive income from the portion of common property where the mast was erected. The court's decision clarified that such rights are confined to development purposes and do not include commercial exploitation. Consequently, the developer was ordered to account for all rental income received since the establishment of the sectional title scheme and was prohibited from further leasing arrangements without the body corporate's consent ([paras 18-19]).

This ruling reinforces the governance structure established in sectional title schemes, ensuring that the interests of the body corporate and individual unit owners remain protected against unilateral actions by developers. The case demonstrates the importance of precise statutory interpretation and the delineation of rights within the context of property law.
