Masako v Masako & Another (Case no 724/20) [2021] ZASCA 168

Despite this, the First Respondent initiated an application in the Regional Court of Garankuwa on 24 May 2016, seeking the appointment of a Receiver to manage the joint estate's assets.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 168

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

The core of this appeal revolves around a dispute between Pauline Masibe Masako (the Appellant) and Molefe Stephens Masako (the First Respondent), originating from their previous marriage and subsequent divorce on 13 February 2013. Their settlement agreement stipulated that each party would retain the assets in their possession, including immovable properties ([para 2]).

Despite this, the First Respondent initiated an application in the Regional Court of Garankuwa on 24 May 2016, seeking the appointment of a Receiver to manage the joint estate's assets. This application was set for a hearing on 17 April 2018, during which an order was granted in the absence of the Appellant. Consequently, the Appellant filed a rescission application on 21 May 2018, supported by an affidavit from her attorney, Ms Nkagiseng Moduka ([paras 3-4]).

The First Respondent contested the validity of this application by challenging Ms Moduka’s locus standi, arguing that, as the attorney for the Appellant, she lacked the necessary standing to bring the application because she was not directly affected by the judgment ([para 5]).

The Regional Court upheld the point in limine, deciding that Ms Moduka had not been authorized by the Appellant to depose to the affidavit. This ruling was affirmed by the High Court ([paras 6-7]).

I – Issues

The main legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were:

  1. Did the attorney require authority from her client to depose to an affidavit in support of a rescission application?
  2. What constitutes proper locus standi in the context of such proceedings?[ [para 1]]

R – Rules

The court referred to several legal authorities and relevant statutes to resolve these issues:

  • Section 36(1) of the Magistrates’ Courts Act 32 of 1944, which allows for rescission of judgments granted in the absence of a party affected by such judgment.
  • Rule 49(1) of the Magistrates’ Court Rules, which pertains to the rescission of judgments and establishes that a party must be affected by the judgment to apply for rescission.
  • Ganes and Another v Telecom Namibia Ltd, where the SCA established that an attorney's authorization to depose to an affidavit is not necessary if the institution of proceedings is authorized and the facts underpinning the affidavit lie within the attorney's knowledge ([paras 10-11]).
  • Rule 52(2)(a) of the Magistrates’ Court Rules, which clarifies that the authority of a person acting for a party may only be challenged under specific procedures ([para 13]).

A – Application

The SCA critically assessed how both the Regional Court and High Court had misapplied the legal principles surrounding locus standi, the authority to depose to affidavits, and the standing of attorneys in court ([paras 8-9]).

In its analysis, the SCA reaffirmed that the Appellant, as the respondent in the main application, had retained Ms Moduka's services to contest the matter and thus had the requisite standing to seek rescission of the default judgment. The court emphasized that the inquiry into the attorney's standing was irrelevant, as the Appellant was the party affected by the resent judgment ([para 9]).

Furthermore, the court distinguished the attorney’s authority to act in the capacity of representing the Appellant in the application from the authority to depose to an affidavit, asserting that the latter did not require separate authorization based on Ganes ([paras 10-11]). The inherent knowledge of the attorney regarding the facts of the case was crucial in supporting her ability to depose to the founding affidavit without needing explicit authorization for that specific action.

The court also noted that the First Respondent failed to challenge Ms Moduka’s authority appropriately, thus reinforcing the Appellant's position ([para 13]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, setting aside the judgments of the lower courts and confirming the dismissal of the point in limine concerning Ms Moduka's authority. The Court reaffirmed that the Appellant had the requisite standing to seek rescission and clarified that the attorney’s involvement did not necessitate explicit permission for affidavit submission ([paras 12-14]). The ruling is significant as it clarifies the parameters surrounding attorneys' roles in proceedings and underscores the importance of proper procedural challenges to authority as outlined in the applicable rules.

The implications of this case extend to the broader understanding of locus standi and the roles attorneys play in motion proceedings, establishing a clear precedent that may impact subsequent litigation where similar questions arise.

The decision showcases the need for courts to adhere strictly to established legal precedents and avoid conflating separate legal doctrines that may obfuscate justice. Consequently, the matter was remitted back to the Regional Court for a determination of the substantive merits of the rescission application.
