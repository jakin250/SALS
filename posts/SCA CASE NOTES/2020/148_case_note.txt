Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Mulaudzi v Mudau and Others (1034/2019) [2020] ZASCA 148

## F – Facts
The case concerns the validity of a sale agreement involving immovable property between the second respondent, Avashoni Thomas Mudau, and the appellant, Rotondwa Mulaudzi, where the former was married in community of property to the first respondent, Matodzi Petronella Mudau. The first respondent initiated divorce proceedings against the second respondent on 7 August 2012, which were defended, culminating in a divorce finalized on 13 February 2014. Before this divorce, the second respondent entered into a sale agreement with the appellant on 5 July 2012, where he sold the property located at erf 571 Makwarela-A township (the property) without the first respondent's consent, which was required under section 15(2)(a) of the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984 ([paras 1-5]).

The first respondent took legal action in August 2013 to set aside the sale agreement on the basis that it lacked her requisite consent, arguing that the transaction was thus void ab initio. Meanwhile, the appellant contended that she was unaware of the second respondent's marital status, citing a declaration wherein the second respondent claimed to be unmarried, which was recorded at the time of the sale ([paras 6-8]).

The Limpopo Division of the High Court initially dismissed the first respondent's application. However, upon appeal, the full court set aside the sale, leading the appellant to seek special leave to appeal from the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 1-2]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue in this case was whether the sale of immovable property by a spouse married in community of property, without the consent of the other spouse, was valid under the Matrimonial Property Act, particularly focusing on whether consent could be deemed given per section 15(9)(a) of the Act. The validity of the sale was contested due to the absence of the first respondent’s consent, which was essential as stipulated by the Act ([paras 1, 4-5, 14]).

## R – Rules
The applicable legal framework is encapsulated in section 15 of the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984. Specifically, subsection (2)(a) requires written consent from both spouses for the alienation or mortgaging of property forming part of the joint estate. Conversely, subsection (9)(a) provides a mechanism whereby a transaction can be deemed valid if the third party can demonstrate that they neither knew nor could reasonably have known about the lack of consent from the non-contracting spouse. This provision exists to balance the interests of non-consenting spouses and bona fide third parties engaged in property transactions ([paras 2-11]).

The judicial interpretation of these provisions was recently addressed in the case of *Marais and Another NNO v Maposa and Others*, which clarified the procedural and substantive applications of sections 15(2)(a) and 15(9)(a), underscoring that a third party must prove that they were unaware of the consent requirement for the contract to be deemed valid ([paras 11-12]).

## A – Application
The court applied the statutory framework to the case at hand by first recognising the facts surrounding the sale agreement, particularly the representations made by the second respondent regarding his marital status. The appellant's reliance on these representations, as well as the absence of any reasonable inquiry required on her part concerning the second respondent’s claims, were pivotal in determining her standing under section 15(9)(a) ([paras 12-14]).

The full court's earlier decision, which misinterpreted section 15(9), was identified as incorrect because it failed to consider that the appellant could assert her position based on the presumed consent due to her reasonable belief in the second respondent's misrepresentations. The Supreme Court found that the appellant did not know and could not reasonably have known of the absence of consent, thus fulfilling the critical conditions stipulated by the Act, allowing for deemed consent to apply ([paras 10-14]).

The court also addressed the counter-arguments presented by the first respondent regarding the appellant's duty to verify the marital status of the second respondent. It concluded that such inquiries were not mandated, particularly given the explicit declarations within the sale agreement and supporting affidavit ([paras 13-14]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, ruling that the sale agreement between the second respondent and the appellant was valid and enforceable, due to the deemed consent under section 15(9)(a). It was asserted that the misrepresentation made by the second respondent regarding his marital status did not impact the appellant's reliance on that information. The ruling underscored the need for a balanced consideration of the rights of both spouses and the protections available to bona fide third parties in transactional contexts. Consequently, it was determined that any potential claims for loss by the non-contracting spouse would be managed through property adjustments within the context of the divorce order already in place ([paras 15-17]).

Thus, the court's ruling not only reinstated the interests of the appellant but also provided clarity on the legal expectations and protections under the Matrimonial Property Act within similar future transactions. The decision serves as a precedent reinforcing the protection afforded to third parties engaging in transactions involving joint assets, notwithstanding potential internal disputes between spouses.
