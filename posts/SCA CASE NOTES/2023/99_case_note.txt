Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

### FIRAC Case Note: MEC: Police, Roads and Transport Free State Provincial Government v Bovicon Consulting Engineers CC and Another (278/2022)

#### F – Facts
The Supreme Court of Appeal's case, **MEC: Police, Roads and Transport Free State Provincial Government v Bovicon Consulting Engineers CC and Another** ([para 1]), addresses a dispute concerning a contractual obligation and accrued interest. The background of the case involves **Bovicon Consulting Engineers CC** (the first respondent), which rendered services to the Free State Department of Police, Roads and Transport (the Department) between May 2012 and March 2013. Bovicon issued a summons against the **Member of the Executive Council** (MEC) on **19 August 2014**, claiming R1,171,774.83 along with interest calculated at **15.5%**, due from the date of each corresponding invoice until final payment ([paras 2–3]). 

By **12 September 2019**, the interest accrued had equalized the principal debt, reaching a total of R2,343,549.66. The High Court, presided by **Chesiwe J**, ruled in which the MEC was ordered to pay the principal sum and interest from the invoice due dates to the date of final payment ([para 3]). The MEC made a payment in full on **14 July 2020**, but contested subsequent entitlements to post-judgment interest, asserting the judgment debt had been fully settled ([para 4]).

Bovicon executed a warrant against the MEC to reclaim alleged outstanding post-judgment interest of R220,332.09 for the period from **December 6, 2019**, to **July 14, 2020**. The MEC sought to quash the attachment, arguing compliance with the **State Liability Act 20 of 1957** and claiming all obligations had been fulfilled ([paras 4–5]). The High Court ruled in favor of Bovicon regarding the post-judgment interest claim but mandated that the MEC bears costs on a punitive scale ([para 6]).

#### I – Issues
The appeal raised multiple legal questions primarily focused on the application of the **in duplum rule** regarding interest accrual and the validity of post-judgment interest subsequent to full payment of the judgment debt ([para 1]). The central issues included: 

1. **Whether payment of R2,343,549.66 fully satisfied the judgment debt, extinguishing any entitlement to post-judgment interest** ([paras 4–7]).
2. **The appropriateness of the interest rate awarded and the corresponding costs** ([paras 8–12]).
3. **The procedural validity of the High Court granting leave for appeal directly to the Supreme Court** ([paras 13–15]).

#### R – Rules
The Supreme Court referenced various legal principles and precedents to address the issues:

1. **In Duplum Rule**: This rule restricts the recoverable interest on a debt to double the capital amount owed. However, it does not prevent post-judgment interest from accruing after the judgment debt has been established ([paras 1, 9]).
   
2. **Paulsen and Another v Slip Knot Investments 777 (Pty) Ltd** ([2015] ZACC 5; 2015 (3) SA 479 (CC)): The court reiterated that post-judgment interest can run anew from the date of judgment until full payment is made ([para 10]). 

3. **Prescribed Rate of Interest Act 55 of 1975**: This statute governs the maximum allowable interest rate to be charged on debts and is critical for evaluating the rate awarded by the High Court ([para 11]).

4. **Judicial Discretion in Cost Awards**: The court emphasized the importance of providing explicit reasons for imposing punitive cost orders to uphold judicial accountability ([para 12]).

#### A – Application
The Supreme Court analyzed the arguments presented by the MEC to determine the validity of the High Court's decisions:

1. **Satisfaction of Judgment Debt**: The Court rejected the MEC's argument that the payment of R2,343,549.66 constituted full satisfaction of the judgment debt, asserting that interest is permitted to run post-judgment until full payment is made. As noted, past judgments support the interpretation that the in duplum rule does not apply post-judgment regarding post-judgment interest ([paras 8, 10]).

2. **Interest Rate Application**: The Court concurred with Bovicon's assertion that the High Court had erred in its determination of the interest rate. The interest rate should correspond with the rate prevailing at the time of the judgment rather than a contractual agreement post-judgment. Consequently, the order was modified to reflect the correct rate prior to judgment being entered ([paras 11–12]).

3. **Costs**: The Court criticized the High Court's failure to provide rationale for the punitive costs awarded, emphasizing that such decisions should be reasoned. However, the Court acknowledged the discretion of the judiciary in cost matter and noted that the MEC could not pinpoint a misdirection to merit overturning the costs awarded ([paras 12–13]).

4. **Leave to Appeal**: The Court denounced the High Court’s failure to follow statutory protocols regarding the granting of leave to appeal directly to the Supreme Court. It reiterated that such permissions should be reserved for cases involving significant legal principles or matters needing resolution due to differing opinions ([paras 13–17]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court's final determination effectively amended the High Court’s order, reaffirming that post-judgment interest accrues on debts until they are completely paid, even when prior interest accrual meets the capital amount due to the in duplum rule ([para 1]). It corrected the interest rate awarded to align with statutory guidelines and maintained the High Court's cost ruling, despite the lack of clear justification for its punitive nature. The appeal was dismissed, with the MEC liable for costs, highlighting the need for adherence to statutory requirements and judicial diligence in cost assessments ([para 18]). This case underscores the court's approach to contractual interests and the judicial framework governing state liability in South Africa.
