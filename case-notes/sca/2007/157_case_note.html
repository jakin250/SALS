Supreme Court of Appeal 2007 [2006] (3) SA 138 (SCA) and Rail Commuters Action Group v Tra

FIRAC Case Note: Shabalala v Metrorail [2007] SCA 157 (RSA)

F – Facts
The appellant, Siphiwe Alton Shabalala, a 44-year-old passenger, was shot and robbed while on a train operated by the respondent, Metrorail, on 21 May 2004. At Dunswart railway station, the appellant observed approximately 11 commuters waiting on the platform but noted the absence of security personnel. After boarding the train, he was confronted by three individuals who demanded money, leading to the shooting that resulted in serious injuries. He was robbed of R130, a train ticket, and a wristwatch, while the assailants remained on the train when it departed at the next station, Benoni. The appellant subsequently instituted legal proceedings against Metrorail in the Johannesburg High Court, alleging negligence for failing to ensure his safety as a passenger ([paras 1–4]). The issue of liability was determined against him, prompting an appeal ([para 1]).

I – Issues
The central issue in this case is whether Metrorail breached its duty of care towards the appellant, thus rendering it liable for the harm suffered. Specifically, the court examined:

  1. Whether Metrorail had a legal duty to ensure the safety of passengers on its trains.
  2. Whether Metrorail’s measures to ensure commuter safety were reasonable under the circumstances, thus determining if there was a negligent omission ([paras 6–10]).

R – Rules
The foundational legal principle in this case revolves around the standard of care required to prevent negligence. The court referred to the legal doctrine that a negligent omission must be wrongful to constitute delictual liability. It relied on the precedent set in Trustees, Two Oceans Aquarium Trust v Kantey & Templer (Pty) Ltd [2006] (3) SA 138 (SCA) and Rail Commuters Action Group v Transnet Ltd t/a Metrorail [2005] (2) SA 359 (CC), where it was established that while Metrorail had a public law obligation to ensure security, this did not automatically create a delictual duty. The court underscored that there must be a reasonable foreseeability of harm and that the burden of proving negligence rested with the appellant ([paras 6–9]).

A – Application
In its application of the law, the court analyzed whether the appellant could substantiate his claims of negligence on a balance of probabilities. The court noted that Metrorail had implemented security measures, such as employing security guards on trains and platforms. The appellant failed to prove that these measures were unreasonable or that their absence was the proximate cause of his injuries ([paras 8–11]). The court highlighted the high standard required to hold Metrorail liable, stressing that the presence of armed security guards in every coach might be economically and logistically impractical considering the scale of operations required to ensure commuter safety. The court concluded that without evidence suggesting a specific need for additional security in the coach where the appellant traveled, the claim of negligence could not be substantiated ([para 10–12]).

C – Conclusion
The court ultimately concluded that the appellant failed to discharge the burden of proof necessary to establish negligence on the part of Metrorail. As a result, the appeal was dismissed with costs, and the order of the lower court was altered to grant absolution from the instance, thereby relieving Metrorail from any legal liability for the incident ([paras 12–13]). This case underscores the rigorous standards applied in negligence claims, particularly in instances involving alleged systemic failures in public safety provisions.
