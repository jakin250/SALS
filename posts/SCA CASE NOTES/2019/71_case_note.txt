Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: The Motor Industry Ombudsman of South Africa v Silver Park Motors CC (479/18) [2019] ZASCA 71

## F - Facts
The case involves an appeal brought by the Motor Industry Ombudsman of South Africa (the appellant), an independent dispute resolution body accredited under the Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008 (the Act). The appellant sought a declaratory order that Silver Park Motors CC, trading as Silverton Motors (the first respondent), was a retailer as defined by the Act and thus liable for contributions towards the appellant's activities under the South African Automotive Industry Code of Conduct (the Code) ([1–4]).

An inspection by the appellant's official on 30 November 2015 revealed that the first respondent sold fuel, engine oils, and cleaning products but had not registered as a retailer under the Act and the Code. The first respondent contended that it did not engage in the motor industry as envisioned in the Code and thus no registration or contributions were warranted ([2–4]).

The appellants argued that the first respondent was a supplier of motor vehicle accessories by virtue of its sale of fuel and lubricants, which purportedly enhanced the functionality of vehicles. Conversely, the first respondent denied being a retailer of accessories, asserting that it was merely a fuel retailer without the obligation to comply with the Code's provisions. In a counter-application, the first respondent sought to join the Minister of the Department of Trade and Industry as a second respondent and requested that the provisions of the Code that mandated contributions be declared ultra vires the Act ([5–8]).

The Gauteng Division of the High Court ruled against the appellant, concluding that the first respondent did not meet the definition of a retailer or supplier of accessories, nor did it provide repair or replacement services as required by the Code ([6, 7]).

## I - Issues
The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

1. Whether Silver Park Motors CC qualified as a retailer or supplier of accessories, as defined in the Automotive Industry Code of Conduct ([6]).
2. Whether the sale of fuel and engine oils constituted a related repair or replacement service to consumers in relation to motor vehicles ([6, 8]).

## R - Rules
The court relied on several statutory provisions and legal principles:

1. **Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008**: The Act aims to protect consumers and promote fair business practices ([13, 14]). 
2. **Definition of 'Automotive Industry'**: Under the Code, the automotive industry encompasses manufacturers, distributors, retailers, and suppliers of vehicles and components, and it includes those who render repair or replacement services ([6, 16]).
3. **Ordinary Meaning of Terms**: The term 'accessories' lacks a statutory definition; thus, it was interpreted using its plain dictionary meaning, which denotes items that are additional or subordinate to something else ([15, 19]).
4. **Statutory Construction Principles**: The interpretation of legislative provisions must consider the language used, the context, the purpose of the legislation, and background understandings ([12]).

## A - Application
The court evaluated the appellant’s claims in light of the statutory framework:

- **Meaning of 'Accessories'**: The court distinguished accessories from essential vehicle components, emphasizing that items like fuel and engine oils do not fit the ordinary definition of an accessory. They are considered essential for the operation of a vehicle and thus do not qualify as minor fittings or additional items ([16, 19, 20]).
  
- **Repair and Replacement Services**: The assertion that selling fuel constituted a repair or replacement service was dismissed. The court noted there was no substantive evidence supporting the claim that delivering fuel equated to providing such services under the Code ([9, 10]).

- **Industry Code Context**: The court stressed that the automotive industry comprises participants who are directly engaged in the retailing or supply of vehicles, components, and services related to motor vehicles. Fuel retailers, like Silver Park Motors, do not align with this definition ([21, 22]).

- **Affordability of Consumer Protections**: Counter-arguments presented by the appellant regarding consumer protections against substandard fuel were disregarded. The court elucidated that consumers could seek remedies under the Consumer Goods and Services Industry Code, which appropriately governs transactions involving fuel ([24, 25]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court’s findings, concluding that Silver Park Motors CC did not qualify as a retailer or supplier of accessories under the Code. The judgment clarified that the definition of 'accessories' pertained only to additional, non-essential parts of a vehicle. The appeal was dismissed with costs, underscoring the importance of adhering to statutory definitions and the specific boundaries of industry codes ([28]).

**Implications**: This verdict reinforces the distinction between essential components necessary for vehicle operation and accessories, shaping the obligations of different stakeholders within the automotive industry and ensuring that consumer protections remain aligned with the intended scope of industry frameworks under the Consumer Protection Act. 

**Reference**: The Motor Industry Ombudsman of South Africa v Silver Park Motors CC (479/18) [2019] ZASCA 71.
