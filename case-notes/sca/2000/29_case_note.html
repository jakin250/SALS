Supreme Court of Appeal 2000

Case Title: REPORTABLE
Case Number: 624 / 98
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Date of Hearing: 11 May 2000
Date of Delivery: 30 May 2000
Judges: Smalberger, Olivier, Scott, Plewman JJA, Melunsky AJA

F – Facts

[1] This appeal concerns the order granted by the Transvaal Provincial Division of the High Court, which required the second appellant, The National Interim Medical and Dental Council of South Africa, to register the first to eleventh respondents as medical practitioners without restrictions. The High Court launched a successful claim by the respondents against the appellants, leading to a punitive costs award for the respondents.

[2] The appellants oversee health services in South Africa, with the second appellant responsible for regulating registrations within the medical profession under the Medical, Dental and Supplementary Health Service Professions Act 56 of 1974 ("the Act"). The first appellant, the Minister of Health, has executive authority pertaining to the Act.

[3] The respondents are all qualified medical doctors with degrees from various overseas universities and held only limited registration in South Africa, allowing them to work solely in public service roles. After a period in South Africa, they gained permanent residency and citizenship.

[4] The central issue is the refusal of the respondents' request for full registration by the appellants, premised on their failure to pass the Examination for Full Registration (EFR). The appellants assert that compliance with this examination is a requisite condition under the Act for attaining full registration.

[5] The respondents argue they should receive equal treatment as South African citizens who benefitted from a 1991 special dispensation, which exempted certain applicants from the EFR, but were all naturalised citizens after the cut-off date of 31 December 1991. They assert the cut-off date is arbitrary and lacks rational justification concerning their qualifications and experience.

[6] The Act provides the framework to recognize foreign qualifications in South Africa. Section 28 details the stipulations for full registration versus limited registration, requiring a two-year limited registration period and passing the EFR, among other conditions.

I – Issues

[11] Central to the appeal is whether the respondents, due to their foreign qualifications and the arbitrary nature of the cut-off date for the special dispensation, are entitled to full registration without taking the EFR. Additionally, it raises questions about the application of principles of equality and administrative discretion under the Act's provisions.

[12] The court must also consider considerations of irrationality regarding the Council's refusal to exempt the respondents from the EFR requirement and potential administrative mala fides in decision-making.

R – Rules

[6] The relevant provisions of the medical regulatory framework include sections 17, 24, 26, and 28 of the Act, along with regulations promulgated under it, including R2274 and R1243 concerning the registration process.

[8] Section 28 (1)(b)–(d) delineates the prerequisites for full registration, including the passage of the EFR, thereby establishing the legal requirements for medical practitioners wishing to practice without restrictions.

[27] Furthermore, Section 4(g) of the Act grants the Council the discretion to recognize qualifications held by an applicant as equivalent to prescribed qualifications, subject to application.

A – Application

[22] The High Court recognized the administrative nature of the Council's decision to refuse the exemption from the EFR requirement and determined that the distinction made between the respondents and other applicants who benefitted from the special dispensation was arbitrary. The court held that the particular context of individual situations should be taken into account, especially considering the background of the respondents and the degree of their integration into South African medical practice.

[28] Despite the evidence of capable qualifications and experience among the respondents, the Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized that the statutory framework remains definitive. Relieving the respondents of the requirement to pass the EFR would require substantial justification grounded in law, which was not present.

[30] The court ultimately determined that the special dispensation's limited application and existence for a defined period meant it could not be retroactively extended to the respondents as a general practice or an automatic entitlement.

[32] With regard to their equal treatment claim referencing South African citizens, the court found no discrimination against the respondents relative to foreign doctors in similar situations. Treating all applicants uniformly according to the law upheld the principles of equality, as the eligibility criteria made no distinction pertinent to citizenship post the cut-off date.

C – Conclusion

[41] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal was meritorious. The High Court's finding was set aside, and the action was dismissed, emphasizing that, while sympathy towards the respondents is warranted given their qualifications and contributions, the legal frameworks and requirements for full registration are firmly established. The court ruled accordingly: the appellants' costs related to preparing an extensive appeal record were liable for punitive costs due to the extravagance in documentation submitted.

The ruling clarified that special dispensations as historical provisions should not create a reasonable expectation of ongoing benefits post their defined application period. Thus, the case delineates substantial boundaries concerning the registration of medical practitioners in the context of changing socio-legal landscapes in post-apartheid South Africa. The judgement solidifies the need for compliance with stipulated professional standards and regulatory frameworks while also critically examining historical anomalies in policy.
