Supreme Court of Appeal

André Van Eck v The State (SCA)

Facts [1] André Van Eck (the appellant) was convicted following a guilty plea on 19 February 1999 in the Port Elizabeth Regional Court for one count of theft and 14 counts of fraud.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2003

Facts

[1] André Van Eck (the appellant) was convicted following a guilty plea on 19 February 1999 in the Port Elizabeth Regional Court for one count of theft and 14 counts of fraud. At the time, he was 37 years old with a criminal record consisting of 43 prior convictions, predominantly involving dishonesty. The Regional Court declared him an habitual criminal under section 286(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (‘the Act’). The appellant's appeal against his sentence in the Eastern Cape Provincial Division was denied, leading to an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal with leave from that Court.

[2] The theft involved the acquisition of a cheque book on 12 August 1997, followed by 14 incidents of fraud where forged cheques were used from 1 to 6 September 1997. The total financial loss amounted to R3,172.02, with purchases including high-value items.

[3] The appellant’s record illustrates a long history of dishonesty, starting from his first conviction in May 1984, which included 21 counts of various thefts and frauds. Sentences for earlier offences were lenient, often resulting in cautions or suspended sentences. He later received actual prison time, albeit briefly due to the nature of his crimes.

[4] Notably, the appellant escaped from Pollsmoor prison before surrendering to authorities in November 1997. The crimes for which he was ultimately convicted were committed shortly before his surrender.

[8] Section 286(1) allows a court to declare a person an habitual criminal if it finds that the individual habitually commits crimes and requires that the community be protected from the individual.

Issues

[11] The primary issue is whether the lower court erred in declaring the appellant an habitual criminal under section 286(1) of the Act. Specifically, this involves:

  • Whether the appellant's actions constituted habitual criminality.
  • Whether the crimes committed warranted the court's decision to impose that specific characterization and consequence.
  • The extent of the trial court's discretion in applying the habitual criminal declaration and whether it was exercised appropriately.

Rules

[8] Section 286(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act provides the framework for declaring someone an habitual criminal. The court must be satisfied that the individual commits offences habitually, poses a risk that necessitates community protection, and meets certain criteria regarding age and sentencing terms.

[10] Precedents such as S v Makoula (1978) and definitions established in S v Niemand (2001) provide guidance on interpreting habitual criminality, emphasizing that habitual offenders exhibit a persistent behaviour of crime rather than a reflection of individual instances.

Application

[12] The court's analysis revealed that the appellant had openly conceded his habitual criminality, failing to argue against the finding that warranted community protection from his actions. His history demonstrated a pattern of recurring crimes, primarily involving fraudulent cheque use, reflecting a tendency towards dishonesty that the community needed to be safeguarded against.

Further, the court noted that although the amounts involved were often modest, the prevalence of cheque fraud in society warranted severe scrutiny. The appellant’s actions during sentencing, juxtaposed with prior warnings about section 286, indicated a clear disregard for the law, reinforcing the trial court's decision not to modify the habitual classification.

The court upheld the trial court's discretion, highlighting the absence of any misdirection and affirming that the punishment aligns with the intent of section 286 – to protect society from persistent offenders. The appellant's previous warnings under this section further substantiated the legality of the declaration.

Conclusion

[13] The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the trial court's declaration of the appellant as an habitual criminal under section 286(1) of the Act. This decision underscored the necessity of protecting the community from habitual offenders, particularly in the context of cheque fraud, which poses significant societal harm. The court reinforced the importance of discretionary sentencing in consideration of the appellant’s history and prevailing crime trends, ultimately concluding that the trial court acted within its mandate and judicial discretion. This case illustrates the application of deterrent justice principles in the framework for habitual criminality under South African law.
