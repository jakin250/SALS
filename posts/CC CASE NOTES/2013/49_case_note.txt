# FIRAC Case Note: Khumalo v Member of the Executive Council for Education: KwaZulu-Natal

## F - Facts

The case revolves around an application brought by Nkosinathi Lawrence Khumalo and Krish Ritchie against the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for Education in KwaZulu-Natal regarding alleged unlawful promotions. The factual background begins with the publication of a job advertisement for the position of Chief Personnel Officer by the Department of Education, KwaZulu-Natal, in March 2004 ([2]). The requirements specified included extensive relevant experience and two years of supervisory experience at level six or seven.

Khumalo, then employed at salary level five, applied for the position and was eventually shortlisted, interviewed, and promoted to the role in April 2004 ([3]). Conversely, Ritchie, at salary level seven, applied but was not shortlisted. Following Khumalo's promotion, Ritchie raised a grievance regarding his non-shortlisting, which led to arbitration resulting in a “protected promotion” for Ritchie in July 2005 ([4]). Subsequent complaints regarding irregularities were raised, prompting the MEC to initiate a Task Team in 2006 to investigate the promotions ([6–9]).

The Task Team identified significant flaws in the promotional processes, stating that Khumalo did not meet the advertised requirements and suggesting that Ritchie's promotion was granted under dubious circumstances. Despite the report being presented to the MEC in January 2007, she only approached the Labour Court for a challenge in October 2008 ([10, 12]).

In the Labour Court, the MEC sought to declare both promotions invalid on several grounds, citing her obligations under the Public Service Act and the Constitution ([11–12]). Khumalo and Ritchie opposed the application, raising issues of unreasonable delay and res judicata ([13]). The Labour Court granted the MEC's requests but ordered no salary deductions to be made ([15]) and recommended further investigations ([12]).

The Labour Appeal Court upheld the decision to set aside the promotions but noted the potential for irreversible prejudice to Khumalo and Ritchie ([18]).

## I - Issues

The issues for determination in this case can be summarized as follows:

(a) Should leave to appeal be granted to the applicants? ([20])  
(b) What is the legal nature of the MEC's challenge regarding her department's decisions? ([21])  
(c) Is there a duty on state functionaries to rectify unlawful administrative actions under their authority? ([21])  
(d) Should the court review Khumalo's promotion despite the MEC's delayed application? ([21])  
(e) Is the review of Ritchie's protected promotion appropriate given the MEC's delay? ([21])  
(f) What is the appropriate remedy if the promotions are found to be unlawful? ([21])

## R - Rules

The legal framework applicable to the case involves several key statutes and principles, including:

1. **Public Service Act**: The MEC referenced Section 9 of the Public Service Act, empowering her to appoint and promote individuals ([12]).
2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA)**: The MEC's application was underpinned by a demand for lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair action as stipulated in Section 33 of the Constitution ([24]).
3. **Labour Relations Act (LRA)**: Section 158(1)(h) allows for review of decisions taken by the state as an employer ([24]).
4. **Principle of Legality**: This principle requires public power exercises to be lawful and rational ([28–29]).

Additionally, the decisions of courts regarding administrative actions must adhere to the standard of reasonableness and employ the guiding principles from Section 195 of the Constitution, governing basic values and principles in public administration ([30, 34]).

## A - Application

The Constitutional Court analyzed the legal standings and fate of Khumalo and Ritchie's promotions, considering the MEC's undue delay in challenging the decisions. It noted that while public functionaries have the duty to uphold the law and rectify violations, the extend of this obligation is tempered by considerations of fairness and the potential prejudice involved, particularly concerning employees’ expectations regarding security and stability in their positions ([39]).

Khumalo's promotion was scrutinized against the backdrop of the requirements stated in the job advertisement. The MEC maintained that Khumalo did not meet the stipulated criteria; however, the case's delay significantly hindered establishing the promotions' legality ([58–61]). The court pointed out the time elapsed negatively impacted its ability to ascertain the full picture regarding the promotion's lawfulness, which, coupled with the MEC's inability to provide a justifiable explanation for the delay, prompted the conclusion that her application should have been dismissed ([68]).

As for Ritchie's protected promotion, the MEC's challenge was found to circumvent statutory limits under Section 145 of the LRA regarding arbitration awards. The MEC had erred in not seeking to challenge the award itself within the stipulated timeframe, leading the court to dismiss her application for Ritchie's promotion due to procedural non-compliance ([70–73]).

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal the Labour Appeal Court's decision, ultimately upholding Khumalo and Ritchie's promotions and setting aside the orders of the Labour Court and Labour Appeal Court. The court found that the MEC's unreasonable and unexcused delay in seeking a review was incompatible with the principles of administrative justice and the rule of law, rendering her application effectively untenable ([74]).

The judgment underscores the importance of timely redress in public sector employment contexts and reinforces the principle that public functionaries must act within the confines of established statutory frameworks and procedural safeguards. Additionally, it affirms the integrity of the employment process within the public sector, highlighting the necessity for clarity, fairness, and accountability in administrative actions, and the detrimental effects of undue delay on judicial review processes. Consequently, the implications for public-sector employees suggest a strengthened adherence to lawful processes and a proactive stance in addressing any identified irregularities.
