Supreme Court of Appeal

Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope v MW Randell (341/2012) [2013] ZASCA 36

In response to these pending criminal proceedings, the appellant, the Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope, initiated an application to have Randell's name removed from the roll of attorneys, based on the same facts underlying the criminal charges ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 36

F – Facts

The respondent, Michael Wharton Randell, is a practicing attorney in Port Elizabeth, facing serious charges of fraud and theft amounting to R2.4 million. These charges stem from allegations that he, along with two other trustees, misappropriated funds from the Greenwood Property Trust, which benefitted the Greenwood Primary School ([1]).

In response to these pending criminal proceedings, the appellant, the Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope, initiated an application to have Randell's name removed from the roll of attorneys, based on the same facts underlying the criminal charges ([2]). Randell did not file an answering affidavit but instead filed a counter-application seeking a stay of the striking off application until the conclusion of the criminal proceedings ([2]). The question arose regarding the court's discretion to grant such a stay, particularly in light of Randell's assertion that he might suffer prejudice if compelled to provide a statement in civil proceedings ([3]).

The High Court initially granted a stay based on the principle that civil proceedings might prejudice the accused's defense, citing the general practice articulated in Du Toit v Van Rensburg and Davis v Tip NO ([12]). This decision was contested by the Law Society, leading to the present appeal.

I – Issues

The central issues for determination were:

  1. Whether the court was entitled to grant a stay of the civil proceedings despite Randell's lack of compulsion to file a response to the striking off application ([3]).
  2. Whether Randell demonstrated he would suffer prejudice by making a sworn statement in opposition to the application ([3]).

R – Rules

The legal framework guiding this case primarily stems from the interpretation of section 35(1)(a) and (c) of the Constitution, which guarantee the right to remain silent and not to be compelled to confess or admit evidence against oneself ([11]).

Key precedents included:

  • Du Toit v Van Rensburg 1967 (4) SA 433 (C), which articulated the practice of staying civil proceedings when related criminal matters were pending, highlighting a concern for potential prejudice to the accused ([12]).
  • Davis v Tip NO 1996 (1) SA 1152 (W), where Nugent J emphasized that the general principle applied only in scenarios involving state compulsion to disclose information, and delineated the limitations of the court’s discretion in cases lacking such compulsion ([19], [23]).

Additionally, the approach to stays in civil proceedings amidst pending criminal allegations has been scrutinized in several cases, chiefly around the nature of any compulsion to testify or provide statements ([19], [23]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal criticized the lower court's decision for overstepping by broadly applying the principle without adequately assessing the implications of state compulsion. The court elucidated that, in absence of such compulsion, the likelihood of prejudice does not automatically justify a stay of civil proceedings. The respondent had the choice to respond to the allegations without legal obligation to disclose potentially self-incriminating information ([18], [22], [30]).

The court found that Randell had not sufficiently shown he would face prejudice due to the striking off proceedings. He had already disclosed parts of his defense in a related civil matter and had not adequately established how responding to the allegations in the striking-off application would compromise his criminal defense ([32]).

Furthermore, the court reinforced the importance of maintaining the attorney's integrity, suggesting that the need for public interest weighed against granting a stay in this case ([33]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the High Court's decision to grant a stay, dismissing Randell's application with costs on an attorney-and-client scale, thereby reaffirming the principles surrounding the interactions between civil and criminal proceedings. The ruling emphasized that a stay is warranted only under conditions involving state compulsion that threatens the accused's right to silence, which was not present in this case. The judgment not only clarified the judicial perspective on stays in civil proceedings but also reinforced the standards of professional conduct required of attorneys ([35]).
