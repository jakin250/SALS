Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 74 (26 May 2023) ## FIRAC Analysis ### F – Fa

CA No: 1270/2021
[2023] ZASCA 74 (26 May 2023)

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

The case revolves around a deed of suretyship signed by two trustees of the Penvaan Property Trust (the Trust) for Mrs. Mignon Renate Volker's legal fees as part of divorce proceedings. The dispute surfaces regarding the validity of this deed, signed on May 25, 2013, in light of the Trust's requirement for decisions to be taken jointly and unanimously ([paras 1–5]).

At the time of the dispute, the trustees included Mr. Thomas Wilhelm Volker (husband of Mrs. Volker), Mrs. Volker, and Mr. Abraham Johannes de Witt ([para 2]). Mrs. Volker, requesting legal representation, engaged the appellant, Shepstone & Wylie Attorneys, which led to the execution of the suretyship ([para 3]). The meeting to discuss this suretyship proceeded without Mr. Volker, who was unavailable due to prior engagements ([paras 4–5]). The appellant sought payment under the deed, but the Trust contended it was invalid as it was not executed with the required unanimity of all trustees, primarily arguing that Mr. Volker’s absence negated the decision's validity ([paras 6–8]).

I – Issues

The principal legal question is whether the deed of suretyship was valid given the absence of one trustee during its enactment ([para 9]). The legal concerns revolve around:

  1. Whether the trustees adequately adhered to their fiduciary obligations under the Trust deed in executing the suretyship.
  2. If the suretyship benefitted the Trust itself or was solely for Mrs. Volker's personal debt ([paras 6–8]).

R – Rules

The resolution of this case is guided by several key legal principles relating to trusts and fiduciary duties:

  1. Joint Action of Trustees: It is a well-established principle that trustees must act jointly unless the trust deed specifically allows a majority decision ([para 20]). This principle ensures that all trustees are involved in decisions impacting the Trust and its assets ([para 21]).
  2. Fiduciary Duties: Trustees owe fiduciary duties that encompass acting in good faith and in the best interests of the Trust and its beneficiaries ([para 20]).
  3. Interpretation of Trust Deeds: The interpretation of clauses within the trust deed and its appendices is crucial, especially regarding the requirement for unanimous decisions ([paras 19–20]). The interpretation framework established in Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality is emphasized for ascertaining meanings within legal documents ([para 39]).

A – Application

In addressing the appeal, the court considered the main provisions of the Trust deed, notably that actions binding the Trust needed unanimous consent from all trustees, as affirmed by several clauses within the deed ([paras 24–30]). Mrs. Volker and Mr. De Witt executed the suretyship without Mr. Volker’s presence or consent, thereby violating the trust requirements ([para 28]).

The court dismissed the appellant's argument that the mere giving of notice and Mr. Volker's absence constituted sufficient involvement. The court emphasized that, even with reasonable notice, Mr. Volker did not participate in the decision-making process, thus rendering the resolution invalid ([paras 24–27],[34]).

Additionally, the explanation of clauses from the trust deed—particularly concerning external dealings—asserted that agreements executed without all trustees' consensus cannot bind the Trust, reinforcing the invalidity of the suretyship ([paras 25–30]).

The significance of the proposed resolutions at the trustee meeting also played a vital role, as the court highlighted that although efforts had been made to include Mr. Volker, his non-participation was definitive and material in contesting the deed’s validity ([paras 20–30]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court's findings that the deed of suretyship was invalid and unenforceable, as it was signed in contravention of the Trust deed’s requirements for unanimous action among trustees ([paras 32–33]). The court’s ruling reaffirms the necessity for trustees to adhere strictly to the terms of the trust deed, especially regarding joint decision-making in significant matters affecting a Trust’s liabilities and obligations. The implications for fiduciary responsibilities are significant, establishing precedence on how trust governance is affected by trustee participation and consent processes in legal dealings.

In conclusion, the appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the legal position on trust governance and liabilities ([para 33]). The decision underscores the critical need for compliance with fiduciary duties and the formal requirements laid out in trust instruments.
