Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: Britz v S (613/09) [2010] ZASCA 71 

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The appellant, Ellouise Britz, was convicted on 6 May 2008 by the Eastern Cape Commercial Crimes Division of the regional court for 67 counts of fraud, which occurred between June 2003 and January 2007. Britz, a bookkeeper for a close corporation, misappropriated over R330,000 by alleging that electronic funds transferred to her husband's account were payments for corporate debts and by purchasing goods from suppliers under false pretenses ([1]). 

Initially sentenced to five years' imprisonment (two years suspended) after a thorough judgement from the regional magistrate, Britz served just over four months of her sentence before applying for condonation, leave to appeal, and new evidence to be presented. The new evidence related to her mother’s death, which left her children without adequate care and emotional support ([2]). The appeal court dismissed both her application and appeal, highlighting that the facts must be determined based on the situation at the time of original sentencing, without consideration of new circumstances unless exceptional reasons were present ([3]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue in this case was whether the appellant could introduce new evidence, not previously available at the time of sentencing, to support her claim for a non-custodial sentence. The court also had to assess whether the interests of the appellant’s children provided grounds for the court to interfere with the original sentencing decision. Additionally, the case raised questions regarding the limits of appellate authority to consider subsequent evidence ([4]-[5]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework dealing with appeals, particularly regarding the introduction of new evidence, is anchored in both the Criminal Procedure Act and the Supreme Court Act. In terms of these legal provisions, a court may receive further evidence during an appeal provided that:

1. There exists a reasonable explanation for not presenting the evidence during trial.
2. The evidence is prima facie likely to be true.
3. The evidence must be materially relevant to the outcome ([5]-[6]).

Precedential cases such as *S v De Jager* and *S v Michele* have further delineated the criteria under which an appellate court can accept new evidence, particularly emphasizing that such acceptance should be the exception rather than the rule ([5]).

### A – Application
Upon analysis of the evidence Britz attempted to introduce, the court found that it did not meet the requisite criteria. The court emphasized that the evidence relating to her mother’s death was not materially relevant since the capacity of the mother to care for the children was already questioned during the initial sentencing process. The lower court had already determined that Britz’s mother was too ill to realistically provide the necessary care ([10]). 

The court also scrutinized the prima facie likelihood of the truth of the new evidence presented. The discrepancies between the affidavits of Britz and the probation officer concerning the capability of her family to care for the children indicated insufficient substantiation of new claims regarding the children's care situation ([13]). 

Finally, the court found that no exceptional or peculiar circumstances justified the introduction of new evidence. The emotional needs of the children, while significant, could not override the need to enforce the law and punishment for serious criminal conduct ([14]). 

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that Britz's appeal was to be dismissed on multiple grounds. The court concluded that the new evidence was neither materially relevant nor was there a prima facie likelihood that it was true. Thus, the original sentencing of five years’ imprisonment stood as it was deemed fair and just given the significant nature of the crimes committed by Britz, her position of trust, and the impact of her actions on both the close corporation and its employees ([16]). The court reaffirmed that while the interests of children are paramount, they do not provide a basis to undermine the integrity of the judicial process or to mitigate the consequences of criminal wrongdoing.

The ramifications of this decision underscore the stringent conditions under which appellate courts can entertain new evidence, thereby maintaining the integrity of the criminal justice system while also balancing considerations related to familial responsibilities.
