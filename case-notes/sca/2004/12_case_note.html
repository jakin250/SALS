THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA

Reportable Case No: 584/2002
In the matter between:
Mushavhani Wilson Madzivhandila (First Appellant)
Nyamundzhedzi Madzivhandila (Second Appellant)
Joyce Mulaudzi (Third Appellant)
Tshamunwe Masindi (Fourth Appellant)
and
Thiambiwi Eunice Madzivhandila (First Respondent)
Maele Jackson Mushasha (Second Respondent)

Coram:
Mthiyane JA, Jones and Van Heerden AJJA
Heard: 17 February 2004
Delivered: 24 March 2004

F - Facts
The appeal arose from a dispute concerning certain assets held by the second appellant, Nyamundzhedzi Madzivhandila. The assets in contention included an immovable property and a business site, both relevant to a joint estate resulting from the marriage between the first appellant, Mushavhani Wilson Madzivhandila, and the first respondent, Thiambiwi Eunice Madzivhandila. Their marriage, which existed in community of property, was dissolved by a decree of divorce on 25 March 1998 ([1]).

The first respondent sought a declaration that these assets formed part of the joint estate. She argued that they were acquired under an agreement entailing that the second appellant (the mother of the first appellant) would hold the assets as a nominee due to the restrictions imposed by their employment as civil servants under the Venda Government ([2]). The first and second appellants contested this assertion, claiming sole ownership of the assets and denying the existence of such an agreement ([3]).

The Thohoyandou High Court ruled in favor of the first respondent, finding that the assets were indeed part of the joint estate. The court ordered that the proceeds from the sale of the Stand be allocated for the division of the estate, and the second respondent was tasked with managing the Site ([4]). The defendants’ application for leave to appeal was initially denied ([5]).

At the appeal, the defendants sought to introduce a new argument regarding the illegality of the agreement based on Section 23 of the Venda Public Service Act 8 of 1986, which reportedly prohibited civil servants from engaging in business ventures ([6]). The plaintiff opposed this amendment, arguing that it had not been raised in the initial proceedings ([7]).

I - Issues
The primary legal issues before the court included:

  1. Whether the alleged agreement that the second appellant would hold the disputed assets as a nominee for the joint estate was valid or rendered illegal under the provisions of Section 23 of the Venda Public Service Act.
  2. Whether the claim of illegality, presented for the first time on appeal, should be considered by the court ([6], [9]).

R - Rules
The relevant legal principles and statutes addressed during the appeal included:

  • Section 23 of the Venda Public Service Act 8 of 1986, which limits the ability of civil servants to engage in remunerative work outside their employment unless authorized.
  • The principle established in Yannakou v Apollo Club, which stipulates that a court may raise issues of illegality mero motu, provided these arise from the evidence before it, and all necessary facts are presented ([8]).
  • The general law of evidence regarding the credibility of witnesses, where a trial court's findings in this area are typically afforded deference by appellate courts ([9]).

A - Application
The court allowed the introduction of the new ground of appeal regarding the alleged illegality associated with the agreement ([7]). In examining the merits, the court acknowledged the conflicting assertions about the existence of the agreement. Nevertheless, it upheld the trial court's credibility findings, emphasizing the deference afforded to the trial judge's assessment of witness reliability ([9]).

The court scrutinized the application of Section 23 of the Act. It concluded that neither the first respondent nor the first appellant participated in business operations in violation of the Act, nor was there compelling evidence suggesting that they were unable to place their full time at the disposal of the state due to this agreement. The mere acquisition of an interest was not deemed to render the agreement illegal under the statutory prohibition, as there was no direct contravention established ([11]).

Finally, the court addressed the argument regarding animus on the part of the appellants to acquire the disputed assets. It stated that the intention to acquire was not in question since the action centered around establishing the status of the assets within the joint estate ([12]).

C - Conclusion
The appeal was dismissed with costs, confirming the lower court's ruling that the Stand and Site were part of the joint estate ([13]). The court held that the agreement was not rendered illegal by the provisions of the Venda Public Service Act. This judgment underscores the importance of recognizing informal agreements, particularly within familial contexts, where such arrangements reflect an equitable understanding of property interests, notwithstanding formal restrictions on civil servants engaging in certain business activities. The decision also illustrates the judicial willingness to consider points of law not initially presented if it aligns with broader principles of justice, particularly in family and property disputes stemming from matrimonial relationships.

References

  1. Yannakou v Apollo Club 1974 (1) SA 614 AD at 623H.
  2. Venda Public Service Act 8 of 1986.
  3. Rex v Dhlumayo 1948 (2) SA 677(A) at 705-706.
  4. Bank of Lisbon and South Africa Ltd v The Master and Others 1987 (1) SA 276 (A).
