Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Road Accident Fund and Others v Mabunda Incorporated and Others; Minister of Transport v Road Accident Fund and Others [2022] ZASCA 169 (1 December 2022)

## F – Facts
In the matter of **Road Accident Fund and Others v Mabunda Incorporated and Others** (case no 1147/2020) and **Minister of Transport v Road Accident Fund and Others** (case no 1082/2020), the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa dealt with two consolidated appeals. In the first appeal, the Road Accident Fund (RAF), along with its Chairperson and CEO, sought to revive an appeal that had lapsed. The Minister of Transport was a party in both appeals.

The RAF is established under the **Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996** and serves to provide compensation for damages resulting from negligent driving. Following a tender process in 2014, the RAF had appointed a panel of 103 attorneys to provide legal services. As these contracts were set to expire at the end of November 2019, the RAF attempted to manage the transition by initiating a new tender process.

However, changes within the RAF’s Board led to a suspension of previously issued instructions and ultimately resulted in the cancellation of the new tender due to financial constraints and a strategic shift towards insourcing legal services. This cancellation was contested by the panel attorneys through several applications to the Gauteng Division of the High Court, asserting that the RAF's decisions were arbitrary and lacked a sustainable operational plan post-termination of the contracts ([1]–[10]).

The High Court found in favor of the attorneys, leading to an order that set aside the RAF’s decisions, mandating compliance with existing contractual obligations. This order was appealed by the RAF ([10]–[11]).

## I – Issues
1. **Mootness**: Whether the appeal was moot given that the High Court order specified a six-month operational period, which had likely lapsed.
2. **Authority to Cancel the Tender**: Whether the cancellation of the tender by the RAF was executed by an authorized entity.
3. **Regulatory Compliance**: Whether the cancellation adhered to the stipulations under **Regulation 13(1)** of the **Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000**.
4. **Handover Decision and Second Addendum**: Issues surrounding the legality of the handover of files and changes in the attorneys’ service level agreements ([12]–[13]).

## R – Rules
1. **Authority to Cancel the Tender**: The Board of the RAF had delegated authority to the Bid Adjudication Committee (BAC) to manage the tender process and cancellation.
2. **Regulation 13(1)** stipulates conditions under which an organ of state may cancel a tender, including circumstances of unaffordability and changed service needs.
3. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000**: Dictates that administrative actions must be lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair.
4. **Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**: Provides for the suspension and implementation of High Court orders pending appeals ([16]–[20]).

## A – Application
### Mootness
The court evaluated whether the appeal was moot by considering that while the High Court order intended to operate for six months, certain orders directly enforcing the reinstatement of the tender were not confined to that timeframe. As such, the issues surrounding the validity of the tender cancellation were deemed alive ([14]).

### Authority to Cancel
The RAF asserted its authority to cancel the tender through the BAC’s decision, initially proposed by the CEO. The court found that the BAC had been duly authorized by the Board and maintained that the Board's delegation remained valid despite a change in its composition. Therefore, the BAC’s authority to cancel the tender was upheld ([16]–[18]).

### Compliance with Regulation 13(1)
The RAF grounded its cancellation of the tender on two prongs: changed circumstances and financial inability to meet projected obligations. The court scrutinized the context of the tender and the proposed new operational model, concluding that the RAF sufficiently demonstrated that changed circumstances justified the cancellation. Furthermore, the evidence of ongoing financial deficits bolstered this position ([19]–[29]).

### Handover Decision and Second Addendum
The court noted that the handover decision and the second addendum to the service agreements must be examined in light of legality. While the High Court did not expressly invalidate the second addendum, the signing by attorneys indicated acceptance of the terms. On the legality of the handover process, as attorneys had already transitioned their files, no additional arguments against this decision were considered relevant ([30]–[32]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately concluded that the RAF had complied with necessary regulatory frameworks to cancel the tender based on demonstrable changed circumstances and fiscal considerations. The High Court's prior finding was set aside, reinstating the RAF’s decision to cancel the tender. The appeal under case number 1082/2020 was struck from the roll due to mootness, while the appeal under 1147/2020 was upheld with costs. The resolution of this case has implications on administrative processes within state institutions, particularly regarding procurement obligations and the persistence of procedural fairness in decision-making ([35]–[38]).
