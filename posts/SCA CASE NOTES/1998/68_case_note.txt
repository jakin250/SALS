# FIRAC Case Note for Case No 625/96

## IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA

### M C C CONTRACTS (PTY) LTD v RUDOLF GIDEON COERTZEN & Others

**Coram:** Howie, Harms, Zulman, Streichler, JJA et Melunsky AJA  
**Date of Hearing:** 17 September 1998  
**Date of Delivery:** 18 September 1998  
**Judgment by:** Howie JA  

---

### F – Facts

In this matter, the appellant, M C C Contracts (Pty) Ltd ("MCC"), was involved as a second third party in a damages suit before the Orange Free State Provincial Division. The procedural history began with the issuance of summons in March 1995 ([para 2]). Subsequently, the first respondent, Hester Wilhelmina Gerrard N.O. ("the defendant"), served a third party notice to the third respondent, South African Eagle Insurance Company Ltd ("SA Eagle"), in April 1995, shortly after filing her plea ([para 2]). 

In May of the same year, SA Eagle gave notice of intention to defend and later excepted to the initial third party notice ([para 2]). The cumulation of procedural steps eventually led to the defendant serving a further third party notice on MCC in October ([para 2]). MCC defended this action and sought to set aside the third party notice as an irregular proceeding under Rule 30 of the Uniform Rules of Court because the leave of the court for such service had not been obtained ([para 2]).

The Orange Free State Provincial Division dismissed this application, leading MCC to appeal ([para 2]). The core argument from MCC was that the defendant needed court permission to serve the second third party notice since it had not been obtained, asserting that such notice was irregular ([para 6]). The defendant countered that leave was unnecessary, as the notice had been served before the close of pleadings, referencing Rule 13(3)(a) which did not necessitate leave in such instances ([para 6]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the pleadings involving the defendant and SA Eagle were "pleadings in the action in connection with which the notice was issued" at the time when the second third party notice was served on MCC ([para 7]). This raised significant questions regarding the interpretation and application of Rule 13 of the Uniform Rules of Court regarding third party notices.

### R – Rules

The relevant provisions under scrutiny were Rule 13 of the Uniform Rules of Court. The Rule outlines the circumstances under which a party may issue a third party notice and the procedural requirements linked to it. Notably, Rule 13(3)(a) states that a third party notice must be served before the close of pleadings in the action to be valid without requiring leave of the Court ([para 4]). 

Key considerations from the Rule include:
1. Definition of "action" as the one commenced by the plaintiff ([para 7]).
2. Framework allowing for the consolidation of issues among multiple parties within a singular action to avoid multiplicity of litigation ([para 7]).
3. The stipulation that all parties involved in the dispute must be integrated within the action's pleadings ([para 7]).

### A – Application

In his analysis, Howie JA determined that the learned Judge in the lower court correctly interpreted the phrase "in connection with which" to indicate that the pleadings need not strictly limit themselves to the interactions between the plaintiff and defendant alone but could encompass all parties in the action ([para 8]). He found that the language of Rule 13 did support a broader interpretation, which emphasizes the consolidation of disputes rather than fragmentation into separate actions.

Moreover, the argument that there existed two separate actions, one between the plaintiff and defendant and another between the latter and the third party, was rejected ([para 8]). Howie JA emphasized the Rule's purpose to promote single proceedings rather than encourage a fragmented trial process. 

With SA Eagle actively engaged in the action and having become a party to it within the context defined by the rules, it followed that the pleadings concerning SA Eagle were indeed part of the same action as it related to the third party notice served on MCC ([para 9]). It was consequently held that proper compliance with Rule 13 had been met, negating any claim by MCC that the notice was irregular due to lack of court leave ([para 9]).

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court dismissed the appeal by MCC, affirming the decision of the court a quo. The ruling underscored the importance of interpreting procedural rules, particularly related to third party notices, in a manner that promotes fair and efficient adjudication of connected claims within a single framework ([para 9]). 

The implications of this ruling reaffirm the broader interpretation of rules governing pleadings in civil procedure, supporting the principle that procedural efficiency can often take precedence over strict adherence to formality when the underlying action's integrity is preserved ([para 9]). As a result, the governance of third party notices would benefit from a clear understanding of how interconnected claims must be treated within the legal framework, encouraging parties to fully engage with all aspects of a case under one unified process. Thus, all costs of the appeal were awarded against MCC, aligning the decision with the principle that unsuccessful parties in litigation generally bear the costs ([para 9]).
