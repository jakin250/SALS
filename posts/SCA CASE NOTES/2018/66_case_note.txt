Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

## FIRAC Case Note: CSARS v Daikin Air Conditioning South Africa (Pty) Ltd

**Neutral Citation:** CSARS v Daikin Air Conditioning (185/2017) [2018] ZASCA 66  
**Coram:** Maya P and Majiedt, Mbha & Van der Merwe JJA and Davis AJA  
**Delivered:** 25 May 2018  
**Heard:** 9 March 2018  
**Case Summary:** Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 – classification of articles for customs duty – correct tariff to be applied concerning ‘window or wall types, self-contained or “split-system”’ air conditioning machines and parts thereof.  

### F – Facts
The respondent, Daikin Air Conditioning South Africa (Pty) Limited (hereinafter "Daikin"), is engaged in the importation and distribution of air conditioning machines and related parts ([1]). This case involves the classification of parts of air conditioning machines for customs duty purposes under the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 (hereinafter "the Act"). Specifically, Daikin appealed the classification imposed by the appellant, the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Services (hereinafter "the Commissioner"), which designated the relevant products as falling under tariff heading 8415.90.05, relating to certain indoor units ([6]).

In the earlier ruling by the Gauteng Division, Pretoria, the court ruled in favor of Daikin, leading to an appeal by the Commissioner ([1]). This appeal centers on the application of the correct tariff headings as stipulated in Schedule 1 of the Act with reference to the Harmonised Commodity Description and Coding System ([3]). The classification of air conditioning units into ‘split-systems’ and ‘self-contained’ types is particularly pivotal, as these definitions directly influence the determination of applicable customs duties ([6], [8]).

### I – Issues
The legal issues in this case revolve around the proper classification of the indoor units for split-system air conditioning machines and whether these should be classified under tariff heading 8415.90.05, as argued by the Commissioner, or as ‘other’ under subheading 8415.90.90, as claimed by Daikin ([7], [18]). The precise legal questions are:

1. What constitutes 'window or wall types, self-contained or “split-system”' air conditioning machines in the context of the relevant tariff headings?
2. How should these terms be interpreted in light of the provisions of the Customs and Excise Act, the general rules of interpretation, and the explanatory notes provided by the World Customs Organization (WCO) ([18], [23])?

### R – Rules
The primary source of law in this context is the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964, specifically section 47, which governs the classification of goods for the purpose of determining customs duty ([1]). The Act incorporates the provisions of Schedule 1, which presents various tariff headings and their corresponding legal interpretations.

The relevant rules of interpretation are outlined in the general rules for the interpretation of the Schedule. As established in *Secretary for Customs and Excise v Thomas Barlow & Sons Ltd* 1970 (2) SA 660 (A), the courts should primarily ascertain the meaning of the relevant headings and notes while utilizing the Brussels Notes issued by the WCO for guidance in complex cases ([4], [25]).

The pertinent tariff headings under consideration in this case include:
- 8415.10: "Windows or wall types, self-contained or ‘split-system’ air conditioning machines."
- 8415.90: Parts thereof, with further specificity in subheadings ([5]).

### A – Application
The court navigated through the interpretative difficulties posed by the terms ‘window or wall types, self-contained or “split-system”.’ The distinction drawn between the two types of air conditioning machines (split and self-contained) became a focal point ([7], [20]). The court affirmed that while the wording of the relevant tariff headings could be interpreted in several ways, the Brussels Notes indicated a clear intent to include split-system air conditioning machines with ceiling-mounted indoor units under tariff heading 8415.10 ([9], [12]).

The important amendment added to the Brussels Notes in December 2012 was critical in establishing that the subheading intended to encompass indoor units mounted in various locations, including ceilings ([9], [23]). This interpretation aligned with the evidence provided, which suggested that the products in question were specifically intended for use within ceilings and not through windows or walls, thus favoring the Commissioner's classification ([14]).

The judgment pointed out that the practical implications of customs duty classification lead to a more commercially sensible interpretation, one that does not differentiate between ceiling-mounted and wall-mounted indoor units where the latter does not play a materially different role in operation ([14], [15]). The legal analysis highlighted the necessity of aligning the classification with the reality of product application and the common commercial practice in the air conditioning industry ([14]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the indoor units classified under tariff subheading 8415.90.05 accurately reflects the products offered by Daikin and upheld the appeal by the Commissioner ([16]). The court ruled that the products are indeed classifiable under this tariff heading, thereby overturning the decision of the lower court that dismissed the Commissioner's classification ([16]).

The decision underscores the need for a meticulous inquiry into statutory wording and the application of international interpretation standards established by the WCO to ensure compliance with the Act ([32]). This ruling enhances the understanding of commercial sensibility within legal frameworks, emphasizing that classification must not only reflect precise wording but also the functional realities of the goods concerned. 

Overall, this case demonstrates the complexities inherent in tariff classification and the critical interface between legislative interpretation and commercial realities, providing substantial implications for importers and customs authorities alike, as they navigate the nuances of tariff application.
