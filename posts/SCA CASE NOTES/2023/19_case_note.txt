Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Gaone Jack Siamisang Montshiwa (Ex Parte Application) [2023] ZASCA 19

## F - Facts

The appellant, Mr. Gaone Jack Siamisang Montshiwa, applied to the North West Division of the High Court seeking admission as a legal practitioner under section 24 of the Legal Practice Act No 28 of 2014 (LPA) ([1]). Initially, he had entered a five-year articles of clerkship with Mr. Lavelle Winston Vere but resigned after 1 year and 11 months on unfavourable terms. Following this, he entered into a second contract with Moetsi Maredi Attorneys Inc. ([2]). Both contracts were registered under different regulatory frameworks, stirring complications because the legal standing of the period served under the first contract was questionable and subject to certain statutory requirements concerning registration ([3], [4]).

Montshiwa left Moetsi Maredi Attorneys in March 2018, shortly before the LPA went into effect, which introduced new requirements for admission that included proving citizenship, academic qualifications, and a demonstration of being a fit and proper person through vocational training ([5]). His application for admission sought to join the contracts of articles and to condone the time served under unregistered conditions ([6]). However, the High Court discerned discrepancies in the timelines presented and doubts about his fitness for admission, leading to a dismissal of his application on 3 September 2020 ([7]).

Dissatisfied with the High Court's decision, Montshiwa sought leave to appeal, only to have this application also dismissed. The dismissal prompted Montshiwa to petition the Supreme Court of Appeal ([8], [9]). A central issue arose regarding whether there was a proper legal basis for the High Court’s refusal of leave to appeal, posing the question of jurisdiction for the Supreme Court of Appeal ([10]-[12]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal questions addressed in this case included:
1. Whether the High Court's order denying leave to appeal constituted a valid decision under the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, or if it was a nullity due to improper court composition ([10], [19]).
2. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal possessed the jurisdiction to consider Montshiwa's application for leave to appeal given the High Court’s perceived irregularities ([16], [19]).

## R - Rules

The legal frameworks pertinent to this case included:
- **Legal Practice Act No 28 of 2014 (LPA)** under which the requirements for admission of legal practitioners are established, particularly regarding being a "fit and proper person" ([5]).
- **Superior Courts Act No 10 of 2013**, specifically sections 16 and 17, delineating the processes for granting leave to appeal, as well as the stipulations about the composition of courts. Section 17(1) mandates that a court can only grant leave "if they are of the opinion that the appeal would have reasonable prospects of success, or that there is some other compelling reason why the appeal should be heard" ([12], [13]).
- Judicial precedents such as *S v Gqeba* and *Matamela v Mulaudzi*, which elaborate that an improperly constituted court renders its proceedings a nullity, thus calling into question the legal efficacy of the High Court's earlier rulings ([14], [18]).

## A - Application

The application of law in this instance highlighted the implications of procedural integrity. The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the High Court was not properly constituted when it dismissed Montshiwa's application for leave to appeal, resulting in the dismissal being a nullity. The judges noted that the procedural configurations outlined in the Superior Courts Act must be adhered to strictly; any substantive deviation could undermine the jurisdictional foundation of subsequent appellate processes ([19]).

Further, the inherent power of the Supreme Court was debated, specifically whether it could exercise jurisdiction despite the prior nullity. The majority opinion held firm that they could not bypass legislative stipulations merely due to the circumstantial hardships of a party ([16], [19]). Conversely, a minority opinion advocated for employing inherent powers to prevent injustice while addressing the apparent procedural failings, indicating a judicial readiness to ameliorate hardships that arose from bureaucratic misalignment ([21], [22]).

## C - Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the principle that a valid court decision is essential for appellate proceedings, rendering the High Court's earlier dismissal a nullity and maintaining that Montshiwa's application for leave to appeal could not proceed. Thus, the Supreme Court dismissed the application, stating that Montshiwa exhibited a lack of the requisite integrity and decorum expected of a legal practitioner, grounded in repeated instances of disparaging conduct throughout his legal journey ([43]). The implications of this judgment reaffirm the importance of procedural integrity in legal proceedings, ensuring that only applicants displaying the highest standards of professionalism and ethical conduct are granted admission to the legal profession. The dismissal was ultimately accompanied by a costs order against Montshiwa, illustrating the court's disapproval of his conduct ([8], [44]).
