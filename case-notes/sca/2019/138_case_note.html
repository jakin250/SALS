Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 138 (01 October 2019)* ## FIRAC Overview ### F

Neutral Citation: Mzalisi NO & others v O & another (630/2018) [2019] ZASCA 138 (01 October 2019)

FIRAC Overview

F – Facts ([paras 1–6])

The first respondent, Mr. E O, a Nigerian national, entered South Africa in 2011 seeking asylum from religious persecution by Boko Haram. Holding an asylum seeker temporary permit under section 22(1) of the Refugees Act 130 of 1998, he was entitled to live, work, and study in South Africa, although his application for asylum had been denied and was pending appeal for seven years.

In September 2015, Mr. O married Ms. Z N, a South African citizen, under customary law. Their child, G, was born on June 23, 2016. In 2016, they approached the Department of Home Affairs (DHA) to register their marriage under the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998. Initially, they received approval, but upon returning in February 2017, they were informed by Mr. Faltein of the DHA that, due to a new circular issued in September 2016, asylum seekers were banned from marrying.

Aggrieved, the respondents sought to challenge the circular in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, arguing that its contents were unlawful and unconstitutional.

I – Issues ([paras 6–9])

The primary legal issues to be determined included:

  1. Whether the circular issued by the DHA, which prevented asylum seekers from marrying, was consistent with the South African Constitution.
  2. The validity of the procedures established under the circular against the rights enshrined in the Bill of Rights, including personal liberty and dignity.
  3. Whether the respondents had a legitimate standing to challenge the circular despite the procedure adopted.
  4. The potential implications of the structural interdict granted by the High Court concerning marriage registration for asylum seekers.

R – Rules ([paras 10–19])

The court considered various statutory and constitutional provisions:

  • Section 22(1) of the Refugees Act authorizes the issuance of asylum seeker permits, allowing individuals to reside lawfully in South Africa pending the outcome of their application.
  • Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 emphasizes the rights of individuals to enter into customary marriages.
  • The South African Constitution, particularly sections related to dignity (Section 10) and personal liberty (Section 12), provided the framework for evaluating the constitutionality of the circular.

The court also relied on precedent from Ahmed & Others v Minister of Home Affairs & Another, which established that departmental directives can be subject to judicial review, particularly if they affect rights recognized by the Constitution.

A – Application ([paras 20–36])

The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the language of the circular and found that its provisions explicitly barred asylum seekers from marrying, thus infringing upon their constitutional rights. Furthermore, the ambiguity present in different sections of the circular undermined its legal effectiveness. The court emphasized the importance of interpreting the circular in light of the rights to dignity and family life, which are central to the South African constitutional order.

The court examined the procedural objections raised by the appellants, ruling that the respondents had indeed brought a review application despite their failure to request a record of the decision. The absence of the record did not preclude the application from being deemed a review, as substantive rights were at stake.

The high court’s grant of a structural interdict requiring the DHA to report back on compliance was critiqued as unwarranted. The appeals court highlighted that the high court had not justified the need for such an order adequately, thus failing to exercise discretion correctly.

C – Conclusion ([paras 37–38])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal against the high court's ruling that declared the impugned circular invalid. The court found that the circular fundamentally violated the constitutional rights of the respondents, and its provisions could not be interpreted to uphold the right to marry. The appellants were found remiss in their duties as state actors regarding the treatment of asylum seekers' rights and were awarded costs against them on a punitive scale.

The implications of the ruling extend beyond the immediate case, reinforcing the notion that administrative directives must align with constitutional principles and highlighting the judiciary's role in safeguarding individual rights against bureaucratic overreach.

This FIRAC case note encapsulates the Supreme Court of Appeal's examination of constitutional rights concerning marital freedoms for asylum seekers, providing essential insights into the legal interpretations at play.
