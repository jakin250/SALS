Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: Irankunda and Another v Director of Asylum Seeker Management and Others (with Scalabrini Centre of Cape Town intervening as Amicus Curiae) (821/2022) [2024] ZASCA 87

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 2–85])

The appellants, Amina Irankunda and Arava Niyonkuru, both Burundian nationals, entered South Africa illegally and initially applied for asylum in 2009 and 2009, respectively. They claimed asylum based on personal circumstances that did not constitute persecution. Their applications were rejected by the Refugee Status Determination Officer (RSDO) on the grounds of being manifestly unfounded, a decision later upheld by the Standing Committee for Refugee Affairs (SCRA) ([para 52]).

Following the rejection, both appellants failed to leave the country or challenge the decisions, essentially becoming illegal foreigners under the Immigration Act. In 2015, the political situation in Burundi deteriorated, prompting the appellants to claim that they were entitled to submit new asylum applications based on sur place refugee claims, alleging a well-founded fear of persecution due to changed circumstances in their country ([paras 53-54]).

The appellants filed an application in the Western Cape High Court seeking to compel the Department of Home Affairs to accept their new claims, but the application was dismissed. The High Court characterized their efforts as an attempt to resubmit previously denied claims without leaving the country, which it deemed an abuse of the asylum process ([paras 56-60]).

The appellants appealed this decision, with the Court needed to determine whether individuals whose applications have been rejected can submit further applications based on new grounds, and if so, under what circumstances ([para 9]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 9–12])

The core issues for determination in the appeal were as follows:

1. **Reapplication Right:** Whether an individual whose application for refugee status has been declined may submit further applications for asylum based on sur place claims.
2. **Criteria for Subsequent Applications:** The conditions under which such applications can be submitted, particularly in light of previous rejections.
3. **Interpretation of the Refugee Act:** How the provisions of the Refugees Act align with international law, particularly concerning the principle of non-refoulement and the recognition of sur place refugee claims ([para 9]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 10–12],[26–30])

The Court analyzed a variety of legal norms, relying on both domestic and international law:

1. **International Treaties:**
   - **UN Refugee Convention (1951)** and **OAU Refugee Convention (1969):** Defining a refugee and protecting against refoulement ([para 10]).
   - Article 33 of the UN Convention prohibits the expulsion or return of refugees to countries where they face threats to their life or freedom ([para 13]). 

2. **Domestic Legislation:**
   - **Refugees Act 130 of 1998:** Ensures that no person can be legally returned if it would violate the principle of non-refoulement ([para 14]).
   - **Asylum Application Process (Sections 21-24):** Describes procedures and legal grounds for submitting asylum applications ([paras 21-24]).

3. **Jurisprudence:**
   - Previous cases such as *Ruta v Minister of Home Affairs* and comparative analysis from Canada and the UK on the treatment of sur place claims provided essential guidance regarding how to handle these claims as they relate to refugee status determination in South Africa ([paras 27-38]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 51–85])

In application of the aforementioned rules, the Court dismantled the notion that an asylum seeker could not resubmit claims without departing the country. The Court emphasized the importance of the principle of non-refoulement, asserting that this principle exists only until the asylum seeker has exhausted all available remedies, including appeals and reviews. 

### Reapplication Framework

The Court established that while a reapplication can be made, it must be based on new or substantially different circumstances than those originally considered. The hypothesis that the appellants could reapply on the same grounds was ruled out as possibly abusive of the asylum framework ([para 75]). The acknowledgment of the change in circumstances was paramount to the acceptance of further claims, particularly when subsequent events in Burundi indicated a risk of persecution ([para 81]).

### Demand for Finality

In critically assessing the High Court's decision, the Supreme Court pointed out the problematic outcome where previous applications are rejected, but subsequent applications could endlessly be made on new grounds. By requiring finality in such matters, the Court affirmed that a legal framework that accommodates human rights also needs to maintain order ([para 75]).

### The Role of Scalabrini Centre as Amicus Curiae

The Court recognized the contributions from Scalabrini Centre of Cape Town as they provided relevant arguments arguing for a holistic interpretation of asylum seekers’ rights and the need to consider continually changing circumstances in their countries of origin ([paras 6-8]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 81–86])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellants, concluding that they have the right to submit their new applications as sur place refugees and directed the Department of Home Affairs to assess their claims ([para 86]). The Court articulated:

1. The principle of non-refoulement upholds even for cases where previous claims have been rejected if new circumstances warrant reconsideration.
2. Subsequent applications must be based on demonstrably altered conditions affecting the asylum seeker’s safety and well-being.

The ruling affirms the role of refugee law as evolving and adaptable, thereby reinforcing the State's obligations under international and domestic law to protect individuals from wrongful return to perilous circumstances. The case serves as a critical precedent in South African jurisprudence regarding asylum procedures and balances state interests against international human rights obligations ([para 81]). 

Overall, *Irankunda and Another* elevates the discourse surrounding refugee rights, highlighting the intersection between domestic law and international standards in the treatment of asylum seekers and their claims for protection from persecution.
