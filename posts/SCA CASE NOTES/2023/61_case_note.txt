Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Police v Gqamane (226/2022) [2023] ZASCA 61

**F - Facts**  
The appeal stems from the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court’s decision in favor of Vuyani Goodman Gqamane, who sued the Minister of Police for damages arising from an alleged unlawful arrest and detention ([paras 2, 6]). On 17 February 2017, Gqamane was arrested without a warrant following an allegation of domestic violence made by his romantic partner, Ms Mini. The arrest occurred eight days after the alleged incident, which included claims of assault that resulted in serious injuries to Ms Mini ([paras 3–4]). Warrant Officer Erasmus, the arresting officer, argued his actions were justified under sections 40(1)(b) and 40(1)(q) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) and section 3 of the Domestic Violence Act 116 of 1998 (DVA) ([paras 6–7]). The trial court initially ruled in favor of the appellant (the Minister of Police), determining that the arrest was lawful based on reasonable suspicion ([para 10]). Following appeal, the high court held the trial court failed to consider whether W/O Erasmus properly exercised discretion in the arrest, resulting in a finding that the arrest was "irrational and arbitrary" ([paras 2–12]). Gqamane was awarded damages of R160,000, which prompted the minister's further appeal ([paras 2, 6]).

**I - Issues**  
The primary legal question raised by the appeal was whether the high court was correct in addressing the lawful exercise of discretion to arrest without an explicit plea for it during the initial trial ([paras 12, 20]). Secondary issues included the burden of proof concerning the lawfulness of the arrest, especially regarding the officer's discretion ([paras 16–17]). The implications of the appeal involved understanding if the arrest met the lawful criteria set under domestic violence provisions, and whether the respondent's actions after the incident required a reassessment of the arrest's lawfulness ([paras 19–20]).

**R - Rules**  
The South African law pertaining to unlawful arrest is primarily governed by the Constitution, the CPA, and the DVA. Section 40(1)(b) of the CPA allows for arrest without a warrant when there is reasonable suspicion of a Schedule 1 offence. Section 40(1)(q) allows for arrest without a warrant in domestic violence incidents where violence is suspected ([para 7]). Case law, including *Minister of Safety and Security v Sekhoto* ([2010] ZASCA 141), clarifies that once jurisdictional requirements for arrest are established, the onus lies on the arrested party to prove any improper exercise of discretion ([paras 16–17]). Further, a party is bound by pleadings, although courts may permit discussion of issues emergent from the evidence ([para 13]).

**A - Application**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed whether the high court erred in its interpretation of the discretion exercised by W/O Erasmus. It established that the burden for proving the lawfulness of the arrest rested with the Minister of Police once the statutory requirements for arrest were met. The high court had conflated the onus of proof for the jurisdictional aspects of the arrest with the broader question of discretion ([paras 16–20]). The ruling further emphasized that the trial court's findings indicated sufficient grounds for the arrest based on evidence that met the legal standards without the need for additional pleadings ([paras 8–12, 19-20]). The Court concluded that the decision to arrest was justifiable under the established legal framework, negating the high court's conclusion that the officer failed to exercise discretion appropriately.

**C - Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court upheld the appeal, setting aside the high court's ruling, and determined that the original trial court's decision was correct with respect to both the legality of the arrest and the application of discretion by W/O Erasmus ([paras 21–22]). The ruling reaffirms the principle that once the jurisdictional grounds for a warrantless arrest are established, the onus shifts to the respondent to demonstrate any failure in the officer's discretion. The implications of this decision impact future cases involving unlawful arrest claims, particularly in the context of domestic violence where the complexities of delayed arrests may arise.
