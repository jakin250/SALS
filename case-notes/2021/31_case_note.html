Case Title: Centre for Child Law v Director-General: Department of Home Affairs and Others [2021] ZACC 31

Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 31 Court: Constitutional Court of South Af

Case Title: Centre for Child Law v Director-General: Department of Home Affairs and Others [2021] ZACC 31

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Coram: Mogoeng CJ, Jafta J, Khampepe J, Madlanga J, Majiedt J, Mathopo AJ, Mhlantla J, Theron J, Tshiqi J, and Victor AJ
Date Heard: 1 September 2020
Date Decided: 22 September 2021
Neutral Citation: [2021] ZACC 31

F - Facts

The case stems from a dispute regarding the registration of a child's birth under the Births and Deaths Registration Act 51 of 1992 (the Act). The applicant, Centre for Child Law, intervened on behalf of the family of Menzile Lawrence Naki and Dimitrila Marie Ndovya, who sought to register their child, NN, born in South Africa. Despite Naki's citizenship, problems arose because Ndovya, a Congolese citizen, lacked valid documentation for registration due to an expired visa ([1]–[3]).

The relevant sections of the Act established a bifurcated procedure for registering births of children born out of wedlock (section 10) as opposed to those born within marriage (section 9). This bifurcation caused numerous difficulties, particularly regarding the rights of unmarried fathers to assert parental recognition without maternal consent. The parents challenged the constitutionality of these sections, with the argument that they violate their rights and those of their child under sections 28 and 9 of the South African Constitution ([8]–[10]).

The High Court found that section 10, which mandates that children born out of wedlock must be registered under the mother's surname, infringed upon the rights of unmarried fathers and their children. It declared sections 9 and 10 unconstitutional to the extent that they imposed such requirements ([12]–[13]).

I - Issues

The case presents two primary legal issues:

  1. Does section 10 of the Act unlawfully prohibit unmarried fathers from registering their children under their surname in the absence of the mother?
  2. Does the interpretation of sections 9 and 10 align with the constitutional guarantees of equality, dignity, and the best interests of the child ([25]–[26])?

R - Rules

The constitutional provisions relevant to this case include:

  • Section 9: Guarantees equality before the law and prohibits unfair discrimination.
  • Section 28: Ensures every child's right to a name and nationality at birth, and mandates that the best interests of the child are of paramount importance ([4]–[5]).

The court also referenced the Harksen test for assessing unfair discrimination, which requires:

  • Identification of differentiation between groups.
  • Examination of whether the differentiation serves a legitimate government purpose.

The interpretation of legislation under section 39(2) of the Constitution mandates that all statutes must align with the spirit of the Bill of Rights ([27]–[28]).

A - Application

The court applied these rules by first interpreting sections 9 and 10 within the broader context of the Bill of Rights. It assessed whether the provisions offered unmarried fathers adequate rights to register their children without maternal consent. The Court focused on the procedural and substantive inequities resulting from the bifurcated approach, highlighting that section 10 imposed unjust barriers on both unmarried fathers and their children ([30]–[32]).

The majority opinion determined that section 10 resulted in unfair discrimination based on marital status, violating both the dignity of the unmarried fathers and the rights of the children to an identity and recognition from birth. The court noted that the provisions did not brook the possibility of registering a child under an unmarried father's surname absent the mother’s consent, thus limiting the capacity for equal recognition of parental rights ([56]–[57]).

Mogoeng CJ, in the dissenting opinion, argued for a different interpretation that justified the differentiation based on the unique circumstances surrounding marriage and family law, suggesting that this structure promotes the best interests of children ([140]–[141]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the Full Court's declaration of invalidity regarding section 10 of the Act. It severed the provisions that distinguished between married and unmarried fathers, thus affirming that unmarried fathers should have the right to register their children under their surname, regardless of the mother's presence or consent.

The court emphasized the need for legislative reform to align with constitutional values and ensure equitable treatment with respect to children's identity rights. The order included the provision that the first respondent must pay the applicant's legal costs ([88]–[89]).

The implications of this ruling extend beyond mere legal recognition; they resonate with broader societal norms concerning parental equity, dignity, and the inherent rights of children born out of wedlock, fundamentally challenging the traditional hierarchies in family law within South Africa.
