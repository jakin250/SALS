Case Title: Hoho v The State Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa Case No: 493/05 Neutral Citation: [2008] ZASCA 98 (17 September 2008) Coram: Streicher, Heher, Mlambo, Cachalia JJA & Kgomo AJA Heard: 15 August 2008 Delivered: 17 September 2008

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 98 (17 September 2008) Coram: Streicher, He

Case Title: Hoho v The State
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Case No: 493/05
Neutral Citation: [2008] ZASCA 98 (17 September 2008)
Coram: Streicher, Heher, Mlambo, Cachalia JJA & Kgomo AJA
Heard: 15 August 2008
Delivered: 17 September 2008

F – Facts

The Supreme Court of Appeal addressed the case where the appellant, Luzuko Kerr Hoho, was convicted by the Bisho High Court on 22 counts of criminal defamation after creating and distributing defamatory leaflets against various political figures, including the Speaker of the Eastern Cape Legislature and the Premier of the Eastern Cape ([1]). The High Court sentenced the appellant to three years' imprisonment, suspended for five years, alongside three years of correctional supervision under section 276(1)(h) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([1]).

The appellant's defense was primarily that he did not author or publish the leaflets, claiming the allegations were politically motivated by particular police members ([2]–[3]). Notably, the appellant did not explicitly plead a defense of 'truth and public benefit' as required by section 107 of the Act. The state brought multiple witnesses to establish that the statements in the leaflets were untrue and that Hoho was indeed the author ([4]).

Upon appeal, the appellant’s counsel did not challenge the actual convictions, focusing instead on two critical legal issues: the validity of criminal defamation as a crime and its consonance with the Constitution ([5]).

I – Issues

  1. Is the crime of defamation still recognized under South African law, or has it been effectively abolished by disuse?
  2. Is the criminalization of defamation consistent with the Constitution, particularly concerning freedom of expression and human dignity? ([6]).

R – Rules

The court relied upon established legal principles and precedents concerning criminal defamation:

  • Historical Recognition: In R v Japel (1906) TS 108 and R v Harrison and Dryburgh (1922) AD 320, it was confirmed that serious verbal defamation remains a crime in South Africa, despite a decline in its prosecution ([7]–[8]).
  • Doctrine of Abrogation by Disuse: The absence of prosecutions does not equate to tacit repeal of the law. It must be assessed whether a significant portion of society has consented to this status ([9]–[10]).
  • Legal Framework: Section 276(1)(h) of the Criminal Procedure Act governs aspects of sentencing, while defamation law fundamentally protects reputation ([10]).
  • Constitutional Framework: Section 16 of the South African Constitution guarantees the right to freedom of expression but permits reasonable limitations to protect human dignity and reputation ([30]).

A – Application

The court critically evaluated both issues. Firstly, it assessed whether the crime of defamation has been tacitly abrogated through disuse. The absence of reported convictions was insufficient to demonstrate societal consent to the repeal of this crime, as the law may still be actively enforced in lower courts ([10]–[13]). Moreover, the existence of a historical framework and some level of past prosecutions indicated that the crime remains valid ([11]).

Secondly, the court examined the compatibility of criminal defamation with constitutional rights. It emphasized the balance between the freedom of expression and the protection of human dignity, recognizing that the criminalization of defamation serves a legitimate societal interest ([30]–[32]). While some legal scholars argue for decriminalization, the judgment observed that criminal prosecution imposes more stringent standards on the state to prove criminal intent and the unlawfulness of publication, making it a high bar that further justifies its presence in law ([33]–[36]).

The court concluded that criminal defamation delivers a necessary check on unfounded allegations that could damage reputations, thereby reinforcing democratic order and societal integrity ([37]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, confirming that the crime of defamation still exists under South African law and that its provisions are consistent with constitutional protections. The court underscored the importance of maintaining criminal defamation as a viable legal tool to protect individual reputations while confirming that such legal frameworks must align with constitutional values ([1], [6], [37]).

Through this ruling, the court reinforced not only the ongoing relevance of defamation as a crime but also the importance of balancing civil rights with the need to protect societal structures against defamation, affirming the complexity and importance of both the legal and moral imperatives at play in safeguarding human dignity and freedom of expression.
