Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: Pretoria Portland Cement Company Limited v. Competition Commission

**Case Title:** Pretoria Portland Cement Company Limited and Slagment (Proprietary) Limited v. The Competition Commission and Others  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No:** 64/2001  
**Judges:** Nienaber JA, Howie JA, Schutz JA, Zulman JA, Nugent JA  
**Heard:** 7 May 2002  
**Delivered:** 31 May 2002

## F–Facts ([1]–[72])

This case arises from a search and seizure operation conducted by the Competition Commission under a warrant issued pursuant to section 46 of the Competition Act 89 of 1998. The operation targeted the premises of two companies: Pretoria Portland Cement Company Limited (PPC), a major cement producer, and its joint venture partner, Slagment (Pty) Ltd., which processes blast furnace slag for use as a cement extender ([1]).

On 2 August 2000, Judge Spoelstra granted the warrant ex parte and in camera, restricting its publication until execution ([2]). The warrant was executed on 3 and 4 August 2000, amidst allegations of PPC's non-compliance with the Commission's requests for information and an abuse of its dominant market position ([50]). Following the execution, PPC and Slagment challenged the validity of the warrant through several court applications, arguing that the warrant was improperly issued, and that the search violated their rights to privacy and just administrative action under the Constitution ([53]).

In a series of subsequent orders, the appellants were required to file their application to set aside the warrant, which culminated in an adverse ruling by Judge Daniels dismissing their applications ([5]–[6]). The Commission, meanwhile, faced allegations of colluding with media representatives during the search, compromising the integrity of the operation ([60]).

## I–Issues ([6]–[75])

The primary legal issues addressed in this case include:

1. **Validity of the Warrant:** Whether the search warrant issued by Judge Spoelstra was issued lawfully and in accordance with section 46 of the Competition Act ([8]–[11]).
2. **Non-joinder of Judge Spoelstra:** Whether the failure to cite Judge Spoelstra as a party to the proceedings warranted dismissal of the application ([6], [39]).
3. **Conduct of the Commission:** Whether the actions of the Competition Commission employees during the search constituted a violation of the appellants' rights under section 49(1) of the Act and relevant constitutional provisions ([71]–[72]).
4. **Ex Parte Proceedings:** Whether conducting the application ex parte and in camera was justified under the circumstances ([48]).

## R–Rules ([7]–[11])

The relevant statutory provisions are encapsulated primarily in the Competition Act 89 of 1998, specifically sections 46-49, which deal with search and seizure protocols. Section 46(1) allows judges to issue search warrants when reasonable grounds exist to believe that a prohibited practice may have occurred ([8]). Section 49(1) mandates that searches must be conducted with respect for individuals’ dignity, privacy, and security ([11]).

Additionally, precedents such as *Investigating Directorate: Serious Economic Offences and Others v Hyundai Motor Distributors (Pty) Ltd* ([22]) outline the nature of warrants issued in similar contexts, emphasizing judicial oversight as a fundamental protective mechanism against arbitrary state action.

## A–Application ([53]–[70])

The court critically assessed the actions taken by the Competition Commission as they pertained to the legality and execution of the search warrant. It found that the Commission's conduct during the operation significantly undermined the appellants' rights to privacy and just administrative action ([52]).

1. **Execution of the Warrant:** The court determined that the inspectors’ failure to provide a copy of the affidavit supporting the warrant and their denial of the appellants' requests to halt the search while seeking judicial review constituted serious procedural irregularities ([56]–[59]). This lack of access hindered PPC's ability to contest the validity of the warrant meaningfully.

2. **Media Involvement:** The court was particularly troubled by the Commission's collusion with media crews who entered the premises during the search, which was determined to be unauthorized and a breach of the court's initial order prohibiting publicity prior to the search’s execution. This behavior illustrated a flagrant disregard for the integrity of the legal process and a violation of the appellants' privacy rights ([60]–[68]).

3. **Non-joinder of Judge Spoelstra:** The court ruled that even if Judge Spoelstra’s function could be classified as administrative, the necessity for joinder was not warranted given the context. It emphasized that judges should not typically be joined in disputes except under specific conditions, reiterating that even administrative decisions made by judges should not alter such principles ([39], [72]).

Through this analysis, the court underscored the principle that while the Commission has a crucial role in ensuring competitive practices, it must also abide by constitutional protections and legal norms ([71]–[72]).

## C–Conclusion ([75])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the search warrants issued against PPC and Slagment were invalid due to the procedural improprieties and violations identified by the court. The ruling decisively set aside the warrants and ordered the immediate return of all materials seized during the search, reinforcing the obligation for state bodies to operate within lawful bounds that respect individual rights. The appeal was decided in favor of the appellants, highlighting the necessity for judicial and constitutional safeguards against potential abuses of power by regulatory authorities ([75]).

In summary, this case illustrates the delicate balance between regulatory enforcement and the protection of constitutional rights, signaling that any deviation from established legal processes can lead to significant legal repercussions, including the dismissal of enforcement actions and a reaffirmation of individual protections under the law.
