Grobler v Phillips and Others [2022] ZACC 32

Constitutional Court 2022 [2022] ZACC 32

F – Facts

The matter concerns an eviction dispute between Mr. Willem Grobler (the Applicant) and Mrs. Clara Phillips, along with her disabled son Mr. Adam Phillips (the First and Second Respondents, respectively). Mr. Grobler acquired a property in Somerset West at a public auction in 2008, which had been occupied by Mrs. Phillips since 1947. Upon purchasing the property, Mr. Grobler requested that Mrs. Phillips vacate, offering her alternative accommodations, which she consistently rejected ([2–4]).

Mrs. Phillips claimed a lifelong right of habitation, supposedly granted by a former owner, which Mr. Grobler’s counsel argued was unenforceable as it was not formalized in writing or registered. Consequently, Mr. Grobler initiated eviction proceedings in the Somerset West Magistrates’ Court. The court ruled in favor of Grobler, declaring Mrs. Phillips an unlawful occupier and granting an eviction order without initially assessing the justness and equity of the eviction ([6–9]).

Mrs. Phillips appealed to the High Court of the Western Cape, which reversed the eviction order based on a determination that she had not been adequately given notice of termination of her right to occupy and that she might hold rights under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act (ESTA). The court emphasized that changing her status from lawful to unlawful occupier without adequate notice was unreasonable ([10–12]).

On further appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the High Court’s decision, stressing judicial discretion and the need to consider Mrs. Phillips' vulnerable circumstances before granting eviction ([12–20]).

I – Issues

The legal questions at the heart of this case include:

  1. Whether it was just and equitable to grant an eviction order under section 4(7) of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act** (PIE).
  2. The applicability of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act** (ESTA) in the current circumstances.
  3. Whether the High Court's allowance for a new and alternative defense on appeal was appropriate and whether Grobler had established that Phillips was an unlawful occupier according to PIE standards ([12–18], [19–24]).

R – Rules

Several legal rules and precedents govern this dispute:

  1. Section 4(7) of the PIE dictates that a court may grant an eviction order if it is "just and equitable," considering all relevant circumstances, including the availability of relocation options and the needs of vulnerable individuals ([28]).
  2. **The principles established in cases such as Blue Moonlight Properties v City of Johannesburg [2011] ZACC 33**, which contemplates a property owner’s obligations concerning unlawful occupiers, notably that they cannot indefinitely provide free housing under the guise of human rights obligations ([14], [38]).
  3. The Extension of Security of Tenure Act** protects rights to occupy land in certain domestic circumstances, particularly in agricultural contexts, which may have implications for urban transfer ([10][20]).

A – Application

Application of the Law:
The Constitutional Court evaluated the previous judgments' adherence to the envisaged justness and equitable principles derived from PIE. The Magistrates’ Court neglected to undertake a proper assessment of whether granting an eviction would be just and equitable, choosing instead to focus solely on ownership claims. This omission was seen as a critical error, which led to substantive review by higher courts ([27][30]).

The High Court’s reasoning was significant, as it ruled against the eviction based on the inadequacy of notice, which came into question during subsequent appeals. In contrast, the Supreme Court of Appeal maintained Mrs. Phillips’ rights under PIE, effectively recognizing social and human rights considerations that play into eviction cases—notably, her advancing age and her son’s disability ([33][34]).

The judgment highlighted that Grobler had made substantial efforts to offer alternative accommodation suitable for Mrs. Phillips, further emphasizing that the final considerations of justice and equity should weigh heavily against arbitrary eviction ([41][42]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately held that the eviction order initially granted should not stand without assessing the justness and equity provisions under PIE. It reversed the decisions of the Supreme Court of Appeal, reinstated certain orders concerning a new dwelling for Mrs. Phillips, and instituted specific timelines for her compliance ([48]).

Mrs. Phillips is directed to take occupation of a newly acquired dwelling within six months while ensuring her right to lifetime residence therein is secured. The Court emphasizes that while Mr. Grobler retains ownership rights, the clear social implications surrounding vulnerable occupiers necessitate that justice and equity be adequately considered ([49]).

This case, thus, underscores the delicate balance between property rights and vulnerable individuals' rights, solidifying the practical application of PIE in modern contexts, particularly in addressing long-term unlawful occupation and ensuring minimal disruption to social stability within the community.
