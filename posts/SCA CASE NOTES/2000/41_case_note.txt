# FIRAC Case Note on **Niemesh Singh v McCarthy Retail Ltd**

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case Number**: 429 / 98  
**Date of Hearing**: 25 August 2000  
**Date of Delivery**: 14 September 2000  
**Judgment by**: Olivier JA, Scott JA, Mthiyane AJA  

---

## F – Facts

The dispute arose from a contract for the sale of a new 1996 model Mercedes Benz E 320 A Sportline motor vehicle between the appellant, Niemesh Singh, and the respondent, McCarthy Retail Ltd ([1]). The respondent, not having the vehicle in stock at its Pinetown location, sourced it from a dealer in King Williams Town and arranged for its delivery to Durban at the respondent's expense. However, the vehicle was driven from King Williams Town to Durban with its odometer disconnected, resulting in a recorded odometer reading of 160 km instead of the actual 920 km ([2]).

Singh contended that he purchased a new, undriven vehicle and alleged that the respondent agreed to transport the vehicle via a carrier, thereby breaching their contract. He claimed both rectification of the written contract, which he argued did not accurately reflect their agreement, and rescission due to the alleged breach ([3]).

The appellant's claim for rectification was upheld by the court a quo, which found that the respondent breached the rectified contract. However, it ruled against Singh's claim for rescission, determining that the breach did not justify cancellation of the contract ([6]).

Key undisputed facts included that both parties entered into a contract on 8 February 1996, and the written contract contained a clause acknowledging the vehicle could have been driven under its own power before delivery ([7]).

## I – Issues

The main legal issues to be addressed by the court included:

1. Whether the respondent's act of delivering the vehicle by driving it under its own power constituted a breach of contract sufficient to justify rescission by the appellant ([11]).
2. Whether a tacit lex commissoria (a cancellation provision derived from the parties' implicit agreement) existed that would entitle Singh to rescind the contract upon breach ([17]).

## R – Rules

The court applied the following legal principles:

1. The standard for determining whether a breach of contract warrants rescission is based on whether the breach is sufficiently serious to undermine the essential purpose of the contract involved ([12]).
2. The test for seriousness of a breach has been previously articulated in various cases, emphasizing that the breach must relate to vital or essential terms of the contract ([13]). 
3. In the absence of a lex commissoria, the court needs to perform a value judgment to ensure fairness between the parties in determining if rescission is appropriate ([15]).

Additionally, relevant past judgments, including *Aucamp v Morton 1949 (3) SA 611 (A)*, inform the court's analysis ([14]).

## A – Application

In applying these principles to the facts:

- **Breach Analysis**: The court acknowledged the respondent's breach through the delivery method of the vehicle. However, it emphasized that the breach's seriousness must involve more than mere violation of the delivery method. The appellant's ultimate concern appeared to be more about who drove the vehicle to Durban rather than the condition of the vehicle itself. The court noted Singh had even suggested an alternative method of transport, indicating a level of flexibility regarding how delivery occurred ([16]).

- **Tacit Lex Commissoria**: The court examined Singh's claim that the parties had implicitly agreed to a tacit term allowing for cancellation upon breach. Notably, the written contract did not include any provisions for cancellation under such circumstances, leading the court to conclude that it was improbable both parties would have agreed to such a tacit term. The court applied the "officious bystander" test but found insufficient evidence that such a term existed in their agreement ([18]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Singh's appeal did not succeed. It upheld the findings of the court a quo regarding rectification but ruled against rescission, determining that the breach did not warrant cancellation. The appellant's actual grievance had less to do with the breach's material impact and more with the manner of vehicle delivery. The court ordered the appeal be dismissed with costs, stating that the breach was not sufficiently significant to justify rescission of the contract ([19]).

The judgment provides critical insights into the court’s stance on contractual obligations and the threshold for rescission, emphasizing a nuanced examination of what constitutes a 'serious' breach of contract in South African law.
