Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Hlela v SA Taxi Securitisation (Pty) Ltd (515/2013) [2014] ZASCA 112

### F – Facts
The appellants, Robert Hlela, Bhabha Dlamini, and Thengezakhe Khwela, are mini-bus taxi operators who sought financing from the first respondent, SA Taxi Securitisation (Pty) Ltd (TS), to purchase mini-bus taxis. TS agreed to provide finance structured as leases on the vehicles. To secure TS's ownership rights in these vehicles under the finance agreements, the appellants were required to insure the vehicles with a registered insurer, an essential condition of the agreements ([paras 2–3]).

The appellants ceded their rights to every insurance policy in relation to these vehicles to TS, intending to protect TS's ownership ([para 3]). They signed an insurance proposal that identified "SA Taxi Finance Holdings (Pty) Ltd" as their chosen broker and "The Hollard Insurance Company Ltd" as the insurer. However, insurance certificates later indicated "SA Taxi Risk Management Services (Pty) Ltd" as the broker instead of the chosen one ([paras 4–5]).

A dispute arose when the appellants appointed South African Insurance Brokers CC to manage their insurance portfolios for better competitive rates. TS refused this change, claiming the right to appoint the broker of its choice under the policy cession. The appellants sought declaratory relief from the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, asserting their right to cancel the brokerage mandate ([paras 6–8]). The court of first instance ruled in favor of the appellants, based on principles of contract law and cession. However, this ruling was overturned by the court a quo, which found in favor of TS ([paras 9–10]).

### I – Issues
The main legal question in this case revolves around whether the appellants, having ceded their insurance policies as security to TS, retained the right to choose their insurance broker during the finance agreement’s term ([para 9]). 

Additionally, the court considered the implications of documents that outlined the appellants' right to substitute their insurance policy and broker, and the extent of this right under the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA) and the Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act 37 of 2002 (FAIS Act) ([paras 16–19]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework governing this dispute includes the following statutes:

1. **National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA)**: 
   - **Section 106** grants consumers the right to waive a proposed policy and substitute with a policy of their choice ([paras 17–18]).

2. **Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act 37 of 2002 (FAIS Act)**: 
   - Defines intermediary services and obliges providers to adhere to a published code of conduct ([paras 18–21]).

3. **Code of Conduct** under the FAIS Act:
   - **Section 20(a)(i)** mandates immediate compliance with a client's request to terminate an agreement regarding a financial product ([para 19]).
   - **Section 21** prohibits any form of waiver of rights conferred on clients ([para 20]).

### A – Application
The court evaluated the appellants' entitlement to choose their broker based on the cession agreement and the applicable statutory provisions. The court emphasized the importance of the appellants' right to choose their broker, which is underpinned by the statutory protections afforded to consumers under the NCA, specifically the right to waive existing provisions of insurance policies in favor of policies of their choosing ([paras 16–28]).

The contractual documentation clearly articulated the appellants' freedom to select a broker, demonstrating an intent not to relinquish this choice entirely to TS upon ceding the policy ([paras 22–29]). The court pointed out that whilst cession might transfer certain rights, it does not automatically negate the clients' rights to their chosen intermediaries, especially where consumer protection laws are concerned ([paras 30–31]).

Moreover, due to the structure of the financial arrangement, the court found that any new policy would necessitate the simultaneous appointment of a new broker ([paras 26–30]). Importantly, the appellants’ declaration of freedom to select a broker indicated a clear contractual intent that such selections would not be extinguished by the cession.

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of the appellants, reversing the decision of the court a quo and affirming their right to appoint their broker for managing their policies. The court emphasized that TS could not unilaterally appoint a broker contrary to the appellants' choice without breaching consumer protection obligations under the NCA and FAIS Act. The judgment underscored the principle that consumer rights should not be undermined by contractual terms or cession, especially where the legislative framework aims to safeguard such rights.

The final order granted by the court clearly delineated the rights of the appellants, allowing them to cancel the existing brokerage mandate and appoint their preferred brokers, subject to TS's approval of the chosen broker ([para 33]). This ruling has significant implications for similar financing arrangements, reinforcing consumer protections in contractual relationships within the realm of financial services.
