A Penglides (Pty) Ltd and Another v Minister of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries and Another [2022] ZASCA 74

In 2004, the Minister of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries published a policy acknowledging the limited capacity of South African vessels and permitting the use of foreign vessels, provided they were reflagged as South African within a year ([4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 74

F – Facts

The present appeal arose from the allocation of fishing rights in South Africa’s large pelagic longline fishery ([1]). The appellants, A Penglides (Pty) Ltd (first appellant) and Tuna South Africa (Pty) Ltd (second appellant), sought to utilize foreign vessels for tuna fishing, in contrast to local vessels that were smaller and less equipped ([1], [2]).

In 2004, the Minister of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries published a policy acknowledging the limited capacity of South African vessels and permitting the use of foreign vessels, provided they were reflagged as South African within a year ([4]). However, by 2008, this reflagging requirement was deemed unfeasible, leading to the 2009 amendment allowing a phased approach to reflagging ([7]). The appellants successfully applied for fishing rights based on policy changes in 2015 ([10], [11]).

However, when the first appellant attempted to substitute a foreign vessel three months post-allocation, the Department of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries denied the request, leading to an appeal to the Minister ([13], [14]). The appeal was dismissed on the grounds of its late filing and for purported violations of conditions set out in the Grant of Right Letter ([15], [16]).

The appellants subsequently sought judicial review in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, which concluded that the appeal was improperly filed late, upholding the Minister's decision ([19]).

I – Issues

The primary issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) consisted of:

  1. Whether the appeal lodged by the first appellant was timeously filed, and,
  2. If the appeal was late, whether a court could still review the validity of condition 8.3 of the Grant of Right Letter in isolation ([19.1], [19.2]).

R – Rules

The rules governing the appeal procedure are primarily derived from the Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998, specifically section 80, which articulates the right to appeal against decisions made by delegated authorities ([19]). Regulation 5 of the Marine Living Resources Regulations further prescribes that appeals must be submitted within thirty days of notification of the decision being appealed against ([20]).

The SCA referenced precedents regarding the computation of time limits and statutory periods, notably the principles established in Pritam Kaur v S Russel & Sons Ltd [1973] 1 QB 336, which stipulate that when the last day of a prescribed period is a day when an office is closed, the period extends to the following business day ([22]-[24]).

A – Application

The SCA evaluated whether the high court had correctly identified the late filing of the appeal as the reason for its dismissal. It examined the timeline, noting that the final day to serve the appeal would have been 15 June 2017, but due to closure of the Department's offices on the succeeding Saturday, the appeal served on 19 June should be deemed timely ([20], [21]).

The court referenced Road Accident Fund v Masindi, where closures prevented timely actions from being completed, positing that a similar rationale must apply in the context of administrative appeals ([22]). It concluded that interpreting the regulations to require service on a weekend contradicts the principle outlined in both domestic and foreign jurisprudence and established a precedent that the statutory period could be permissibly extended until the next business day when the offices were open ([26]-[33]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA ultimately overturned the high court's ruling, asserting that the first appellant's appeal was improperly rejected based on an erroneous interpretation of the statutory time limits ([34]). The court held that delaying the right to appeal due to administrative closures would thwart the legislative intent, thereby upholding principles of justice and fair administrative action. The appeal was thus successful, reinstating the matter for reconsideration by the high court on its merits ([34]).

The implications of this judgment extend beyond the immediate parties, reinforcing the necessity for clear interpretation of procedural rules in administrative law and ensuring that substantive rights are preserved against the effects of procedural technicalities. The court emphasized the importance of adhering to the overarching principles of fairness and judicial accessibility in administrative procedures.
