Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: SATAWU v Garvas and Others

**Case Citation**  
Constitutional Court of South Africa, Case CCT 112/11, [2012] ZACC 13.

---

## F - Facts

This case arises from an application for leave to appeal against a judgment of the Supreme Court of Appeal that dismissed an appeal concerning the constitutionality of certain provisions of the Regulation of Gatherings Act 205 of 1993. The first applicant, the South African Transport and Allied Workers Union (SATAWU), and the second applicant, the Congress of South African Trade Unions (COSATU), sought to contest the validity of section 11(2) of the Act, which outlines the conditions under which organizers of gatherings may be held liable for riot damage ([1]–[4]).

In May 2006, SATAWU organized a gathering in Cape Town to address employment-related concerns in the security industry. Despite taking precautionary measures such as notifying the local authority and appointing marshals, the gathering resulted in significant property damage and injuries, estimated at R1.5 million. Subsequently, the respondents, consisting of business owners who suffered damages, initiated a claim against SATAWU under section 11(1) of the Act, which imposes wrongful liability on gathering organizers for damage caused during their events ([10]–[12], [19]).

In its defense, SATAWU argued against its liability, asserting that the words “and was not reasonably foreseeable” in section 11(2)(b) of the Act were constitutionally invalid because they limited the right to freedom of assembly under section 17 of the Constitution ([13]–[16]). The High Court ruled against SATAWU, leading to an appeal in the Supreme Court of Appeal, which held that the statutory defense under section 11(2) was real and capable of being substantiated, thereby affirming the High Court’s decision ([21]–[23]).

## I - Issues

The primary issues for determination by the Constitutional Court revolved around:

1. Whether the phrase “and was not reasonably foreseeable” in section 11(2)(b) creates internal inconsistencies that render the section constitutionally invalid ([26]).
   
2. If the provision does present a limitation on the right to freedom of assembly under section 17 of the Constitution, then whether such limitation is justifiable under section 36 of the Constitution ([26]).

## R - Rules

The relevant legislation for this case includes:

1. **Regulation of Gatherings Act 205 of 1993**:
   - Section 11(1) imposes liability on organizers for riot damage caused during gatherings.
   - Section 11(2) outlines the defense against such liability, requiring organizers to prove:
     - (a) No permission or connivance for the acts causing damage.
     - (b) The acts were not reasonably foreseeable.
     - (c) All reasonable steps were taken to prevent such acts ([2], [3], [11]).

2. **Constitution of South Africa**:
   - Section 17 guarantees the right to assemble peacefully and unarmed.
   - Section 36 allows for limitations on rights if such limitations are reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society based on human dignity, equality, and freedom.

The precedent set in the earlier judgments pointed to the necessity of rationality in the application of statutory provisions limiting constitutional rights ([37]).

## A - Application

The Court assessed the phrase “and was not reasonably foreseeable” for its coherence and alignment with constitutional values ([34]–[45]). The applicants argued that the conjunctive nature of the defense made it implausible for organizers to meet the requirements of proving both foreseeability and that reasonable steps were taken. The Court noted that an interpretation should avoid yielding absurdity or irrationality in the statutory framework.

Conversely, the Court asserted that the statutory structure indeed allows organizers a legitimate defense if they follow the requisite legal steps with regards to planning and managing gatherings. The defense is seen as viable provided the organizer can demonstrate due diligence in their efforts to prevent foreseeable harm, hence aligning the practicalities of organizing public gatherings with the need for public safety and property protection ([38]–[44]).

Moreover, the examination of the rights guaranteed under section 17 concludes that the right to assemble is contingent upon the peaceful exercise of such rights and does not absolve organizers from liability arising from violent outcomes stemming from their gatherings. The court emphasized the multifaceted nature of the right, which must balance civil rights against public safety ([52]–[59]).

Furthermore, regarding the question of justification under section 36, the Court concluded that while the limitation does affect the right to assemble, the imperative for public order and the protection of individuals' rights to dignity and property provides a substantial basis for such limitation to withstand constitutional scrutiny ([60]–[84]).

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming that while section 11(2) does impose limitations on the right to assembly under section 17, this limitation is justifiable in an open, democratic society. The provisions set forth in the Regulation of Gatherings Act, particularly the defense available to organizers, has a rational basis and serves an essential purpose in maintaining public order while facilitating the exercise of democratic rights. Thus, the detailed examination addressed both the internal consistency of statutory provisions and their alignment with constitutional norms ([84]–[86]).

The decision underscores the critical balance between exercising the right to peaceful assembly and holding organizers accountable for damages, reaffirming the legislative intent to ensure public safety without disproportionately infringing upon constitutional rights.
