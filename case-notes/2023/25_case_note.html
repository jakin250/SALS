FIRAC Case Note: Democratic Alliance and Another v Public Protector of South Africa and Others [2023] ZACC 25

F – Facts

This case comprises three significant applications concerning the suspension of the Public Protector of South Africa. The first two applications (CCT 251/22 and CCT 252/22) were appeals brought by the Democratic Alliance (DA) and the President of the Republic of South Africa, respectively, challenging specific orders from a Full Court of the High Court. These orders declared the decision to suspend the Public Protector, Adv. Busisiwe Mkhwebane, unconstitutional and invalid. The third application (CCT 299/22) was a cross-appeal from the Public Protector contesting the dismissal of her application for judicial relief.

The sequence of events leading to these proceedings began with the initiation of a motion for the removal of the Public Protector by the DA, culminating in the President’s decision to suspend her on June 9, 2022, amidst ongoing investigations, including the controversial Phala Phala incident involving the President. On March 17, 2022, the President had invited the Public Protector to provide reasons against her suspension, which she contested as abusive and reflective of a conflict of interest given the investigation she was undertaking against him ([paras 1–11]).

The Full Court of the High Court subsequently ruled in favor of the Public Protector, declaring the suspension and actions of the Section 194 Committee unconstitutional and ordered their invalidation. The DA and the President then appealed this ruling, seeking to overturn the High Court’s findings regarding the legality of the suspension and the constitutionality of the Section 194 proceedings ([paras 87–104]).

I – Issues

The legal issues for determination included whether the President's suspension of the Public Protector was executed with bias or an apprehension of bias, and whether the actions of the President were in breach of section 96(2)(b) of the Constitution, which prohibits Cabinet members from acting in ways inconsistent with their official responsibilities. Furthermore, the court needed to evaluate the implications of the High Court’s ruling regarding the validity of the public protector’s suspension and whether the procedures established under section 194 of the Constitution were followed ([paras 139–164]).

R – Rules

This case invokes several key constitutional provisions, primarily:

  1. Section 194(3) of the Constitution, which allows the President to suspend the Public Protector after commencement of the removal proceedings.
  2. Section 96(2)(b) of the Constitution, which outlines the prohibition against conflicts of interest for Cabinet members and Deputy Ministers.
  3. Section 172(1)-(2), which governs the nature of declarations of invalidity and the necessity for their confirmation by the Constitutional Court.
  4. The sub judice rule [Rule 89 of the National Assembly Rules], which prevents members from commenting on cases pending before the courts ([paras 46–60]).

The principles of judicial review also apply here, notably regarding the standard for reviewing executive action vis-à-vis the principles of legality and bias set forth in Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000.

A – Application

The Constitutional Court applied a thorough analysis of the facts in relation to the legal framework. It found that the suspensive decision made by the President was rational, justified by the credibility issues raised against the Public Protector stemming from an independent panel's report. The Court reasoned that the ongoing allegations warranted precautionary suspension to maintain the integrity of the office of the Public Protector while the Section 194 proceedings were underway ([paras 237–348]).

Furthermore, the Court addressed the apprehension of bias claims against the President. While acknowledging the potential for perceived bias due to the overlapping investigative interests, it determined that there was no actual bias. The President’s actions had been premeditated and followed due process, without an ulterior motive to compromise the Public Protector's investigatory function. The Court emphasized that both the President's discretion in suspending and the procedural fairness involved were consistent with the constitutional framework members of the executive act within ([paras 350–400]).

In regards to the Public Protector's cross-appeal and submission regarding the alleged infringement of the sub judice rule, the Court concluded that the ongoing inquiry regarding her conduct did not inhibit the Section 194 Committee's ability to carry out its functions, reinforcing the principle that accountability mechanisms such as these could not be stymied by litigation ([paras 401–452]).

C – Conclusion

The Court held unanimously that the applications brought by the DA and the President were to be upheld. Consequently, it set aside the orders of the Full Court declaring the Presidential suspension of the Public Protector invalid. Further, the Court found no merit in the Public Protector’s conditional cross-appeal and dismissed her application for leave to appeal the High Court's earlier rulings. It ruled that the President's decision to suspend her was lawful and preserved the integrity of the office during the investigatory process ([paras 453–499]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the complex balance of powers between the executive and oversight mechanisms in South Africa. It reinforces the principles governing the conduct of public office-bearers, with clear guidelines on both procedural and substantive standards required for invoking constitutional provisions surrounding suspension and removal from office ([paras 500–529]). The decision sets a precedent about how conflicts of interest and behavioral expectations within the executive are construed, thus contributing to the broader discourse on accountability amidst the critical oversight role played by institutions designed to protect democracy ([paras 530–550]).
