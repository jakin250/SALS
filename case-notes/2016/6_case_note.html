Constitutional Court 2016 [2016] ZACC 6

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History

[1] In the matter of Molusi and Others v Voges N.O. and Others, the applicants, six individuals, occupied homes on Boschfontein farm, owned by the Voges Family Trust, represented by the first and second respondents. The applicants had resided there since approximately 2001 on the basis of leases, with the first two being written leases and the remaining four oral leases ([3], [4]). On 19 May 2009, the sheriff served termination notices claiming that the applicants had breached the lease by failing to pay rent since May 2008, thus terminating their right of residence ([5]).

[2] The applicants refuted these claims, arguing they had attempted to tender payment, and contested the circumstances surrounding the cancellation of their leases ([11]). The Land Claims Court, relying on ESTA, granted an eviction order ([12]). The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld this, asserting the applicants had breached the lease agreements, which provided grounds for cancellation ([14]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

[1] The central legal issue addressed by the courts was whether the termination of the applicants’ right of residence complied with the provisions of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA) and, particularly, whether the grounds for eviction were just and equitable as mandated by both ESTA and Chapter 26(3) of the Constitution ([2]).

[2] Additionally, the courts had to examine whether the respondents could rely on common law grounds for eviction that were not expressly stated in the eviction notice and pleadings, raising concerns of procedural fairness ([39], [30]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon

[1] The Constitutional framework is anchored in Section 26(3) of the Constitution, prohibiting arbitrary eviction and mandating that no individual be evicted without a court order considering all relevant circumstances ([6]). The purpose of ESTA is to enhance protections for occupiers, manifesting the Constitution’s commitment to social justice and the right to housing ([7], [8]).

[2] The relevant sections of ESTA include Section 8, which outlines lawful grounds for terminating an occupation, requiring fairness in the procedure and ground of eviction. Section 9 mandates that eviction of an occupier occurs only by court order, after proper notice that details the grounds for eviction ([9], [10]). Previous case law, including Brisley v Drotsky [2002] ZASCA 35, frames the legal standards for such matters ([19]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

[1] The Constitutional Court, led by Nkabinde J, found that prior courts had erred by not properly considering whether the eviction was just and equitable. The respondents' reliance on common law grounds for eviction was deemed improper, as such grounds had not been clearly pledged in the original pleadings or notice of eviction, violating the procedural fairness owed to the applicants ([24], [30]).

[2] The Court emphasized the importance of the statutory provisions under ESTA and the constitutional imperative to balance the interests of owners and vulnerable occupiers. It assessed whether the basic procedural steps required by ESTA had been followed and recognized that the increasing harshness of changing grounds for eviction undermined fairness ([32], [38]).

[3] The absence of consideration for the applicants' claims of an expectation of renewed leases and their attempts to pay rent were noted as critical factors that should have influenced the outcome. The Court underscored that failing to allow the applicants the chance to make representations regarding the eviction and its implications further undermined the just and equitable assessment ([36], [44]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

[1] The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal, primarily focusing on the procedural failings of the Land Claims Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal. The Court found that the rights of the occupiers had not been adequately safeguarded under ESTA, emphasizing the essential non-arbitrariness anchored in Section 26(3) of the Constitution ([47]).

[2] Consequently, the Court set aside the earlier ruling of the Supreme Court of Appeal, thus dismissing the eviction application outright, reinforcing that compliance with the specific procedural requirements of ESTA is paramount in matters of eviction ([48]).

[3] The decision highlights the Court’s commitment to protecting vulnerable occupiers and ensuring that evictions are conducted within a framework that emphasizes justice and equity, aligning with broader constitutional aims of social justice and the right to housing.
