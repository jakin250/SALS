# Case Note: City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v Zibi and Another (234/2020) [2021] ZASCA 97

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])
The appeal revolves around the City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality's (the municipality) decision to impose a penalty tariff for illegal or unauthorized use of residential property owned by Mr and Mrs Zibi (the respondents). The property, categorized as 'Residential 1,' had its rates increased from R898.01 to R3,592.05 as of October 2015 due to the respondents renting out portions of their residence without requisite municipal authorization ([paras 3–4]).

The municipality's records indicated a history of unauthorized use dating back to at least 2013, leading to a penalty being imposed after the property was noted to be used as a student commune ([paras 7–10]). The Gauteng High Court ruled in favor of the respondents, determining that the municipality could not apply a penalty tariff without first reclassifying the property on its valuation roll, leading to the municipality's appeal against this decision ([paras 2, 6, 12]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–2, 12])
The central legal issue is whether a municipality possesses the authority to levy a penalty tariff for illegal or unauthorized use of residential property without changing the property's category on the valuation roll. The question arises from both the interpretation of the Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004 (the MPRA) and compliance with its prescribed processes ([paras 1, 12]).

Additional considerations include the municipality's adherence to legislative requirements related to the determination and imposition of different property rates while ensuring equitable treatment of property owners versus arbitrary increases ([paras 13, 19]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 14–17])
The judgment relies on several statutory frameworks, encompassing:

- **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996:** Section 229(1)(a) grants municipalities the authority to impose rates on properties and surcharges for provided services ([para 14]).
  
- **Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004 (MPRA):** Key sections include:
  - Section 2, which permits municipalities to levy rates;
  - Section 3, mandating equitable treatment of those liable for rates and requiring a consistent rates policy ([para 14]);
  - Section 75A(1)(a) from the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act, which allows municipalities to levy fees for functions or services ([para 17]).

- **Precedents:**
  - **Smit v The City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality** echoed the requirement for appropriate categorization before imposition of differential rates based on property use ([para 12]).
  - **Kungwini Local Municipality v Silver Lakes Homeowners Association** established that rating mechanisms are legislative acts, subject to legality and the rule of law ([para 19]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 20–34])
In deliberating whether the municipality's actions were ultra vires, the Court noted that tariff imposition aligns with a validly adopted property rates policy, which included provisions for ‘illegal use’ ([para 20]). The respondents did not challenge the policy's validity but contested its application. The Court affirmed that while properties must be categorized for accurate rate assessment, existing illegal use tariffs did not require immediate reclassification on the valuation roll to be enforceable ([paras 21–23]).

The Court emphasized that the penalty tariff served not merely as an increased tax but as a measure against unlawful conduct. This differentiation was critical in adjudicating the claim of arbitrary action and the municipality's right to enforce penalties pursuant to its policy ([paras 28–29, 34]). The Court further argued that the demand for re-categorization before imposing the penalty would place an unreasonable burden on municipal operations and responsiveness to unauthorized properties ([para 33]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 36–66])
The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the High Court's ruling, affirming that the municipality possesses the authority to impose a penalty tariff for illegal use without prior reclassification on the valuation roll. The judgment determined that the penalty is a valid exercise of the municipality's legislative power under the MPRA, reflective of both the function and accountability required for managing local governance ([para 37]).

The implications of this ruling extend to local governance and the regulatory environment, affirming municipal autonomy to act against illegal properties while ensuring they adhere to established procedural fairness. The case sets a significant precedent concerning the scope of penalties that municipalities may impose, highlighting the balance between regulatory enforcement and adherence to statutory procedures ([paras 61–64]). 

The dissenting opinion raised substantive concerns regarding the categorization of illegal use under the rates legislation, suggesting that such categorization may contravene established law and principles, emphasizing the complexity and potential for future litigation on this issue ([paras 38–66]). 

This case solidifies the framework within which municipalities can operate, reinforcing the principle of legality while providing clarity on legislative interpretation in terms of municipal rates policy.
