Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Mahlangu v S (20737/2014) [2015] ZASCA 73

## F – Facts

The case of **Mahlangu v S** revolves around the appeal filed by Mteweni Zweli Banzi Mahlangu, who contested the refusal of leave to appeal a conviction concerning two counts of robbery ([2]). The events leading to the conviction occurred at the residence of Mr. Marx senior, a cabinet maker, and involved the appellant along with three accomplices.

On the day of the incident, the four individuals arrived at Marx's home in a vehicle, where two of them displayed firearms and assaulted Mr. Marx senior, forcing him to surrender R10,000 from his pocket. Following this, Mr. Marx attempted to alert the police but was violently pushed through a window ([3]). 

Simultaneously, Mr. Marx junior, engaged in his workshop, recognized the danger when he heard glass shattering and his father’s cries. He attempted to obstruct the robbers’ escape by maneuvering his vehicle behind theirs. However, he was confronted by Mahlangu, who threatened him with a firearm, took his phone, and demanded cash—eventually taking R2000 ([3]). 

Mahlangu was ultimately convicted on both counts of robbery, resulting in a combined effective sentence of 20 years' imprisonment after the court ordered certain sentences to run concurrently. However, the appellant contended the second conviction was a duplication of charges stemming from the singular incident targeting their business ([2]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal question addressed by the court was whether the appellant possessed reasonable prospects of success on appeal against his conviction for the second robbery count. Specifically, the court needed to evaluate whether the incident involving Mr. Marx junior constituted a protected distinct robbery or merely formed part of the prior robbery against Mr. Marx senior ([4]). 

Additionally, the issue of whether the trial court had erroneously concluded that both incidents warranted separate convictions formed an underlying aspect of the arguments presented, which could have implications on the validity of the conviction and sentence received by the appellant ([4]).

## R – Rules

The court's analysis was situated within the framework of the **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977** (CPA), specifically Section 309C, which governs applications for leave to appeal. This statutory provision requires the appellant to demonstrate reasonable prospects of success on appeal to overturn a conviction or sentence imposed by a lower court ([1]). 

Precedents and legal principles related to the concept of duplicative charges were also relevant to the court's determination. The doctrine of *ne bis in idem*—which prevents an individual from being tried for the same offense more than once—was implicitly significant, as was the jurisprudence surrounding the contextual understanding of robbery and whether incidents arising from a single transaction warrant separate convictions ([4]).

## A – Application

In evaluating the facts of the case, the court concluded that there was insufficient basis to establish reasonable prospects of success for the appellant’s claim of duplicative charges. It underscored that both robberies were separate incidents that warranted distinct legal outcomes: the assault on Mr. Marx senior and the subsequent engagement with Mr. Marx junior were not merely extensions of a single act but rather constituted their own separate criminal actions under South African law ([4]).

The ruling specifically noted that the assault on Mr. Marx junior contained its independent elements, distinct from what had occurred with his father. As per the factual context, Mr. Marx junior was approached and victimized in a manner that constituted a separate robbery as defined by the law. The court emphasized that the argument of intended robbery of the cabinet-making business as a singular entity did not align with how each victim experienced the confrontation and robbery ([4], [5]).

## C – Conclusion

The **Supreme Court of Appeal** ruled to dismiss the appeal, affirming the lower court's findings. It concluded that there were no reasonable prospects of success in overturning the appellant’s conviction for the second robbery, rooted in the absence of a duplicative offense analysis. Hence, both charges were appropriately sustained as distinct supportive of individual culpability ([6]).

This judgment has implications for understanding the delineations of separate incidents within criminal law and reinforces the necessity for strict application of statutory charges, specifically around robbery, ensuring clarity in prosecution and adjudication of separate offenses arising from a single event. The court's affirmation ultimately upheld both the integrity of the initial convictions and the principles guiding the application of the Criminal Procedure Act concerning leave to appeal.
