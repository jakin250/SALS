Case Note: Golden Fried Chicken (Pty) Ltd v Vlachos and Another [2022] ZASCA 150

Case Note: Golden Fried Chicken (Pty) Ltd v Vlachos and Another [2022] ZASCA 150

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case concerns the alleged infringement of a registered trademark under the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (the “Act”) ([1]). The Appellant, Golden Fried Chicken (Pty) Ltd, operates in the food service sector as “Chicken Licken,” having commenced trading in 1981. The Appellant holds numerous registrations, including the terms “Soul” and “Soul Food”, specifically in class 43 of the Act, relating to restaurant services ([1], [4]).

The First Respondent, Dino Vlachos, is the owner of the Second Respondent, Soul Souvlaki (Pty) Ltd, which has been trading under the name “Soul Souvlaki” since 2012 ([2]). The Second Respondent applied for the registration of the trademark “Soul Souvlaki” in class 35 and class 43, indicating that it too operates in the restaurant sector ([2]).

The Appellant contended that the Respondents' use of “Soul Souvlaki” infringes its trademark ([3]). Consequently, the Appellant sought an interdict from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which dismissed both the Appellant’s application and the Respondents’ counter-application to remove the Appellant's trademarks from the register ([3]). The High Court refused the Appellant leave to appeal, leading to this appeal after the Supreme Court of Appeal granted the Appellant's request for leave to appeal ([3]).

Initially, the Appellant sought interdicts in multiple classes but narrowed its focus in the appeal solely to the “Soul” registration in class 43 ([4]).

I – Issues

The main legal questions to be addressed in this appeal were:

  1. Did the Respondents engage in unauthorized use of the similar mark “Soul Souvlaki” that would likely cause deception or confusion among consumers with the Appellant’s registered trademark “Soul”? ([5])
  2. Did the Appellant meet the legal requirements for establishing infringement under section 34(1)(a) of the Act? ([5])

Additional considerations included whether the trademarks at issue were identical or merely similar, which would affect the likelihood of confusion among consumers ([8]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework was primarily governed by:

  • Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, particularly section 34(1)(a): This section stipulates that a trademark is infringed by unauthorized use in the course of trade of an identical mark or a mark so nearly resembling it as to be likely to deceive or cause confusion ([5]).

To establish infringement, the Appellant had to prove:

  1. The existence of a registered trademark.
  2. Unauthorized use of an identical mark or a mark so similar as to likely cause confusion.
  3. The use must pertain to the goods or services for which the trademark is registered ([5]).

Judicial precedents such as Lucky Star Ltd v Lucky Brands (Pty) Ltd and Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd were cited to clarify that the court should assess whether the average consumer is likely to be deceived or confused when comparing the marks in question ([6], [11], [14]).

A – Application

In adjudicating the case, the court carefully examined whether “Soul Souvlaki” was likely to deceive or confuse consumers when compared to “Soul” ([9]). The Appellant posited that the inherent distinctiveness associated with its trademark warranted protection, despite the word “Soul” being commonly used ([12], [24]).

The court emphasized that its analysis should focus on the impression created in the marketplace by the two marks as a whole ([14]). It found significance in the fact that “Soul Souvlaki” comprises two distinct components: “Soul” and “Souvlaki,” highlighting that the addition of “Souvlaki” coupled with the context of food offerings creates a discernible difference ([25]).

Key Points of Argument:

  • The Appellant argued there was confusion due to the shared word “Soul,” drawing parallels to previous cases like PepsiCo v Atlantic Industries, where a likelihood of confusion was noted among similar names ([16], [21]).
  • In contrast, the Respondents argued that the visual, conceptual, and phonetic differences mitigated any potential for confusion. They also contended that the significant meaning of “Souvlaki” within South African culture served to distinguish their services ([13], [25]).

The court referenced Century City Apartments Property Services CC v Century City Property Owners’ Association, asserting that likelihood of confusion must be assessed globally and considering the context in which the services are offered ([14], [20]).

Ultimately, the court found that the Respondents’ mark “Soul Souvlaki” did not constitute infringement as it did not create a likelihood of confusion among consumers given the distinct element of “Souvlaki” ([26]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the High Court's decision ([28]).

Ratio Decidendi:

The court clarified that the words that make up a trademark must be analyzed collectively to determine their overall impression on the consumer, not solely based on shared elements. It reaffirmed that simply sharing a common word does not lead to infringement where there are distinctive secondary elements that meaningfully differentiate the marks in terms of visual impact and context within the industry. The decision emphasizes the importance of interpreting trademarks through the lens of the ordinary consumer in a marketplace.

Implications:

This ruling reinforces the necessity for trademark owners to demonstrate clear evidence of confusion or deception in trademark disputes, and it reflects the court’s commitment to balancing the rights of trademark owners with the realities of brand differentiation in the market. The case also underscores the dynamic nature of trademark law, highlighting how courts navigate the complexities of consumer perception in relation to distinctive elements in branding.
