Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 175 (14 December 2023) Court: Supreme Court

Case Note: Grupo Bimbo S.A.B. de C.V. v Takis Biltong (PTY) Ltd

Neutral Citation: Grupo Bimbo S.A.B. v Takis Biltong (293/2022) [2023] ZASCA 175 (14 December 2023)

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Coram: Molemela P, Nicholls and Meyer JJA, Koen and Kathree-Setiloane AJJA
Heard: 8 November 2023
Delivered: 14 December 2023

F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-5], [10-12])

The appellant, Grupo Bimbo S.A.B. de C.V., operates as a global food company and is the registered proprietor of the trademark “TAKIS FUEGO,” covering snack products, specifically corn and wheat chips, registered in South Africa since June 6, 2012 ([para 1]). The respondent, Takis Biltong (PTY) Ltd, a South African company dealing primarily in biltong and related snack foods, has two registered trademarks of the TAKIS logo since May 8, 2007, covering meat products ([paras 3-4]).

The respondent sought to expunge the appellant's mark under Section 24 of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, claiming that the TAKIS FUEGO mark was likely to deceive or cause confusion ([para 5]). Initially, the court of first instance (Vorster AJ) dismissed the expungement application, leading the respondent to appeal. The full court subsequently overturned this decision, cancelling the appellant's trademark on the grounds of similar marks likely to deceive consumers ([paras 1-5]).

The appellant's application for special leave to appeal against this cancellation was dismissed on March 8, 2022. However, on October 31, 2022, Judge Petse referred the application for reconsideration and oral argument as per Section 17 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([para 1]).

I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 7-8], [10-12], [24-29])

  1. Likelihood of Confusion or Deception: Whether the similarity between the TAKIS marks creates a likelihood of confusion or deception among consumers as per Sections 10(12) and 10(14) of the Trade Marks Act.
  2. Reputation and Prior Rights: Whether the respondent established the requisite reputation and existing rights in relation to its marks in order to invoke Sections 10(14) and 10(17) for the expungement of the appellant's mark.
  3. Existence of Special Circumstances for Leave to Appeal: Whether the appellant sufficiently demonstrated special circumstances to warrant the granting of special leave based solely on a split in judicial opinion.

R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 6, 9], [10-12], [24-25], [27])

  1. Sections of the Trade Marks Act: The court primarily applied Sections 10(12), 10(14), and 10(17):
  • Section 10(12): Prohibits deceptive trademarks likely to confuse the public.
  • Section 10(14): Disallows registration or permits expungement of marks that are identical to or similar to existing trademarks, creating confusion ([para 10]).
  • Section 10(17): Addresses unfair advantages or detriment to the reputation of a well-known mark ([para 24]).
  1. Case Law Precedents:
  • British Sugar PLC v James Robertson & Sons Ltd indicated standards for determining confusion ([para 12]).
  • The Pepsico Inc v Atlantic Industries outlined assessing the dominant features of trademarks when comparing them ([para 12]).
  • National Brands Limited v Cape Cookies CC clarified the threshold of evidence required to establish unfair advantage under Section 10(17) ([para 25]).

A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 6-23], [27-29])

  • Comparison of Marks: The court performed a dominant feature comparison and determined that "TAKIS" is the primary distinguishing element. The inclusion of "FUEGO" was seen as a descriptive element that does not materially differentiate the marks ([para 14-16]). The court noted that the average consumer's purchasing habits, which do not involve close comparisons of trademark details, could lead to confusion ([para 11-12]).
  • Comparison of Goods: The court recognized that both parties’ products are considered ‘snack foods’, hence demonstrating similarity across trade channels and consumer base. The majority established that whether the goods are registered in separate classes is immaterial when they are marketed in the same channels ([para 18-23]).
  • Substantial Reputation: The respondent’s reputation in the snack food sector was uncontested, underpinning their argument under Section 10(17). The court noted that it was reasonable to conclude that consumers might mistakenly think the appellant’s goods originated from or were associated with the respondent ([para 25-26]).
  • Special Circumstances for Leave to Appeal: It was held that the appellant's argument of a dissenting opinion, alone, did not satisfy the high threshold for establishing special circumstances necessary to justify the granting of special leave to appeal ([][para 7-8]). The court concluded that the appellant failed to show any additional grounds that would merit reconsideration of the earlier decision ([para 29]).

C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 29-30])

In dismissing the application for special leave to appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the findings of the majority in the lower court, concluding that:

  1. The "TAKIS FUEGO" mark was found to be deceptively similar to the respondent's registered marks, creating a likelihood of confusion or deception among consumers.
  2. The respondent successfully established prior rights and a significant reputation, substantiating claims for expungement under the Trade Marks Act.
  3. No special circumstances were present to justify the appellant's attempt to appeal, especially in light of established legal precepts regarding trademark law and consumer perception.

The court's decision confirms the rigorous standards employed regarding trademarks in South Africa, emphasizing the preeminence of consumer protection in applications for expungement under the Trade Marks Act. The substantive pro-consumer focus of the ruling reinforces the importance of maintaining a clear and distinct marketplace, aimed at minimizing potential pitfalls of deception and confusion in the public domain.
