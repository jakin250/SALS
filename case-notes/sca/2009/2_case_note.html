Supreme Court of Appeal

RMR Commodity Enterprise CC v The Chairman: Bid Adjudication Committee (092/08)

RMR submitted a bid of R108.30 per blanket, which was lower than Africhoice’s bid by R3.65 and it also secured the highest preference points for equity ownership by historically disadvantaged individuals as per the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000 ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009

F – Facts
The case revolves around the tender process initiated by the National Treasury in October 2006 for the supply of over 183,000 blankets for the Department of Correctional Services and the South African Police Service. The appellant, RMR Commodity Enterprise CC ('RMR'), and Africhoice Tenders CC ('Africhoice') were two bidders among others ([1]).

RMR submitted a bid of R108.30 per blanket, which was lower than Africhoice’s bid by R3.65 and it also secured the highest preference points for equity ownership by historically disadvantaged individuals as per the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000 ([2]). However, despite RMR's favorable bid, the Evaluation Committee, later endorsed by the Adjudication Committee led by the first respondent, recommended awarding the tender to Africhoice, citing "reasonable and justifiable grounds" as outlined in the tender invitation clauses ([2]). This decision was formally published by the National Treasury on 12 July 2007 ([1]).

Upon realizing the contract was awarded to Africhoice, RMR applied urgently to the Durban High Court seeking to set aside the award and establish an interim interdict against the order being placed under Africhoice's contract. The interim interdict was granted on 17 August 2007, but the review application was ultimately dismissed by the High Court in November 2007 ([3]).

RMR appealed the dismissal with the leave obtained from the court ([4]). Four respondents opposed the appeal, raising a preliminary argument that the matter was moot since the contract period had already lapsed on 31 March 2008 ([5]).

I – Issues
The principal issues in this case concern:

  1. Whether the appeal is moot given the lapse of the contract period, which may render the appeal's outcome devoid of practical effect ([6]).
  2. The implications of the various grounds of review raised by RMR against the Adjudication Committee's decision, particularly concerning compliance with the audi alteram partem principle, the alleged bias of the Adjudication Committee, and the interpretation of the tender conditions ([9]–[12]).
  3. Whether any exceptional circumstances exist that warrant the court addressing an otherwise moot issue, specifically for purposes of determining costs ([13]).

R – Rules
The relevant legal principles include:

  1. Section 21A(1) and (3) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, which provides that an appeal may be dismissed if the issues are such that the judgment sought will have no practical effect or result ([7]).
  2. The audi alteram partem principle, which serves as a fundamental precept of procedural fairness requiring parties to be heard ([9]).
  3. Precedents that outline handling moot appeals, particularly the approach taken in Chairperson, Standing Tender Committee v J F E Sapela Electronics (Pty) Ltd ([13]).

A – Application
In its analysis, the court evaluated the mootness of RMR's appeal, noting that the contract period had terminated and that any ruling in favor of RMR would not provide any practical remedy. The court acknowledged RMR's argument that a decision might guide future tender processes; however, it found this assertion factually unsupported, as it was geared towards future litigations rather than the immediate dispute at hand ([8], [9]).

Significantly, the court explained that while RMR's appeal raised valid concerns regarding the compliance of the Adjudication Committee with fair administrative practices, these grounds were tightly linked to specific facts of the case. The court pointed out that natural justice principles, including the audi rule, require flexibility and depend heavily on the context of each case ([9]). Similarly, the review grounds concerning the alleged bias and compliance interpretation were seen to hinge directly on the factual circumstances, underscoring an absence of general legal principles or significant public interest that would have warranted a ruling ([12], [15]).

Moreover, although RMR attempted to argue that the completion of the contract years ago does not preclude the court from determining the costs arising from the litigation, the court mirrored its stance on the mootness issue, indicating that costs alone do not constitute "exceptional circumstances" that would justify adjudicating moot issues ([14]).

C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed RMR's appeal on grounds of mootness, affirming that there were no extraordinary circumstances or substantial legal points of jurisprudential importance that necessitated the court's intervention in what had become an academic exercise ([15]). The court’s decision reinforces the principle that issues arising from expired contracts cannot sustain appeals unless they establish a precedent with immediate relevance. The case exemplifies the judiciary's commitment to resolving disputes impacting practical outcomes and underscores the necessity for parties to bring actionable issues before the courts timely.

This dismissal also highlights the importance of procedural compliance and adherence to principles of fairness in administrative processes while illustrating how the courts navigate moot appeals in the ambit of administrative law.
