# Case Note: Saaknommer: 672/94

### F – Facts:

In this case, the appellant, Anna Maria Prinsloo NO, represented the minor children of her deceased husband who died in a vehicular accident on June 28, 1990, following a collision with a cow owned by the respondent, Jan Andries Nicolaas Schoeman. The accident occurred on the Middelburg-Bethal road, adjacent to the property owned by Gerrits, where the cattle were kept. The appellant initiated a claim for loss of support in the Transvaal Provincial Division on two alternative grounds: negligence on the part of the respondent, leading to the cow being on the road, and strict liability based on ownership of the animal ([para 2]).

The following key agreed facts were noted during the pre-trial conference:
1. The cow was from the respondent's herd grazing in a designated camp.
2. The herd breached the boundary fence separating the two camps.
3. Subsequently, the cattle moved from the first camp to the road, leading to the accident, approximately 800 meters from the fence ([paras 2-3]).

During the proceedings, it was revealed that the only evidence suggesting negligence was from the respondent's former herdsman, who contradicted himself during testimony. The trial court discredited this evidence, finding the witness untrustworthy ([para 3]).

The appellant’s initial claim was modified during the trial. The revised assertion claimed that one or more of the respondent's cattle had breached a closed gate, moving onto the road, resulting in the collision with the deceased ([para 3]).

### I – Issues:

The primary legal questions arising from this case are:
1. Did the respondent act negligently, leading to the cow being on the road at the time of the accident?
2. Did the respondent incur strict liability by virtue of being the owner of the cattle involved in the accident?
3. Did the conduct of the cattle in breaking through the fence amount to behavior outside the ordinary nature of cattle, thereby invoking liability under 'paupere' responsibility? ([paras 4-5]).

### R – Rules:

The rules governing liability for animal-related incidents in South Africa can generally be categorized under:
1. **Negligence:** The standard principle that a party may be held liable for failing to exercise reasonable care to prevent foreseeable harm ([para 4]).
2. **Strict Liability:** Under strict liability, the owner of an animal is liable for damages caused by that animal regardless of fault, particularly in cases where the animal behaves contrary to its species' instincts (contra naturam sui generis) ([paras 5-6]).
3. **Case Law Precedents:**
   - *South African Railways and Harbours v Edwards:* Established that if an animal acts out of character, the owner bears the burden to prove otherwise.
   - *Da Silva v Coetzee:* Reinforced the need for clear evidence to establish liability based on the nature of the animal's behavior ([para 5]).

### A – Application:

The court began by addressing the claims of negligence and dismissed them, noting that the evidence presented was insufficient to establish that the respondent was negligent in any manner that directly contributed to the accident. The testimony of the former herdsman was found lacking in credibility, which failed to establish a direct link between the respondent's actions and the presence of the cow on the road ([para 4]).

Moving on to the issue of strict liability, the court explored whether the conduct of the animal breached the standard of behavior expected of cattle. The evidence was deemed inadequate to demonstrate that the cattle acted contra naturam. There was no direct eyewitness account of the events leading up to the breach of the fence, and therefore any purported unusual behavior of the cattle could not be substantiated. Possible external factors, such as environmental disturbances or human interference, could not be excluded, and there was no evidence of how the cattle managed to break through the boundary fence ([para 5-6]).

The court highlighted that speculation about external influences did not meet the necessary threshold to impose liability on the respondent. Without established unusual conduct from the animals, the appellant could not fulfill her burden of proof. It was concluded that the animals’ behavior could not be reasonably classified as being outside their normal conduct as cattle ([para 5]).

### C – Conclusion:

The court ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's decision with costs awarded to the respondent. The judgments emphasized the importance of substantial evidence when asserting claims of negligence or strict liability in animal-related accidents. The ruling clarified the requisite burden of proof on the appellant to demonstrate atypical behavior by the cattle that would violate the strict liability principles. The outcome underscores the significant challenges faced by plaintiffs in establishing claims against animal owners unless compelling evidence can substantiate that the incident resulted from conduct outside the expected nature of the animal species involved ([para 6]).

This case serves as a critical reference point for future litigation involving liability related to animals, emphasizing the need for clear and persuasive evidence to hold owners accountable under both negligence and strict liability doctrines.
