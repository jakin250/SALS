Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Loyiso Ludidi & Others v The State [2024] ZASCA 162

## F - Facts
The case involves three appellants: Loyiso Ludidi, Thando Chwayi, and Sivuyile Shasha, who were convicted of murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances on 19 May 2022. The events leading to their conviction occurred on the night of 23 June 2016, when the appellants entered the home of the deceased, Mr. Pasika Kwaza, and fatally shot him while he was in bed with his wife, Ms. Patience Kwaza. The couple's tumultuous relationship, marked by domestic abuse, prompted Ms. Kwaza to seek an interdict and file maintenance complaints against her husband. Unbeknownst to Ms. Kwaza, a hit was placed on her life by her husband, leading to a switch in targets from her to him, with the appellants being hired to carry out the murder ([para 2–4]).

The sentencing hearing revealed that the appellants had been incarcerated for five years and eight months. Although Ms. Kwaza was also implicated in the murder plot, she received a 12-year sentence on account of mitigating circumstances, such as her reduced moral blameworthiness ([para 6–8]).

Upon appeal, the question arose whether the lengthy period of pre-sentencing incarceration, in conjunction with their convictions, constituted "substantial and compelling circumstances" that might justify a deviation from the mandatory life sentence minimum prescribed in section 51(3) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([para 1, 8]).

## I - Issues
The central legal question presented for appeal was whether the lengthy period spent in custody as awaiting trial prisoners qualified as a substantial and compelling circumstance that would warrant a lesser sentence than the statutory minimum of life imprisonment ([paras 1, 8]).

## R - Rules
The legal framework for assessing the appropriateness of life sentences is grounded in the principles articulated in **S v Malgas** ([2001] 3 All SA 220 (A)), which stipulates that a court may deviate from the prescribed minimum sentences if the specific circumstances of a case render the mandatory sentence unjust or disproportionately severe in relation to the crime and its contexts ([para 9]). The Constitutional Court has further recognized that proportionality is essential in ensuring that sentences are not grossly disproportionate, thus safeguarding rights in terms of section 12(1)(a) of the South African Constitution ([para 10]).

Moreover, the judgments in **S v Brophy and Another** and **Radebe and Another v S** established that while lengthy pre-sentencing detention may be a factor in sentencing, it cannot be mechanically quantified. Courts must assess each case's nuances instead of applying rigid formulas ([paras 11-12]). In **Ncgobo v S**, it was affirmed that time spent in custody before conviction does not itself constitute a substantial and compelling circumstance; it must be evaluated as part of the overall circumstances of the case ([para 15]).

## A - Application
Applying these legal principles, the court noted that while the periods spent in custody were indeed lengthy, they were predominantly attributable to the actions of the appellants and their legal representatives, who contributed to significant delays in the judicial process. This compounded the view that the lengthy pre-sentencing incarceration period was not enough, alone or under these circumstances, to warrant a deviation from life imprisonment ([paras 16-18]). 

The court maintained that the heinous nature of the crimes committed by the appellants — contracted killings with premeditated intent — justified the life sentences without the perception of injustice. The court found no evidence that the life imprisonment sentences were disproportionately severe given the severity of the offences ([para 17]).

## C - Conclusion
In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the lengthy period of pre-sentencing detention did not meet the threshold of substantial and compelling circumstances that would justify a deviation from the mandated life sentences. This ruling reinforces the principle that each case must be assessed on its own merits and highlights the rigorous nature of scrutiny applied to sentencing in cases involving grave crimes. The judgment reflects a commitment to upholding justice while acknowledging the complexities of pre-sentencing detention ([paras 18-19]). 

In light of these considerations, the overall implications of the decision underscore the necessity for the judiciary to balance the rights of defendants while ensuring accountability for severe criminal behaviour, thereby contributing to the body of law surrounding mandatory sentencing in South Africa.
