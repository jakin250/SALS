Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Famanda v State (930/2017) [2018] ZASCA 139

## F – Facts ([paras 1–10])
The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa involved Meshack Famanda, a prosecutor employed by the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA), who was convicted of corruption under section 9(1)(a) of the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004 (the PCCA Act). The conviction arose from an undercover operation initiated due to widespread complaints regarding corrupt activities involving legal officials at the Randburg Magistrate’s Court. Famanda, along with two police officers, was implicated when an undercover agent, posing as a defendant's girlfriend, engaged in transactions that facilitated the withdrawal of charges through bribery.

Following his conviction on November 30, 2015, the trial court sentenced him to ten years’ imprisonment, notably below the 15-year minimum set forth in the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 for such offences, citing "substantial and compelling circumstances." However, both the Magistrate’s Court and the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court denied his requests for leave to appeal the conviction and sentence ([paras 1–8]).

## I – Issues ([paras 3–4])
The primary legal issue revolved around whether the High Court erred in refusing Famanda's petition for leave to appeal against his sentence. Specifically, the court needed to evaluate whether Famanda could demonstrate "reasonable prospects of success" on appeal. Further, the court examined whether the trial court misidentified the circumstances under which Famanda operated as a law enforcement officer under the PCCA, which could affect the applicability of the minimum sentencing provisions ([paras 3–4]).

## R – Rules ([paras 4, 12–15])
The principle governing the appellate review was established in *S v Smith* 2012 (1) SACR 567 (SCA), which articulated the necessity of a dispassionate evaluation of the facts and law to ascertain the likelihood of a different conclusion being reached by the appellate court. Additionally, the definition of “law enforcement officer” as per section 51(8) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act was central to the analysis. This section specified the parameters for who qualifies as a law enforcement officer, and it included a narrow interpretation impacting the minimum sentencing requirements.

The court also acknowledged the necessity of distinguishing varying degrees of corruption, focusing on the amounts involved to inform the seriousness of the offence, as enshrined in the PCCA ([paras 4, 12–15]).

## A – Application ([paras 11–16])
Upon reviewing the specifics of the appellant's case, the court noted several key factors that could support Famanda’s position on appeal. The amount of R3,500 that was involved in the alleged corruption was considerably lower than the amounts typically warranting harsher sentences under the minimum sentencing framework ([paras 12–13]). The court suggested that while corruption is inherently serious, the context of the amount involved could lead to an argument that a lesser penalty might be justified, indicating the possibility of a material misdirection by the trial court.

Furthermore, the prosecution's characterisation of Famanda as a law enforcement officer was questioned. The court posited that there might be a reasonable interpretation that a member of the prosecuting authority does not fit within the statutory definition of a law enforcement officer as set out in the PCCA, thereby weakening the basis of imposing a minimum sentence ([paras 13–15]).

In assessing the appellant’s potential for success on appeal, the court concluded that there were reasonable prospects for Famanda to demonstrate that the trial court either misapplied the law or arrived at a sentence that was grossly inappropriate given the circumstances ([paras 16]).

## C – Conclusion ([paras 17])
The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favour of Famanda, allowing the appeal and overturning the prior court's refusal to grant leave for appeal against the sentence. The judgment concluded that reasonable prospects of success existed on specific grounds raised regarding both the application of the minimum sentencing laws and the classification of Famanda as a law enforcement officer under the PCCA ([paras 17]). This ruling holds significant implications for the assessment of corruption offences, particularly regarding the relevant thresholds that influence sentencing decisions in South African law. 

In light of these considerations, the court's order provided Famanda with the opportunity to appeal his sentence formally, indicating a meaningful engagement with issues concerning judicial discretion and the nature of public office as it pertains to criminal responsibility in corruption cases.
