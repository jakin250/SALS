Supreme Court of Appeal

Puma AG Rudolf Dassler Sport v Rampar Trading (Pty) Ltd

The case revolves around the interpretation and application of the Counterfeit Goods Act 37 of 1997 (the Act) concerning allegations of trademark counterfeiting.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–8])

The case revolves around the interpretation and application of the Counterfeit Goods Act 37 of 1997 (the Act) concerning allegations of trademark counterfeiting. The appellant, Puma AG Rudolf Dassler Sport (Puma), is a prominent German sportswear company, renowned for its trademarked designs, including its distinctive form strip device, used primarily on sporting shoes ([para 2]). The first respondent, Rampar Trading (Pty) Ltd, operates a chain of shoe retail stores known as Dodo’s and markets various branded shoes, including those bearing its own Dodo trademark ([para 4]).

In September 2009, inspectors apprehended a shipment from Rampar, suspecting that the shoes contained counterfeit trademarks violating Puma's intellectual property rights ([para 5]). A subsequent search and seizure warrant was issued, leading the inspectors to confiscate the goods, which Rampar contested. They sought a declaration before the Durban High Court asserting that the seized shoes were not counterfeit and requested their return under section 7(4) of the Act ([para 7]).

The Durban High Court accepted Rampar's assertions, particularly aligning with the argument that counterfeiting required "cloning" of the trademark, which they argued had not occurred in this instance. The High Court’s ruling prompted the current appeal by Puma ([para 9]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 9–22])

The primary legal questions to be determined were:

  1. Is "cloning" a requirement for a finding of counterfeiting under the provisions of the Counterfeit Goods Act? ([para 9–10])
  2. Were the shoes seized from Rampar genuinely counterfeit according to the definitions provided in the Act? ([para 23])

The first issue concerns whether the definition of counterfeiting includes the requirement that the goods in question must be exact copies or replicas ("cloned") of the protected trademarks, as asserted by Rampar ([para 8]). The second issue centers around whether the markings on Rampar's shoes constituted a direct infringement or imitation of Puma's registered trademarks, ultimately determining if the contested footwear qualifies as counterfeit ([para 22]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 11–19], [23–29])

The Counterfeit Goods Act 37 of 1997 serves as the central legislative framework governing this case. The Act's definitions specify counterfeiting in section 1, which presents two primary forms:

  • Paragraph (a): Defines counterfeiting in terms of goods being made, without the authority of the rights owner, that are substantially identical copies of protected goods based on registered intellectual property rights ([para 14]).
  • Paragraph (b): Addresses manufacturing or applying goods such that they might confuse consumers with those of the rights owner, focusing on "colourable imitations" ([para 15]).

The court looked at relevant interpretations from previous rulings, such as AM Moolla Group Ltd v The Gap Inc [2005] ZASCA 412 ([para 1]), and considered international obligations stemming from the TRIPS Agreement, albeit observing that TRIPS is not directly enforceable in South African municipal law ([para 10]).

The court firmly posited that counterfeiting is distinct from ordinary trademark infringement, necessitating a deeper scrutiny into the replication quality and potential for consumer confusion rather than a mere similarity ([para 13]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 10–30])

The First Issue: Requirement of Cloning

The court found a significant legal discrepancy in the High Court's ruling that required evidence of cloning to substantiate a claim of counterfeiting. The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the Act does not mandate cloning as a precondition to establish counterfeiting. The ruling clarified that contextually, counterfeiting can occur even without the creation of exact replicas, as long as the goods mislead consumers regarding their origin ([paras 19, 22]).

The Second Issue: Assessment of Counterfeiting

The court then moved to examine if the shoes in question bore sufficient similarities to Puma's registered marks that would confuse consumers. The judges scrutinized the characteristics of the marks on the disputed shoe products:

  • The first Rampar shoe (C) incorporated a blue and white representation of the split form stripe trademark, suggesting an evident attempt to imitate Puma’s registered mark ([para 27]).
  • The second shoe (B), containing an inverse split design, was similarly identified as utilizing the distinguishing features of Puma's mark, further suggesting potential for consumer deception ([para 28]).
  • The third shoe (A), with added features that also paralleled the split form device, was equally found to infringe on Puma's rights under the definition of counterfeiting, reinforcing the court's assertion that variations in design do not exempt such goods from being classified as counterfeit ([para 29]).

Consequently, the court declined Rampar’s arguments that their use of designs was decorative rather than functional as trade marks, affirming that all products utilizing Puma’s established marks improperly were counterfeits ([paras 27–29]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 30])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the previous High Court ruling erred in its interpretation of the Act, particularly concerning the required criteria for establishing counterfeiting. By clarifying that cloning is not a requisite factor, the court upheld Puma’s appeal, thereby declaring Rampar’s shoes as counterfeits of Puma's registered marks.

The final order overturned the High Court’s decision and dismissed Rampar's application with costs, highlighting the legal and commercial significance of protecting trademark rights against counterfeiting ([para 30]). This ruling underlines the comprehensive approach required when addressing counterfeiting allegations, emphasizing the balance between protecting intellectual property and ensuring market integrity. The decision further clarifies the interpretation of existing definitions within the Counterfeit Goods Act, providing essential guidance for future cases involving the protection of registered trademarks.

The ruling underscores the critical enforcement of intellectual property rights which not only impacts the involved parties but also resonates with broader commercial practices within the South African legal landscape.
