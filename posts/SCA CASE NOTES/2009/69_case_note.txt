# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis for Makhanya v University of Zululand (218/08)

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Neutral Citation:** Makhanya v University of Zululand (218/08) [2009] ZASCA 69  
**Date of Judgment:** 29 May 2009  
**Coram:** Nugent, Lewis & Snyders JJA  

## F - Facts
[1] Edward Mbuyiselo Makhanya ("Appellant") initiated a claim against the University of Zululand ("Respondent") in the High Court at Durban, asserting that his employment contract with the University had been wrongfully terminated. Makhanya claimed that despite the termination, he continued to provide his services or tendered to do so, and that he had not received due remuneration and other contractual entitlements. Two claims were presented; however, they were essentially merged into a single claim for enforcement of the employment contract.

[2] The University challenged the jurisdiction of the High Court to consider Makhanya's claim through a special plea. Historically, similar claims had arisen in other cases, particularly referencing the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA). The High Court upheld the University’s jurisdictional challenge and dismissed the claim, leading to the current appeal. 

## I - Issues
[3] The case raises critical legal questions regarding:
1. Whether the High Court has the jurisdiction to hear claims related to the enforcement of employment contracts, especially in light of previous case law such as **Chirwa v Transnet Ltd** and **Fedlife Assurance Ltd v Wolfaardt**.
2. The implications of jurisdiction under the LRA, specifically whether a claimant’s pursuit of rights under the LRA divests the High Court of its jurisdiction to hear related claims.
3. Whether the dismissal of the claim by the High Court was valid based on jurisdictional grounds.

## R - Rules
[4] The legal framework pertinent to this case includes:
- **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA)**: Establishes the jurisdiction of Labour Courts and forums for the enforcement of labour rights.
- **Basic Conditions of Employment Act 75 of 1997 (BCEA)**: Section 77(3) stipulates that the High Courts have jurisdiction to hear claims concerning contracts of employment.
- Relevant case law, including:
  - **Chirwa v Transnet Ltd** [2008] ZACC 23, where it was held that the High Court lacked jurisdiction in some employment-related disputes due to overlapping statutory provisions, notably those arising under the LRA.
  - **Fedlife Assurance Ltd v Wolfaardt** [2002] 1 SA 49 (SCA), which affirmed that claims for the enforcement of employment contracts could be heard in the High Court.

## A - Application
[5] The court emphasized that the doctrines of precedent and jurisdiction are foundational to the rule of law. The High Court's jurisdiction over contractual claims and the concurrent jurisdiction with Labour Courts under sections of the BCEA was clarified. It was noted that while the LRA provides exclusive mechanisms for specific claims (unfair dismissals), claims seeking enforcement of contracts are generally maintainable in the High Courts. 

[6] The Supreme Court of Appeal found it imperative to dissect the earlier ruling in **Chirwa** carefully. It highlighted a misunderstanding or conflation of issues where the High Court in **Chirwa** ruled both on jurisdictional limits and the merits of the claim, leading to a potential contradiction. The present court maintained that its jurisdiction might not be dependent on a claim's merits, a misconception that had previously led to confusion in both **Chirwa** and the current appeal.

[7] The judgment reiterated that having multiple rights originating from the same facts should not strip the claimant of the right to approach the appropriate forum – in this instance, the High Court for contractual enforcement.

## C - Conclusion
[8] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Makhanya's appeal, concluding that:
1. The High Court does possess the jurisdiction to consider claims for the enforcement of employment contracts as relevant provisions under the BCEA retained such powers.
2. The jurisdictional challenge, as upheld by the High Court, was flawed and not justifiable under established legal principles, especially as it conflated jurisdiction with substantive issues of whether the claim was good in law.
3. The Court set aside the High Court's order, reinstated the claims, and remitted the matter for further adjudication on the remaining issues, asserting that liability and merits would require resolution in the High Court.

[9] This case underlines the necessity for clarity in delineating jurisdictional boundaries while ensuring that employees retain access to appropriate legal remedies for their contractual and constitutional rights. The implications of this ruling may impact future employment litigation, reiterating the principle that rights to seek redress should not be inadvertently curtailed by procedural misinterpretations.
