Constitutional Court

Corruption Watch NPC and Others v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [2018] ZACC 23

The matter at hand involves two related cases, CCT 333/17 and CCT 13/18, concerning the legitimacy of the actions surrounding the termination of Mxolisi Sandile Oliver Nxasana as the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP).

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 23

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–14])

The matter at hand involves two related cases, CCT 333/17 and CCT 13/18, concerning the legitimacy of the actions surrounding the termination of Mxolisi Sandile Oliver Nxasana as the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP). Nyasana was appointed as NDPP on 1 October 2013. Rapid instability within the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) became evident following Nxasana's appointment, primarily stemming from a process initiated by then-President Jacob Zuma aimed at investigating Nxasana's fitness for office. The investigation culminated in a mediation process that resulted in a settlement agreement on 14 May 2015, whereby Nxasana agreed to vacate his position in exchange for R17.3 million.

The settlement agreement fit within a broader context of political and institutional instability at the NPA, exacerbated by prior leadership issues. Following the settlement, Nxasana received R10.2 million, with the balance withheld for tax purposes. Corruption Watch NPC, Freedom Under Law NPC, and the Council for the Advancement of the South African Constitution petitioned the High Court for a review of this settlement agreement, claiming it was unconstitutional. The applications included challenges to the appointment of Nxasana's successor, Advocate Shaun Abrahams, and the constitutional validity of sections 12(4) and 12(6) of the National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998.

The Gauteng Division of the High Court ruled that the settlement was constitutionally invalid, prompting the respondents, including Abrahams and the NPA, to appeal the High Court's decisions while the applicants sought confirmation of constitutional invalidity at the Constitutional Court.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 15–17])

The issues before the Constitutional Court included:

  1. Validity of the Settlement Agreement: Whether the settlement agreement under which Nxasana vacated his office as NDPP and received a settlement payment is constitutionally valid.
  2. Constitutionality of Nxasana's Removal: Whether Nxasana's removal from office constituted a lawful action under the NPA Act.
  3. Rights to the Settlement Payment: If Nxasana's removal was unconstitutional, whether he should be required to return the R17.3 million paid as part of the settlement agreement.
  4. Validity of Abrahams' Appointment: Whether the appointment of Advocate Shaun Abrahams as NDPP, following Nxasana's alleged removal, was constitutionally valid.
  5. Constitutionality of NPA Act Sections: Whether sections 12(4) and 12(6) of the National Prosecuting Authority Act are valid under constitutional scrutiny.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 18–31])

The court relied on several key principles and statutory provisions, including:

  1. Constitutional Guarantees: Section 179(1) mandates that national legislation must ensure the independence of the prosecuting authority, emphasizing the rule of law.
  2. Judicial Precedent: The Court's previous rulings regarding the independence and non-removability of the NDPP reinforced the necessity of a lawful and constitutionally compliant process for any changes to the officeholder.
  3. NPA Act: Sections 12(1), (4), (5), (6), and (8) outline the conditions under which the NDPP may be suspended or removed, emphasizing the requirement that such actions cannot undermine the integrity of the NPA.
  4. Constitutional Control: The rule established in Democratic Alliance v President of the Republic of South Africa emphasized scrutiny of any executive action inconsistent with constitutional values.
  5. Public Interest and Standing: In cases of public interest litigation, the courts have affirmed the ability to entertain abstract challenges, especially when constitutional principles regarding independence are at stake.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 32–89])

The Constitutional Court, led by Madlanga J, determined that the circumstances surrounding Nxasana's departure were shaped by undue pressure exerted by President Zuma, which ultimately undermined the integrity of the NDPP position. The Court found that Nxasana did not vacate his position in compliance with the provisions set out in the NPA Act and that the settlement agreement constituted a means of circumventing due process.

  1. Validity of the Settlement Agreement: The Court concluded that the manner in which Nxasana vacated office was unconstitutional; he had not voluntarily resigned under section 12(8) of the NPA Act, and the settlement could be seen as an act that undermined the independence of the NPA.
  2. Repayment of Settlement: Given the declaration of invalidity surrounding Nxasana's termination, the Court deemed that he must return the R10 240 767.47 received, as the payment was inconsistent with the law underpinning his position.
  3. Abrahams' Appointment: The Court ruled that Abrahams’ appointment was inherently tied to the unconstitutional actions regarding Nxasana’s removal, rendering it invalid.
  4. NPA Act Sections: The Court deemed sections 12(4) and (6) unconstitutional, particularly highlighting that these sections allowed for indefinite suspension by the President without a clear recourse to judicial oversight or parliamentary involvement, which posed a threat to the independence of the prosecuting authority.
  5. Assessment of Condonation Application: The Court ultimately decided in favor of accepting Nxasana's explanatory affidavit that had initially been refused by the High Court, arguing that fairness demanded that all sides be heard fully, particularly given the significant political overtones and public interest involved in ensuring the integrity of the NPA.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 90–96])

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court upheld the High Court’s findings of constitutional invalidity regarding the settlement agreement, Nxasana's termination, and Abrahams' appointment. Key takeaways include:

  1. Constitutional Safeguards: The case confirms the critical importance of legislative provisions designed to ensure the independence of the prosecution, affirming judicial scrutiny of executive actions that may infringe upon this independence.
  2. Public Accountability: The ruling illustrates the judiciary's role in reinforcing accountability within public offices, especially those charged with enforcing the law.
  3. Legislative Action Required: The Court ordered that Parliament must amend sections of the NPA Act to remedy the identified constitutional defects within 18 months, insisting on correcting the powers vested in the President regarding the suspension of the NDPP.
  4. Enduring Implications: The implications of this case extend beyond the individuals involved, reinforcing the judiciary’s ability to intervene in executive decisions that pose potential risks to constitutional democracy and the rule of law.

The judgment marks an essential point of reference for future cases involving the executive’s authority, the independence of prosecuting agencies, and the significance of lawful procedures in public service employment.
