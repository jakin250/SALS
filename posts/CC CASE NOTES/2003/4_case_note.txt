Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2003

# FIRAC Case Note: National Director of Public Prosecutions v Yasiem Mac Mohamed N.O and Others [2003] CCT 44/02

### F – Facts
The case revolves around a declaration of constitutional invalidity by the Johannesburg High Court concerning section 38 of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (the Act) ([1]). The National Director of Public Prosecutions (first appellant) sought to enforce a preservation order on property belonging to the Zunaid Family Trust (the respondents), following an ex parte application made on 4 October 2000 ([2], [3]). The respondents, trustees of the Trust, challenged the validity of section 38 claiming it violated their constitutional right to a fair hearing ([4], [8]). The High Court declared section 38 unconstitutional to the extent that it mandated ex parte applications without providing for a rule nisi, which would notify interested parties to show cause against the order ([5], [9]).

The case history reveals that all substantive issues, except the challenge against section 38 and its radical implications for property rights, were ruled favorably for the appellants, including the preservation order and the following forfeiture proceedings initiated by the National Director ([6], [10]). 

### I – Issues
Three main issues were raised for the Constitutional Court's determination:
1. Whether the High Court's declaration of invalidity regarding section 38 was correct ([11]).
2. The validity and appropriateness of the remedial orders issued by the High Court in light of the declaration ([11]).
3. The correctness of the costs orders made by the High Court ([11]).

Central to the discussion was whether section 38 unjustifiably limited the section 34 right to a fair hearing ([12]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal framework included:
- **Section 34 of the Constitution**: Guarantees the right to a fair hearing in legal proceedings ([8]).
- **Section 36 of the Constitution**: Dictates the justification of limitations on rights, requiring that a law limiting a right must be reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([14]).
- **Prevention of Organised Crime Act**: Specifically, the intricate regulations of chapters 5 and 6 governing asset forfeiture and property preservation orders ([14], [16]).

Precedents such as De Beer NO v North-Central Local Council [2001] also formed part of the court’s reasoning around the fair hearing right ([11]).

### A – Application
The Constitutional Court critically examined the High Court's interpretation that section 38 limited or excluded the possibility of granting a rule nisi together with a preservation order, thereby infringing the audi alteram partem principle ([20], [21]). The Court found that the High Court misapplied statutory construction principles and overlooked inherent judicial powers to adapt procedural rules to uphold fairness ([32], [51]).

The Court endorsed a more favorable interpretation of section 38, confirming that the National Director could seek a provisional order alongside a rule nisi at the same time, which would align procedures with constitutional principles ([52]). 

The Court also determined that even if section 38 imposed a limitation on the right to a fair hearing, it could be justified under section 36. The need to act quickly to prevent potential dissipation of illegally obtained assets was deemed to serve a legitimate government purpose ([52], [58]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal against the High Court’s declaration of constitutional invalidity, concluding that section 38 was not unconstitutional when properly interpreted. The Court found that the High Court’s approach limited interpretation to exclude the flexibility allowed in procedural law, a viewpoint that was inconsistent with the constitutional mandate ([61]).

As a result, the holding confirmed the legislative intent and preserved the efficiency and functionality of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act while safeguarding the essential rights underpinning a fair trial under section 34. This decision reinforced the potential for courts to issue preservation orders with conditions ensuring fairness without derailing the urgent objectives of law intended to combat organized crime ([61]).

This case emphasizes the delicate balance between constitutional rights and legislative responses to organized criminal activity, and stresses the necessity for procedural fairness even within expedited contexts. The implications extend to how courts interpret and apply statutes relating to property rights and individual freedoms in the face of pressing state interests.
