Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA **[062/04]**

## F – Facts
The appellants, Yoshin Naidoo and others, were arrested in November 1997 and charged with several counts of motor vehicle robbery. Their trial was ongoing in the Regional Court at Parow and later in Bellville. In August 2000, a Regional Magistrate, Mr. Botes, struck the matter from the roll due to the State's unreasonable delay in bringing it to trial. He issued an order pursuant to § 342A(3)(c) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) that the prosecution against the appellants could not resume or be instituted de novo without a written instruction from the attorney-general ([paras 1-7]).

On 20 November 2000, Ms. Susanna Galloway, a senior State Advocate in the office of the second respondent, issued a certificate purporting to resume the prosecution. This certificate was contested by the appellants, who argued it lacked valid authority as it was not issued by the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) or a properly delegated authority. The Cape High Court upheld the validity of the certificate ([paras 1-14]).

After leave to appeal was granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal, the key issues identified included the authority of the second respondent to issue the certificate and whether it had been properly authorized ([paras 15-16]).

## I – Issues
1. Whether the second respondent, Director of Public Prosecutions for the Cape of Good Hope, had the authority to issue a written instruction under § 342A(3)(c) of the CPA.
2. If so, whether the written instruction issued by Ms. Galloway constituted a valid resumption or institution de novo of prosecution.
3. If neither the second respondent nor Ms. Galloway had the requisite authority, what implications arise concerning the validity of the prosecution ([paras 15-16]).

## R – Rules
1. **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (§ 342A(3)(c))**: Prohibits resumption or institution de novo of a prosecution without written instruction from the attorney-general.
2. **National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998** (NPAA):
   - § 20(1): Vests the power to conduct criminal proceedings in the prosecuting authority.
   - § 20(3): Provides that Directors exercise those powers subject to oversight from the NDPP.
   - § 45: Clarifies that references to "attorney-general" refer to the NDPP, and references to "attorney-general" or "deputy attorney-general" in a High Court context refer to Directors or Deputy Directors under the NPAA ([paras 26-35]).

## A – Application
The court meticulously analyzed the powers under both the CPA and the NPAA. It concluded that the authority to issue the instruction for resumption of prosecution had to reside with a higher authority to ensure proper oversight and compliance with the constitutional rights of accused persons.

1. **Authority of the Second Respondent**: The court observed that while § 20(3) of the NPAA allows Directors to exercise prosecutorial powers, it must not be interpreted as granting the power to unilaterally resume hazardous prosecutions without proper authority from the NDPP. The legislative intent behind § 342A(3)(c) emphasized that such critical decisions should not rest with subordinate prosecutors due to the implications on fair trial rights and judicial fairness ([paras 38-41]).

2. **Validity of the Instruction**: The court scrutinized the details surrounding the issuance of the certificate. Ms. Galloway, while acting under the direction of Mr. Niehaus and the second respondent, did not possess the required authority to issue the instruction herself, given that the decision was not made with direct involvement or clear authorization from the NDPP. Thus, it was established that the initial assumptions surrounding the legitimacy of the written instruction failed, as the foundational authority was lacking ([paras 42-46]).

3. **Constitutional Implications**: The court noted the overarching necessity for accountability in prosecutorial actions. It emphasized the importance of ensuring that such critical decisions regarding prosecution are made at a high level to prevent any unjust outcomes stemming from potential biases or failures of prosecutors, thus supporting the rights enshrined in § 35 of the Constitution ([paras 46-47]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that:
1. The second respondent had acted beyond his authority in issuing the certificate of resumption of the prosecution.
2. The certificate dated 20 November 2000 was invalid due to lack of requisite authority; hence, it was set aside.
3. The court's order to refer the matter back to the Regional Court was also set aside, significantly impacting the prosecution against the appellants ([paras 48-49]).

The decision reinforces the required alignment between prosecutorial authority and constitutional standards of fair trial and justice, establishing clear boundaries on the exercise of prosecutorial discretion. It underscores the necessity for oversight and higher-level decision-making in matters of prosecution, particularly in situations where previously established delays and rights violations have been recognized.
