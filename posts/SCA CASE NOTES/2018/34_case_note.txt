Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Eastern Cape Parks and Tourism Agency v Medbury (Pty) Ltd (816/2016) [2018] ZASCA 34

## F – Facts
This case arises from a dispute over the ownership of a herd of Cape Buffalo that escaped from the Thomas Baines Nature Reserve, managed by the Eastern Cape Parks and Tourism Agency (the Agency). The escape occurred during an extraordinary period of drought when the water level of the Settlers Dam, which separated the reserve from the Medbury Game Reserve (owned by Medbury (Pty) Ltd), fell sufficiently to allow the buffalo to cross into Medbury’s territory ([1]–[8]).

The buffalo, considered res nullius at common law, were initially regarded as owned by the Agency due to their containment within the reserve, which historically was deemed sufficiently enclosed. However, after the buffalo crossed into Medbury’s property, Medbury contended that it had acquired ownership because the animals were now under its control ([10]). The Agency filed an action in the Eastern Cape High Court seeking the return of the buffalo, while Medbury asserted that the absence of a certificate from the Premier, as required by § 2(2)(a) of the Game Theft Act 105 of 1991 (the GTA), meant the buffalo had transferred ownership to it ([9]–[10]).

Ultimately, the High Court found that the lack of such a certificate meant the Agency had lost ownership, leading to its appeal in the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([16]).

## I – Issues
Two legal issues were presented for adjudication:
1. Whether a certificate as stipulated in § 2(2)(a) of the GTA is the sole prerequisite for the protection against loss of ownership as defined in § 2(1)(a) of the GTA.
2. Whether the common law should be developed to provide that wild animals in a protected area managed by an organ of state remain publicly owned, in line with constitutional principles, particularly § 24(b)(ii) pertaining to environmental protection and conservation ([11]).

## R – Rules
The key statutory provision at play is § 2 of the GTA, particularly subsections (1) and (2):
- § 2(1)(a) states that individuals or entities who keep game on sufficiently enclosed land shall not lose ownership if the game escapes.
- § 2(2)(a) outlines that land is deemed sufficiently enclosed if a certificate from the Premier confirms it so ([9]).

Judicial interpretations of deeming provisions establish that they aim to facilitate the proof of specific facts, often emphasizing the legislative intent underlying these rules ([29]–[33]). Relevant case law also establishes the status of wild animals under common law as res nullius, indicating that ownership can be claimed based on occupation and control ([18]).

## A – Application
The SCA critically analyzed whether the certificate requirement created an exhaustive rule that precludes any other evidence regarding sufficient enclosure. The Court held that the certificate, while serving as a practical necessity to establish proof of proper enclosure, does not negate the possibility for landowners to demonstrate sufficient enclosure through other means ([35]–[37]). 

The interpretation advanced by Medbury, asserting that the absence of a certificate leads automatically to the loss of ownership, was rejected. The Court emphasized that such a narrow interpretation would thwart the GTA's purpose of protecting the interests of owners who genuinely enclose their land to contain wildlife ([36]).

Regarding the second issue, the SCA found no necessity to develop common law to align with constitutional values, deciding that the existing legal framework effectively protected the rights of landowners to assert ownership of wild animals that had escaped under certain circumstances. The High Court's dismissal of the Agency's claim was overturned, with the SCA concluding that the presumption of ownership remained with the Agency due to the Agency's prior control over the buffalo and their status within a managed nature reserve ([38]) .

## C – Conclusion
The SCA upheld the appeal, reversing the decision of the Eastern Cape High Court. It determined that the Agency retained ownership of the buffalo despite the lack of a certificate, allowing for other forms of evidence to substantiate their claim regarding sufficient enclosure ([39]).

The implications of this decision reassert the importance of the statutory framework intended to protect wildlife, while simultaneously affording the Agency the opportunity to demonstrate the measures in place to maintain the buffalo within their reserve. The ruling reiterates that legislative intent underscoring the GTA enables the management of wildlife while safeguarding property rights in the conservation context. The case ultimately encourages greater clarity regarding the responsibilities of wildlife managers and the conditions under which ownership may be claimed.
