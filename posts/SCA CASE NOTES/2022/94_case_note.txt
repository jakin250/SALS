Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Naka Diamond Mining (Pty) Limited v Johannes Frederick Klopper NO & Others [2022] ZASCA 94

## F – Facts ([1]–[19])

This case arose out of a dispute regarding the Klipspringer Joint Venture Agreement (KJV Agreement) entered into on 31 July 2001. The parties involved were Naka Diamond Mining (Pty) Ltd (formerly Steppon Investments (Pty) Ltd), SouthernEra Diamonds (Pty) Ltd (formerly SouthernEra Resources Limited), and De Beers Consolidated Mines Limited. This agreement aimed to prospect for, mine, and sell diamonds. Each party had specified obligations and contributions, with Naka responsible for development costs, SouthernEra contributing mining rights and infrastructure, and De Beers providing its own mining rights ([2]–[3]).

Over the years, operations were fraught with difficulties, culminating in SouthernEra contending that the KJV Agreement had been terminated due to Naka’s failure to uphold its obligations ([7]–[8]). In March 2020, SouthernEra entered voluntary business rescue and sought a High Court declaration regarding the termination of the KJV Agreement and the cessation of any obligations owed under it ([10]–[11]).

Initially, the Gauteng Division of the High Court declared the KJV Agreement was terminated but did not address the subsequent obligations owed by SouthernEra ([1]). This prompted Naka to appeal, arguing that SouthernEra remained bound under certain clauses of the KJV Agreement despite its claims of termination. The appeal also raised issues surrounding the consequences of a breach of contract and the implications for ongoing obligations under the KJV Agreement ([17]).

## I – Issues ([1]–[2], [11], [21])

The primary legal issue was whether certain obligations that SouthernEra had under the joint venture agreement survived the termination of the agreement. Specifically, it involved interpreting the terms of the agreement, particularly the clauses concerning breach and obligations that purportedly survived termination. The parties presented conflicting views on the timing and implications of the termination.

Moreover, the Court had to consider whether contractual obligations could survive cancellation due to breaches under specific clauses of the KJV Agreement and the applicability of the Companies Act regarding business rescue proceedings ([11]–[29]).

## R – Rules ([4]–[12], [19]–[28])

The governing legal principles centered around contract law, specifically the rules on termination of contracts and the obligations of parties following termination. According to general contract law principles, cancellation of a contract results in the termination of obligations created under that contract. The court relied on precedents affirming that obligations cease upon termination, unless expressly stated otherwise in the contract.

The Court specifically referred to clauses 26 (dealing with breach and termination) and 8.7 of the KJV Agreement. Clause 26.2 permitted a party to cancel the agreement for material breaches, and Clause 26.3 set out that upon termination, certain provisions would apply ([19]–[21]). Additionally, the case law cited included interpretations from *KPMG Chartered Accountants (SA) v Securefin Limited* and *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality*, which adhere to the established approach of contract interpretation ([24]–[27]).

Lastly, the court considered the applicability of Section 136(2)(b) of the Companies Act, which addresses the conditions under which a business rescue practitioner might seek to resolve outstanding obligations ([11]).

## A – Application ([16]–[29])

In its analysis, the court determined that following the cancellation of the joint venture agreement, no residual obligations remained on the part of SouthernEra. Despite Naka’s arguments advocating for the survival of obligations under Clause 8.7 following the breach by SouthernEra, the Court found that once the agreement was canceled, all associated obligations were extinguished. The intention of the parties, as evident from the specific wording of the KJV Agreement, was that obligations would cease upon termination ([23]–[28]).

The Court emphasized that Naka's proposed interpretation would effectively result in a reconstruction of the terms, which was impermissible. The distinct linguistic choices within the agreement (i.e., "withdrawal" versus "termination") also informed the Court’s conclusion that the joint venture relationship could not survive the agreement's termination ([26]–[28]).

Ultimately, the court upheld the cross-appeal, meaning that it concurred with the assertion that all obligations of SouthernEra ceased upon termination, thereby providing clarity for both parties moving forward ([29]).

## C – Conclusion ([29])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Naka’s appeal and upheld the cross-appeal by the business rescue practitioners, establishing that all obligations under the KJV Agreement had been terminated no later than 24 May 2020. Furthermore, the Court ruled in favor of the practitioners, clarifying the termination of any outstanding obligations owed by SouthernEra. The Court’s holding reinforces the principle that contractual obligations cannot survive the termination of the contract unless clearly stipulated, providing critical precedent for future disputes regarding joint venture agreements and the importance of explicit drafting in contracts.

This ruling has implications for the interpretation of joint ventures and contractual obligations in the context of business rescue and corporate governance, emphasizing the significance of adherence to contractual terms and the enforceability of cancellation clauses within business agreements.
