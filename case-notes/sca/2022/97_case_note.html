Supreme Court of Appeal

Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Capitec Bank Limited (94/2021)

The case revolves around an appeal by the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS) against a ruling from the Tax Court in Cape Town ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022

F – Facts

The case revolves around an appeal by the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS) against a ruling from the Tax Court in Cape Town ([1]). The Tax Court had previously decided in favor of Capitec Bank Limited (Capitec), allowing it to deduct an amount of R71,520,811.85 as a notional input tax deduction for the November 2017 VAT return. This deduction concerned the tax fraction of payouts from a loan cover insurance policy, which Capitec claimed was a taxable supply eligible for VAT deduction under section 16(3)(c) of the Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991 (VAT Act) ([2]).

Capitec is a registered retail bank primarily providing essential banking services, including unsecured lending. As part of its business model, it offers a loan cover feature, which guarantees that in the event of a customer’s death or retrenchment, the loan amount will either be settled or reduced using an insurance payout from Guardrisk Life Limited ([3]).

During the specified VAT period, Capitec received payouts amounting to R582,383,753.66 from Guardrisk in relation to this loan cover scheme and claimed a notional input tax deduction of R71,520,811.85 ([5]).

SARS disallowed this deduction, arguing that the loan cover payments did not qualify for input tax deduction under section 16(3)(c) because they constituted an ‘exempt supply’ rather than a ‘taxable supply.’ SARS maintained that since no consideration was charged for the loan cover, it failed to meet the necessary criteria ([6]–[7]).

The Tax Court, however, ruled in Capitec's favor, establishing that the loan cover was integral to Capitec's overall business of providing credit, thereby entitling it to the requested deduction ([8]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue is whether the tax fraction of the loan cover payouts qualifies for a deduction in terms of section 16(3)(c) of the VAT Act ([9]). This question necessitates an assessment of whether the loan cover constituted a taxable supply as defined in the VAT Act or whether it was an exempt supply as argued by SARS ([9]).

Sub-Issues:

  1. Whether the loan cover supplied by Capitec was charged for any consideration ([7]–[8]).
  2. Whether the loan cover can be considered to fall within the ambit of a business that makes taxable supplies ([10]–[12]).

R – Rules

To analyze the central issue, the judgment cites several statutory provisions from the VAT Act and interpretations thereof:

  • Section 16(3)(c) provides for the deduction of an amount equal to the tax fraction of any payment made to indemnify another person under a contract of insurance, contingent on that insurance being a ‘taxable supply’ ([11]).
  • Definition of ‘taxable supply’ in section 1 of the VAT Act states that it encompasses any supply of goods or services chargeable with tax under section 7(1)(a) ([12]).
  • Section 7(1) imposes VAT on supplies made in the course of furthering any enterprise ([13]).
  • Financial services supplied as described under section 12 are exempt from VAT ([15]).
  • Consideration is understood as any payment or counterpart provided in relation to the supply of goods or services ([16]).

Additionally, the ruling refers to the established jurisprudence from previous cases, particularly Metcash Trading Limited v SARS and De Beers Consolidated Mines Ltd v SARS, which elucidate the application of VAT in business contexts ([19]–[20]).

A – Application

The court meticulously applied these legal provisions to the facts:

  1. Nature of Consideration: The Tax Court identified that the loan cover was provided without a distinct charge to clients, suggesting that customers did not pay specifically for the loan cover. This pointed towards the conclusion that Capitec’s supply was an exempt supply ([27]).
  2. Definition of Enterprise: The court examined whether the loan cover fit within the definition of an enterprise making taxable supplies. Capitec's argument hinged on the assertion that while the provision of credit itself was exempt, the loan cover was inextricably linked to its credit operations and thus should be considered a taxable supply ([23]).
  3. Characterization of Supply: The court emphasized that the supply of credit, being a financial service, was exempt under the VAT Act. The insurance payouts facilitated by Guardrisk, while benefiting Capitec, did not amount to a direct taxable supply made to Capitec's customers since no identifiable charges were imposed for the loan cover ([30]).
  4. Penalties: The court considered that the penalty levied by SARS for underpaying VAT should be remitted in light of the circumstances, concluding that there were reasonable grounds for Capitec to claim the deduction based on a favorable opinion from senior counsel ([46]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of SARS, overturning the Tax Court's ruling. It concluded that Capitec’s loan cover did not comprise a taxable supply as required by section 16(3)(c); rather, it was an exempt supply linked to an underlying supply of credit ([49]). The merit of the appeal primarily lay in confirming the interpretations of what constitutes a taxable supply versus an exempt supply in the VAT framework.

Furthermore, the Court's order upheld the decision to remit the previously imposed penalty, recognizing that Capitec had reasonable grounds for its deduction claims, albeit unsuccessfully ([50]).

This ruling underscores the critical importance of accurately characterizing supplies under VAT law to maintain compliance and navigate the nuances of business activities that include mixed taxable and exempt components.

Implications

The decision clarifies the distinction between taxable and exempt supplies, particularly in contexts involving insurance products tied to financial services. It highlights the complex nature of the VAT system and the requisite conditions for vendors to secure input tax deductions.
