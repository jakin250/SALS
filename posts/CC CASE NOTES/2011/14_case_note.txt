Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: *Gundwana v Steko Development CC and Others* CCT 44/10

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

The applicant, **Elsie Gundwana**, sought direct access to the Constitutional Court of South Africa to challenge the constitutional validity of a High Court registrar’s authority to declare her mortgaged property, her home, executable for a default judgment under Rule 31(5)(b) of the Uniform Rules of Court. This action followed a history where Gundwana had purchased property in 1995 and secured a loan from **Nedcor Bank Ltd** through a mortgage bond. After falling into arrears, a default judgment was granted by the registrar in 2003, allowing the Bank to declare the property executable.

Gundwana did not take immediate action upon learning of the default judgment and only discovered the impending sale of her property in 2007. A sale took place, leading to her eviction from her home by the purchaser, **Steko Development CC**. Despite initiating a rescission application for the default judgment in the Western Cape High Court, which remained pending, Gundwana approached the Constitutional Court for a declaratory order regarding the constitutional validity of the High Court Rules, particularly regarding the execution of homes. The Bank opposed her application, arguing that there were no reasonable prospects of success for her situation.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 16–30])

The primary issue revolved around several constitutional queries:
1. **Constitutional validity of Rule 31(5)(b)**: Whether the registrar's authority to declare immovable property specially executable during default judgment contravenes the Constitution.
2. **Enforcement of Sale in Execution**: Whether executing the sale of Gundwana’s home was constitutionally permissible given the circumstances.
3. **Impact of Subsequent Transfer**: If the execution was impermissible, what implications followed for the rights of the involved parties post-transfer.

Additionally, the court examined procedural issues regarding Gundwana’s direct access to the Constitutional Court and the appropriateness of addressing the constitutional matter without the prior determination of the High Court.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 35–54])

The court grounded its analysis primarily on the provisions of the **Constitution** (specifically section 26 on the right to housing and protection from arbitrary eviction) and previous judgments, including:
- *Jaftha v Schoeman* 2005 (2) SA 140 (CC), where it was established that execution against homes infringes constitutional rights without proper judicial oversight.
- *Chief Lesapo v North West Agricultural Bank* 2000 (1) SA 409 (CC), which ruled that judicial processes are essential in protecting property rights under section 34 of the Constitution.

The court also evaluated the **Uniform Rules of Court**, particularly Rule 31(5) and Rule 45, which outline the procedures for default judgments and subsequent executions.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 35–61])

In its application, the court underscored the necessity of judicial evaluation in matters involving a person's home. It determined that allowing a registrar to declare a home executable effectively bypassed crucial judicial oversight meant to protect homeowners from unjust dispossession. The court noted that the practice of declaring properties executable when entering judgments had changed post-constitutional developments, necessitating court intervention to evaluate the circumstances surrounding each case.

The court found that the registrar's authority, as it stood, lacked constitutional legitimacy since it enabled potential violations of the right to adequate housing and open avenues for arbitrary eviction, especially in circumstances involving indigent homeowners. The court dismissed arguments suggesting that such enforcement was acceptable due to the applicant’s willingness to collateralize her home through a mortgage, highlighting that a waiver of constitutional rights could not be implied merely by entering into a loan agreement.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 62–65])

The Constitutional Court ultimately declared that it is unconstitutional for a registrar to grant an order declaring a person’s home executable during a default judgment under Rule 31(5) of the Uniform Rules of Court. This ruling necessitated a return to judicial oversight in execution matters involving residential homes, reaffirming the protection of homeowners against potential abuse within the judicial process.

The implications of this ruling are broad-reaching, as they not only affect Gundwana’s specific case but also provide significant guidance for future cases involving similar circumstances. The court’s decision may compel financial institutions to reconsider how they proceed with executions against residential properties, ensuring that protective measures conform with constitutional mandates. Furthermore, the court remitted the matter back to the High Court to reconsider the rescission application, ensuring that the applicant's rights were addressed and protected moving forward, while also setting a precedent for future litigants facing similar issues in the context of homeowner protection.
