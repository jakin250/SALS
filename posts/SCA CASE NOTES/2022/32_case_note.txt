Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Yolanda Dyantyi v Rhodes University and Others

**FIRAC Method**

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–18])
The appellant, Ms. Yolanda Dyantyi, was a third-year Bachelor of Arts student at Rhodes University during 2017 when she was subjected to disciplinary proceedings following student protests related to allegations of sexual violence on campus. These protests led to the alleged unlawful detention of three male students, for which Ms. Dyantyi was charged with kidnapping, insubordination, assault, and defamation ([paras 1–4]).

The disciplinary inquiry was chaired by the third respondent, Mr. Wayne Hutchinson NO, acting in his capacity as proctor. A hearing held from June to October 2017 culminated in Ms. Dyantyi being found guilty and permanently expelled from the university ([paras 7–8]). Despite her legal representation during part of the proceedings, conflicts arose regarding the scheduling of further hearings. On 24 October 2017, the proctor dismissed Ms. Dyantyi's application for a postponement to allow her legal representatives to attend, leading to her withdrawal from the proceedings ([paras 15–16]).

Ms. Dyantyi subsequently sought to review the proctor's decisions in the High Court, alleging procedural unfairness. The High Court dismissed her application with costs but granted leave to appeal on the specific ground of procedural unfairness ([para 18]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 18–21])
The central issue on appeal was whether the actions of the proctor in the disciplinary proceedings were procedurally unfair, constituting a violation of Ms. Dyantyi’s rights under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) and the principles of fairness inherent in public administrative actions ([paras 21]). Specifically, the court needed to determine whether Ms. Dyantyi was entitled to legal representation of her choice and whether the refusal to postpone the hearing, thereby preventing her from adequately preparing and presenting her case, constituted a breach of procedural fairness ([para 22]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 19–28])
The court relied on several key legal rules and precedent cases to evaluate the procedural fairness of the disciplinary inquiry. Under section 3 of PAJA, every person affected by administrative action has the right to be treated fairly and justly, which includes the opportunity to present their case effectively ([para 19]). The constitutional principle of administrative justice implies that fairness is context-dependent, and must be adapted to the circumstances of each individual case ([paras 20–21]).

The court referenced the case of *Hamata and Another v Chairperson, Peninsula Technikon Internal Disciplinary Committee and Others [2002] ZASCA 102*, which established that the seriousness of potential sanctions in administrative proceedings warranted adequate legal representation ([para 24]). It underscored the necessity for a detailed assessment of the implications of procedural rights, especially for students facing severe disciplinary measures ([para 26]).

Additionally, the analysis of the proctor's refusal to postpone the hearing was evaluated against the common law understanding of procedural fairness, emphasizing the right to present one's defense with assistance from qualified legal representatives ([para 23]). The importance of context in assessing procedural irregularities was reiterated through relevant cases, establishing the requirement for transparency and reasoned decision-making in formal inquiries.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 24–31])
The court assessed the balance between the university's administrative needs and Ms. Dyantyi's right to a fair hearing. It concluded that the standards for procedural fairness were not met, particularly noting the absence of compelling justification for the proctor's refusal to postpone the disciplinary proceedings ([para 24]). 

The court highlighted that Ms. Dyantyi was denied her right to have her legal counsel, who had already represented her through significant portions of the hearing, participate at a critical juncture — when she was to testify and present her defense [paras 25–28]. The significant delay in the conclusion of the inquiry presented additional hurdles for her to secure alternative representation within the specified timeframe, exacerbating the prejudice against her ([para 25]).

Moreover, the assertions made by the prosecution regarding the urgency of concluding the hearings were not substantiated, as the court found no compelling reason to enforce a strict schedule other than administrative expedience, which should not overshadow an individual’s rights to a fair process ([para 27]).

The court also criticized the proctor’s decision to declare Ms. Dyantyi and her representatives as having waived their rights to participate further in the proceedings, viewing this as a misinterpretation of her legal representation's clear intentions ([para 28]). In light of these findings and the overarching principle of procedural fairness, it was determined that Ms. Dyantyi's right to a fair hearing had been invaded, necessitating a review and cancellation of the proctor's decisions ([para 31]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 32])
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Ms. Dyantyi's appeal, concluding that her rights under PAJA had been materially compromised due to procedural unfairness. The decision of the proctor was set aside, along with Ms. Dyantyi's permanent exclusion from Rhodes University. The matter was remitted for reconsideration before a new proctor, ensuring that any future proceedings would adhere to fair standards of administrative justice).

The ruling reinforced the significance of procedural fairness in administrative proceedings, particularly highlighting a student's entitlement to adequate legal representation and the necessity for decision-making bodies to provide transparent and reasoned responses to requests for fair treatment. The court also mandated that the university cover the costs of the appeal, recognizing the implications of the ruling for broader institutional practices regarding student disciplinary proceedings ([paras 32]). 

This case establishes important precedent regarding student rights in disciplinary contexts within public educational institutions, underscoring that administrative bodies must respect due process and the principles of fairness enshrined in administrative law ([para 32]).
