Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Serengeti Rise Industries (Pty) Ltd & another v Aboobaker NO & others (845/2015) [2017] ZASCA 79

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([para 1–8])
In the matter **Serengeti Rise Industries (Pty) Ltd & another v Aboobaker NO & others**, the appeal was heard by the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa regarding the validity of a demolition order issued by the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, Durban. The judgment in question was delivered by Steyn J after the Respondents, comprising six property owners representing interests in the vicinity of a property located at 317 Currie Road, Berea, Durban, sought to challenge three decisions made by the eThekwini Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality) ([para 2]).

The facts indicate that Serengeti Rise Industries purchased the property in 2009, which was zoned General Residential 1 (GR1). The Municipality approved building plans for a four-storey residential complex in August 2010. However, in December 2011, Serengeti the Municipality approved a rezoning application to General Residential 5 (GR5), granting permission for the construction of a nine-storey building despite objections from the nearby property owners ([para 4]). This led to construction reaching seven storeys before the Respondents filed a challenge against the Municipality’s decisions on 16 October 2014, citing unfair procedural practices and violation of the town planning scheme ([paras 4–6]).

The appellants contended that the Respondents’ review application was untimely under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), arguing that proper procedure had been adhered to ([para 7]). However, the High Court found in favor of the Respondents, asserting that the building’s approvals were rendered unlawful ([para 8]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 9–10])
The core legal issues in this case revolve around:

1. The validity of the demolition order issued by the High Court—specifically whether it was a justified remedy under the constitutional framework and relevant statutes ([para 10]).
2. The question of whether the Municipality adhered to the requisite public notification and procedural requirements in approving the rezoning and building plans, as mandated by the KwaZulu-Natal Ordinance 27 of 1949 and the Building Standards Act 103 of 1977 ([paras 8–9]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 10–20])
In adjudicating the issues, the Supreme Court relied upon several legal principles and statutes, including:

- The **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act**, particularly section 7(1), which enforces strict timelines on the initiation of review proceedings ([para 7]).
- The **Constitution of South Africa**, specifically sections 172(1)(b) which articulates the obligation of courts to grant just and equitable remedies ([para 15]).
- The principle established in **Lester v Ndlambe Municipality**, which discusses the implications of demolishing illegal structures ([para 18]).
- The importance of clarity and certainty in court orders, highlighted by cases such as **BSB International (Pty) Ltd v Readam South Africa (Pty) Ltd**.
  
The Court underscored that remedies under PAJA and common law must promote constitutional rights, necessitating careful consideration of context when issuing remedies ([para 19]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 11–19])
In analyzing the case, the Court recognized multiple deficiencies with the High Court’s demolition order. Primarily, they noted that while the High Court invalidated the rezoning and deviation approvals, it failed to explicitly set those decisions aside, which rendered the demolition order unsustainable ([para 12]). Given that the Municipality’s approvals remained valid, the order's stipulation to demolish only that which “exceeded GR1 zoning” lacked clarity, as it did not specify which parts of the building were illegal, potentially necessitating the demolition of the entire structure ([paras 13–14]).

Furthermore, the Court criticized the High Court for not exercising its discretion effectively when determining the appropriate remedy, as required by section 172 of the Constitution. The learned judge wrongly assumed that a demolition order was mandated merely from finding a violation of the law ([para 15]). The application emphasized that remedies should be contextualized and tailored to the specifics of each case, rather than being strictly punitive ([para 19]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 20–21])
In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal and set aside the High Court's demolition order, instead opting for a dismissal of the original application brought forth by the Respondents. The Court's decision ultimately reaffirmed the principle that remedies, particularly concerning property rights, should be thoughtfully applied within the scope of constitutional obligations and administrative justice ([para 21]).

This ruling holds significant implications for future cases regarding property rights and administrative law in South Africa, emphasizing the necessity for clarity in judicial orders and the prudent exercise of judicial discretion in remedy provision. It serves to protect against unnecessarily punitive measures that could disrupt established property interests without just cause. 

The outcome of this case also reiterates the challenges faced in balancing municipal decision-making with the rights of property owners, importantly illustrating how procedural missteps can affect substantive outcomes in administrative law.
