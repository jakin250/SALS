Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

### FIRAC Case Note: Prinsloo v The State (613/2013) [2013] ZASCA 178

#### F – Facts: 

The appellant, Jacobus Michael Prinsloo, was convicted in the North Gauteng High Court, presided over by Mavundla J, on charges of murder, theft, and unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition, resulting in a sentence of twenty-five years' imprisonment ([1]). Following the conviction and while pending an application for leave to appeal, Prinsloo was initially granted bail under specific conditions. This included a bail amount set at R20,000, later increased to R30,000 post-conviction, but he was allowed to travel outside South Africa due to his profession as a geologist. However, his bail was revoked on 29 May 2013, the day of his sentencing, and he subsequently applied for bail again, which was denied on the grounds that he had not demonstrated exceptional circumstances ([3]-[5]).

Subsequently, after leave to appeal was granted on 4 June 2013, Prinsloo's legal representative sought to file another bail application, citing new facts including his granted leave to appeal and the availability of R800,000 for bail ([5][6]). Nonetheless, the High Court ruled it was functus officio regarding further bail applications, asserting this had already been adjudicated ([7]). This refusal prompted Prinsloo to appeal the decision to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1][7]).

#### I – Issues: 

The central legal question emerged around whether the High Court was indeed functus officio concerning Prinsloo's further bail application based on the new facts presented. This underlying issue had two sub-questions: 

1. Did the High Court err in ruling it could not entertain a new bail application after leave to appeal was granted?
2. Were the conditions precedent for a fresh bail application satisfied by the new facts post-appeal ([5]-[10])?

#### R – Rules: 

The ruling drew upon established legal principles regarding the concept of *functus officio*. This principle holds that a judicial officer’s functions are completed once a final decision is made on a matter, barring further applications for reconsideration (exceptions included). The court referenced Section 65(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which prohibits appeals concerning new facts unless these are presented to the court which made the original decision. Citing *S v Bruintjies* 2003 (2) SACR 575 (SCA), the ruling also indicated that new or changed circumstances need to be considered by the trial court, not the appellate court ([9]-[10]).

#### A – Application: 

The Supreme Court of Appeal, through Theron JA and concurring opinions from Lewis and Majiedt JJA, criticized the High Court's rejection of the new bail application on functional grounds. The assertion that the court was functus officio contradicted the established norms allowing for the consideration of new evidence regarding bail. The high court's refusal was characterized as both inappropriate and erroneous, particularly since the new bail application had not been formally heard in an open court, but rather in chambers, which diminished the fairness and transparency of the judicial process ([8][10]).

The court underscored the potential prejudice against Prinsloo created by the High Court’s decision, as it effectively left him in a procedural limbo, incapable of pursuing his bail rights while his appeal was being adjudicated. The Supreme Court reinforced that a judicial officer is not only authorized but required to hear a bail application based on new facts, reaffirming that such applications can fundamentally alter an accused's legal standing and rights ([10]) based on the recent developments in their case.

#### C – Conclusion: 

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the High Court had erred in finding itself functus officio with respect to the fresh bail application under the circumstances described ([1][2]). The decision clarified that judicial officers are obligated to entertain new bail applications when fresh facts arise, asserting the necessity for a new hearing regarding Prinsloo's bail. The appeal's implications extend to reinforcing the rights of convicted individuals to adequately contest their detention under evolving factual circumstances, ensuring orderly judicial processes are upheld ([5]-[11]). 

Overall, the Supreme Court emphasized the significance of allowing the trial court to hear the new bail application, thereby safeguarding the legal integrity and procedural fairness requisite for the judicial process. The matter has been referred back to the trial court to conduct the necessary proceedings ([2]).
