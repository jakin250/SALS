Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Beijers v Harlequin Duck Properties 231 (Pty) Ltd t/a Office Space Online

## F – Facts
This case involves an appeal from the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court concerning a contract dispute between Danielle Beijers (the Appellant) and Harlequin Duck Properties 231 (Pty) Ltd t/a Office Space Online (the Respondent) ([1]). On 17 October 2011, a written agreement was established, appointing Beijers as a commercial property broker for the Respondent on a commission-only basis. The contract specified that if Beijers was the ‘effective cause’ of a property sale, she would be entitled to 50% of the total commission earned from completed transactions ([2]).

The contract included a non-variation clause (clause 20), stating that no amendments would be valid unless documented in writing and signed by both parties. It also contained additional clauses emphasizing that the contract constituted the sole agreement between the parties, with previous drafts inadmissible in any legal proceedings ([3]). 

Beijers was involved in two transactions, referred to as the Woodmead and Chislehurston transactions, but was not fully compensated for her commission. She subsequently instituted legal action claiming the outstanding amount of R205,000, asserting her role as the effective cause of both transactions. In response, the Respondent contended that an oral agreement was made, modifying her commission entitlement to 25% ([4]).

The full bench of the High Court set aside the regional court's judgment which had favored Beijers and opted to remit the case for further evidence on the alleged oral agreement ([1], [7]). 

## I – Issues
The core issues in this appeal focus on:
1. Whether the interpretation of the phrase ‘effected by yourself’ in clause 5.1 of the contract requires Beijers to be the sole effective cause of the property transactions to claim full commission ([6]).
2. The admissibility of oral evidence concerning the alleged agreement to modify commission entitlements, which was purportedly reached prior to the actions taken against the Respondent ([8]).
3. The implications of the contract's non-variation and sole agreement clauses on the validity of oral agreements regarding commission ([9], [11]).

## R – Rules
The legal principles applicable in this case include the contractual parol evidence rule, which stipulates that extrinsic evidence cannot contradict the express terms of a written contract intended to be a complete memorial of the agreement ([9], [10]). The court also referenced relevant case law asserting that unless a tacit term is expressly pleaded, extrinsic evidence of such terms cannot be introduced to modify the written terms of a contract (KPMG Chartered Accountants (SA) v Securefin Ltd & Another) ([10]).

Moreover, in interpreting contractual terms, the court must give effect to the clear and unambiguous language contained within the contract, reinforcing the principle that contracts must be interpreted as they are written ([9]).

## A – Application
In applying these legal principles, the Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the terms of the contract, particularly clause 5.1. The court found that the plain language was not ambiguous and did not support the Respondent's assertion that 'effected by yourself' meant 'solitary effective cause' ([9]). Instead, the court concluded that Beijers was entitled to commission on all transactions she had effected, irrespective of whether others were involved in bringing about those transactions ([12]).

The court found that the magistrate was correct in disallowing any oral agreement as evidence, which would contradict the written terms of the contract. The focus was on maintaining the integrity of the written agreement as it was not explicitly stated in the pleadings and pertained to a potential modification that was not documented as required ([10]). The lack of clarity about what specific further evidence the full bench anticipated introducing was criticized by the Supreme Court, emphasizing that variations to clear contract terms cannot be based on post hoc oral agreements ([7], [12]).

Furthermore, the court questioned why, if commission splitting was a prevalent practice within the Respondent’s operations, it was not incorporated into the written agreement. This lack of inclusion led to the reasonable inference that the parties had consciously opted against allowing for such additional terms ([11]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the original judgment of the High Court was incorrect in remitting the case for further evidence. The court held that Beijers was entitled to her full commission as per the contractual terms ([13]). The case highlights the importance of adhering to written contractual stipulations and the limitations of introducing oral agreements that contradict express contract terms. The ruling reinforces the enforceability of non-variation clauses in contracts, marking a crucial point for the practice of contract law in South Africa. Hence, the ruling re-established the lower court's finding in favor of Beijers, ultimately rendering significant implications for future cases involving contract interpretation and the admissibility of modifications not explicitly documented.
