# FIRAC Case Note: Greys Marine Hout Bay (Pty) Ltd & Others v Minister of Public Works & Others

**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No:** 347/04  
**Judgment Delivered:** 13 May 2005  
**Judges:** Nugent JA, Scott JA, Navsa JA, Mthiyane JA, Maya AJA  
**Citations:**  
1. *Minister of Public Works v Kyalami Ridge Environmental Association* 2001 (3) SA 1151 (CC).  
2. *Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Environmental Affairs* 2004 (4) SA 490 (CC).  
3. *National Director of Public Prosecutions v Mohamed NO* 2003 (4) SA 1 (CC).

---

## F – Facts 

The case revolves around the allocation of state-owned property at Hout Bay, which features various enterprises, including fish-processing facilities, a yacht club, and boat repair companies ([1]–[2]). The quayside at Hout Bay is significantly limited in available space, which is essential for operations by local businesses and public access. The property in question (Lot 86), previously leased to the Hout Bay Yacht Club, was subsequently leased to Bluefin Holdings (Pty) Ltd. This decision was made by the Minister of Public Works ([3]–[5]).

The history leading to this decision involved the Yacht Club’s inability to fulfill its contractual obligations to construct a clubhouse on Lot 86, leading to negotiations with the state culminating in the relinquishment of that lease ([8]–[12]). Bluefin's application for leasing Lot 86 arose amidst the Yacht Club's inability to proceed, and despite initial opposition from the Department of Environmental Affairs, the Minister approved the lease in October 2001. Bluefin intended to establish a fish-processing plant and a restaurant to support historically disadvantaged local fishermen ([11]–[12]).

The appellants, comprising Greys Marine, the Hout Bay Yacht Club, and C-Craft Close Corporation, argued that the leasing of Lot 86A to Bluefin would disrupt their businesses, resulting in traffic congestion and reduced accessibility to their facilities. They sought to challenge the Minister's decision via a court application for judicial review, which was ultimately dismissed by the Cape High Court ([6]).

## I – Issues 

The key legal issues can be summarized as follows:

1. Did the Minister's decision to lease state land constitute "administrative action" under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([19])?
2. Were the rights or legitimate expectations of the appellants adversely affected by the Minister's decision, thereby warranting procedural fairness ([29])?
3. Was the Minister's decision irrational or arbitrary, and thus subject to review under PAJA ([34])?

## R – Rules 

The relevant legal frameworks and principles applied in determining the appeal included:

1. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA):** Section 3(1) mandates procedural fairness for administrative action materially and adversely affecting rights or legitimate expectations ([29]).
2. **Constitutional Protections:** The exercise of state power, particularly in the allocation of public resources like land, must adhere to constitutional provisions ensuring lawful and fair administrative processes ([20]–[21]).
3. **Precedents:** The court relied on prior cases that clarified the concept of administrative action, including *Minister of Public Works v Kyalami Ridge Environmental Association* and *Bullock NO v Provincial Government, North West Province* ([26]).

## A – Application 

The court reasoned that the Minister's decision to lease state land fell under administrative action, consistent with PAJA due to its direct legal implications and public nature ([28]). The court examined whether the appellants demonstrated any legal interest or legitimate expectation adversely affected by the Minister's action, ultimately concluding that:

- The appellants did not possess any rights or even prospective rights regarding the use of the property that could inform a claim to consultation or procedural fairness.
- The absence of evidence showing that their businesses would be unfairly impacted was critical; thus, their lack of consultation prior to the Minister’s decision did not establish a legitimate expectation of involvement in the leasing process ([29]–[32]).
- The court also found no irrationality in the Minister's decision, despite concerns about traffic and accessibility, emphasizing that the Minister was well informed about the implications but still deemed the decision beneficial to local economic transformation ([34]).

## C – Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's decision and upholding the Minister's actions as lawful. The court concluded that the appellants failed to substantiate their claims regarding procedural unfairness or irrationality. They had not established sufficient grounds demonstrating that their operational rights or legitimate interests were materially affected by the lease granted to Bluefin. The outcome underscored the importance of balancing local business interests with broader government policies aimed at transformation and development of historically disadvantaged communities in the fishing industry ([36]).

---

This case illustrates the complexities inherent in administrative law, particularly regarding the exercise of governmental power over public resources and the necessity for procedural fairness where rights and expectations are concerned. The court's analysis reaffirms the principles behind administrative action and the necessity for a clear legal interest to claim procedural protections under PAJA.
