Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: New Balance Athletic Shoe Inc v Dajee NO (251/11) [2012] ZASCA 3 

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–8])
New Balance Athletic Shoe Inc (hereafter referred to as "New Balance Inc" or "the appellant") is a United States corporation that has established a subsidiary in South Africa, New Balance South Africa (Pty) Ltd, catering to the local market ([para 1]). The appellant holds two registered trademarks in South Africa: P-F FLYERS (registration No. 1972/1361) and P-F (registration No. B 1965/02999), both registered in class 25 for footwear. These trademarks were acquired from the former proprietor, Leif J. Ostberg Inc, on 5 February 2001, and the assignment was registered on 19 September 2002 ([paras 2–3]).

The late Abdullah Mohamed Dajee, who managed a family business since 1968, operated in clothing and footwear retail. He was the registrant of the trademark POSTURE FOUNDATION – PF (registration No. 1996/06063), also in class 25, and applied for its removal from the register of New Balance Inc’s trademarks, claiming they had not been used and alleging infringement of his rights ([paras 3–6]). Dajee’s assertions led to the reinstatement of his estate representatives as respondents following his death ([para 4]).

Subsequent to Dajee's demand for cessation of alleged trademark infringement, he sought the removal of New Balance Inc's trademarks under section 27(1)(b) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, which allows a trademark to be struck from the register after a period of non-use ([paras 6–7]). New Balance Inc countered by seeking to remove Dajee's trademark on grounds including lack of bona fide claim to proprietorship under section 10(3), and the likelihood of causing confusion under section 10(13) of the same Act ([paras 8]).

The registrar denied New Balance Inc's application but upheld Dajee's removal request. The High Court upheld these decisions, prompting New Balance Inc to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 8–9]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–10])
The key legal issues raised in the appeal include:

1. Whether New Balance Inc's trademarks should be removed from the register due to non-use as stipulated under section 27(1)(b) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([paras 8–10]).
2. Whether Dajee’s trademark could be removed based on New Balance Inc's claim of lack of bona fide proprietary rights under section 10(3) and potential for confusion under section 10(13) of the Trade Marks Act ([paras 24–36]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 10–27])
The appeal was framed within the context of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, particularly sections 10(3) and 27(1)(b), which govern the registration, use, and potential removal of trademarks.

1. **Section 27(1)(b)**: This provision allows for the removal of a trademark if it has not been in bona fide use for an uninterrupted period of five years prior to the application for removal ([para 10]). The application must demonstrate that no use of the trademark occurred within the relevant time frame.

2. **Section 10(3)**: This section addresses the registration of trademarks, stipulating that a mark can be removed if the applicant for registration has no bona fide claim of proprietorship over the mark ([para 24]).

3. Court precedents such as *Plascon-Evans Paints (Pty) Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd* and *Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd* were cited to illustrate the evidentiary requirements and principles guiding trademark dispute resolutions ([paras 16–18]).

4. The case of *Victoria's Secret Inc v Edgars Stores Ltd* was referenced to clarify the definition of proprietorship under trademark law, particularly concerning territoriality and the good faith adoption of foreign marks ([paras 27–30]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 10–36])
### Removal of New Balance Inc’s Trademarks
The court evaluated whether New Balance Inc had established bona fide use of its trademarks between 3 July 2000 and 3 July 2005. The evidence presented by New Balance was considered inadequate since it relied heavily on vague allegations without substantial evidence. Mr. Haddad’s affidavit did not reference the use of trademarks in South Africa, while supporting affidavits, including Mr. van Rooyen's, did not provide specific details about sales occurring during the required period and lacked documentary substantiation ([paras 12–19]).

The court upheld the evaluative framework established in *Plascon-Evans* to assess evidence, concluding that New Balance Inc's failure to provide adequate corroborating documentation (such as invoices) undermined its claims of use ([paras 15–22]). The vagueness of dates and general claims of sales "during the past four years" without indicating specificity under the relevant timeframe supported the court's conclusion that there was insufficient evidence to prevent removal of the trademarks from the register ([paras 18–21]).

### Validity of Dajee’s Trademarks
In assessing the validity of Dajee's trademark, the court noted Dajee’s good faith adoption of the mark in 1996, contrasting with the lack of evidence from New Balance Inc regarding the use of the POSTURE FOUNDATION mark prior to Dajee’s registration ([paras 24–30]). The court recognized the principle of territoriality entrenched in trademark law, and noted that simply being registered elsewhere does not prevent another party from using a mark in South Africa, provided it hasn’t been previously established in that territory ([paras 31–33]).

For the second appeal regarding section 10(13), the court reiterated that for a mark to be deemed likely to cause confusion or deception, there must be evidence showing such use. The appellant’s allegations were insufficient since they did not adequately reference specific uses of the trademark that aligned with the requisites of the section ([paras 35–36]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 34–37])
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the High Court, dismissing both appeals while affirming the removal of New Balance Inc’s trademarks and the validity of Dajee’s trademarks ([para 36]). The dismissal of the appeal regarding New Balance Inc's claims was primarily based on insufficient proof of bona fide usage, which is critical for trademark retention under the relevant law. The court underscored that mere registration of a trademark without usage violates the purpose of trademark law, which is to protect consumer interests by preventing the warehousing of trademarks ([para 10]).

The court maintained that the principles of territoriality mentioned in the case highlight the complexity of trademark law in the global marketplace, aiming to establish a balance between local and international interests while underscoring the pragmatic enforcement of trademark rights on a territorial basis ([paras 30–33]). This judgment reiterates the importance of substantial proof in trademark claims, potentially influencing future litigation and enforcement of trademark rights in South Africa.
