The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa [1998]

Case No.: 261/94, 59/96, 245/94, 230/94, 320/94 In the Matter Between: Phillip Malephane & Others (Appellants) and The State (Respondent) Coram: Hefer, Olivier JJA & Farlam AJA Heard on: 19 May 1998 Delivered on: 1 June 1998

Supreme Court of Appeal 1998

Case No.: 261/94, 59/96, 245/94, 230/94, 320/94
In the Matter Between: Phillip Malephane & Others (Appellants) and The State (Respondent)
Coram: Hefer, Olivier JJA & Farlam AJA
Heard on: 19 May 1998
Delivered on: 1 June 1998

F - Facts

On 15 July 1991, a group of armed men executed a robbery at the Hartebeesfontein Gold Mine in Klerksdorp, resulting in the murder of Fidelity Guards employee Hendrik Willem Daniel Coetzee and serious injury to another employee, Hendrik Willem Christiaan Kleynhans ([para 2]). The assailants stole six cashboxes containing R 7,220 and fled in a Ford utility vehicle. Following the robbery, Fidelity Guards employees and police pursued the robbers, leading to multiple confrontations and gunfire at various scenes ([paras 3-5]).

The accused were later apprehended, with evidence linking them to the robbery through fingerprints, blood found in the getaway vehicle, and statements made to police ([para 7]). A total of ten counts were brought against the seven accused, including murder, armed robbery, attempted murder, and unlawful possession of firearms ([para 6]).

The trial resulted in convictions for several counts, with varying implications for each accused. The trial court imposed harsh penalties, including death sentences for certain counts, which reflected the gravity of the offenses committed ([paras 9-10]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issues in this case include:

  1. Admissibility of Statements: Whether the statements made by the accused during police interrogation and their subsequent pointings-out were obtained lawfully and thus admissible as evidence ([paras 10-11]).
  2. Common Purpose Doctrine: The application of the common purpose doctrine, particularly whether the accused shared a common plan to commit the robbery and whether they could be held liable for murders and attempted murders occurring in furtherance of that plan ([paras 15-23]).
  3. Applicability of Sentences: The legal implications of the death penalty in light of the unconstitutionality following the Constitutional Court's decision, and what alternative sentences should be imposed ([paras 25-27]).

R - Rules

The court referred to several legal principles and precedents, notably:

  1. Common Purpose: The doctrine allows for all individuals who participate in a crime to be held accountable for the actions that stem from their collective plan ([para 15]).
  2. Principles of Admissibility: Following precedents such as R v Dhlumayo 1948 (2) SA 677 (A), the court focused on the credibility of witnesses and the reliability of the evidence presented during trials-within-the-trial ([para 12]).
  3. Constitutionality of the Death Penalty: Post-*S v Makwanyane* 1995 (3) SA 391 (CC), the court recognized that capital punishment is unconstitutional, impacting the sentences imposed ([para 25]).

A - Application

The court assessed the admissibility of the accused's statements in light of claims of duress. The trial court ruled these statements were admissible, citing reliable State witness accounts. The appellate court upheld this, stating that the trial court achieved proper conclusions based on evaluating witness credibility ([para 10-24]).

In terms of the common purpose doctrine, the court found that all accused participated in the robbery and thus could be held liable for subsequent crimes, including attempted murder and possession of firearms. For example, despite various defenses raised, the court concluded that the actions of one could be attributed to others, reinforcing the principles articulated in earlier cases ([paras 15-19]).

Regarding the sentences, the court recognized the necessity for severe consequences given the heinous nature of the crimes. However, in light of the ruling against capital punishment, it replaced death sentences with life imprisonment for the most serious counts while allowing lesser sentences to run concurrently ([paras 25-27]).

C - Conclusion

The court upheld the convictions of all accused on the various counts, confirming the appropriateness of the common purpose doctrine in linking them to the broader scheme of robbery and violence ([para 27]). However, the death sentences were set aside and replaced with life sentences, reflecting both the seriousness of their crimes and compliance with constitutional standards ([para 25-27]).

The judgment reinforces essential principles surrounding the admissibility of evidence, the doctrine of common purpose in criminal law, and the constitutional implications of punitive measures, having profound implications for similar cases in South Africa.
