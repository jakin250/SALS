Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Waymark Infotech (Pty) Limited v Road Traffic Management Corporation

**Case Title**: Waymark Infotech (Pty) Ltd v Road Traffic Management Corporation  
**Neutral Citation**: Waymark Infotech v Road Traffic Management Corporation (440/2017) [2018] ZASCA 11 (6 March 2018)  
**Coram**: Lewis, Seriti and Mathopo JJA; Davis and Plasket AJJA  
**Heard**: 19 February 2018  
**Delivered**: 6 March 2018  

---

### F – Facts

[1] This case centers on the interpretation of sections 66 and 68 of the Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 (PFMA), primarily concerning a contract for professional services. The appellant, Waymark Infotech (Pty) Ltd (“Waymark”), successfully won a public tender administered by the respondent, the Road Traffic Management Corporation (“RTMC”), for developing and installing an Enterprise Resource Planning System.

[2] Following the conclusion of the contract on 31 March 2009, which spanned three years and involved a total cost of approximately R33.7 million, Waymark began providing services. However, in February 2010, the RTMC suspended some of these services, resulting in litigation concerning the contract's validity.

[3] Waymark filed a claim for damages exceeding R6.7 million in May 2014 after asserting that the RTMC had repudiated the contract. The RTMC responded with various defenses, including a claim that the contract was invalid due to non-compliance with section 66(3)(c) of the PFMA, requiring ministerial authorization for financial commitments.

[4] The trial court, Ranchod J, upheld the RTMC's argument, concluding that the contract was invalid as it lacked the necessary authorization. Waymark appealed this decision.

### I – Issues

[5] The pivotal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the contract between Waymark and the RTMC constituted a financial commitment that required the approval of the Minister of Finance under section 66 of the PFMA. Specifically, the appeal examined the interpretation of "future financial commitment" within the statute and its implications for the enforceability of the contract.

### R – Rules

[6] The relevant statutes considered in this case involve sections 66 and 68 of the PFMA. Section 66 prohibits government entities from entering into transactions that bind them to future financial commitments without ministerial authorization. Section 68 stipulates that any transaction that contravenes section 66 is not binding.

[7] Furthermore, sections 216 and 217 of the Constitution of South Africa also provide critical context for the PFMA, emphasizing the need for transparency, accountability, and competition in public procurement.

[8] These constitutional provisions align with section 51 of the PFMA, which addresses procurement, requiring public entities to maintain systems that ensure fairness and transparency in their financial dealings.

### A – Application

[9] The SCA analyzed the context and purpose of the PFMA in determining whether the contract fell within the ambit of section 66. The court noted that section 66 is concerned primarily with avoiding unplanned governmental expenditure and applies to borrowing and similar financial commitments, not standard procurement contracts.

[10] The court found that the RTMC's interpretation, which classified the contract as a “future financial commitment,” was overly broad. It emphasized that the contract with Waymark did not constitute a financial commitment requiring ministerial oversight, since the agreement was initiated within a properly allocated budget for the 2008-2009 financial year.

[11] The SCA referenced previous cases, including *Putco Ltd v Gauteng MEC for Roads and Transport*, to highlight the distinction between ongoing financial commitments and future obligations that would necessitate ministerial consent. The court clarified that the interpretation contended by the RTMC was inconsistent with the designed purpose of the PFMA, which does not aim to obstruct efficient government operations.

[12] The SCA also scrutinized the trial court's interpretation, finding that it mistakenly suggested that all contracts requiring a budget approval for future expenditure must have ministerial authorization, an interpretation that could hinder governmental functionality. 

### C – Conclusion

[13] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the contract did not require the Minister of Finance's approval, affirming that it was enforceable as it constituted a current commitment and not a conditional future obligation. The appeal was upheld with costs, and the earlier decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court was set aside, with the counterclaim dismissed.

[14] This judgment clarified the boundaries within which section 66 applies, emphasizing that standard procurement processes do not automatically invoke the stringent requirements applicable to financial commitments under the PFMA. The decision maintains the balance between necessary oversight of public finances and the practical needs of governmental entities, promoting effective administration while adhering to constitutional mandates for transparency and accountability.
