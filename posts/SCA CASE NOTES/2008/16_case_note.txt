# Case Note: De Villiers v McKay NO (231/07) [2008] ZASCA 16

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts ([paras 1–3])

In **De Villiers v McKay NO**, the appellant, Izak Adriaan Johan de Villiers, entered into a contractual agreement with the West Coast Trust (WCT), represented by the first respondent David Lawrence Cornelius McKay NO and the second respondent Marlene McKay NO. The contract involved the sale of the WCT’s entire right, title, and interest in the Sixteen Mile Beach Development Trust for a purchase price of R1 million, which was duly paid by the appellant ([para 1]). 

Prior to the contract's execution, the appellant provided a written undertaking to a third party at the insistence of the first respondent. This undertaking stipulated that the appellant would procure the transfer of plots from the development to the first respondent or his nominee ([para 2]). This undertaking was significant, as the first respondent asserted that without it, the contract would not have been signed. Notably, the undertaking was not signed by the first respondent or any other party ([para 2]).

Subsequent to signing the contract, the Development Trust was sequestrated, creating a situation where a sale of property occurred that rendered the appellant's obligation regarding the transfer of plots impossible ([para 3]). The respondents, as trustees of the WCT, thus refused to remit dividends owed to the appellant from the insolvency recovery ([paras 3–4]).

### I – Issues ([paras 4–5])

The primary legal issues presented in this case were: 
1. Whether the appellant's obligation to transfer plots, as stipulated in the undertaking, was part of the legally binding contract and thus mutual between the parties.
2. The relevance and enforceability of the undertaking in light of the "entire agreement" clause in the contract, which expressly stated that all conditions are contained within the contract and that modifications must be in writing and signed by both parties ([para 4]).

### R – Rules ([paras 4–5])

The legal framework applied in this case primarily involved principles of contract law, particularly the enforceability of written contracts and the implications of entire agreement clauses. A significant precedent cited was **Wynns Car Care Products (Pty) Ltd v First National Industrial Bank Ltd**, which established that obligations must be reciprocal for one party’s breach to suspend the performance of the other ([para 5]). The court affirmed that where the agreement explicitly excludes other obligations or conditions outside the written terms, such provisions cannot be considered as part of the contractual obligations for enforcement purposes ([para 5]).

### A – Application ([paras 6–10])

The court analyzed whether the appellant's obligation under the undertaking formed part of the consideration for the contract with WCT. It was determined that there were fundamentally two possibilities: 
1. If the undertaking was not part of the consideration, then the appellant's inability to perform his obligation due to impossibility was irrelevant.
2. If it was part of the consideration, the clear provisions of the contract, especially the entire agreement clause, rendered it unenforceable unless rectification was sought and proven ([para 7]).

The court concluded that the undertaking did not establish reciprocity with the obligations under the contract. This was affirmed by the precedent in **Wynns**, emphasizing that merely interlinking agreements does not make obligations reciprocal unless explicitly acknowledged within the contracts ([para 6]).

The respondents had sought to argue for the rectification of the contract to bypass the entire agreement clause; however, the court found that they failed to produce adequate evidence to support their claims of a mutual intention that would warrant rectification ([paras 8–9]), thereby rendering the undertaking irrelevant to the claim for performance under the contract.

Furthermore, the respondents attempted to frame the undertaking as a separate, enforceable agreement. However, the court maintained that such a proposition was moot because the undertaking could not be used to impose additional obligations on the appellant that contradicted the terms of their written contract ([para 9]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 11–26])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellant, reversing the lower court's decision and ordering the respondents to pay the total outstanding amount derived from the dividends of the Development Trust’s sequestration, including costs ([para 11]). The court emphasized the sanctity of written agreements and the limitation imposed by the entire agreement clause, thereby reaffirming that extrinsic agreements or oral modifications cannot alter legally binding contracts once drafted and signed ([para 10]). 

This decision underscores the necessity for clarity in contractual obligations and the importance of upholding written terms, particularly in commercial transactions where parties seek to integrate multiple agreements. The implications for future cases make it clear that unless parties explicitly stipulate reciprocal obligations and ensure formalization through signatures, courts will not entertain ambiguities introduced by non-signatory documents.
