Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Platinum Wheels (Pty) Ltd v The National Consumer Commission & Another (612/2023) [2024] ZASCA 163

## F – Facts

This appeal stems from a distressing consumer transaction involving Platinum Wheels (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "Platinum Wheels"), a second-hand car dealership, and Mr. Hyram Clinton Links (Mr. Links), who purchased a 2012 BMW M5 from the dealership. The purchase was executed on June 7, 2018, for the price of R450,000, significantly below the inflated financing amount of R586,956.52 (excluding VAT), which was arranged to accommodate an outstanding settlement on Mr. Links’ BMW 330D ([paras 1–3]).

Upon purchasing the M5, the vehicle was transferred to JSN Motors for a standard inspection and the transfer of a remaining motor plan. However, necessary documentation was not provided for the plan transfer, which set a precedent for subsequent complications ([paras 4–5]). In the months that followed, the M5 exhibited serious mechanical issues, culminating in a diagnosis of engine failure shortly after Mr. Links took delivery of the car ([paras 6–7]).

Following unsuccessful mediation attempts, Mr. Links lodged a complaint with the National Consumer Commission (the "Commission"), asserting that Platinum Wheels had contravened sections 55(2) and 56(3) of the Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008 (the "CPA") ([paras 8–9]). The National Consumer Tribunal subsequently ruled against Platinum Wheels, leading to an appeal by the dealership to the Gauteng Division of the High Court ([paras 9–10]). Here, the court confirmed the Tribunal's ruling, resulting in further appeals culminating in this Supreme Court of Appeal case ([paras 11–12]).

A significant procedural complication arose when it was discovered that the attorney representing the Commission, Mr. Ludwe Mbasa Biyana, had been struck off the roll of attorneys before the case, raising concerns about the integrity of the judicial process ([paras 12–14]).

## I – Issues

The key legal questions emerging from this appeal are: 
1. Whether the conduct of Mr. Biyana, who represented the Commission without a valid right of appearance, invalidated the earlier judgments of the high court and the Tribunal ([paras 15–17]);
2. Whether the appeal court possesses jurisdiction given that the high court had previously ruled on this matter ([paras 11]);
3. The relevance of sections 5(2)(d), 55, and 56 of the CPA regarding the sale of the M5 and the nature of the consumer's rights ([paras 24–30]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal framework is primarily rooted in the CPA, specifically:
- **Section 5(2)(d)**, which delineates exclusions for credit transactions;
- **Section 55** which guarantees consumers the right to receive goods that are safe, of good quality, and compliant with applicable standards;
- **Section 56**, which articulates the implied warranty of quality for goods sold to consumers ([para 28]).

Additionally, precedent cases cited include *S v Mkhise*, where the integrity of the judicial process was a central concern due to irregularities involving unqualified representation, raising facets related to judicial fairness and public confidence in legal procedures ([paras 18–19]).

## A – Application

In evaluating the case, the Court first clarified its jurisdiction, stating that it could hear the appeal due to the nature of prior appeals stemming from administrative bodies, rather than judicial entities ([paras 11]). On the issue of Mr. Biyana's representation, the Court deemed that while his conduct involved severe breaches of duty, it did not logically nullify the outcome of the prior proceedings since the Commission itself was not implicated in his fraudulent actions. Thus, the appeal would not succeed on this basis alone ([paras 12–16]).

Subsequently, the Court examined the applicability of the CPA. Platinum Wheels contended that since the transaction was a credit agreement under the National Credit Act, the provisions of the CPA did not apply ([paras 24–25]). However, the Court asserted that even if a transaction were classified as a credit agreement, it does not inherently exclude the consumer's protections under the CPA, aligning its interpretation with legislative intent to safeguard consumer rights against unscrupulous practices in the market ([paras 27–30]).

In inferring that Mr. Links was indeed entitled to protections under the CPA, the court determined that it would not be reasonable to uphold defenses asserting that Mr. Links knowingly accepted a defective vehicle, particularly in the context of the evident and serious defects observed shortly after purchase ([paras 29–30]). 

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of Platinum Wheels on the appeal's merits, emphasizing the importance of protecting consumers while simultaneously addressing the procedural irregularities attributed to Mr. Biyana’s misrepresentation. Although the previous judgments were not entirely voided, the Court did substantively adjust the amount owed to Mr. Links to R515,500 (inclusive of value-added tax), highlighting the need for strict adherence to consumer protection laws ([paras 33–35]). This case delineates the necessity of maintaining rigorous employment standards within regulatory bodies and underscores the implications of misrepresentation in judicial contexts, ultimately serving as a reminder to uphold integrity in legal proceedings and protect the rights of consumers against fraudulent practices. 

### Subsequent Implications
The decision not only reinstates protections under the CPA but also mandates a heightened scrutiny of legal representatives in future litigation involving consumer rights. The referral of issues arising from Mr. Biyana's conduct to the Legal Practice Council crystallizes the accountability measures necessary within legal frameworks to preserve public trust in judicial processes.
