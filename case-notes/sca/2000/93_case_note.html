Supreme Court of Appeal

Graham v Cape Metropolitan Council (2000)

The case revolves around an incident that occurred on 26 June 1994, when Noel Raymond Graham, the respondent, sustained severe injuries after his vehicle was struck by a landslide on Chapman's Peak Drive, a road managed by the Cape Metropolitan Council, the appellant.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2000

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([1]–[6])

The case revolves around an incident that occurred on 26 June 1994, when Noel Raymond Graham, the respondent, sustained severe injuries after his vehicle was struck by a landslide on Chapman's Peak Drive, a road managed by the Cape Metropolitan Council, the appellant. The accident took place during a significantly wet period, following several days of rainfall near the Cape Peninsula, with 156 mm of rain recorded for the week ending 24 June and an additional 87.4 mm by the morning of the accident ([3]).

Graham, on that day, proceeded over the drive, which is a significant tourist and commuter route. Since its completion in 1922, it has been known for its steep rock cuts and inherent risks of rockfalls and landslides ([2]). Upon approaching the scene, Graham observed a "falling rocks" warning sign ([4]). However, he lost consciousness after the landslide struck and was later rescued from his vehicle, which was badly damaged by the falling debris ([5]).

The Cape Metropolitan Council was the successor to the regional council previously responsible for managing the road, and it was acknowledged in court that it had a legal duty to take reasonable precautions to ensure the safety of road users ([6]). Graham filed suit against the appellant, and the trial court found the appellant liable for damages owing to negligence. The case was thereafter contested in the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([9]–[16])

The main issues before the court included:

  1. Negligence: Did the Cape Metropolitan Council fail to take reasonable steps to prevent harm to road users by either:
  • Adequately warning users of the dangers of falling rock and earth; and
  • Closing the road when conditions made it hazardous?
  1. Legal Liability: Given the history of rockfalls on Chapman’s Peak Drive, did the council’s conduct demonstrate a failure in the duty of care owed to Graham and other road users?
  2. Contributory Negligence: Was there any evidence of contributory negligence on the part of the respondent?

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([6]–[15])

The court examined several principles to determine negligence:

  • The standard of care required of public authorities was outlined in Cape Town Municipality v Bakkerud 2000 (3) SA 1049 (SCA), highlighting that authorities must act according to the legal convictions of the community.
  • Principles of negligence, as established in Ngubane v South African Transport Services 1991 (1) SA 756 (A), were applied: assessing the reasonableness of precautions by weighing the risk created against the burden of prevention measures.
  • The implications of the Sea Harvest Corporation case 2000 (1) SA 827 (SCA) were also referenced, affirming the necessity of the authority to take reasonable precautions to prevent foreseeable harm.

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([10]–[16])

In determining negligence, the court evaluated whether the appellant acted within the acceptable bounds of care given the prevailing circumstances. The appellant conceded its duty to warn motorists and to close the route when conditions warranted it ([6]). However, the council contended that the existing warning signs were adequate and that they acted reasonably by not closing the road before the incident.

The trial court found that Graham's danger from falling rocks was further exacerbated by the unusual rainfall conditions, which had significantly heightened the risk of landslides. Despite the appellant's arguments regarding the unpredictability of rockfalls, the court agreed with Graham that the heightened risk should have prompted the council to either install additional warnings or close the road altogether ([10]–[11]).

The evidence presented, particularly from engineering specialists, indicated that while minor rockfalls are not uncommon, the likelihood of major landslides had increased dramatically due to the saturated state of the ground ([11]–[13]). The appellant's failure to appoint a qualified engineer to assess the dangers and make informed decisions regarding road safety was identified as a critical negligence factor ([14]). Ultimately, the court determined that the risks were significant enough such that the existing warnings were insufficient, leading to an unreasonable failure to act on the part of the council.

In its deliberations, the court found no evidenced contributory negligence on Graham's part, thus dismissing that aspect entirely ([16]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([16])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the trial court's ruling, confirming that the Cape Metropolitan Council was liable for the damages incurred by Graham due to its negligent failure to act appropriately under the wet weather conditions. The court concluded that the risk of harm due to the unaddressed potential for rockfalls had escalated to a level requiring intervention, both in terms of warning signs and road closures ([16]).

This case reinforces public authorities' responsibility to proactively manage road safety risks, especially in known hazardous conditions, and illustrates the legal expectations for maintaining an appropriate standard of care in public road management. Furthermore, the ruling prompts reevaluation of safety protocols and criteria for road closures under adverse weather conditions across similar jurisdictions.
