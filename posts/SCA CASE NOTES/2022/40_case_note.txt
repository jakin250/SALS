Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Timothy Maluleke N.O. v Daniel Phellimon Sibanyoni and Others

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) concerns the appellant, Timothy Maluleke N.O., who represents the Hlaniki Trust, seeking to evict the first respondent, Daniel Phellimon Sibanyoni, from a portion of the farm Tweefontein 541 in Gauteng. The Trust acquired the farm in 2015, unaware of Mr. Sibanyoni’s occupancy. Mr. Sibanyoni, aged 56, has lived on the property since his childhood, sharing a laborer’s cottage with his family and possessing livestock. 

The Trust contended that Mr. Sibanyoni resided on the farm without the necessary consent after February 4, 1997, a key date under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA). However, during the proceedings, the Trust conceded that Mr. Sibanyoni was an “occupier” who had been on the land prior to this date, thus triggering the protections afforded by ESTA. After Mr. Sibanyoni refused to vacate, the Trust initiated the eviction application in April 2018.

The Land Claims Court (LCC) dismissed the eviction application, determining the termination of Mr. Sibanyoni’s residence did not comply with the procedural requirements under ESTA. On appeal to the SCA, the Trust sought to challenge this ruling ([paras 1–9]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 10–13])

The core issue raised in this appeal is whether the termination of Mr. Sibanyoni’s right to reside on the farm was just and equitable in both substantive and procedural aspects as mandated by Section 8(1) of ESTA. Specifically, the questions are:

1. Did the Trust provide Mr. Sibanyoni with an effective opportunity to make representations before the termination of his right of residence?
2. If the Trust failed to afford such an opportunity, was the termination therefore unjust and inequitable?

These issues are critical as they determine the legality of the eviction proceedings initiated by the Trust and reflect the sensitive balance between property rights and the rights of long-term occupiers under ESTA ([paras 10–13]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 1–27])

The SCA's analysis is framed within the provisions of ESTA, particularly Section 8 which mandates that termination of an occupier's right of residence must be “just and equitable” considering several factors ([para 10]). These include:

- The fairness of agreements or provisions under which the occupier resides ([para 10(a)]).
- The conduct of the parties ([para 10(b)]).
- Comparative hardship to the owner and the occupier if the right is not terminated ([para 10(c)]).
- Any reasonable expectation of renewal of residency ([para 10(d)]).
- Procedural fairness, including whether the occupier had a chance to make representations prior to termination ([para 10(e)]).

The case of **Snyders and Others v De Jager** [2016] ZACC 55 was particularly pertinent, as it emphasized that both substantive and procedural fairness are required under ESTA, reaffirming that a fair process must be followed before eviction takes place ([para 13]).

The LCC's findings were grounded in the interpretation of these statutes and the principles from previous rulings, particularly **Port Elizabeth Municipality v Various Occupiers** [2004] ZACC 7, which discussed the need for equity in eviction proceedings ([para 12]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 14–28])

The SCA meticulously examined whether the Trust fulfilled its obligations under ESTA. In addressing whether Mr. Sibanyoni was afforded procedural fairness, the Court noted that the Trust engaged only with the prior owner and did not interact with Mr. Sibanyoni prior to terminating his right of residence. This failure meant the Trust did not provide Mr. Sibanyoni with an adequate chance to make representations, a requirement under ESTA ([paras 22–27]).

The Court underscored the significance of establishing a pre-termination dialogue, particularly given Mr. Sibanyoni’s long history on the farm. The LCC had already found that the Trust's claims regarding the necessity of eviction for operational reasons lacked substantive evidence, such as specific plans for development or details on alternative accommodations for Mr. Sibanyoni ([para 18]).

Furthermore, the SCA agreed with the LCC that the hardship Mr. Sibanyoni faced upon eviction greatly outweighed that of the Trust. The absence of a viable alternative for Mr. Sibanyoni’s livestock raised further questions about the fairness of the eviction sanctions proposed ([para 20]).

Ultimately, the SCA concurred with the LCC's ruling that the Trust's actions did not constitute a just and equitable termination of the right of residence ([paras 26–28]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 28–29])

In conclusion, the SCA dismissed the appeal and upheld the LCC's finding that the Trust had failed to comply with the procedural requirements of ESTA. The Court emphasized that the failure to afford Mr. Sibanyoni an effective opportunity to make representations rendered the termination of his right of residence unjust and inequitable.

This decision reaffirms the necessity for landlords to adhere strictly to procedural fairness in eviction matters, particularly regarding the rights of long-term occupiers. It underscores the judiciary's role in ensuring that property rights do not override individual rights to housing and livelihood, setting a precedent for similar cases under ESTA in future [para 29].

### References

The case relies on various legal precedents and statutory provisions for its determination, specifically:

1. Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA)
2. **Port Elizabeth Municipality v Various Occupiers** [2004] ZACC 7, 2005 (1) SA 217 (CC)
3. **Snyders and Others v De Jager** [2016] ZACC 55; 2017 (3) SA 545 (CC)
4. **Aquarius Platinum (SA) (Pty) Ltd v Bonene and Others** [2020] ZASCA 7; 2020 (5) SA 28 (SCA)

This judgement serves as a foundational case for adjudicating similar disputes about land tenure and eviction in South Africa, emphasizing the importance of fairness in both substance and process.
