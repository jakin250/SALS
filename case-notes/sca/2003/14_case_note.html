Supreme Court of Appeal

The Richtersveld Community and Others v Alexkor Limited

The community primarily alleged that their customary law interest in the land, rooted in their continuous occupation and traditional laws, predated the 1913 cut-off date stipulated by the Act.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2003

F – Facts

The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa adjudicated the case of Richtersveld Community and Others v Alexkor Limited, where the appellants, comprising the Richtersveld community, claimed restitution of land rights under the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 ([1]). The community, consisting of inhabitants from four villages (Kuboes, Sanddrift, Lekkersing, and Eksteenfontein), sought the restitution of a strip of land covering approximately 85,000 hectares along the Richtersveld coastline. Following the discovery of diamond deposits in the 1920s, the South African Government granted the land, including mineral rights, to Alexkor Ltd., the first respondent, effectively dispossessing the Richtersveld community ([2]).

The community primarily alleged that their customary law interest in the land, rooted in their continuous occupation and traditional laws, predated the 1913 cut-off date stipulated by the Act. The Land Claims Court (LCC) initially ruled against them, asserting that their rights were extinguished upon the annexation of the Richtersveld by the British Crown in December 1847 ([4], [6]).

They argued that the dispossession occurred as a result of past racially discriminatory practices, a claim the LCC ultimately rejected, stating such dispossession lacked a direct connection to discriminatory intent ([7], [100]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues to be resolved were:

  1. Did the Richtersveld community have a rightful claim for restitution as defined in section 2(1) of the Restitution of Land Rights Act? The requirements included:
  • Existence as a community.
  • Possession of a right in land deemed as customary law interest.
  • Dispossession occurring after June 19, 1913, under racially discriminatory practices.
  • Lodging claims before the prescribed deadline ([4], [6]).
  1. Did the dispossession amount to a result of past racially discriminatory practices as defined by the Act? ([99], [100]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework for the determination of rights in this case was established by the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994, specifically section 2, which outlines conditions under which communities may claim restitution of land rights due to historical dispossession ([4]).

Additionally, the court considered precedents regarding customary law interests and aboriginal rights, drawing from international perspectives on indigenous land rights, especially the principles established in Mabo v Queensland and Delgamuukw v British Columbia ([36], [37]). The LCC’s earlier ruling in Minister of Land Affairs v Slamdien was also pivotal, establishing that dispossessions needed to connect directly to spatial apartheid for entitlement under the Act ([99]).

A – Application

The court found that:

  1. Community Status: It was accepted that the Richtersveld community qualified as a community under the Act ([6]).
  2. Customary Law Interest: The court determined that the Richtersveld community occupied the land prior to annexation, thus holding a 'customary law interest' akin to common law ownership. Evidence demonstrated their exclusive occupation and governance systems predating colonial annexation ([12]-[29]).
  3. Dispossession and Racial Discrimination: The court concluded that the State's policies constituted racially discriminatory practices, grounded in the false premise that the community lacked civilizational status to assert land rights. Thus, their dispossession was viewed as based on racial discrimination, failing to recognize their rights, which were acknowledged historically ([110], [111]).

The Supreme Court's rulings also clarified perceived issues of previous laws deemed discriminatory, allowing the current interpretation under the Act to include broader definitions of dispossession beyond spatial apartheid ([102], [106]). The failure of the LCC to recognize indirect racial discrimination was criticized, reaffirming that racially discriminatory practices do not necessitate explicit malicious intent ([106]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the appeal was upheld, establishing the Richtersveld community's entitlement to restitution of the land rights they had historically held. The Supreme Court ordered the state and Alexkor to return the land to the community, thus reaffirming their rights to exclusive beneficial occupation and associated mineral rights ([111]).

The implications of this ruling extend to broader issues of indigenous rights in South Africa, emphasizing the importance of recognizing customary law interests and addressing historical injustices in land dispossession cases. The judgment represents a critical development in South Africa's legal landscape regarding land restitution, potentially influencing future cases dealing with indigenous land rights and affirmative redress mechanisms ([111]).

  • Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994.
