Agribee Beef Fund (Pty) Ltd and Another v Eastern Cape Rural Development Agency and Another [2023] ZACC 6

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 6

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–11])

The case concerns a tripartite agreement between two private entities, Agribee Beef Fund (Pty) Ltd (Agribee) and Berlin Beef (Pty) Ltd, and two organs of state, the Eastern Cape Rural Development Agency (Agency) and the Member of the Executive Council for Rural Development and Agrarian Reform, Province of the Eastern Cape (MEC) ([paras 1, 5]). The dispute arose from the applicants' assertion that the agreement was a mechanism to provide support to smallholder farmers in the Eastern Cape, aimed at enhancing their cattle production capabilities through funding for the purchase of beef weaners, veterinary services, and agricultural training ([para 1]).

The applicants initially sought a significant financial investment from the Department, with a total of R263 million earmarked for various agricultural projects ([para 8]). Following the signing of a service level agreement and the subsequent contracts delineating the parameters of the financial transfers and services, the respondents later challenged the legality of the contract on the grounds that it had not undergone a proper procurement process as mandated by Section 217(1) of the Constitution ([paras 12–14]).

Initially, the High Court upheld the applicants' position, finding the agreement not subject to procurement stipulations, as it was premised on facilitating economic upliftment rather than procurement of goods and services ([para 14]). However, the Supreme Court of Appeal reversed this decision, declaring the agreement invalid, thereby necessitating the present appeal ([paras 15–16]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 20–22])

The primary issue presented before the Constitutional Court revolves around whether the tripartite agreement constitutes a procurement of goods or services under Section 217(1) of the Constitution. The applicants argued that the matter engaged the court's jurisdiction as it raised significant constitutional questions related to public procurement, which are vital for transparency and accountability within state spending ([paras 20–21]).

Moreover, the question of mootness became pertinent, as the respondents contended that the agreement’s expiration before the appeal invalidated the need for judicial intervention, while the applicants maintained that it raised issues of public interest and potential claims for punitive damages against the respondents ([para 22]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 3, 17, 18])

The legal framework governing public procurement in South Africa is encapsulated within Section 217 of the Constitution, which mandates that state organs must contract for goods and services in a manner that is fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([para 2]). The case Evans underlined that the Constitution's procurement provisions are not limited to state procurements for their direct use, but extend to situations where public funds are involved in supporting third parties to fulfill public objectives ([para 17]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal applied the principles from Airports Company SOC Ltd v Imperial Group Ltd to assert that Section 217(1) encompasses agreements involving external parties when public institutions are funding these initiatives ([para 17]). Additionally, the court distinguished it from Auditor-General of SA v MEC for Economic Opportunities, establishing that the nature of the funding arrangements played a significant role in determining the applicability of the procurement rules ([para 18]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 23–28])

The Constitutional Court closely examined the nature of the agreement in light of the statutory framework and the objectives pursued by the parties involved. The majority opinion opined that despite the applicants’ assertion that their agreement functioned purely as a vehicle for development funds, the agreement fundamentally aimed to secure goods and services — specifically, the procurement of beef weaners and related agricultural training for emerging farmers ([paras 4, 20]).

The court highlighted that the contract's primary purpose aligned with the statutory responsibilities of the Agency and the Department, necessitating compliance with the constitutional procurement mandate. The mere fact that the funds directed towards the project were framed in terms of support did not exempt the transaction from the requirements of Section 217(1) ([paras 5, 15]).

Furthermore, the court found that the agreement, being one deemed ‘unlawful’ due to the absence of a proper procurement process, qualified as moot, given that any ruling made post-agreement expiration would yield no practical resolution ([para 25]).

Ultimately, the Court assessed the "interests of justice" with respect to whether it should still entertain the appeal even if moot. The Court determined that a ruling on the matter, absent any current operational effect or the ability to rectify the situation, did not serve the public interest or the parties involved ([para 28]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 30–33])

The Constitutional Court concluded by dismissing the application for leave to appeal, thereby affirming the Supreme Court of Appeal's ruling that the tripartite agreement was invalid for failure to adhere to constitutional procurement norms as delineated in Section 217(1) ([para 32]). The case underscores the stringent adherence to constitutional compliance in public procurement, illustrating that even agreements framed under developmental pretenses remain governed by procurement law ([para 32]).

Furthermore, the court clarified the implications surrounding mootness, establishing that even where a legal matter could invoke constitutional questions, it would not be considered if it did not materially affect the parties post-termination of the agreement ([para 29]). As such, the court's refusal to entertain the moot appeal solidifies the precedent that compliance with procurement procedures remains vital to the integrity and accountability of public funds, ultimately shaping future engagements in similar public-private partnerships ([para 33]).
