Supreme Court of Appeal

Brian Basil Nel NO & Michael De Villiers NO v The Master of the High Court Eastern Cape & Others

The Master reduced the liquidators' initial fee claim citing reasons such as the nature of Intramed being well run and profitable, leading to fewer onerous duties during its liquidation process ([para 1–10]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2004

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case arises from the appeal of Brian Basil Nel and Michael De Villiers, joint liquidators of Intramed (Pty) Limited, against a decision by the Master of the High Court, which reduced their claimed remuneration from R21,049,941.74 to R3,250,000. The context includes the provisional liquidation of Macmed Health Care Limited and its subsidiaries, including Intramed. Intramed was placed into liquidation due to substantial debts. The liquidators opted to continue trading the business to sell it as a going concern rather than liquidating immediately. They successfully sold Intramed for R154,300,000, significantly above previous offers.

The Master reduced the liquidators' initial fee claim citing reasons such as the nature of Intramed being well run and profitable, leading to fewer onerous duties during its liquidation process ([para 1–10]). The appellants sought a review of this reduction, contending they were entitled to remuneration according to the prescribed tariff in the Companies Act 51 of 1973 ([paras 4–5]).

I – Issues

The central issue for the court was the interpretation and application of sections 384(1) and (2) of the Companies Act concerning the Master’s discretion to reduce or increase a liquidator’s remuneration based on the existence of “good cause” ([para 1]). Additionally, there was the legal question of the extent of the court’s power to review the Master’s decision under the provisions of the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936, especially with respect to what constitutes reasonable remuneration and the standards for any judicial review ([para 1]).

R – Rules

The main statutory provisions under consideration were:

  • Section 384(1) and (2) of the Companies Act 51 of 1973: This section stipulates that a liquidator is entitled to reasonable remuneration that can be taxed by the Master according to a prescribed tariff. The Master is given the discretion to increase or reduce this remuneration if there is “good cause”.
  • Section 151 of the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936: This section allows individuals aggrieved by a decision of the Master to seek review of the decision in court, indicating the court’s broad powers to engage in the matter not just as a review but also as an appeal ([para 22]).

A – Application

In applying these rules, the court evaluated whether the Master had appropriately exercised the discretion afforded to him under section 384(2). The Court noted that while the liquidators were entitled to fees calculated according to the tariff, the Master had the authority to adjust remuneration based on the actual circumstances of the administration of the estate ([paras 17–21]).

The appellants argued that the Master improperly based his decision on considerations of time spent on tasks, asserting that their remuneration should strictly follow the tariff provisions ([para 38]). However, the court determined that the Master was justified in considering various factors, including the complexity of the case, the responsibilities involved, and the nature of the work undertaken. The Master’s opinion regarding “good cause” had to be rationally connected to the principles underlying remuneration for services rendered ([paras 30–31]).

C – Conclusion

The court ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the Master’s decision to set the remuneration at R3,250,000. The court held that the Master had exercised his discretion properly, considering all relevant factors and concluding that the prescribed tariff amounted to excessive remuneration given the circumstances of the liquidation ([para 43]). Furthermore, the court ruled that the appellants were acting in their personal capacities, as the review was primarily for their benefit rather than for the estate’s creditors, leading to an imposition of personal costs upon them.

This case underscores the Master’s discretion in adjusting liquidators’ fees based on the actual services rendered, the complexity of the case, and the necessity for judicial review to critically assess the reasonableness of such remuneration without strictly adhering to the prescribed tariff. The implications of this decision are significant for future liquidators, as they highlight the need to justify remuneration claims that align with the realities of the liquidation process ([para 43]).

References:

  • Companies Act 51 of 1973, s 384
  • Insolvency Act 24 of 1936, s 151

Citation: Brian Basil Nel NO & Michael De Villiers NO v The Master of the High Court Eastern Cape & Others, Case No: A9/03 (SCA, 1 April 2004).
