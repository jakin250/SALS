Supreme Court of Appeal

Selota v South African Legal Practice Council ([2021] ZASCA 169)

Before the primary hearing set for 21 November 2019, Selota filed an urgent application seeking a declaratory order for entitlement to a Fidelity Fund certificate for 2019.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 169)

F - Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 2–4])

The case of Selota v South African Legal Practice Council revolves around the appellant, Mr. Mamolatelo Alfred Selota, who was admitted to the legal profession as an attorney on 1 August 2008. Following his admission, he practiced law independently under the name MA Selota Attorneys. In June 2018, the South African Legal Practice Council (the SALPC) initiated action against him, seeking removal from the roll of attorneys based on various allegations, including his operation without a required Fidelity Fund certificate, as stipulated under sections 84(1) and (2) of the Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014 (LPA). The SALPC's concerns encompassed observed trust fund deficits and allegations of misconduct regarding client engagement ([para 2]).

Before the primary hearing set for 21 November 2019, Selota filed an urgent application seeking a declaratory order for entitlement to a Fidelity Fund certificate for 2019. This urgent application was dismissed on 9 March 2020 by Van Olst AJ, who granted leave to appeal. Unfortunately, Selota failed to submit his heads of argument on time, leading to his appeal lapsing under Rule 10(2A). Consequently, he sought condonation for the late filing and reinstatement of the appeal ([paras 2–3]).

In parallel, on 7 February 2020, Selota was suspended from legal practice by the High Court due to his lack of a Fidelity Fund certificate. Both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) denied his applications for leave to appeal against the suspension. Various further attempts by Selota to contest this suspension were also unsuccessful ([para 4]).

I - Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 6–7])

The primary legal question before the SCA focused on whether the appeal concerning Selota's entitlement to a Fidelity Fund certificate would have any practical effect or result. Section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 permits dismissal of appeals that do not yield practical effects. Central to the SCA's consideration was whether a favorable decision in this appeal might influence the existing suspension imposed on Selota ([paras 6–7]).

R - Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 6–8])

The court drew upon section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which states that an appeal may be dismissed if it will have no practical effect. The SCA referenced Madibeng Local Municipality v DDP Valuers and Another ([2020] ZASCA 70) to illustrate the importance of this provision in managing the court's docket and ensuring that only deserving matters are considered ([para 6]).

Furthermore, the provisions of sections 84 and 85 of the LPA were critical to the decision, particularly concerning the precondition of compliance with Chapter 7 regarding trust monies before the issuance of a Fidelity Fund certificate ([para 5]).

A - Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 7–8])

Upon analyzing the situation, the SCA concluded that Selota's appeal lacked practical significance. His argument that a favorable ruling on the Fidelity Fund certificate could potentially alter the grounds for his suspension was deemed untenable. The court emphasized that regardless of the appeal's outcome, Selota's previous practice without a Fidelity Fund certificate remained unchanged and was a substantial foundation for the suspension order ([paras 7–8]).

The SCA highlighted that the act of declaring Selota entitled to a Fidelity Fund certificate would not address the fundamental issue leading to his suspension; hence, it would serve no useful purpose. Consequently, the court found that reinstating the appeal would be a futile exercise, validating its decision to dismiss the application for condonation and strike the main matter from the roll ([para 8]).

C - Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 4–8])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the application for condonation and reinstatement of the appeal was dismissed, along with the main matter being struck from the roll with costs. The significant takeaway from the judgment was the SCA’s determination that appeals lacking practical benefits would be dismissed to conserve judicial resources and maintain procedural efficiency within the court system ([para 4]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the necessity for legal practitioners to adhere strictly to administrative and statutory requirements regarding Fidelity Fund certificates, particularly given their critical role in the regulation of attorney practice in South Africa. The decision also serves as a cautionary reminder on the importance of timely submissions and adherence to procedural rules within appellate jurisdictions ([para 8]).

This case note effectively encapsulates the procedural history, issues, rules, and outcomes of Selota v SALPC, serving as a comprehensive academic and legal resource.
