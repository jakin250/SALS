Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2000

## Case Note: Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of South Africa v President of the Republic of South Africa (CCT 31/99)

### FIRAC Method Case Note

#### F – Facts

The case arises from an application initiated in the Transvaal High Court concerning the validity of Proclamation R49, issued by the President of South Africa on April 30, 1999, which purported to bring the South African Medicines and Medical Devices Regulatory Authority Act (Act 132 of 1998) into force ([1]–[4]). This Act repealed significant provisions of the Medicines and Related Substances Control Act No. 101 of 1965, alongside amending the Fertilizers, Farm Feeds, Agricultural Remedies and Stock Remedies Act No. 36 of 1947 ([2]).

Prior to the Act's promulgation, there existed a regulatory framework for medications and agricultural products under the aforementioned acts. The new regulatory authority intended to ensure the continued oversight of medicines, medical devices, and related substances through a detailed scheduling system ([3]–[4]). 

Following the issuance of the Proclamation, the medical and pharmaceutical community raised concerns regarding the Act's readiness to take effect due to the absence of necessary schedules and regulations essential to its operation ([5]–[7]). A declaration was sought by the President and the other applicants, including various health and agricultural authorities, to set aside the Proclamation and related Government Notice R567, as they argued the regulatory framework was incomplete, rendering the Proclamation invalid ([8]).

The High Court initially dismissed the application, asserting the President acted within his powers and in good faith, despite acting on incorrect advice ([9]). However, upon appeal by the Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association and the Crop Protection and Animal Health Association, the Full Bench ruled the Proclamation null and void, stating that the President’s authority must be executed within statutory limits and that the necessary steps to operationalize the Act had not been taken prior to its implementation ([11]–[11]). The matter was thus referred to the Constitutional Court for confirmation of the Full Bench's decision concerning constitutional validity ([14]).

#### I – Issues

The central legal question before the Constitutional Court was whether the court possesses jurisdiction to review the President's decision to promulgate an Act of Parliament and whether such a decision can be characterized as having constitutional implications, specifically whether it constituted a violation of the principle of legality and relevant restrictions imposed upon public officials ([1], [14], [20]).

Another pertinent issue arose around the procedural legitimacy of the requirements necessary for the Act's implementation, and whether the absence of these requirements invalidated the President's Proclamation ([59], [90]). Additionally, the court needed to resolve whether the President's decision was justiciable, considering it falls under the executive functions where judicial oversight is traditionally limited ([70]).

#### R – Rules

The court grounded its application of law in the principles of legality as enshrined in Section 2 of the South African Constitution, which establishes it as the supreme law of the Republic, asserting that any conduct inconsistent with it is invalid ([19]). Furthermore, relevant precedent such as **Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd & Others v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council** highlighted that public power must be exercised within the limits conferred by law and that this principle remains part of the constitutional fabric post the enactment of the new Constitution ([17]).

Moreover, the court invoked Section 172(2)(a) of the Constitution, which states that any order concerning the constitutional validity of an Act of Parliament requires confirmation by the Constitutional Court to have effect ([14], [56]). Judicial review mechanisms also reflect a historical context in South African law, grounded in common law principles that uphold rationality, good faith, and adherence to statutory provisions and procedural fairness ([84]).

#### A – Application

In its deliberation, the Court highlighted how the President misinterpreted his powers under Section 55 of the Act when he brought it into force without the requisite regulatory framework in place ([13], [58]). The absence of the essential schedules, which governed the manufacture and sale of medicines, was seen as fundamental to the operation of the Act. Without these schedules being established prior to the Proclamation, the Act’s enforcement was rendered impractical and irrational by virtue of the regulatory vacuum it created ([88], [90]).

Moreover, the Court rejected the argument that a good faith mistake by the President absolved the decision from review. It asserted that rationality is a minimum threshold for all exercises of public power, and the President's action, therefore, failed this standard despite his good intentions ([90]). The court emphasized that the principles of public power and legality dictate that all governmental actions must be justifiable and conducive to the broader legislative objectives ([86]).

Consequently, the Full Bench’s ruling was confirmed as a valid interpretation of the law. It underscored that since the Act was not validly put into effect, the previous legislation remained operative until proper measures were established ([95]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the Full Bench's order, thereby holding that the Proclamation was null and void due to the President's failure to adhere to statutory constraints regarding the effective implementation of the Act ([95]). This case reinforces significant principles of legality and the rule of law entrenched within the South African constitutional framework, asserting that even the highest offices of government are not above the law. Furthermore, it indicates the judiciary's role in ensuring that executive actions maintain compliance with constitutional mandates. This judgment serves as a critical affirmation of the delicate balance between legislative authority and executive discretion, emphasizing the judiciary's duty to oversee lawful governance and maintain the integrity of constitutional mechanisms.
