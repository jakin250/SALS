# FIRAC Case Note: The Director-General, Department of Home Affairs and Another v Islam and Others (459/2017) [2018] ZASCA 48

## F – Facts

The case involved Mr. Nurul Islam, a Bangladeshi citizen, who held a spousal visa issued by the South African Department of Home Affairs (DHA) due to his marriage to South African citizen Wageema Islam ([1]–[2]). The controversy began when, upon re-entry into South Africa via Cape Town International Airport on June 30, 2016, Mr. Islam presented immigration officials with a visa that was later found to be fraudulent ([6]–[7]). This action led to his immediate refusal of entry based on section 29(1)(f) of the Immigration Act 13 of 2002, which deems individuals in possession of fraudulent documentation as prohibited persons ([7]). 

Mrs. Wageema Islam subsequently initiated an urgent application to the Western Cape High Court, seeking an order that would permit Mr. Islam's entry into South Africa pending the Minister of Home Affairs' review of that decision, or pending judicial review if necessary ([2]–[3]). The High Court initially ruled in favor of the Islams but later rescinded its order after determining that the respondents had failed to disclose the reason for the refusal of entry ([8]–[9]). Undeterred, the Islams pursued a subsequent application resulting in the appeal in question.

## I – Issues

The appeal raised two significant legal questions:

1. **Appealability of the High Court’s Interim Interdict**: Can the interim interdict granted by the High Court, which allowed for Mr. Islam's entry pending a ministerial review, be appealed? ([9]–[10])
   
2. **Merits of the Interim Interdict**: Did the Islams establish sufficient grounds (i.e., a prima facie right, apprehension of irreparable harm, balance of convenience, and no alternative remedy) for the granting of the interdict? ([10]–[14])

## R – Rules

The legal framework governing the case is predominantly under the **Immigration Act 13 of 2002**. Particularly relevant is section 29(1)(f), which categorizes individuals possessing fraudulent visas as prohibited persons, thus barring them from entry into South Africa ([7]). Additionally, the requirements for granting an interim interdict were highlighted, necessitating that the applicant demonstrates:

- A prima facie right (even if open to doubt),
- A reasonable apprehension of irreparable harm,
- The balance of convenience favors the interdict,
- No other adequate remedy is available ([14]).

The case also examined principles relating to **separation of powers**, emphasizing judicial restraint in interventions against executive authority, particularly concerning issues of immigration and national security ([20]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the High Court’s interim interdict was indeed appealable as it met all traditional requirements set forth in common law jurisdiction, including being final in effect and definitive of the parties’ rights. The interdict compelling Mr. Islam's admission effectively displaced DHA's statutory powers regarding immigration control, thus constituting a direct contravention of the Immigration Act ([10]–[13]).

Examining the merits, the Court ruled that the Islams had failed to establish a prima facie right. The High Court’s decision relied too heavily on the assumption that the spousal visa remained valid without acknowledgment of the misrepresented document presented during entry. The absence of a responding affidavit from DHA, which clarified the fraudulent nature of Mr. Islam's visa, further challenged the weight of the Islams' claims ([15]–[17]). The Court noted that Mr. Islam’s possession of a fraudulent visa stripped him of an entitlement to entry into the Republic under the Immigration Act, underscoring that the granting of the interdict could inadvertently produce legal consequences of his illegal status ([18]).

Moreover, the Court remarked that the High Court had failed to consider the implications of the order on the separation of powers doctrine. The interdict threatened to undermine the DHA's regulatory framework, complicating immigration enforcement ([20]–[21]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellants, effectively setting aside the High Court's order and dismissing the application with no order as to costs ([22]). The decision reiterated the necessity of adhering to statutory requirements when granting interim relief, particularly against the exercise of statutory powers. It emphasized that the integrity of immigration laws and border security could not be compromised by judicial overreach. The ruling not only clarifies the requirements for interim interdicts against public authorities but also reinforces the fundamental principle of separation of powers in matters where the state is tasked with safeguarding national interests. 

This judgment serves as a critical reminder that courts must operate within their bounds, ensuring that executive functions are respected unless constitutional breaches demand intervention.
