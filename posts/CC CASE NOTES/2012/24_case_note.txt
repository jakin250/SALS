Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

### FIRAC Case Note: Democratic Alliance v President of the Republic of South Africa

#### F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–4])
This case examines the constitutionality of the appointment of Mr. Menzi Simelane as the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) by the President of the Republic of South Africa. The Democratic Alliance (the applicant) sought confirmation of an invalidity ruling made by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). The SCA found that the appointment process and the decision itself were irrational and thus invalid ([para 1]). 

The facts leading to the decision involved several key events. Mr. Simelane, while serving as Director-General in the Department of Justice, was involved in the controversial suspension of his predecessor, Mr. Vusi Pikoli, triggering a commission of inquiry led by Dr. Frene Ginwala into Pikoli’s fitness for office. The inquiry revealed serious criticisms of Simelane’s conduct and credibility ([paras 4a–4l]). Subsequent to the inquiry, despite recommendations for disciplinary action against Simelane, the President appointed him as NDPP. The appointment occurred shortly after the Minister of Justice, Mr. Jeff Radebe, opted to disregard the Public Service Commission's disciplinary recommendations, sparking allegations of improper interference ([paras 4f–4i]).

#### I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 12-12])
The primary issues identified by the Court include:
a. Whether the requirement that the NDPP be a "fit and proper person" constitutes an objective jurisdictional fact that must be met for valid appointment.
b. The implications of rationality, particularly the relationship between reasonableness and rationality in both the decision-making process and the decision itself.
c. The rationality of the President's appointment decision, including whether relevant factors (like the Ginwala Commission findings) were ignored, adversely affecting the rational basis for the appointment.
d. Implications arising if the appointment is deemed irrational, specifically regarding Simelane’s qualifications as a "fit and proper" person and any ulterior motives behind the appointment ([para 12]).

#### R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 13-13])
The appointment of the NDPP is governed by section 179 of the South African Constitution, which imposes specific qualifications for the role, including a mandate for the individual to be "fit and proper" ([para 13a]). The National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998 outlines further specific qualifications, reiterating the importance of integrity and experience in the appointment of a National Director ([para 13c]).

The Court referenced past decisions regarding rationality, such as *Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism* ([para 29]), and established principles from the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) whereby decisions must be rationally related to the objective under which the power is granted ([para 27]).

#### A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 20–90])
The Court noted that the SCA had deemed the President’s actions irrational for various reasons, including the premature judgment regarding Simelane’s suitability prior to considering critical evidence about his conduct. The Court stressed that deciding on a candidate’s fitness for office must not only involve subjective judgments but also objective evaluations of qualifications. Thus, the requirement of being fit and proper is an objective criterion that needed to be met ([paras 14–26]).

In evaluating the rationality of the President’s decision, the Court held that several relevant factors were ignored, particularly the Ginwala Commission’s critical findings regarding Simelane’s credibility. Not taking into account these assessments was inconsistent with the purpose of ensuring an adequately qualified NDPP, thereby rendering the appointment irrational ([paras 50–69]).

The Minister's justifications for disregarding the evidence were extensively critiqued as inadequate. The lack of a coherent and rational basis for dismissing the implications of the inquiry ultimately facilitated the Court’s conclusion that the appointment lacked a rational connection to its purpose, as the decision made would not withstand scrutiny under constitutional norms governing executive power ([paras 79–90]).

#### C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 91–95])
The Constitutional Court confirmed the SCA's order declaring the appointment of Mr. Simelane as NDPP as invalid due to irrationality in both process and substance. The decision underscored the necessity for a rational link between the criteria for appointment and the individual designated to carry out such a pivotal role in the justice system ([paras 91-92]).

The Court recognized that while Simelane’s appointment was invalid, this did not affect the legality of decisions made during his tenure as NDPP, maintaining the functionality of the prosecuting authority unaffected from the implications of the invalidity ruling ([para 93]). The implications of this judgment extend beyond the individual appointment, reinforcing the judicial principle that actions of the executive must remain accountable to constitutional standards to uphold democratic values such as integrity, credibility, and the rule of law. 

In summary, the case reaffirmed the scrutiny to which executive actions are to be subjected under the rationality principle, reinforcing checks and balances essential to democratic governance in South Africa.
