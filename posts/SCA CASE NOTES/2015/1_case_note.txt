Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Stupel & Berman Inc v Rodel Financial Services (Pty) Ltd - FIRAC Method

**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Neutral Citation:** Stupel & Berman v Rodel Financial Services (1075/2013) [2015] ZASCA 1 (27 February 2015)  
**Judges:** Brand, Mhlantla, Willis JJA and Fourie and Gorven AJJA  
**Heard:** 17 February 2015  
**Delivered:** 27 February 2015  
**Order:** Appeal upheld with costs.

## F – Facts

This case emerged from a dispute between Stupel & Berman Incorporated (the Appellant) and Rodel Financial Services (Pty) Ltd (the Respondent) regarding an undertaking by the attorneys as conveyancers to pay proceeds from the sale of a property ([paras 1–2]). 

The facts are complex, originating from a sale by Amber Falcon Properties 3 (Pty) Ltd (Amber Falcon) to Cross Atlantic Properties 186 (Pty) Ltd (Cross Atlantic) at an auction for R7.2 million, while the property was encumbered by a bond to Mercantile Bank Ltd of R5 million ([para 2]). The Appellant acted as conveyancers in the transfer, and Rodel had extended bridging finance to Amber Falcon through two discounting agreements that stipulated that the proceeds of the sale would be paid to Rodel ([paras 2–3]).

On 22 October 2010, Amber Falcon cancelled the sale agreement and communicated this to Rodel, indicating that the property would be remarketed at a higher price ([para 4]). Subsequently, Rodel attempted to cancel the discounting agreements on 4 February 2011 via a letter that was ambiguous in its reference to which agreements were being cancelled ([paras 6–7]). 

On 21 February 2011, following instructions from Amber Falcon, Stupel & Berman withdrew their undertakings to Rodel, asserting that developments had negated their obligation to pay the proceeds ([paras 8–9]). Rodel ultimately sought recourse from Stupel & Berman after unsuccessful claims against Amber Falcon and its surety ([para 9]).

## I – Issues

The key legal questions before the Court were:
1. Whether Stupel & Berman were entitled to withdraw their undertaking at the instruction of Amber Falcon ([para 12]).
2. Whether Rodel had a valid claim against Stupel & Berman despite the cancellation of the discounting agreements ([para 11]).
3. The nature of the undertaking and if Rodel was considered an *adjectus solutionis causa* under the law of obligations ([paras 14–15]).

## R – Rules

The Court cited relevant principles from contract and agency law, particularly concerning the nature of undertakings by agents ([para 17]). The concept of *adjectus solutionis causa* from Roman law was examined, with reference to Pothier’s definition and its interpretation in cases such as *Norman Kennedy v Norman Kennedy Ltd* ([para 14]). 

The legal authority regarding the withdrawal of agency mandates and the nature of irrevocable instructions was also discussed, with the Court acknowledging the exceptions to this rule, particularly in contexts involving ‘interests’ ([paras 17–18]). The principle drawn from cases such as *Consolidated Frame Cotton Corporation Ltd v Sithole* was significant in determining the extent of Stupel & Berman’s obligations ([para 17]).

## A – Application

The Court analyzed the applicable legal rules against the facts presented. It found that Rodel could not be considered an *adjectus solutionis causa*, thus leading to the conclusion that the relationship between the parties did not bind Stupel & Berman to the irrevocable instructions claim ([paras 15–19]).

The Court addressed Stupel & Berman's status as an agent acting under mandate. The undertaking to pay Rodel was made in the context of their role as a conveyancer, indicating that they were not personally liable, and that the instructions to pay could be legitimately revoked by Amber Falcon ([paras 12, 19]). 

It evaluated the competing contentions regarding the validity of the cancellation of the discounting agreements, concluding that Rodel’s authorization to collect payment was extinguished upon the cancellation ([para 21]). Given the findings that Amber Falcon was entitled to terminate Stupel & Berman’s mandate, it followed that Stupel & Berman were right to withdraw the undertaking. 

Thus, the appeal was upheld, reinforcing the principle that agency instructions, especially when properly mandated, can be revoked, nullifying the obligations of the agent toward third parties ([para 21]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held in favor of Stupel & Berman, concluding that the undertakings made to Rodel could be revoked due to the cancellation of the underlying discounting agreements by Amber Falcon. Consequently, Rodel's claim against the Appellant was dismissed with costs, including the costs associated with employing two counsel.

This decision significantly clarifies the law surrounding the obligations of agents in the context of third-party interests and reaffirms the principle that a principal can revoke instructions to an agent, fundamentally impacting the enforceability of such undertakings ([paras 21]). The ruling also emphasizes the necessity for clear communication and unambiguous agreements to prevent future disputes in financial and property transactions in South Africa.
