Constitutional Court

Skhumbuzo Jerome Mthembu v The State

The applicant, Skhumbuzo Jerome Mthembu, sought leave to appeal against his convictions for armed robbery, illegal possession of firearms, and illegal possession of ammunition, which resulted in an effective sentence of 15 years’ imprisonment.

Constitutional Court 2010 [2010] ZACC 8 Decided on: 25 March 2010. ## F – Fa

CCT 115/09
[2010] ZACC 8
Decided on: 25 March 2010.

F – Facts

The applicant, Skhumbuzo Jerome Mthembu, sought leave to appeal against his convictions for armed robbery, illegal possession of firearms, and illegal possession of ammunition, which resulted in an effective sentence of 15 years’ imprisonment. Mthembu was initially convicted on 3 October 2001 in the Vereeniging Regional Court. His convictions were confirmed and the sentence upheld by the North Gauteng High Court on 7 October 2002, and subsequent attempts to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were rejected on 25 February 2003. Despite being out on bail throughout the appeal process, Mthembu failed to report to the Clerk of the Court to commence his sentence after his appeals were denied. It was not until he was arrested at his home on 3 April 2009, over six years later, that he began serving his sentence ([paras 1–4]).

Upon imprisonment, Mthembu launched a new application to the Constitutional Court, claiming that his arrest for the sentence violated his right to freedom and security of the person under section 12 of the South African Constitution. He argued that at 60 years old, he should not be expected to serve a sentence that could have been completed much earlier, and sought a suspended or non-custodial term ([paras 4–5]).

I – Issues

The core legal issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the applicant's arrest and subsequent service of a sentence after a delay of more than six years constituted an infringement of his constitutional right to freedom and security of the person. In particular, the Court considered the implications of this delay in the context of the criminal justice system and the applicant’s obligations while out on bail ([paras 7–9]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal authority in this case included section 12 of the Constitution of South Africa, which articulates the right to freedom and security of the person. This section ensures that individuals are not deprived of their freedom arbitrarily or without just cause. The applicant’s claims were evaluated against established precedents concerning the obligations of convicted individuals to report for sentencing and the duty of courts and prosecution services to enforce sentences promptly ([paras 1, 5]).

Additionally, case law regarding the principles of bail and the accountability of judicial and prosecutorial services in ensuring that sentences are executed in a timely manner was also pertinent ([paras 6, 8]).

A – Application

The Court found that Mthembu’s constitutional claim was fundamentally flawed. The applicant, despite being educated and legally represented, was under an obligation to ascertain the outcome of his appeals and to ensure compliance with court orders. The court noted that any claimed ignorance regarding the outcome of his appeals was implausible given his background and the available judicial resources.

The judgment emphasized the responsibilities of the applicant while on bail, which included the requirement to monitor the status of his appeals actively ([paras 8–9]). While acknowledging the broader systemic issues identified in the delay of arrest and execution of sentence—highlighting gaps in the efficiency of the National Prosecuting Authority, court administration, and the South African Police Service—the Court maintained that this did not excuse Mthembu’s own obligations and failures ([paras 10–11]).

The Court concluded that delays and inefficiencies in the justice system cannot automatically translate into violations of constitutional rights, particularly when the convicted individuals fail to act in accordance with their legal obligations ([paras 12–13]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the application for leave to appeal was dismissed due to the lack of prospects for success. The Court found that Mthembu’s argument did not sufficiently substantiate a violation of his constitutional rights, particularly given his own failure to adhere to legal processes during the extended period following his conviction ([paras 14–15]).

The decision also reinforced the importance of the expeditious execution of sentences in maintaining the integrity of the criminal justice system, while recognizing the systemic issues that may contribute to delays. The Court’s order was that Mthembu’s appeal would not proceed, underscoring that adherence to judicial processes is paramount for both defendants and the legal system as a whole ([paras 15–16]).

The implications of this case underline the responsibilities of individuals in custody and the importance of system accountability in ensuring timely justice, while also serving as a cautionary note on the intersection of individual rights and judicial authority in South Africa.
