Supreme Court of Appeal

THE SUPREME CO

The appellant initiated legal proceedings against the respondent in the Welkom magistrate's court, alleging that the respondent's negligence caused the collision and subsequent damages to his vehicle.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1998

F – Facts

On 11 August 1991 at approximately 11:00 AM, a motor collision occurred between the vehicles of Johannes Molefe (the appellant) and Freddy Mahaeng (the respondent) on Mothusi Road in Thabong, Welkom. The respondent's vehicle unexpectedly veered onto the incorrect side of the roadway, resulting in a collision with the appellant's car, and subsequently, a second collision with a vehicle operated by Mr. Jacob Khatiti, a traffic officer ([paras 2–3]).

The appellant initiated legal proceedings against the respondent in the Welkom magistrate's court, alleging that the respondent's negligence caused the collision and subsequent damages to his vehicle. In his defense, the respondent denied negligence, claiming he experienced a sudden, unforeseen blackout (referred to colloquially as "breinfloute") which rendered him unable to control his vehicle. Although he initially claimed that the appellant's negligence contributed to the collision, this assertion was not pursued further ([paras 3–4]).

During the trial concerning negligence, the magistrate believed the respondent had to establish his defense of automatism, ultimately rejecting the respondent's account and ruling in favor of the appellant. The case then proceeded to the Orange Free State Provincial Division, where the decision was reversed. The appellate court found that the evidence supported the respondent's claim that he suffered a blackout, leading to the conclusion that the collision was not due to his negligence ([paras 4–5]).

Since the particulars of the case and the activities leading to the blackout were critical, it was noted that the respondent had fallen earlier in the day at a minimarket, striking the back of his head, which purportedly contributed to the blackout he later experienced while driving ([paras 5–6]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues in this case pertain to:

  1. Negligence: Was the respondent liable for the damages caused to the appellant due to his inability to maintain control of his vehicle?
  2. Burden of Proof: Did the respondent successfully demonstrate that he suffered an involuntary blackout that absolved him of negligence?
  3. Automatism Defense: How should the court interpret the legal standards regarding automatism in this context, particularly concerning the evidentiary burden imposed on the respondent? ([paras 12–14]).

R – Rules

The legal framework underpinning this case includes principles from delictual liability which establish that a defendant's involuntary actions, such as those resulting from automatism, do not constitute delictual liability ([para 13]).

Notably, reliance on previous case law, such as The Government v Marine and Trade Insurance Co Ltd 1973 (3) SA 797, articulated by James JP, indicated that when automatism is invoked as a defense, it is the defendant's responsibility to demonstrate this condition on a balance of probabilities ([para 13]).

In addition, the court considered distinctions in the burden of proof depending on whether an automatism arises from a mental disorder or a non-psychological cause ([para 14]).

The magistrate and the appellate court referenced foundational principles from cases such as Arthur v Bezuidenhout and Mieny 1962 (2) SA 566, noting that in matters concerning motor vehicle accidents, the plaintiff bears the burden to prove both the defendant's negligence and the causative link ([paras 15–16]).

A – Application

The court's analysis began with evaluating the circumstances surrounding the respondent's blackout. The evidence suggesting that the respondent experienced a black-out was weighed against the requirements of proving negligence. The Provincial Division found that, as a matter of probability, the respondent had lost consciousness while driving, and therefore, he could not be held liable for the collision ([paras 12–18]).

Despite the evidence alluding to a prima facie inference of negligence—the respondent's vehicle suddenly moving onto the incorrect side of the road—this did not automatically translate into liability. The court emphasized the need to ascertain whether the appellant proved, on a preponderance of probabilities, that the respondent's actions were voluntary. The presence of corroborating evidence—such as medical evaluations indicating a possible blackout linked to the head injury sustained earlier—supported the respondent's claim ([paras 10–17]).

The magistrate's discounting of the testimony from Khatiti was critical; however, the appellate court highlighted flaws in this reasoning, noting that Khatiti's observations regarding the respondent's state post-collision should have been given more weight. The decision ultimately hinged on the fact that the evidence demonstrated a reasonable doubt regarding the respondent's negligence, compelling dismissal of the appellant's claim ([paras 10–18]).

C – Conclusion

The conclusion reached by the court was to dismiss the appeal, affirming the Provincial Division's judgment that the collision was not due to the respondent’s negligence, as he had suffered a sudden blackout, rendering him incapable of controlling his vehicle at the time of the incident. Consequently, the appellant failed to fulfill the burden of proving negligence as required in such cases ([paras 18–19]). This case underscores the complexity of establishing liability in instances of automatism, reinforcing the necessity of rigorous standards of proof and the courts' careful consideration of the evidentiary nuances surrounding such defenses. As a result, the ruling remains significant in clarifying the intersection of automatism and negligence within motor vehicle accident legislation.
