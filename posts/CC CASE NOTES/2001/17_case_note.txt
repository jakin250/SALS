Source Folder: 2001

# FIRAC Case Notes: The State v Russell Mamabolo (CCT 44/00)

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1–4])
The case of *The State v Russell Mamabolo* concerns a contempt of court conviction against Russell Mamabolo, the spokesperson for the Department of Correctional Services (DCS) in South Africa. The facts unravel in the context of a criminal proceeding involving Eugene Terre Blanche, the leader of the Afrikaner Weerstandsbeweging, who was sentenced for attempted murder and assault. Following his conviction, Terre Blanche applied for bail pending appeal, which was granted by Judge Johan Els of the Pretoria High Court. The DCS subsequently issued a media release criticizing the judge's decision, arguing that Terre Blanche was not eligible for bail due to the nature of his conviction and ongoing appeal. Mamabolo, as the spokesperson, publicly expressed the view that the judge had erred.

These statements resulted in the judge initiating contempt proceedings against Mamabolo. The judge summoned Mamabolo and the DCS commissioner to court to explain their comments, which were interpreted as suggesting that the judge made a mistake and contributed to public confusion about the case ([4]). The High Court, having heard the matter, sentenced Mamabolo to a fine of R2,000 or six months imprisonment, finding him guilty of scandalising the court ([5]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([1–3])
The case raised two primary constitutional issues:
1. Whether the law pertaining to contempt of court, specifically the offence of scandalising the court, unjustifiably limited the right to freedom of expression laid out in the Constitution ([1–4]).
2. Whether the summary procedure by which the judge dealt with the alleged contempt violated the right to a fair trial, as guaranteed by the Constitution ([1–4]).

An additional ancillary issue concerned the nature and binding force of judicial orders and the obligation of state organs to assist in upholding court orders ([1]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([1–4])
The judges relied upon several constitutional provisions:
- **Section 16** of the Constitution guarantees the right to freedom of expression, which includes freedom of the press and other media ([1]).
- **Section 35(3)** outlines rights associated with fair trial protections, including the rights to be informed of charges, to have a public trial, and other rights that mark a fair judicial process ([5]).
- **Section 165** delineates the concept of judicial authority as independent, subject only to the Constitution and the law, emphasizing that no organ of state may interfere with the functioning of the courts ([1]).

Significant judicial precedent was drawn from both South African Criminal Law and comparative international law concerning contempt proceedings, including the definition of contempt as both a public injury and the notion that its purpose is to protect the integrity of the judiciary rather than the feelings of individual judges ([12][14]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([10–60])
In determining whether Mamabolo’s conviction for scandalising the court violated his right to free expression, the court carried out a nuanced assessment of the public interest in maintaining judicial integrity versus the fundamental right to criticize public officials, including judges. The court acknowledged that any limitation on the right to freedom of expression must meet the requirements of *justifiability* and reasonableness as stated in Section 36 of the Constitution. It found that the law concerning contempt only aims at protecting the public's perception of judicial integrity, and thus should be applied sparingly in alignment with the freedoms guaranteed under the Bill of Rights ([23][48]).

On the procedural safeguards afforded under Section 35(3) of the Constitution, the court highlighted substantial flaws in the summary procedure employed—specifically, the absence of an adversarial system where the accused could adequately defend themselves ([53–56]). It concluded that the procedure bore insufficient protections and led to an unjustifiable infringement on Mamabolo's fair trial rights ([56]).

Consequently, the court noted that the words spoken by Mamabolo, while potentially critical, did not rise to the constitutional threshold required for a finding of contempt. His statements reflected an opinion rather than definitive fact, and thus, did not impair the dignity of the judge or the judiciary ([60]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([66–78])
The Constitutional Court of South Africa ultimately held that the conviction was unconstitutional both substantively and procedurally, leading to the immediate annulment of Mamabolo's conviction and sentence. The court emphasized that while judges must maintain their dignity, this should not impinge upon the public's right to express criticism of judicial decisions that are perceived as erroneous.

The ratio decidendi rests on the court’s determination that the offence of scandalising the court, while legally valid, must be scrutinized critically in light of constitutional freedoms. Furthermore, the court underscored the importance of transparent and rigorous debate regarding the judiciary as essential in a healthy democracy. It highlighted that a critical public discourse could strengthen, rather than diminish, the judiciary's stature.

This ruling has important implications for freedom of expression in the context of judicial accountability, possibly reforming how contempt laws are applied in the future and reinforcing the principle that robust criticism should coexist with judicial dignity, rather than be quenched under the specter of contempt charges. The court thus acknowledged the necessity of balancing the administration of justice with the democratic ideals enshrined in the Constitution ([66–78]).
