Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Neil Harvey & Associates (Pty) Ltd v Medscheme Holdings (Pty) Ltd (621/10) [2011] ZASCA 75

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

In this case, the appellant, Neil Harvey & Associates (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "NHA"), was involved in arbitration against Medscheme Holdings (Pty) Ltd and several of its executives concerning allegations of misappropriation and unfair competition related to software developed by NHA ([para 1]). The arbitration proceedings were overseen by Adv T W Beckerling, who was later the subject of a removal application initiated by the respondents, who contended that there was good cause for the arbitrator's removal under section 13(2)(a) of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965 ([para 2]).

The respondents sought to remove the arbitrator based on allegations of a gross irregularity and reasonable apprehension of bias, alleging that the arbitrator had engaged in discussions with NHA, excluding the respondents ([para 6]). A pivotal moment occurred during a meeting on February 26, 2009, concerning the resolution of disputes under a Confidentiality Agreement between NHA and a third party, ITQ Business Solutions (Pty) Ltd ([para 10]).

NHA contended that the arbitrator’s involvement in the ITQ matter was irrelevant to the Medscheme arbitration ([para 11]), while respondents argued that allowing the arbitrator to preside over both matters breached the principles of an adversarial process ([para 12]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11–18])

The case presented several legal questions surrounding the alleged grounds for the removal of the arbitrator. The primary issues for determination included:

1. Whether the actions of the arbitrator constituted a gross irregularity that warranted removal under section 13(2)(a) of the Arbitration Act ([para 6]).
2. Whether there was a reasonable apprehension of bias based on the arbitrator's conduct during the meeting of February 26 ([para 18]).

Moreover, the applicability of procedural rules regarding communication with arbitrators and the rights of parties in an adversarial process were central to the respondents’ arguments ([para 12]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 12–21])

The court addressed various legal principles and precedents relevant to arbitration, including procedural fairness and the necessity of avoiding actions that could lead to an apprehension of bias. The case of **Lufuno Mphaphuli & Associates (Pty) Ltd v Andrews & Another** 2009 (4) SA 529 (CC) was cited to establish the need for transparency and fairness in arbitration proceedings ([para 13]). 

In addition, the general principle that nothing should be conducted "inaudita altera parte"—without the knowledge of a party—was underscored through comparisons with established judicial principles in arbitration ([para 13]). The test for reasonable apprehension of bias was articulated in **President of the RSA v South African Rugby Football Union** 1999 (4) SA 147 (CC), which required an objective assessment of whether a reasonable person would perceive a lack of impartiality ([para 18]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 17–21])

Upon reviewing the events at the meeting of February 26, the court found that there were no substantive rulings or actions taken that adversely affected the respondents’ interests. The arbitrator’s role was described as facilitative rather than determinate regarding questions of bias or evidence that might impact the merits of the Medscheme arbitration ([para 17]). 

The court emphasized that while pre-meeting communications were not copied to the respondents, the arbitrator acted under the impression that the respondents were aware of the meeting arrangement. This belief contributed to the court’s finding that no actual bias occurred since no substantial matters relevant to the Medscheme arbitration were discussed ([para 19]). 

Ultimately, the court concluded that the apprehension of bias alleged by the respondents lacked a factual basis given the context of the meeting, and emphasized that an unjustified removal of the arbitrator would undermine the goals of private arbitration ([para 21]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 22–23])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of NHA, allowing the appeal against the removal of the arbitrator and dismissing the application with costs ([para 23]). 

The ratio decidendi highlighted two key points: first, the necessity for a concrete showing of gross irregularity or bias to warrant removal of an arbitrator, and second, the importance of preserving the integrity of arbitration processes by limiting intervention from courts based on unfounded claims of procedural defects ([para 22]). The implications of this judgment reinforce the principles of procedural fairness in arbitration while granting arbitrators considerable latitude in managing disputes without interference unless clear and demonstrable injustices are presented.

This case adds to the evolving jurisprudence on arbitration in South Africa, underscoring the balance between ensuring fairness in dispute resolution and respecting the autonomy of the arbitration process.
