Supreme Court of Appeal 2016

FIRAC Case Note: Hotz v University of Cape Town (2016 ZASCA 159)

F – Facts

The case of Hotz v University of Cape Town emerged in the context of significant student protests across South African universities from March 2015, particularly surrounding the issues of tuition fees and accommodation, primarily affecting Black students ([1]–[3]). The protests at the University of Cape Town (UCT) were characterized by actions termed "Shackville," which began on February 15, 2016, and sought to dramatize the struggles of Black students and highlight issues of transformation ([4], [5], [8]).

The appellants—Alexandria Gabriella Hotz and four others—were involved in erecting a shack on University property, obstructing a major thoroughfare ([3], [5]). The protest saw participants erecting a structure that significantly impeded traffic and access to important university facilities, leading to escalating confrontations between protesters, university staff, and other students ([6]–[9]). The situation escalated to include acts of vandalism—such as defacing university property—and blocking road access, resulting in considerable disruption ([10]–[17]).

On February 17, 2016, UCT secured an interim interdict against the appellants, and later sought a final interdict, which was granted by the High Court on May 11, 2016 ([3], [24]). This appeal was made with the authority of Justice Allie, who had presided over the initial proceedings ([1], [3]).

I – Issues

The central issues for the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

  1. Were the actions of the appellants during the protest on February 15-17 unlawful?
  2. Did UCT demonstrate a reasonable apprehension that similar unlawful actions would recur, thereby justifying the grant of a final interdict?
  3. Did UCT have recourse to any alternative remedy other than the interdict sought? ([2], [29]).

R – Rules

The legal principles applied in determining the grant of a final interdict are well-established. An applicant must demonstrate:

  1. A clear right,
  2. An injury that has occurred or is reasonably apprehended,
  3. The absence of adequate alternative remedies ([29]).

These requisites were reiterated through reference to several key cases, including Setlogelo v Setlogelo [1914 AD 221] and Van Deventer v Ivory Sun Trading 77 (Pty) Ltd [2015 ZASCA 99] ([29]). The legislation regarding public gatherings, particularly Section 17 of the South African Constitution—recognizing the right to assemble, demonstrate, and present petitions—also served as a critical framework, along with the university's code of conduct and municipal by-laws ([30], [64]).

A – Application

The Court assessed the appellants' actions against these legal standards. The evidence presented included detailed accounts of the protests that transpired between February 15 and 17, which involved the obstruction of university traffic, vandalism of property, and confrontations that involved verbal and physical altercations ([10], [14], [24]).

The appellants claimed that their actions were rooted in a legitimate struggle for social justice and transformation at UCT. However, the Court noted that even a robust claim for social justice does not absolve individuals from the requirement to protest lawfully. The conduct of the appellants—which included vandalizing university property, erecting unauthorized structures, and inciting violence—was found to constitute serious unlawful actions that infringed on the university's rights to maintain order and safety ([31]–[39]).

In evaluating the second requirement for the interdict, the Court found that UCT had a reasonable apprehension of future harm due to the actions and expressions of the appellants, notably their failure to disavow unlawful conduct and express any intent to refrain from similar actions in the future ([75], [76]).

The Court further dismissed the proposition that UCT had alternative remedies available that could adequately address the alleged wrongs without an interdict. Internal disciplinary actions and mediation attempts had proven ineffective amid the escalating tensions ([75], [78]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the High Court’s decision to issue a final interdict against the five appellants. The altered order included specific prohibitions against unlawful actions that had been engaged in previously, focusing on preventing future occurrences of the same conduct ([84]).

While the appellants were found to have engaged in protest action, their methods were deemed unlawful due to their disruption of university operations and the damage inflicted upon property. The implication of the decision highlights the delicate balance between the right to protest and the necessity for lawful conduct in exercising that right. The case underscores the repercussions that arise from a failure to adhere to the legal frameworks governing public demonstrations, particularly within an academic setting.

The Court’s ruling effectively reinforced the university's right to safeguard its environment and maintain order while granting the appellants a chance to participate in lawful protests, implying an ongoing debate on how best to ensure students' voices are heard without infringing on the rights of others ([84]). This decision does not merely resolve a conflict over specific actions but may also shape future student engagements in protests, emphasizing the importance of lawful conduct in advocacy for socio-political change.
