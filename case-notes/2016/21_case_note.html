Constitutional Court

Minister of Police and Others v Kunjana [2016] ZACC 21

Kunjana was arrested and charged with drug possession and dealing, leading her to challenge the constitutionality of the warrantless search and seizure provisions under section 11 of the Drugs Act.

Constitutional Court 2016 [2016] ZACC 21

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

This case arises from an application for confirmation of a declaration of constitutional invalidity made by the High Court of South Africa, Western Cape Division, in relation to section 11(1)(a) and (g) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992 (the “Drugs Act”) ([paras 1–6]). On 14 March 2011, police officers from the South African Police Service (SAPS), acting on information received from an informant, executed warrantless search operations at two properties leased by the respondent, Ms. Grace Nomazizi Kunjana, where large quantities of illegal drugs were suspected to be stored. The searches resulted in the seizure of 24,719 Mandrax tablets and significant amounts of cash ([paras 2–3]).

Kunjana was arrested and charged with drug possession and dealing, leading her to challenge the constitutionality of the warrantless search and seizure provisions under section 11 of the Drugs Act. The High Court found these provisions to infringe upon the right to privacy under section 14 of the Constitution, concluding that the infringement was not justifiable in terms of the limitation clause provided in section 36 of the Constitution ([paras 6–8]).

The High Court declared section 11(1)(a) and (g) unconstitutional, determining that the declaration should take effect immediately. However, the applicants sought confirmation of this order ([paras 10–12]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented in this case include:

  1. Constitutionality of section 11(1)(a) and (g) of the Drugs Act:
  • Do these provisions infringe upon the right to privacy as encapsulated in section 14 of the Constitution?
  1. Retrospective vs. Prospective Application:
  • If unconstitutional, should the declaration of invalidity be retrospective or prospective?
  1. Costs:
  • Should the court grant a costs order in favor of Kunjana, and if so, to what extent? ([paras 13]).

R – Rules

To determine the constitutionality of the statute at issue, the court relied on established legal principles from several key precedents and constitutional provisions.

  1. Right to Privacy (Constitution, section 14):
  • Guarantees every individual the right to privacy, which includes safeguards against unlawful searches and seizures ([para 1]).
  1. Reasonable Limitation of Rights (Constitution, section 36):
  • Allows for the limitation of rights only when justified by a significant state interest weighed against factors such as the nature and importance of the right, the extent of the limitation, and alternative means to achieve the purpose ([paras 15–18]).
  1. Case Law Precedents:
  • The court referenced previous rulings including Thint, Mistry, Gaertner, and Ngqukumba to reinforce its evaluation of privacy expectations, warrant requirements, and the severity of state intrusions ([paras 14–32]).

A – Application

The court undertook a detailed evaluation to assess whether the infringement of privacy rights by sections 11(1)(a) and (g) of the Drugs Act was justifiable.

  1. Infringement of Rights:
  • The court found a clear violation of the right to privacy, particularly given the broad scopes of warrantless searches which allowed police officers to conduct searches without sufficient guiding principles ([paras 14–22]).
  1. Public Interest and State Security:
  • While the need to combat drug-related crimes serves a significant public purpose, the court ultimately concluded that the law’s broad mandates and lack of restrictions resulted in an unjustifiable infringement of individual rights ([paras 19–32]).
  1. Objective of Declaration:
  • The court rejected the idea of retrospective validity for the declaration, emphasizing the importance of maintaining judicial comity and consistency in the application of constitutional principles. The prospective application of the ruling was seen as most appropriate to avoid dislocations in the administration of justice while still allowing Kunjana the opportunity to challenge the legality of the searches during her trial ([paras 33–39]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court’s declaration of the invalidity of sections 11(1)(a) and (g) of the Drugs Act, recognizing that these provisions violate the right to privacy protected by the Constitution and cannot be justified under section 36. The declaration was ruled to have prospective effect, ensuring that past searches conducted under the invalidated provisions would not be retroactively invalid. Furthermore, the Court ordered the first and third applicants to pay Kunjana’s costs related to the confirmation proceedings up to the date she notified her support for the application, solidifying her interests in the judicial outcome ([paras 47]).

This ruling underscores the delicate balance between the imperative of effective law enforcement and the vital protections afforded to individual rights in a democratic society, emphasizing the necessity of constitutional safeguards against overreach by state authorities.
