Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: S S v V V-S [2018] ZACC 5 

## F – Facts

This case arises from an application for leave to appeal against a ruling by the High Court, which authorized a warrant of execution against the immovable property of the applicant, S S. This warrant was issued to enforce maintenance obligations owed by the applicant to his ex-wife, V V-S, for their minor child, K. The couple married in 2007 and divorced in 2010, with a settlement agreement outlining the terms of maintenance payment, which stipulated a basic monthly maintenance of R2,500, along with additional responsibilities towards educational and medical expenses ([paras 1-4]).

The respondent alleged that the applicant defaulted in his maintenance obligations, resulting in arrears amounting to R306,550.18. This claim was supported by an affidavit detailing the applicant's defaults but lacked corroborative evidence such as receipts and invoices for the alleged unpaid amounts ([paras 5-6]). After an unsuccessful attempt to execute the initial warrant against the applicant's movable goods, the respondent sought to execute against his immovable property, which was met with opposition from the applicant ([paras 10-11]).

The High Court ultimately ordered the execution against the applicant's immovable property, citing the applicant's "revolting attitude" and his efforts to frustrate compliance with maintenance obligations ([paras 13-14]). The applicant's subsequent appeals to higher courts were also dismissed, prompting the current application for leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([paras 15-16]).

## I – Issues

The principal issues presented to the Constitutional Court were:

1. Whether the amount of R306,550.18 constituted a 'judgment debt' thereby establishing a judgment debtor-judgment creditor relationship ([para 15b i]).
2. Whether the High Court had appropriately employed the rule 46(1)(a)(ii) execution process as a measure of last resort ([para 15b ii]).
3. The implications of the applicant's admitted failure to comply with court orders and maintenance obligations ([paras 30-35]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework relevant to this case includes several key statutes and principles:

1. **Section 28 of the Constitution**: Emphasizes that a child's best interests are paramount in all matters concerning the child.
2. **Rule 46(1)(a)(ii) of the Uniform Rules of Court**: Stipulates that execution against immovable property requires a court order, especially when the property is the primary residence of the debtor ([para 11]).
3. **Judicial Authority (Section 165 of the Constitution)**: Establishes that courts must maintain their authority and establish compliance with their orders to uphold the rule of law ([paras 19-20]).
4. **Biowatch principle**: This principle generally prohibits imposing costs on unsuccessful constitutional litigants unless the case is deemed frivolous or vexatious ([paras 37-39]).

Furthermore, previous case law, such as *Bannatyne v Bannatyne* and *Fakie N.O. v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd*, reinforces the necessity of enforcing maintenance obligations and the significance of judicial authority in safeguarding the interests of children ([paras 26, 20]).

## A – Application

Justice Kollapen, writing for a unanimous Court, highlighted that the applicant's conduct indicated a significant non-compliance with court orders regarding maintenance. The Court noted the applicant's acknowledgment of indebtedness to the respondent, yet emphasized his failure to provide sufficient evidence of compliance with maintenance obligations ([para 12, 16-34]). Importantly, it determined that while the proceedings were not technically contempt actions, they had similarities with contempt proceedings, particularly in terms of the significant implications that arise from non-compliance—specifically regarding the integrity of the judiciary and the welfare of the minor child ([paras 18-21]).

The Court argued that allowing the matter to proceed without addressing the applicant's failure to meet maintenance requirements would undermine both judicial integrity and the rights of the child involved. Therefore, it ruled that the interests of justice wouldn't be served by allowing the applicant to pursue the appeal while remaining in default of his obligations ([paras 30-36]).

Despite the applicant's assertion that the respondent had agreed to offset part of the outstanding payment, the Court found this assertion unsubstantiated. It emphasized the importance of ensuring compliance with court orders and maintaining the authority of the judiciary over matters concerning children's welfare ([paras 32-33]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal, reiterating that the applicant had not remedied his non-compliance with the court's earlier order. The Court ordered the applicant to pay the respondent's legal costs on an attorney and client scale, reflecting the seriousness of his conduct and the necessity to uphold the integrity of the justice system ([para 44]).

This ruling underlines the judiciary’s commitment to enforcing child support obligations strictly and serves as a crucial reminder of the need for compliance with court orders—particularly when a child's welfare is at stake. By imposing a punitive costs order against the applicant, the Court sought both to deter frivolous litigation and to affirm the legal obligation of parents to prioritize their children's needs and uphold court-mandated responsibilities.
