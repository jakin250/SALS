Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: Boxer Superstores Mthatha v Mbenya [2007] SCA 79 (RSA)

## F – Facts

The matter revolved around a dispute arising from the dismissal of Noma Hlubi Lorraine Mbenya (the respondent) by Boxer Superstores Mthatha (the first appellant). Mbenya’s employment was terminated on 7 December 2004, following disciplinary proceedings. However, Mbenya claimed that her dismissal was both substantively and procedurally unfair, arguing that the disciplinary hearing was conducted improperly and without due process (see [1]). 

Seven months later, well outside the time limits for challenging unfair dismissals under the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA), Mbenya approached the High Court at Mthatha seeking several forms of relief, which included declaring her dismissal "unlawful," reinstatement, back-pay, and costs ([1], [2]). Boxer Superstores Mthatha raised an objection to the High Court's jurisdiction, asserting that the matter fell exclusively under the Labour Court as prescribed by the LRA ([2]).

In her founding affidavit, Mbenya contended that her dismissal violated her rights under the Constitution, emphasizing her entitlement to equal protection before the law (s 9(1) of the Bill of Rights) and the right to a fair public hearing (s 34 of the Bill of Rights) ([3]).

On the objection from Boxer Superstores, the High Court dismissed the jurisdictional challenge, which led to the appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([4], [11]).

## I – Issues

The main legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the High Court had jurisdiction to entertain Mbenya’s application, considering her dismissal arose within the employment context typically governed by the LRA, which confers exclusive jurisdiction upon Labour Courts for unfair dismissals ([2], [5]). 

Additionally, a secondary issue was whether, despite Mbenya framing her claim as one of unlawfulness rather than unfairness, the substance of her grievance could be interpreted as falling within the jurisdiction of the Labour Court ([11], [12]).

## R – Rules

The court relied on several legal principles and precedents:

1. **Jurisdiction Under the LRA**: Section 157(1) of the LRA provides that the Labour Court has exclusive jurisdiction regarding matters dictated by the Act. However, it also acknowledges that the High Court retains jurisdiction over claims arising from the employer-employee relationship that do not exclusively fall under LRA provisions ([5]).

2. **Case Law**:
   - **Fedlife Assurance Ltd v Wolfaardt**: This case established that the Labour Court's jurisdiction is not absolute concerning employer-employee disputes ([5]).
   - **Fredericks v MEC for Education and Training, Eastern Cape**: Reinforced that the High Court could hear cases not expressly falling under the jurisdiction of the Labour Court ([5]).
   - **Old Mutual Life Assurance Co SA Ltd v Gumbi**: Notably held that an employee has common law rights to a pre-dismissal hearing, which can be asserted in the High Court ([6]).

3. **Legal Principles Regarding Forms**: The court noted that whether a dispute falls under the Labour Court's jurisdiction often depends on the substance of the complaint rather than its form ([12]).

## A – Application

In applying these rules, the Supreme Court of Appeal acknowledged Mbenya's careful formulation of her claim as one of unlawfulness, strictly avoiding references to fairness, which would typically invoke the Labour Court's jurisdiction ([11], [12]). It noted that an employee could sue in the High Court for breaches of the common law contract of employment, even if the claim overlaps with the Labour Court's jurisdiction ([5], [6]).

The court considered the fact that Mbenya’s claims, while extending to complex relief forms such as reinstatement and back-pay, fundamentally involved a complaint about the lawfulness as opposed to the fairness of her dismissal ([10], [12]). Therefore, the High Court's retention of jurisdiction over the matter was justified, as her claims did not solely revolve around allegations of unfairness under the LRA.

Furthermore, the court pointed out that to deny the High Court jurisdiction based on the form of Mbenya’s claim would undermine the Constitutionally guaranteed rights to fair hearing and protection under the law ([3], [7]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court had jurisdiction to entertain Mbenya’s application. The Court's decision underlined the principle that while the LRA confers certain exclusive jurisdictions, it does not entirely preclude common law claims arising from employment relationships, particularly those framed as challenges to the lawfulness of dismissals ([13]).

The appeal was dismissed with costs, affirming the High Court's ruling. This decision sets a significant precedent by clarifying that employees can challenge the lawfulness of dismissals in the High Court independent of Labour Court exclusivity, enhancing the pathway for contractual claims related to employment disputes ([13]). 

**Implications of this ruling extend to further refine the delineation of jurisdiction between the High Court and Labour Court, emphasizing the role of contractual rights vis-à-vis statutory protections in employment law.**
