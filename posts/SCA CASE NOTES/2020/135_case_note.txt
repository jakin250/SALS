Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Fletcher v McNair (1350/2019) [2020] ZASCA 135

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
In *Fletcher v McNair* ([1]), the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa dealt with the contested removal of Warren John Fletcher (the Appellant) as a trustee of the McNair Family Trust (the Trust), initiated by Gillian Claire McNair (the Respondent). The initial decision by the Gauteng Division of the High Court rejected McNair’s application to remove Fletcher as trustee. However, a subsequent appeal to the Full Court overturned this decision, leading to Fletcher's removal and his replacement by McNair’s sister ([1]–[2]). 

The Trust, created in 2006, includes significant assets such as a share portfolio valued at R2.8 million and a 75% stake in Top Spin Investments 101 (Pty) Ltd (Top Spin) ([2]). The relationships among trustees and beneficiaries deteriorated substantially due to conflicts between McNair and Mr. McNair, her brother-in-law, which expanded to include Fletcher and Mr. Crossman, another trustee ([7]–[8]). Tensions rose around financial matters involving the Trust and the operations of Associated Pneumatics SA (Pty) Ltd (Applied), with allegations of mismanagement and personal liability for debts ([9]–[14]).

Despite these interpersonal disputes, the functioning of the Trust appeared stable, with evidence of ongoing meetings and financial distributions made to beneficiaries ([43]). Fletcher was a director and accountant of relevant companies, yet he faced accusations from McNair regarding his conduct and alleged collusion ([7]–[14]).

### I – Issues
The primary issue before the court was whether the Full Court erred in its decision to remove Fletcher as a trustee. Key sub-issues included:
1. Whether the breakdown in the relationships among the trustees, especially between Fletcher and McNair, warranted removal under the Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988 (the Act) ([18]–[19]).
2. Whether the Full Court had properly applied the standards established in precedent cases regarding removals of trustees, specifically in reference to the interests of the Trust and its beneficiaries ([19]–[28]).
3. Whether the events described sufficiently demonstrated that the Trust's administration was jeopardized by continuing conflicts among the trustees and the accusations levied against Fletcher ([42]).

### R – Rules
The court referred to the provisions of s 20(1) of the Trust Property Control Act, which permits the removal of a trustee if it is in the interest of the Trust ([18]). The principles derived from the case of *Gowar v Gowar* provided a comprehensive framework for assessing removal requests, emphasizing:
- Removal must be in the best interests of the Trust and its beneficiaries ([19]).
- The court possesses inherent powers to regulate its processes, which have been defined and supported by South African constitutional law ([21]).
- Disharmony among trustees does not automatically justify removal; the critical question is whether such disharmony imperils the Trust's administration or property ([20]–[26]).

### A – Application
The judgment's application of these rules revealed key missteps in the Full Court's reasoning. While acknowledging the significant interpersonal tensions and mutual distrust among trustees, the Supreme Court contended that this alone did not justify Fletcher's removal ([30]). The essence of the court’s critique hinged on these points:
- The Full Court did not adequately demonstrate how Fletcher’s actions directly harmed the Trust or its beneficiaries and incorrectly conflated personal disputes with actual detriment to Trust properties ([31]–[35]).
- The finding that Fletcher's alleged misrepresentations had caused damage was premised on incorrect factual assumptions, especially since he had not sent the contested letter to the distributor ([33]).
- Both crucial issues surrounding Applied and the sale of property were addressed without adjudicating the populated narrative of financial distress allegedly caused by Fletcher's actions; rather, these resolutions were consistent with the Trust’s operations framework ([37]–[42]).

Fletcher's apparent misconduct in his role was evaluated through the lens of the overall efficacy of the Trust's administration. The court underscored that merely having disputes among trustees does not equate to imperiling Trust operations ([43]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Full Court failed to apply the requisite standards for trustee removal, centering too heavily on the discord among trustees without showing a direct impact on the Trust's function ([47]). Thus, the court upheld the appeal, reinstating Fletcher as a trustee, highlighting the need for concrete evidence of risk to Trust operations prior to such drastic sanctions ([1]–[47]).

### Implications
This ruling reinforces the judicial caution required in trustee removal cases, requiring courts to carefully assess the genuine interests of the Trust and its beneficiaries rather than acting on interpersonal disputes alone. It also emphasizes the necessity for judicial decisions to adhere to established statutory frameworks and case law, ensuring a rigorous standard of proof must be met to justify trustee removals in future cases.
