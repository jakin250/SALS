# Case Note: Association of Mineworkers and Construction Union and Others v Anglo Gold Ashanti Limited and Others [2021] ZACC 42

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

The present case arises from an appeal to the Constitutional Court of South Africa involving the Association of Mineworkers and Construction Union (AMCU) and various mining companies, collectively referred to as the respondents. AMCU, along with its members, intended to initiate secondary strikes in support of a primary strike at Sibanye Gold Limited due to ongoing wage negotiations that had begun on 11 July 2018 and resulted in a certified dispute on 26 September 2018. The primary strike commenced on 21 November 2018, and by February 2019, AMCU informed the respondents of its intention to conduct secondary strikes against ten mining operations. In opposition, the respondents argued that these secondary strikes would disrupt their businesses significantly while exerting no direct pressure on the primary employer, Sibanye. This claim led to the respondents seeking an interdict from the Labour Court on the grounds that the secondary strikes were unprotected as per section 66(2)(c) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA). The Labour Court agreed, determining that the anticipated harm to the secondary employers outweighed any potential effect on the primary employer.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 24–32])

The critical issues before the Court stemmed from interpretations of section 66(2)(c) of the LRA. Specifically, two overarching questions required resolution:
1. Does section 66(2)(c) of the LRA import a principle of proportionality when assessing the substantive lawfulness of secondary strikes?
2. Must an assessment of the reasonableness of a secondary strike consider the nature and impact of the strike on each secondary employer individually or collectively? 

Additionally, the implications of whether past instances of violence during strikes should influence assessments of future strikes were probed.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 34–48])

The Court’s interpretation of section 66(2)(c) was informed by established principles of constitutional interpretation and relevant labour law, notably:
- The **Constitution of South Africa**, particularly sections 23(2)(c) conferring the right to strike, affirmed the necessity of collective bargaining and the regulation of industrial action (paras 34–35).
- Previous cases, such as **SALGA I** and **SALGA II**, which involved the application of proportionality assessments in the context of secondary strikes, were central in guiding judicial interpretation of the LRA – establishing that a proportionality test is essential in evaluating reasonable cause for secondary strikes.

The LRA itself outlines the procedural requirements for secondary strikes and stipulates the conditions under which secondary employment considerations come into play. Additionally, international law and various precedents underscored the importance of protecting the right to strike while balancing competing interests linked to workplace rights, aligning with ILO conventions.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 70–102])

The Court reaffirmed that section 66(2)(c) does indeed invoke a proportionality assessment when determining the lawfulness of secondary strikes. The interpretation held that the primary aim is to balance potential harms to both secondary and primary employers:
1. **Proportionality Requirement**: The Court noted the significance of assessing the possible effects of a secondary strike on both the primary and secondary employers. If the harm to the secondary employers surpasses the impact on the primary employer, the secondary strikes could be deemed unprotected (paras 70–73).
2. **Individual versus Collective Assessment**: It was determined that the assessment must focus on each secondary employer individually, rather than aggregating impacts across multiple employers. This approach prevents the dilution of protections afforded to secondary employers and necessitates specific evaluations of how secondary strikes affect their businesses (paras 102–106).

The Labour Court had concluded that the anticipated secondary strikes posed minimal risk to Sibanye while endangering secondary employers' economic viability; the Constitutional Court affirmed that such determinations were essential to safeguarding against undue disruption to businesses uninvolved in the primary dispute.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 109–111])

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court ruled that section 66(2)(c) indeed incorporates a proportionality assessment concerning secondary strikes. It held that the nature and extent of the proposed secondary strikes were unreasonable given their potential devastative impacts on secondary employers without any corresponding pressure on Sibanye. The appeal against the Labour Court's decision was dismissed, but the costs orders from both the Labour Court and Labour Appeal Court were overturned, necessitating each party to bear its own costs.

This judgment affirmed critical balances within South African labour law, reinforcing the intricate relationships between workers' rights and economic realities while providing a clearer framework for the application of section 66(2)(c) in secondary strike assessments. It underscores the legislative intent to safeguard businesses while recognizing the need for effective collective bargaining as a foundational pillar of democratic workplace relations.
