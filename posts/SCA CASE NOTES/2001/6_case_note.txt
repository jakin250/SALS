Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2001

# FIRAC Case Note: EBN Trading (Pty) Ltd v The Commissioner for Customs and Excise

**CASE NO. 653/98**  
**IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA**  
**Heard: 20 February 2001**  
**Delivered: 2 March 2001**  

## F - Facts

The case revolves around the appellant, EBN Trading (Pty) Ltd (“EBN”), which sought to establish whether it was an “importer” under the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 (“the Act”) concerning video cassette recorders (VCRs) imported through the Durban harbour in April 1995 ([1]). EBN had provided financing for the importation of these goods; however, the actual importation and title lay with a Hong Kong company, Dragon Best Investment Ltd (“Dragon”) ([1]–[3]).

EBN contended it was a mere financier with no beneficial interest in the goods, while the respondents, the Commissioner for Customs and Excise and the Controller of Customs and Excise, argued otherwise, citing the extensions in the definition of “importer” that would include anyone “beneficially interested” ([2]–[3]). EBN entered into a series of agreements to finance the importation of VCRs from Daewoo Corporation ([3]). The case arose from the respondents' claims of unpaid customs duties amounting to R4,421,837.89, stemming from the allegation that the goods entered under false documentation indicating Zaire as the country of origin, thereby evading duties ([5]–[6]).

Through lengthy legal proceedings, the Natal Provincial Division ruled for Customs, leading to EBN's appeal ([6]). The court needed to determine if EBN's financing role placed it within the definition of an “importer” necessitating duty payment ([7]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal questions to be addressed were as follows:

1. Was EBN Trading (Pty) Ltd an “importer” as defined under the Customs and Excise Act based on its operational role in the transactions related to the imported goods? ([2]–[3])
2. Did EBN demonstrate a lack of responsibility for customs duties, particularly concerning the shipment’s connection to the alleged fraudulent entry of goods? ([29]–[30])

## R - Rules

The relevant rules stem from the Customs and Excise Act, particularly the definition of “importer,” which includes several extended meanings. Specifically, the definition states that the term “importer” also includes any person who “is beneficially interested in any way whatever in any goods imported” ([2]). 

The obligation to prove payment of customs duty rests, under s 102(4) of the Act, on the importer ([29])—a critical point in adjudicating the liability of EBN.

## A - Application

In applying the law to the facts, the court sought to clarify the relationship between EBN and the imported goods. The judgment examined whether EBN had a beneficial interest, as characterized by its involvement in securing funding through letters of credit and the possession of bills of lading, which are essential for the control of goods ([23]–[24]). 

The court critically analyzed EBN’s agreements and found substantial evidence of EBN's active role in the transaction. EBN’s claim that it was merely a financier did not negate its involvement where it took a significant position as the holder of the bills of lading, thereby acquiring a "lively interest" in the goods ([24]). 

Notably, the court refuted EBN's argument that the beneficial interest lay with Effective Barter, the purported agent, stating that the agent's actions could still impose liability on EBN under paragraph (f) of the Act’s definition of “importer” ([25]). Furthermore, the contracts established that EBN had accepted responsibility for securing finance and managing the sales process, which underpinned its classification as an importer ([23]–[27]).

Regarding the issue of whether customs duties had been paid, the burden of proof on this matter fell upon EBN. The court emphasized that EBN failed to provide satisfactory evidence to disprove the outstanding duties, thereby reinforcing the respondents’ assertions ([29]). Since the documents and evidence aligned significantly with the duties claimed, the court found a strong probability that the goods linked to the false entries were indeed the same that were delivered to the traders ([30]).

## C - Conclusion

The court dismissed the appeal, concluding that EBN qualified as an “importer” under the Customs and Excise Act due to its beneficial interest in the goods imported ([28]). The court's interpretation hinged upon the realities of the contractual framework rather than the mere labels assigned by EBN. The court reinforced that liability could arise from meaningful involvement in a transaction, as evidenced by possession of bills of lading and agreements made with stakeholders.

Consequently, the court's ruling carries significant implications for financial entities engaging in import/export transactions, indicating that mere documentation and terminology do not insulate parties from obligations under statute, especially regarding customs duties. With costs awarded to the respondents, the judgment further solidified the threshold for defining beneficial interest in the context of customs law ([31]).
