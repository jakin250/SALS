Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: Wessels v Pretorius [2007] SCA 108 (RSA)

---

**F – Facts**  
[1] The case revolves around an accident occurring on March 2, 2002, involving Benjamin Pretorius, a nearly seventeen-year-old matric student, and Albert Wessels, the appellant's sixteen-year-old son who was driving a vehicle (a bakkie) owned by Stephanus Wessels (hereinafter "the appellant"). At the time of the incident, Albert was an unlicensed driver. The vehicle was carrying Benjamin and three other teenagers in an unsecured manner on the open back when Albert executed a ‘handbrake turn,' resulting in Benjamin being ejected from the vehicle and sustaining serious injuries. It was agreed that Albert had warned the passengers before performing this maneuver, yet Benjamin reportedly did not heed the warning, resulting in him not holding on to the vehicle at the moment of the accident [1].

[2] Following the accident, Benjamin, represented by his father, initiated two claims against the appellant: one in his capacity as the owner of the vehicle and another against him personally, asserting negligence for permitting Albert to drive without supervision when it was foreseeable that Albert would not drive with reasonable care. The appellant denied these allegations [2].

[3] The High Court, under the presiding judge Landman J, found that Albert's negligence contributed to Benjamin's injuries and attributed fault between Albert and Benjamin in a ratio of 65:35 [4]. The appellant was found to have exercised a duty of care over Albert and was held liable for allowing him to operate the vehicle in circumstances where he was likely to act recklessly.

---

**I – Issues**  
The key legal questions before the court included:

1. Was the appellant negligent in allowing Albert to drive without adequate supervision, given that his son was an unlicensed and inexperienced driver who might buckle under peer pressure?
2. Can Benjamin's injuries be directly linked to the appellant's alleged negligence in permitting Albert access to the vehicle? [4-5].

---

**R – Rules**  
The court relied on principles of delictual liability, particularly focusing on negligence as delineated in South African law. In specific terms, the court emphasized the duty of care owed by parents to their children, particularly when granting permission for activities that pose potential dangers. The court referenced precedents such as *De Beer v Sergeant* [1976] 1 SA 246 (T) and *Godfrey and Others v Campbell* [1997] 1 SA 570 (C) to highlight the necessity of establishing a causal link between negligence and the injury sustained [15].

Key legal standards involved:

- The foreseeability of harm and the standard of care required from a parent in supervising a minor.
- The degree of control over the behavior of a minor and the extent to which parental negligence can lead to liability for actions taken by the child [15-16].

---

**A – Application**  
In examining the facts presented, the court analyzed the dynamics around the appellant's supervision of Albert, including granting access to the vehicle despite knowing his son lacked a driver's license. The testimony revealed that the appellant had indeed given permission for Albert to operate the vehicle and failed to secure the keys or withhold consent, both critical measures to prevent potential harm [8-12].

The court concluded that the appellant acted negligently by failing to foresee the likelihood of peer pressure influencing his son’s driving behavior. The lack of supervision, combined with the environment of teenage recklessness and the inherent dangers of driving without proper licensing, substantiated claims of negligence. By permitting Albert to drive the bakkie in these conditions, the appellant's actions were deemed culpable [16-17].

---

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appellant's appeal. The court held that the evidence sufficiently established that the appellant negligently allowed his son access to the vehicle, which ultimately led to the injuries suffered by Benjamin. The ruling underscored the important principle that parents are responsible for exercising care in enabling minors to engage in potentially hazardous activities, particularly when unlicensed and inexperienced individuals are involved. The court reiterated that the appellant's failure to take reasonable precautions resulted in personal liability for the injuries sustained by Benjamin [19].

The implications of this ruling extend to parental responsibilities in similar contexts, reinforcing that neglect in supervision of young drivers can lead to significant legal repercussions, thus providing a cautionary structure for future cases involving minor drivers and parental consent.

--- 

This case serves as a pivotal precedent that emphasizes the duty of care owed by parents, particularly in situations where minors are given the autonomy to operate potentially dangerous machinery, and it ultimately contributes to a heightened awareness of the legal implications surrounding negligence and supervision.
