Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: S v Tandwa [2007] SCA 34 (RSA)

### F – Facts
[1] On the morning of 18 November 1998, a robbery occurred at the Standard Bank in Mthatha, Eastern Cape, where the perpetrators stole R9.6 million from the strongroom. Initial police response revealed three bank employees locked inside the strongroom, who claimed to have complied with the robbers under duress. Suspicions quickly fell upon two employees, Siyabulela Tandwa (Accused 1) and Aubrey Godolozi (Accused 2), leading to their arrest along with six others on charges of robbery under the Transkei Penal Code, Act 9 of 1983 ([para 1]).

[2] During the trial commencing in November 1999, all eight accused pleaded not guilty. Seven of them received prison sentences between 17 to 20 years after conviction ([para 2]). Accused 4 was acquitted. The prosecution's evidence against the accused involved direct witnesses, circumstantial evidence, and testimonies regarding internal bank conflicts, particularly lapses in security protocols associated with the robbery ([para 4]).

### I – Issues
[6] The case raised multiple significant constitutional issues, particularly surrounding the rights of the accused under Section 35 of the Bill of Rights, including:

1. Whether accused 1's right to a fair trial was violated due to alleged misconduct by his legal representative ([para 7]).
2. Whether the failure of accused 1 to testify constituted a breach of his right to silence ([para 8]).
3. The admissibility of evidence obtained unlawfully through alleged police brutality ([para 86]), which raised questions under Section 35(5) of the Constitution regarding the integrity of the trial ([paras 116-121]).

### R – Rules
[7] Key rules derived from constitutional, statutory, and case law guide the evaluation of these issues:

1. **Right to Fair Trial**: As enshrined in Section 35(3) of the Constitution, guarantees the accused the right to choose competent legal representation ([para 7]).
   
2. **Right to Silence**: Under Section 35(3)(h), emphasizes that the silence of an accused may lead to inferences of guilt, a critical consideration in cases reliant on circumstantial evidence ([para 8]).
   
3. **Admissibility of Evidence**: In terms of Section 35(5), any evidence obtained unlawfully that violates a right in the Bill of Rights must generally be excluded unless its admission would not render the trial unfair ([para 121]).

### A – Application
[17] The Supreme Court of Appeal examined whether the allegations of misconduct by accused 1’s counsel warranted a further inquiry. Although accused 1 claimed that he had not been informed of the consequences of his silence and had not been allowed to testify, the court found the allegations lacked the requisite credibility and plausibility due to contradictions in the accused’s timeline and actions ([paras 23-24]). Legal professional privilege was deemed waived when the court found that the advocate’s response was admissible for assessing the veracity of claims ([para 29]).

[30] Additionally, discussing the circumstantial evidence against accused 1 and 2, the court highlighted a failure to account for deviant behaviors in compliance with bank security measures, which, when considered collectively, demonstrated complicity ([paras 54-56]). As for accused 2, the state relied on corroborating testimony from an accomplice and circumstantial evidence indicating participation in the robbery, fostering conclusions of guilt based on behaviors and director identification patterns ([paras 58, 64]).

On unlawfully obtained evidence due to alleged police conduct, the trial court's initial reasoning was scrutinized, emphasizing an evaluation of whether the tainted evidence adversely affected the trial's fairness. The court emphasized that evidence discovered through coercion or torture not only undermines the specific accused's rights but might tarnish public trust in the justice system ([paras 120-121])—a consideration met with significant jurisprudence on evidence admissibility ([para 128]).

### C – Conclusion
[134] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeals of all accused, affirming that the evidence against them, comprising both circumstantial and direct testimony, met the thresholds for conviction beyond reasonable doubt. The court ruled that any alleged misconduct by counsel did not demonstrate the required threshold of incompetence or misconduct resulting in an unfair trial ([para 134]). 

The implications of this judgment reaffirm the judicial commitment to maintaining the integrity of trial processes, upholding the rights of the accused, and ensuring the public's faith in fair judicial proceedings. Such rulings underscore the essential balance between effective law enforcement and the preservation of constitutional safeguards critical in a democratic society.
