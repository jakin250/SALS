Verimark (Pty) Ltd v Bayerische Motoren Werke AG [2007] SCA 53 (RSA)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

In the case of Verimark (Pty) Ltd v Bayerische Motoren Werke AG ([1]), the South African Supreme Court of Appeal addressed issues of trade mark infringement concerning the well-known BMW logo. The BMW logo is registered under two classes pertinent to this case: (a) class 3, which includes cleaning and polishing preparations (the polish mark TM 1987/05127), and (b) class 12, which relates to vehicles and automobiles (the car mark TM 1956/00818/1).

BMW, the respondent, sought an interdict in the High Court under section 34(1) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, claiming that Verimark, the appellant, was infringing its trade marks by using the BMW logo on packaging and in television advertisements for its car care products, specifically the Diamond Guard car polish. The High Court ruled in favor of BMW regarding the polish mark but dismissed the claim related to the car mark, leading to a dual appeal: Verimark appealed the finding of infringement of the polish mark, while BMW cross-appealed against the dismissal of the car mark claim ([2]).

Verimark, a leader in direct response television marketing, showcased its products on various vehicles, particularly BMWs, to demonstrate effectiveness—showing, for instance, the resistance of BMW paint to fire after the application of its polish. BMW claimed this visibility led to trade mark infringement, interpreting the inclusion of its logo as unauthorized use, potentially leading to confusion or dilution of its brand ([2–3]).

I – Issues

The key legal questions posed in this case relate to:

  1. Does Verimark's usage of the BMW logo constitute 'use as a trade mark' leading to infringement under section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act ([3])?
  2. Can BMW establish a claim under section 34(1)(c) for unfair advantage due to the well-known status of its trade mark ([11])?

R – Rules

The applicable rules are found in the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, particularly section 34(1), which delineates instances of trade mark infringement. This section is divided into three main clauses:

  • Clause (a) addresses unauthorized use of an identical mark in the course of trade in relation to goods for which the trade mark is registered, presumed to cause confusion or deception.
  • Clause (b) concerns unauthorized use of a mark similar to a registered trade mark in connection with similar goods, again likely leading to confusion.
  • Clause (c) extends protection to well-known trade marks against uses that may take unfair advantage of the distinctiveness or repute of the mark, even without confusion ([1], [5–6]).

The court also considered interpretations from both European and English law, including guidelines from European Court of Justice (ECJ) rulings on the functions of trade marks—specifically focusing on whether a third party's use impacts the trade mark's role as a source identifier ([5–6]).

A – Application

The court applied the legal standards to assess whether Verimark's use of the BMW logo amounted to 'trade mark use'. It emphasized that for infringement under section 34(1)(a) to be established, the perceived use must convey the mark's origin function. The court noted that Verimark's usage of the BMW logo was more about illustrating the car rather than indicating the source of the product (the polish). This led to the conclusion that the logo on the car was incidental and did not imply any direct link between the BMW brand and Verimark’s product ([8]).

The court also analyzed the cross-appeal under section 34(1)(c). Recognizing BMW's well-known trademark status, the court contemplated whether Verimark’s use gained an 'unfair advantage'. It determined that there was no substantial evidence of undue exploitation of BMW's reputation, as any mental association consumers may have held did not translate into actionable dilution or damage to the brand's reputation ([12–16]). The emphasis was placed on the consumer's perception of the presence of the logo as incidental rather than as indicative of a direct product source ([15]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld Verimark’s appeal against the High Court's ruling concerning the polish mark, concluding that the interference with BMW's trade mark was misconceived ([10]). Conversely, the court dismissed BMW's cross-appeal regarding the car mark. The ruling signifies a measured approach to trade mark law, reinforcing that for a claim of infringement to prevail, unequivocal evidence must show that the questioned use affects the mark's functions in the consumer's mind, particularly as a source identifier.

By clarifying the thresholds for establishing unfair advantage and trade mark use, the court has contributed significantly to the understanding of branding law in South Africa, impacting subsequent cases concerning trademarks and advertising practices.
