Supreme Court of Appeal

103 Case Note

The appellant, Joseph Mfula Mulula, was convicted of two counts of rape involving his stepdaughter, PZ, who was under ten years of age at the time of the incidents (January and February 2004).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014

FIRAC Case Note: Mulula v The State (074/14) [2014] ZASC 103 (29 August 2014)

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–2])

The appellant, Joseph Mfula Mulula, was convicted of two counts of rape involving his stepdaughter, PZ, who was under ten years of age at the time of the incidents (January and February 2004). Following his conviction by the Secunda Regional Court, he was sentenced to 15 years’ imprisonment by the North Gauteng High Court (Moshidi J). In support of his case, PZ testified that Mulula assaulted her on two occasions in her bedroom, employing threats to prevent her from reporting the incidents. The primary challenge to the conviction revolved around the identification of Mulula as the assailant, with PZ expressing some uncertainty during cross-examination regarding whether the light was on, a crucial detail influencing her ability to identify Mulula ([paras 3–4]).

The appellant sought to introduce new evidence on appeal, specifically, a blood test indicating he was not infected with herpes 2—a condition PZ claimed to have contracted from her assailant. The potential implications of this evidence would be significant in terms of corroborating or undermining PZ's testimony and establishing his innocence ([paras 6–9]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 10–12])

The primary issues presented in this appeal included:

  1. Whether Mulula could introduce further evidence at the appellate stage and under what conditions this would be permissible.
  2. Whether the court should admit the new evidence in the form of affidavits or remit the matter back to the trial court for further hearings.
  3. The legal ramifications of the blood test results concerning the soundness of the original convictions and sentences ([paras 10–12]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 10–14])

The key legal framework relevant to this case derived from:

  1. Section 22 of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 and, although superseded, the principles contained in Section 19 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which provide courts the authority to admit new evidence on appeal under exceptional circumstances.
  2. The requirements established in S v De Jager 1965 (2) SA 612 (A), which set out that:
  • There must be a reasoned explanation for why the evidence was not presented at trial.
  • The new evidence should prove prima facie true.
  • The evidence must be materially relevant to the trial's outcome ([paras 10–11]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 12–18])

The court evaluated the appellant's application to introduce new evidence against the De Jager criteria. It recognized that the newly presented blood test results were prima facie true and relevant, significantly undermining the original basis for his conviction. The court found it troubling that the prosecution did not request the blood test before, given its potential relevance in confirming PZ's identification of Mulula ([paras 12–13]).

The court also concluded that the objections raised by the State—regarding the chain of custody of the blood sample and the medical legal report by Dr. Richter—did not outweigh the strong justification for accepting the new evidence nor were they sufficient to obscure its relevance. PZ’s infection with herpes 2 was convincingly linked to an assailant, and the absence of evidence indicating that Mulula had been infected with the virus further favored the appellant ([paras 14–17]).

Ultimately, the conviction's validity rested on the reliability of PZ’s identification combined with the medical evidence relating to herpes 2. Given the new evidence suggested a compelling argument for Mulula's innocence, the court found it necessary to uphold the appeal ([paras 18–19]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 19])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Mulula’s appeal, granting his application to introduce further evidence and ordered:

  1. The appeal against the conviction and sentence was upheld.
  2. The previous convictions of the appellant on charges of rape were set aside.
  3. The sentence of 15 years’ imprisonment imposed by the High Court was also set aside.

The court’s decision underscores the importance of maintaining justice and the potential for new evidence to significantly alter previously determined outcomes. The case demonstrates that appeals must accord substantial weight to the interests of justice, particularly in criminal matters where the stakes involve personal liberty and the integrity of the judicial process ([para 19]).

The ruling has broader implications for future criminal proceedings, reinforcing the necessity for rigorous examination of evidence and identification processes, particularly when the testimony of young victims is central to the prosecution's case.

This judgment also highlights a crucial principle in South African law: the courts' retention of the ability to rectify injustices arising from potential misidentification or the failure to consider materially relevant evidence in prior proceedings.
