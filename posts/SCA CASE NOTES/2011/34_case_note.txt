Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Slip Knot Investments 777 (Pty) Ltd v Willem Malan Du Toit

**Case Citation**:  
Slip Knot v Du Toit (176/2010) [2011] ZASCA 34 (28 March 2011)  

**Court**:  
Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  

**Coram**:  
NAVSA, NUGENT, HEHER, CACHALIA, and MALAN JJA  

## F – Facts

The case revolves around a suretyship agreement signed by the respondent, Willem Malan Du Toit (“the respondent”), in favor of Slip Knot Investments 777 (Pty) Ltd (“the appellant”). The respondent, a trustee of the Smitskop Trust, signed the suretyship deed under urgent circumstances without reading its contents. He claimed he was mistaken about the nature and implications of the documents he was signing ([para 1]).

On 6 November 2007, the respondent was approached by his nephew and pressured to sign several documents pertaining to the trust's dealings in Africa. The documents included a loan agreement for R6 million, for which the respondent signed without understanding the stipulations of the suretyship. His brother, also a trustee, had previously signed these documents. The respondent asserted that he was unaware of the serious liabilities his signature could incur and assumed it to be a formal requirement as a trustee ([paras 3–4]).

After a judgment was obtained against the trust, the respondent sought rescission of this judgment, which Kruger J of the Free State High Court granted. However, this order was contested by Slip Knot, which sought to enforce the suretyship ([paras 4–5]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal question was whether the respondent’s defense of **iustus error** (a justifiable mistake) was valid in relation to enforcements of the suretyship agreement. Specifically, the court had to consider:

1. Was the respondent bound by the suretyship agreement despite his claim of a misunderstanding regarding his obligations?
2. Did the respondent's circumstances, including the influence of his brother and nephew, negate his liability under the suretyship? ([paras 1–2]).

## R – Rules

The court relied on several legal principles relevant to contractual liability and the doctrine of **iustus error**:

1. **General Principles of Contract Law**: A contracting party is generally not bound to inform the other party of the terms unless those terms are unforeseeable or unusual ([para 12]).
   
2. **Test for iustus error**: The decisive question asks whether the party attempting to rescind the agreement led the other party to reasonably believe they were agreeing to the same terms ([para 9]).

3. **Historical Precedents**: Cited cases like *George v Fairmead (Pty) Ltd* and *National and Overseas Distributors Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Potato Board* provide benchmarks for assessing reasonable mistake ([para 1]). Additionally, the principle established in *Karabus Motors* distinguished culpable fraud induced by an independent party from that which affects contract validity directly from one of the parties.

4. **Capacity as a Trustee**: A trustee, in signing documents, has duties and insights that extend beyond personal ignorance or misunderstanding in relation to the trust they manage ([para 12]).

## A – Application

The court analyzed the nuances of the respondent’s claim of mistake. Although he faced pressure to sign the documents, the court found that he failed to demonstrate a reasonable basis for his misunderstanding regarding the suretyship’s inclusion. The respondent did not engage with the contract’s implications, assuming his brother and nephew had dealt with them adequately. This lack of inquiry into the content of the documents and the assumption of non-implication was insufficient to negate contractual liability ([paras 5–8]).

The appellant did not mislead the respondent, nor did it engage in any negotiation or discussion prior to the signing of the suretyship. Therefore, it could not be held liable for the third-party omissions or misunderstandings that led to the respondent's perceived misapprehension ([paras 10–11]).

Furthermore, the court emphasized that the burden of understanding the signature was on the respondent as a trustee, who ought to have been aware of his duties. Notably, the respondent's argument that he was a farmer not versed in business did not absolve him from the responsibility attached to his role as a trustee ([para 12]). 

Kruger J's view that the suretyship's inclusion in a lengthy bundle of documents constituted a "hidden" liability was found to be erroneous. The court concluded that even a cursory review of the documents would have revealed the suretyship, and the responsibleness for not doing so fell squarely on the respondent ([paras 10–12]).

## C – Conclusion

The appeal was upheld, and the original judgment was overturned, enforcing the suretyship agreement. The court ruled that the respondent could not escape liability due to a unilateral mistake, particularly given his position and responsibilities as a trustee. This decision reinforces the notion that an individual's contractual obligations are not easily set aside by claims of misunderstanding, especially when those claims are based solely on the influence of third parties without culpable conduct from the contracting entity ([paras 13]).

The ruling thereby clarified the application of the doctrine of iustus error in South African contract law, underscoring the necessity for individuals in fiduciary roles to actively engage with their obligations and not rely on assumptions or third-party representations. Ultimately, the judgment affirmed the principle of contractual accountability and the appropriate weight of trust responsibilities in the face of claimed misunderstandings.
