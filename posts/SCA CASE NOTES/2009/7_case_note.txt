Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: KPMG Chartered Accountants v Securefin (644/07)

## F – Facts
The case KPMG Chartered Accountants (SA) v Securefin Ltd and Ing BHF-Bank Aktiengesellschaft involves a complex contractual dispute arising from a procurement agreement between Securefin Ltd (the first respondent) and KNA Insurance & Investment Brokers (Pty) Ltd (KNA), with KPMG Chartered Accountants (SA) (the appellant) serving as the verification agent ([paras 1–2]). Securefin alleged that KPMG breached their obligations under the verification contract, leading to substantial financial losses ([para 1]).

The background details that when the South African Mutual Life Assurance Society decided to demutualize, members needed to have active policies to qualify for share allocations. Consequently, shareholders could either surrender their policies for cash or assign them to third parties wishing to hold onto the policies until maturity ([paras 2–3]).

A procurement contract was entered into on 12 June 1998, which established KNA as the procurement agent for a suite of policies that Securefin intended to acquire ([para 10]). The contract included stipulations regarding the verification procedures for these policies, which were to be verified by KPMG in accordance with specific clauses in the contract ([para 12]).

Disputes arose primarily related to the verification of acquisition prices, with KPMG admitting to relying on information provided by KNA. This led to Securefin overpaying KNA, ultimately causing a financial loss of approximately $40 million when it was revealed that KNA, under the leadership of David Alexander—who was later incarcerated—had manipulated the scheme ([paras 7, 19, 26]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal were focused on:

1. Whether there was a binding verification contract between KPMG and Securefin ([para 16]).
2. The interpretation of the contract, specifically concerning the obligations of KPMG to verify acquisition prices and whether KPMG could qualify its verification certificates ([paras 12–42]).
3. Whether KPMG could rely on the defense of ‘iustus error’ regarding its verification responsibilities ([para 26]) and if the contract was void on grounds of error ([para 19, 35]).

## R – Rules
The court primarily dealt with contractual interpretation and the valid construction of obligations within procurement contracts. Relevant legal authorities included:

1. The **Parol Evidence Rule**, which restricts the use of extrinsic evidence to interpret a contract that is intended to be a complete and final expression of the parties’ agreement ([para 39]).
2. The principle outlined in **Glen Comeragh (Pty) Ltd v Colibri (Pty) Ltd** about the presumption that signing a document implies knowledge of its contents ([para 28]).
3. The standards for admissibility of evidence regarding contractual interpretation, clarified in relevant case law, such as **Johnson v Leal** ([para 39]).

Furthermore, the court examined the role of expert evidence in contract interpretation, deeming that evidence presented by experts should not be used to interpret the meaning of the text but only clarify technical terms ([para 38]).

## A – Application
The court evaluated the evidence regarding the existence of the verification contract, confirming that while KPMG claimed that no binding agreement existed, the facts and findings showed that the procurement contract included KPMG's obligation to verify policy acquisition costs ([para 16]). The ambiguity arising from the letter exchanged between Securefin and KPMG was not enough to negate the existence of a contract, as the intention of both parties was to affirm KPMG's responsibilities ([paras 19, 33]).

In addressing the ‘iustus error’ defense, the court found that KPMG's reliance on information from KNA, particularly from Alexander, constituted a breach of their duty to independently verify acquisition costs ([para 26]). The justifications KPMG provided were deemed insufficient, particularly given the severe implications of overreliance on KNA’s information, which directly led to the financial loss suffered by Securefin ([paras 26–28]).

The court's analysis indicated that the errors claimed by KPMG did not negate its liability. The conclusion was that the so-called 'annotation' regarding maturity dates did not constitute an amendment of the contract that would void it ([para 20]). In evaluating KPMG's performance, the court’s observation of its systematic failure to adhere to the verification obligations led to the dismissal of its appeal for lack of sufficient justification or credible defense ([paras 46]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court’s decision, affirming that KPMG failed to perform its contractual obligations competently, resulting in significant losses for Securefin. The court dismissed KPMG's appeal with costs, including fees for three counsel, reinforcing the duty of verification and the consequences of failing to adhere to contractual responsibilities ([paras 46]). The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of contractual clarity, the significance of independent verification in financial agreements, and the ramifications of errors in financial practices, particularly within the accounting and assurance industry. This case serves as a cautionary tale regarding the duties and responsibilities of professionals acting as third-party verifiers in complex transactions.
