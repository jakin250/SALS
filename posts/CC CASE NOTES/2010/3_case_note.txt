Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

### Case Note: Billiton Aluminium SA Ltd t/a Hillside Aluminium v Khanyile and Others 
**CCT 72/09 [2010] ZACC 3**

#### F – Facts
The case pertains to an employment dispute involving Mr. Ntokozo Archibald Khanyile (the employee) and Billiton Aluminium SA Ltd t/a Hillside Aluminium (the employer) ([para 2]). The employee, who was employed by the employer since 1995, faced dismissal in 2001 after he acted as a witness in a co-employee's arbitration hearing and revealed what was considered confidential information. After the dismissal, the employee contested its fairness, leading to arbitration under the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA). 

The first arbitration, conducted on March 18, 2002, concluded with a ruling in favor of Khanyile, deeming the dismissal substantively unfair and ordering his reinstatement without additional reasons provided. However, the employer’s review application resulted in the Labour Court setting aside this first arbitration award on April 15, 2003, due to procedural irregularities ([para 4–6]). 

Following a second arbitration that commenced in March 2004, the same reinstatement order was issued on April 20, 2004. Nevertheless, subsequent reviews by the Labour Court led to the reversal of the reinstatement order in 2006, wherein compensation was awarded instead ([para 7]). The Labour Appeal Court reinstated Khanyile's position in February 2009, emphasizing errors in the Labour Court's decision-making process ([para 8]). The employer, dissatisfied with this outcome, sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, raising issues regarding systemic delay in the tribunal processes and questioning the "just and equitable" nature of Khanyile's reinstatement ([para 11, 12]).

#### I – Issues
The primary legal issue under review was whether an appellate court, including the Labour Appeal Court, had a constitutional duty to consider new facts arising after decisions were made at lower levels, specifically in regard to the remedy of reinstatement ([para 1, 12]). Furthermore, related questions were whether there was sufficient basis for the employer's requests for condonation due to late submissions and if a just and equitable remedy could be granted despite the delays experienced in the adjudication process ([para 13–15]).

#### R – Rules
The case cited Section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution, which empowers courts to make orders that are just and equitable when ruling on constitutional matters, and 193 of the LRA, which addresses the remedies available upon finding that a dismissal was unfair ([para 32]). Several precedential cases were also reviewed, including *Equity Aviation Services (Pty) Ltd v Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration and Others* [2009] ZACC 16, which suggested that reinstatement is the primary remedy for unfair dismissal ([para 26–27]).

The judgment also referenced the necessity of timely raising constitutional issues in earlier courts and the principle that new evidence should generally not be introduced at advanced stages of appellate review unless exceptionality justifies such inclusion ([para 16–19]).

#### A – Application
The Court found that the delay inherent in the process, while regrettable, did not warrant the development of a new constitutional rule as the employer had failed to timely raise relevant concerns regarding the appropriateness of reinstatement ([para 51]). The employer's argument centered on a supposed constitutional obligation for the Labour Appeal Court to evaluate after-the-fact evidence regarding a remedy, spurred by systemic delays. However, the Court determined that the employer’s own procedural choices contributed significantly to both the delay and the constitutional question raised, ultimately failing to meet the burden of justification necessary to seek an alternative remedy beyond reinstatement ([para 49–53]).

In terms of the condonation applications, the Court recognized that all parties offered valid explanations for their delays, which did not cause identifiable prejudice to either side. Hence, it granted condonation ([para 13–14]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court denied the employer's leave to appeal on the basis that the core arguments, particularly regarding the systemic delay and appropriateness of reinstatement, lacked timeliness and substantial merit. The Court upheld the Labour Appeal Court's findings that reinstatement was appropriate given the circumstances documented in the arbitration process and the lack of empirical basis to question the remedy retrospectively ([para 54]). This case reinforces judicial adherence to procedural protocol within labour disputes and emphasizes the significance of timely action in the adjudication of constitutional issues related to employment law. 

The implications of this judgment reiterate the established rationale in South African labour law that reinstatement is the primary form of remedy for unjust dismissals, even under systemic delays in legal processes. The judgment further elucidates the boundaries of judicial intervention vis-a-vis evidence and remedy evaluation in appellate proceedings, showcasing the balance between adherence to legislative frameworks and judicial discretion in ensuring equitable treatment of employees ([para 32–44]).
