Supreme Court of Appeal

Educated Risk Investments 165 (Pty) Ltd v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality ([2016] ZASCA 67)

The case revolves around an informal settlement on Payneville Extension 3, known as Everest or Gugulethu.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 67)

F – Facts ([paras 1–2])

The case revolves around an informal settlement on Payneville Extension 3, known as Everest or Gugulethu. Located on the outskirts of Springs, this settlement lacks basic amenities such as potable water, sewage systems, and electricity, posing health risks to its residents, particularly from radon gas emissions ([1]). The Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (Ekurhuleni), as the responsible authority, was obligated under Section 26 of the South African Constitution to provide adequate housing and ensure a dignified living environment for residents ([1]).

Ekurhuleni owned the adjacent Payneville Extension 1, a 63-hectare approved township zoned as Residential 1, which allowed for the construction of one dwelling house per erf ([2]). In February 2012, the local authority approved the subdivision of several erven in Payneville Extension 1, increasing the number of Residential 1 erven to 1115 ([2]). Ekurhuleni planned to temporarily relocate families from the informal settlement to these erven, allowing them to erect informal dwellings until proper housing could be provided. This plan was met with opposition from the appellants—three companies and an individual, who alleged that the further subdivision was an unlawful attempt to establish an informal settlement and violated the terms of the town planning scheme ([4]).

I – Issues ([paras 10–11], [36])

The central issues before the court were:

  1. Zoning Compliance: Whether the informal housing to be erected on Payneville Extension 1 contravened the Residential 1 zoning stipulations defined within the Springs Town Planning Scheme, particularly concerning what constituted a "dwelling house" under the existing zoning regulations ([10]).
  2. Authority to Amend Usage: Whether Ekurhuleni’s authority included the ability to use the property for purposes not in accordance with its zoning, especially in a manner beneficial to the community as envisaged by the Town Planning and Townships Ordinance ([36]).

The appellants contended that allowing informal housing would amount to a backdoor rezoning and was inconsistent with the approved conditions of the township development, which were not fully met before occupation could be allowed ([4], [8], [29]).

R – Rules ([paras 12–18], [36])

Legislation and Precedents

  1. Town Planning Scheme Definitions: The Springs Town Planning Scheme defines a "dwelling house" as a free-standing dwelling unit designed for occupation by a single family, which does not limit the materials used for construction ([12], [16]).
  2. Section 26 of the Constitution provides for the right to adequate housing, mandating that state obligations must be progressively realized, further emphasized by the Housing Act 107 of 1997 and the principles of social justice ([1]).
  3. Clause 32 of the Springs Town Planning Scheme allows the local authority to use property in any zoning for purposes deemed beneficial to the community, thereby providing Ekurhuleni with some flexibility in its developmental plans ([36]).

Relevant Case Law

  • Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality ([2012] ZASCA 13) illustrates the necessity of contextual interpretation when assessing property law.
  • Transvaal Consolidated Land and Exploration Co Ltd v Black (1929) acknowledged the diverse ways in which residences could be construed, further supporting a wider understanding of "dwelling houses" as inclusive of informal structures.

A – Application ([paras 18–24], [29], [36])

Interpretation of Zoning

The court analyzed whether informal housing could be classified as "dwelling houses." The judgment asserted that the term 'dwelling house' in the context of the Springs Town Planning Scheme should not be strictly confined to traditional brick-and-mortar houses but must accommodate diverse forms accommodating housing needs ([19]).

The court identified that even temporary structures made of materials such as wood and corrugated iron could be classified as "dwelling houses" under the Scheme. This flexibility acknowledges the urgent housing crisis faced by many South Africans and aligns with the obligations of dignified living standards established by the Constitution ([20]).

Ekurhuleni’s Authority

The court also addressed the appellants' assertion that the occupation of Payneville Extension 1 was contingent upon fulfilling all development conditions attached to the township’s approval. The judgment articulated that Ekurhuleni was permitted to use its property in a way that would provide temporary housing, notwithstanding the incomplete infrastructure development – stressing that buildings could be erected as long as they conform to zoning regulations ([30]).

In concluding, the court emphasized the provision in Clause 32 of the Springs Town Planning Scheme, affirming that Ekurhuleni’s plans did not constitute a circumvention of the Scheme but exercised its lawful discretion to address community needs ([36]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 36–38])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, confirming that Ekurhuleni’s plans to relocate families to Payneville Extension 1 by allowing the erection of informal housing were lawful within the context of the Scheme. The interpretation favored a broader understanding of "dwelling houses," recognizing the dire housing needs of the community and the local authority's obligations under the Constitution.

The court’s ruling not only validated Ekurhuleni’s approach to resident relocation and socio-economic upliftment but also set a significant precedent for local authorities navigating similar urban housing crises. The implications of this judgment indicate a shift toward accommodating informal settlements within existing zoning frameworks, reflecting a pressing need to balance legal parameters with social realities in the realm of urban planning and development in South Africa.
