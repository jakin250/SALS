Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1998

# Case Note (FIRAC Method): Republic of South Africa – T G Manyasha v The Minister of Law and Order

## F – Facts
The appellant, T G Manyasha, filed a summons on 8 November 1993 against the respondent, The Minister of Law and Order, in the Grahamstown Magistrate's Court, seeking damages for an alleged assault on his minor son, Sonwabo. The summons was served on the respondent on 11 November 1993. The respondent entered an appearance to defend on 2 December 1993. A request for further particulars was made by the respondent on 8 December 1993, but the appellant only delivered these particulars on 13 February 1995, after repeated requests from the respondent, which included threats of an application to compel (which never occurred) ([paras 2–3]).

On 6 April 1995, the respondent filed a special plea asserting that the summons had lapsed in accordance with Rule 10 of the Magistrates' Court Rules. Subsequently, on 6 November 1995, the appellant sought to extend the twelve-month period provided for in Rule 10 by invoking Rule 60(5). The magistrate dismissed this application, upheld the special plea, and imposed costs. The appellant appealed unsuccessfully to the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court but was later granted leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 4–6]).

## I – Issues
The case presented two main legal questions regarding the interpretation of the Magistrates' Court Rules:

1. **Does Rule 10 apply to both defended and undefended actions, or only to undefended actions?**
2. **Can Rule 60(5) be invoked to revive a summons that has lapsed pursuant to Rule 10?** ([paras 2–4]).

## R – Rules
The pertinent rules considered were:

1. **Rule 10**: This rule states that if a summons is not served within twelve months of its issue or, having been served, no further steps in the prosecution are taken within that timeframe, the summons shall lapse. It also includes a proviso for extensions under certain conditions ([para 4]).

2. **Rule 60(5)**: This rule allows for the extension of any time limit prescribed by the rules, subject to the written consent of the opposite party or, if consent is refused, by the court on application ([para 18]).

The court also referenced historical precedents and previous decisions, including *Sibiya v Minister of Police* and *Langenhoven v Comyn*, which noted conflicting interpretations regarding the applicability of Rule 10 ([para 5]).

## A – Application
In interpreting Rule 10, the court addressed the meaning of "lapse" and held that it signifies that a summons has lost its legal efficacy once the twelve-month period has expired without the necessary further action being taken by the plaintiff. The court noted that the historical context and changes in the wording of the rule suggested that the intention of the rule-maker was to apply Rule 10 to all actions, including defended ones, to penalize inaction by a plaintiff ([paras 12–14]). 

The court further reasoned that the objectives of Rule 10—to prevent undue delays in litigation—apply equally to defended and undefended actions. This finding was supported by the principle that no reasonable distinction could be drawn between a plaintiff who fails to act after the issue and service of a summons, regardless of whether the action is defended or not ([paras 16–17]).

Regarding Rule 60(5), the court examined whether a summons that has lapsed could still be revived. The court rejected the argument that the revival was not possible due to the definitive nature of the term "lapse." Instead, it reasoned that the absence of a specific exclusion in Rule 60(5) concerning Rule 10 allowed for an extension and subsequent revival of the summons. Thus, the court concluded that a magistrate's discretion could be invoked to extend the time limit imposed by Rule 10, allowing for the revival of the lapsed summons ([paras 27–30]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that Rule 10 applies to both defended and undefended actions, reaffirming the decision of the lower court and previous precedents that support this interpretation. Moreover, the court concluded that Rule 60(5) does allow for an extension of the twelve-month period under Rule 10, facilitating the revival of lapsed summonses, which serves the overarching goal of ensuring that litigants can pursue their claims without losing legal recourse due to procedural delays.

The court's order included the allowance of the appeal, set aside the previous court's decision, granted the appellant's application for an extension under Rule 60(5), and dismissed the special plea with costs ([paras 30–31]). 

This judgment underscores the need for clarity in the application of procedural rules, balancing the importance of timely litigation with the principle of access to justice.
