Supreme Court of Appeal

BS v PS (291/2017) [2018] ZASCA 37

This case revolves around the divorce proceedings between two parties, BS (the appellant) and PS (the respondent), who had been married for approximately 28 years.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 37

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–30])

This case revolves around the divorce proceedings between two parties, BS (the appellant) and PS (the respondent), who had been married for approximately 28 years. Following irreconcilable differences, the appellant initiated divorce proceedings in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, which included a request for the division of their marital property. Their antenuptial contract specified that the net values of their respective estates at the commencement of the marriage were ‘nil.’

In the proceedings, it was established that the appellant’s estate did not show any accrual during the marriage, whereas the respondent’s estate showed significant growth, evidenced by their matrimonial property valued at R1,450,000 and a pension interest valued at R4,537,231. The High Court ordered a partial forfeiture of the appellant's rights to share in the accrual of the respondent’s estate, attributing an 80% forfeiture due to the appellant's infidelity which purportedly contributed to the breakdown of the marriage.

The High Court also deferred the payment of the appellant’s share in the respondent's pension benefits under the Government Employees Pension Fund until the respondent’s pension benefits accrued. The appellant appealed this decision, leading to the current proceedings before the Supreme Court of Appeal.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–30])

The appeal presented two primary legal questions for the court to resolve:

  1. Whether the High Court correctly ordered the partial forfeiture of the appellant's interest in the respondent's estate under the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984, particularly in light of the appellant's contributions to the marriage and the mutual infidelity of both parties.
  2. Whether the High Court's deferral of the pension benefit payment to the appellant, made in accordance with section 10 of the Matrimonial Property Act and section 24A of the Government Employees Pension Law, was lawful and appropriate given the legislative intent of these provisions.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 16–28])

The court primarily relied on the provisions of:

  1. Divorce Act 70 of 1979: Specifically, section 7(8)(a)(i) relates to the assignment of pension interests and the equitable division of property in divorce proceedings.
  2. Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984: Particularly, sections 9 and 10 govern accrual systems and the circumstances under which a court may order the deferral of payment for accrual claims.
  3. Government Employees Pension Law, 1966: Section 24A, which was introduced in the GEPL Amendment Act 19 of 2011, serves to ensure that a non-member spouse does not have to await an exit event to realize their pension interest upon divorce. The intent of the amendment was to achieve a ‘clean-break’ principle in divorce settlements.

The decision also navigated through precedent established in Wiese v Government Employees Pension Fund & others [2012] ZACC 5, which articulated the historical context and objectives behind the legislative changes to divorce and pension law.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 10–30])

The Supreme Court of Appeal found merit in the appellant's grievances regarding both the forfeiture of her interest in the estate and the deferral of her pension benefits.

  1. Forfeiture of Accrual: In assessing the High Court's reasoning for the partial forfeiture, the Supreme Court highlighted the shared responsibility for the breakdown of the marriage. Both parties were infidel, and thus the court held that it was unjust to place the blame solely on the appellant, whose contributions to the marital finances were substantial. The court emphasized that the appellant's substantial contributions were critical to the respondent's ability to maintain the matrimonial household. The Supreme Court, therefore, overturned the High Court's forfeiture decision, affirming the appellant’s right to her proportional share of the estate.
  2. Deferral of Pension Payments: The court determined that the High Court erred in ordering the deferral of pension payment. It held that section 24A of the GEPL explicitly aims to negate such deferral for non-member spouses, emphasizing that pension interests assigned via divorce decrees must be paid without delay. The Supreme Court underscored that allowing a deferral conflicts with legislative intentions to facilitate a clear and prompt resolution concerning pension interests and affirmed that delays would undermine the ‘clean-break’ principle.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 1–30])

The Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the appeal, ruling that the High Court erred both in ordering partial forfeiture of the appellant’s accrual claim and in permitting payment deferment of her pension benefit. Consequently, the court instructed the respondent to pay the appellant R433,000 in satisfaction of her matrimonial claims and mandated the Government Employees Pension Fund to process the pension interest payment within statutory timelines without deferral.

The ruling carries significant implications for the application of family law in South Africa, reinforcing the respect for spouses’ contributions irrespective of marital challenges. The court’s interpretation of the applicable laws reinstates the urgency of resolving pension benefits post-divorce, thereby ensuring equitable treatment of non-member spouses in financial settlements. This case underscores the evolving legal landscape regarding the treatment of marital assets and supports a more just approach to property division in divorce proceedings.
