FIRAC Case Note: H M v A M (2019) ZASCA 12

F – Facts
The case pertains to an appeal against a decision by the Gauteng Division of the High Court regarding the validity of a postnuptial agreement between H M (Appellant) and A M (Respondent). The parties were married out of community of property, excluding the accrual system. The postnuptial agreement was drafted by A M in April or May 2014, and was presented to H M for his signature eight months prior to the initiation of divorce proceedings, which began on January 15, 2015 ([1–4]).

The agreement stipulated that H M would waive his rights in relation to the estate, granting A M half of his net assets, reflecting a commitment to provide maintenance. Initially, H M refused to sign the agreement, deeming its contents "ridiculous." He eventually signed it on November 10, 2014, without any discussions about divorce occurring at that time. The marital relationship continued until a confrontation on November 30, 2014, after A M discovered H M's extramarital affair. Subsequently, A M sought a declaratory order asserting that the agreement was valid and enforceable due to its issuance in contemplation of divorce. The Mbombela Regional Court, however, found the agreement to be invalid ([5–6]).

Upon appeal, the high court overruled the regional court's decision, asserting that the agreement constituted a binding settlement made with the contemplation of divorce. The high court failed to convert the agreement into an order of court per the Divorce Act, prompting H M to appeal against this finding ([6–7]).

I – Issues
The key legal question revolves around whether the postnuptial agreement was valid and enforceable as a settlement agreement, specifically if it was concluded in contemplation of divorce ([4, 8]). Additionally, the case raises the issue of whether a court can enforce an agreement made outside the context of ongoing litigation, based on the requirements of the Divorce Act ([10]).

R – Rules
The legal framework governing this case includes the Divorce Act 70 of 1979 and the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984. The latter’s Section 21 demands court approval for any change in matrimonial property regime. Furthermore, precedents such as Eke v Parsons [2015] ZACC 30 illuminate that an agreement must relate to an ongoing legal issue to be enforced by a court ([10]).

The court highlighted the standard of proof placed on the respondent, who had to establish that divorce was contemplated at the time of signing the agreement ([10]).

A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal examined the surrounding circumstances of the signing of the agreement and the parties' subsequent interactions. The court noted that A M had not successfully proven that divorce was contemplated when the agreement was signed. Testimonies suggested that the marital relationship proceeded normally post-signing until the confrontation over the extramarital affair ([9–14]).

The court rigorously addressed the assertion that the agreement was an "insurance policy," reinforcing that the lack of detail on divorce-related matters undermined A M's position. Recognizing that no divorce proceedings were initiated at the time of signing, the court concluded that A M did not meet the burden of proof regarding the anticipation of divorce, and hence the agreement was not valid ([8–14]).

Consequently, the court found that the high court had erred in ruling the agreement as enforceable, as it failed the necessary legal standards established in prior cases. The ruling also mandated remitting the issue of maintenance back to the regional court, helping to ensure that any dependent children received appropriate financial support despite the invalidation of the agreement ([15]).

C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal with costs, including the expenses incurred for two counsels, and replaced the prior ruling with a dismissal of A M’s claims concerning the agreement. The court asserted that no enforceable settlement existed between the parties at the relevant time, which has significant implications for the enforceability of similar agreements entered into absent clear contemplation of divorce ([15]).

This judgement emphasizes the necessity for competent and relevant legal frameworks within matrimonial contexts and reinforces the burden of proof on the parties claiming such agreements as enforceable. This decision serves to clarify the boundaries of marital agreements particularly in relation to divorce proceedings, indicating a court-regulated approach to the enforcement of private settlements made in sensitive personal contexts.
