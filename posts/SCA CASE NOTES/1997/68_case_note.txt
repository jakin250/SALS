Case Note: REPUBLIEK VAN SUID-AFRIKA
Source Folder: 1997

### Case Note: REPUBLIEK VAN SUID-AFRIKA: RANDBURG TOWN COUNCIL v KERKSAY INVESTMENTS (PTY) LTD

#### F – Facts   
[2] The respondent, Kerksay Investments (Pty) Ltd (the claimant), is the registered owner of erf 843, Ferndale, Randburg, as per a deed of transfer dated 3 July 1974. The property, bordered by Oak Avenue, was partially expropriated by the appellant, Randburg Town Council (the council), through a notice of expropriation effective on 3 April 1990. The expropriation pertained to a road widening servitude comprising 147.83 square meters. Under section 12(1)(b) of the Expropriation Act 63 of 1975, the compensation for such an expropriation could not exceed an amount to cover "any actual financial loss or inconvenience caused by the expropriation."

[3] The council offered R1,401 as compensation—R1,400 for seven trees in the servitude area plus R1 for the land. This offer was rejected by Kerksay, which claimed R40,000. The compensation court ultimately dismissed the claimant's compensation claim, leading Kerksay to appeal successfully to the Transvaal Provincial Division, which awarded R40,000 (minus R1,401 previously paid) along with interest and costs. Leave to appeal was subsequently granted to the council.

[4-6] The issues arose following an amendment made to the zoning of the property in November 1974 and the subsequent impact of a new town-planning scheme effective from 29 December 1976. The 1976 scheme limited the use and anticipated diminishing value of the property, though no claim for compensation was filed by Kerksay within the stipulated time, and no development occurred to exploit the new zoning. The relationship between the expropriation and these prior regulations formed the basis of the legal dispute. 

#### I – Issues   
[11-12] The central issue for determination by the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the depreciation in the value of the claimant's property, resulting from the 1976 town-planning scheme, should be disregarded when calculating the compensation due under the Expropriation Act. Specifically, this required an interpretation of section 12(5)(f) of the Act, which provides restrictions on taking into account enhancements or depreciations caused by the expropriation purpose or related state actions. 

#### R – Rules  
[9-10] Section 12(5)(f) of the Expropriation Act stipulates that any enhancement or depreciation in the property value caused by its expropriation purpose, or connected state actions, shall not be considered in compensation determinations. The Act's longstanding rules emphasize preventing property owners from benefitting from value depreciation as a result of state actions, which has also been established in case law, such as the Pointe Gourde principle noted in relevant precedents from England, exemplified by *Pointe Gourde Quarrying and Transport Co Ltd v Sub-Intendent of Crown Lands* [1947] AC 565 (PC).

[8] The 1976 town-planning scheme's implications and its relation to prior zoning regulations were crucial here. By virtue of these regulations, potential claims for compensation initially available to the claimant under section 45 of the Town-planning and Township’s Ordinance 25 of 1965 were significant in assessing the legality of claims post-expropriation.

#### A – Application   
[14-19] In applying the aforementioned rules, the court examined the evidence surrounding the zoning amendments, establishing that the claimant had failed to develop the property following the changes in regulation. Although the compensation court concluded that the 1976 scheme did not materially influence the property's value, this interpretation contradicted the parties' pre-trial agreement and the fundamental principles enshrined in the Expropriation Act. 

The court opined that, based on the facts agreed upon, the evident purpose of the state’s actions in relation to the property (the widening of Oak Avenue) was straightforward, thus supporting the claimant’s contention that the depreciation resulting from the 1976 scheme was relevant for the compensation calculation. Particularly, the Act's wording was consistent, allowing for disregarding depreciations incident to the expropriation process, irrespective of previous zoning encumbrances that might have affected the property.

[17-19] The arguments presented by the council’s counsel—particularly surrounding the notion that prior acknowledgments of depreciation should adjust compensatory calculations—lacked substantive grounding within the Act as it stood, and as such, were dismissed by the court. The legislative intent did not appear to support a bifurcation of depreciation, neither for reasons of compensatory fairness nor due to the manner in which the compensation ought to be calculated.

#### C – Conclusion  
[25-26] The Court ultimately confirmed the decision of the court a quo, ruling against the council's appeal. Retirement of the interpretation formed here underscores the need for plain meanings of legislative languages when interpreting statutes governing expropriation. The depreciation in the property due to the 1976 town-planning scheme must be disregarded in the compensation calculation, a principle consistent with the legislative aim of the Expropriation Act. 

The Court further directed that, in the event of a successful appeal on cost, the interest awarded should reflect the original agreement between the parties. This ruling reinforces vital tenets of statutory construction in expropriation law while clarifying the importance of adhering strictly to agreed-upon terms in pre-trial contexts, which ultimately shapes jurisprudential calibration concerning property rights in South Africa. 

This decision reaffirms the importance of considering statutory provisions in their literal sense, thus providing a necessary balance between the rights of individual property owners and the state's authority to expropriate for public use under the relevant legal framework.
