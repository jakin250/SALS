Phineas Lekolwane and Elizabeth Lekolwane v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development [2008] ZACC 18

Constitutional Court 2008 [2008] ZACC 18

F – Facts

The applicants, Mr. Phineas Lekolwane and Mrs. Elizabeth Lekolwane, sought reinstatement of their application for leave to appeal, which had been struck from the roll on 22 August 2006. The refusal for condonation and postponement led to the striking of their appeal, which concerns the interpretation of a regulation governing financial allowances for individuals in a witness protection programme ([1–2]). This application arose after a history of postponements, where the first applicant was warned that no further postponements would be granted unless good cause was shown ([3]). The applicants returned to court nearly two years later, on 4 June 2008, requesting the reinstatement of their application ([6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue at hand is whether the applicants could demonstrate sufficient "good cause" and a satisfactory explanation for their delay in proceeding with their application for leave to appeal. This includes assessing the importance of procedural adherence in judicial proceedings and the balance between the interests of justice and an applicant's right to appeal ([5–7]).

R – Rules

In addressing reinstatement applications, the Constitutional Court emphasized the necessity for applicants to provide a compelling justification for their delay and for the merits of their case. The standard for reinstating an application requires that it be in the interests of justice ([4]). The Court relied on precedents from prior cases, including its earlier ruling in Lekolwane and Another v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development [2006] ZACC 19, which underscored the importance of timely and responsible conduct by litigants ([4]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the Court found that the affidavit submitted by the applicants did not adequately substantiate their claims or highlight the reasons for their delay since the original ruling ([5]). They argued that they required a new legal team and needed to apply for legal aid anew, yet failed to clarify why such steps were necessary, particularly given the nearly two-year gap from granting legal aid to the current application. The Court also noted the lack of a convincing explanation for the delay following the approval of legal aid, thus failing to meet the criteria of 'good cause' necessary for reinstatement ([6]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately denied the reinstatement of the application for leave to appeal, reinforcing the principle that litigants must adhere strictly to procedural rules and demonstrate unequivocal reasons for any delays in their dealings with the court. The implications of this ruling stress the necessity for diligence in the legal process, particularly for individuals under the witness protection programme, thereby setting a precedent for future cases involving similar procedural lapses ([7]).

The Court's order was clear: the application is refused, and there is no order as to costs, which illustrates a measure of leniency towards the applicants despite their procedural missteps ([4]). This outcome reflects the Court's commitment to ensuring that the legal process is respected, even while balancing the need for fairness in allowing litigants the opportunity to pursue their legal rights.
