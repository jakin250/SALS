Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: Kouga Municipality v Bellingan (121/11) [2011] ZASCA 222

### F – Facts

The appellant, Kouga Municipality, sought to appeal a decision by the Eastern Cape High Court, Grahamstown, which had declared the Kouga Municipality Liquor (Trading Hours) By-law invalid. The respondents, Mark Bellingan, The Mexican CC, and Rapicorp 118CC, were registered to sell liquor under the Eastern Cape Liquor Act 10 of 2003.

The by-law was promulgated on 27 December 2006 and initially advertised in the Herald and Die Burger newspapers in December 2004 and again via local newspapers in February 2006 ([1]–[4]). The Municipality argued that the respondents' application for judicial review should have been dismissed due to significant delays and adherence to procedural requirements under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([4]).

The by-law purported to regulate liquor trading hours but suffered from procedural failings. The High Court found that the Municipality did not adequately inform the public about the changes in the by-law and thus did not comply with the legal requirements for public consultation stipulated under section 160(4)(b) of the Constitution and section 12(3)(b) of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 ([5]–[10]).

### I – Issues

The main legal issue dealt with the validity of the Kouga Municipality Liquor (Trading Hours) By-law due to alleged non-compliance with statutory requirements for public notice and consultation. The court also had to address whether the order issued by the court a quo, which suspended the declaration of invalidity for twelve months, was appropriate and justified under administrative law principles ([3]–[4]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework relevant to the case comprised the following:

1. **Section 160(4)(b) of the Constitution:** Requires that any proposed by-law must be published for public comment.
2. **Section 12(3)(b) of the Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000:** Amplifies the constitutional requirement by mandating that such publication allow the public an opportunity to make representations concerning the proposed by-law.
3. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA):** Establishes procedures for judicial review of administrative actions, suggesting that applications for review must be brought without unreasonable delay.
4. **Principles from Oudekraal Estates Pty Ltd v City of Cape Town:** Distinction between direct and defensive challenges to the validity of administrative acts ([12]).

### A – Application

The court found that the processes undertaken by the Municipality were inadequate as the public was not properly notified about the changes to the by-law. The 2006 publication of the draft by-law did not sufficiently inform the public of its contents, significantly impairing the opportunity for public participation, which was essential for compliance with the constitutional framework ([6]–[10]).

The Municipality's argument that the earlier notices provided adequate notice was rejected, as the changes between the two drafts were substantial enough to warrant republication ([9]). The court emphasized that the public’s right to comment must be safeguarded and that merely relying on post-publication meetings did not satisfy the statutory requirements ([10]–[18]). The court further clarified the distinction between direct challenges (where a party seeks to nullify an administrative act) and defensive challenges (used as a defense against legal action based on an administrative act).

Specifically, the court noted that the reliance on PAJA and the procedure required for review was misapplied by the Kouga Municipality. In the case at hand, the applicants were entitled to mount a defensive challenge without facing the limitations posed by a direct challenge approach ([15]–[18]).

In terms of relief, the court deemed that the order of the High Court was not suitable, as it effectively left the applicants in a worse position, subjecting them to prosecution under the invalid by-law during the suspension period. The court noted that it would be equitable to issue a declaratory order stating that the by-law was invalid specifically for purposes of prosecution, rendering any criminal proceedings void and ensuring compliance with the rule of law ([17]–[21]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court's declaration that the Kouga Municipality Liquor (Trading Hours) By-law was invalid regarding the prosecution of the respondents. The court substituted a declaration that the by-law was invalid for prosecution of the applicants, which provided the necessary legal certainty, as the Municipality’s failure to comply with statutory processes could not impose penal liability on the respondents ([22]).

The court concluded that this ruling not only rectified the immediate injustices faced by the respondents but also served to reinforce the necessity for proper public consultation in the law-making processes at the municipal level. This judgment is a critical affirmation of administrative law principles, emphasizing the role of open governance and public input in the legislative process.
