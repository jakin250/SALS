### FIRAC Case Note: Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council

#### F – Facts

The case at hand arose from a significant increase in property rates levied within the Eastern Metropolitan Substructure (EMS) by the Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council (TMC) in June 1996, impacting various property owners (the appellants) in the Witwatersrand area ([1]–[2]). Historically, the local government framework had been characterized by significant disparities between affluent “White” areas and underdeveloped “Black,” “Coloured,” and “Indian” areas, particularly in terms of municipal services and property rates ([2]). The interim Constitution and the Local Government Transition Act (LGTA) established a new governance framework, mandating the restructuring of local government without prescribing specific methodologies ([3]–[4]).

Premier’s Proclamation 24 of 1994 initially unified local government structures, bringing together disparate councils under a TMC endowed with extensive powers to facilitate governance reform ([5]–[7]). While initially funded through intergovernmental transfers and limited property rates, the TMC moved towards imposing rates as delegated functions progressed ([7]). By June 1996, a uniform rate of 6.45 cents per Rand was established, which corresponded to a budgetary approach intended to ensure fiscal equilibrium across various substructures ([12]–[14]).

Dissatisfied with the new rates' impact—where some regional property owners saw substantial increases while others experienced decreases—the appellants approached the Witwatersrand High Court to declare the TMC’s decisions illegal under the provisions of the interim Constitution and the LGTA ([15]). The High Court dismissed their application, prompting an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which subsequently referred key constitutional matters to the Constitutional Court for determination ([17]–[20]).

#### I – Issues

The Constitutional Court was tasked with determining:

1. Whether the resolutions by the TMC regarding the rate hike and inter-substructure levies were consistent with the interim Constitution ([20]).
2. Whether the interim Constitution preserved jurisdiction for the SCA regarding common law challenges to administrative actions by the TMC and the EMS ([20]).

#### R – Rules

The court's analysis centered on the interim Constitution, particularly sections 178(2) and 24, which stipulate powers for local governance and the requirements for lawful administrative action, respectively ([21]–[22]). Section 178(2) permits local governments to levy property rates, provided these adhere to a uniform structure, whereas section 24 secures a right to procedural fairness in administrative actions ([44]). Relevant statutes included the LGTA and Proclamation 35, which redefined the roles and responsibilities of the TMC in the transitional local governance framework ([8]–[9]).

The key judicial precedents drawn upon involve the principles of administrative justice and the interpretation of local government agencies' powers under constitutional law. Administrative actions are deemed subject to judicial review based on legality and adherence to constitutional imperatives ([25]–[30]).

#### A – Application

The Court held that while the rates and levies established by the TMC were considered legislative, not administrative, actions under the interim Constitution, this characterization affected the review standards applied to such decisions ([45]–[46]). As such, the core issue was whether the TMC operated within its constitutional bounds regarding the levies imposed on the EMS and NMS.

The appellants argued that the levies were calculated based on necessity for the TMC's functions rather than the EMS’s requirements, infringing on section 178(2)’s stipulation for a uniform structure. However, the Court ultimately resolved that the TMC’s approach of applying a uniform rate across substructures constituted an equitable means of redistributing resources among them, thereby complying with the constitutional and legislative frameworks ([61]–[64]).

The Court further noted that the TMC's reliance on budgetary estimates and its deliberative processes in configuring proposed rates sufficed to meet the necessary requirement of substantive fairness and legislative compliance in authority allocation ([65]–[72]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Court concluded that the resolutions from the TMC regarding the levies imposed on the EMS and NMS were not inconsistent with the interim Constitution, as they adhered to the mandatory principles for local governance established therein ([114]). It emphasized the necessity of the levies contingent on the systemic restructuring aimed at addressing historical inequities, dismissing the appeal with costs ([115]–[116]).

The judgment underscored the evolving nature of South Africa's local governance and the inherent complexities involved in transitioning from an apartheid legacy to a more integrative democratic framework, establishing key precedents for future local government operations under the Constitution ([117]–[118]).
