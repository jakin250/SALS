Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2004

# FIRAC Case Note: Koos Stevens v. The State

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No:** 417/03  
**Date of Hearing:** 26 August 2004  
**Date of Delivery:** 2 September 2004  

**Coram:** Navsa, Van Heerden JJA et Ponnan AJA

---

## F – Facts 

The appellant, Koos Stevens, a detective sergeant at the Claremont police station, was charged with three counts of indecent assault against three complainants—Rachel Vuyiswa Gxekwa, Samantha Lumkwana, and Norooi Gogotya—following an incident that occurred on 7 August 1998 ([paras 3-4]). The events unfolded after Rachel was arrested for shoplifting, at which point she was questioned by Stevens. According to Rachel, Stevens made her undress and inappropriately touched her ([para 5]). Subsequently, he brought her two friends, Samantha and Norooi, to the police station under the pretext of questioning them about their involvement in the theft ([paras 6-7]). 

The prosecution's case relied solely on the testimony of the three complainants, all of whom alleged similar indecent behaviors from Stevens. They reported the incidents to Bantoe Bonwana, who confronted Stevens immediately afterward ([paras 7-9]). The defence rested on Stevens' testimony that he did not commit any indecent acts, asserting he was merely conducting an investigation ([para 13]). 

The trial court convicted Stevens, highlighting the credibility of the complainants' testimony—despite acknowledging they were single witnesses—and sentenced him to one year of imprisonment for each count ([paras 3, 14]). The Cape High Court upheld the conviction, leading to the further appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 3, 16]).

---

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case relate to the assessment of witness credibility and whether the trial and subsequent appellate courts had adequately adhered to the necessary legal standards in evaluating evidence provided by single witnesses—particularly in cases involving sexual assault ([paras 2, 18-19]). The court was tasked with determining whether the prior courts had properly applied the cautionary rules regarding the evidence of single witnesses and if they correctly weighed this evidence against the probabilities established within the case ([paras 18, 29]).

---

## R – Rules

In assessing the reliability of evidence provided by single witnesses, the courts are guided by the provisions of section 208 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which permits conviction based solely on the testimony of a competent witness. However, established jurisprudence dictates that such evidence must be approached with caution, particularly regarding the assessment of credibility ([paras 17-18]). Notably, the Supreme Court referred to multiple leading cases, including *S v Webber* and *S v Sauls*, to underscore the necessity of a holistic review of the evidence ([para 17]). Furthermore, the court highlighted the skepticism surrounding the resolution of contradictions in witness testimony and the inherent probabilities of the case ([paras 19-20, 29]).

---

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal conducted a meticulous review of the evidence and the lower courts' decisions. It found that the magistrate had improperly assessed the single witnesses' testimony and failed to apply the cautionary approach necessary for such cases ([paras 18, 21]). The evidence presented had significant contradictions and showed discrepancies when compared to police statements made shortly after the events ([paras 20-21]). 

Moreover, the testimonies of the complainants regarding their circumstances and actions following the alleged assaults raised critical doubts about their credibility. The Court noted that the complainants had opportunities to discuss their experiences, contrary to the magistrate's finding that they had not ([paras 22-24]). Notably, the appellant’s version also had plausible elements, as it remained consistent with the presence of other personnel at the police station during the questioned times ([paras 26-27]).

Ultimately, the Supreme Court emphasized that the individual components of each witness's testimony should not be assessed in compartments but rather within the totality of evidence presented and its inherent probabilities ([paras 18, 29]). With material contradictions and a failure to convincingly affirm the reliability of the complainants' testimonies, it concluded that the prosecution had not met the burden of proof beyond a reasonable doubt.

---

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the evidence, when considered in its entirety, did not support the indecent assault charges against Koos Stevens. Thus, the Court overturned the previous convictions and sentences, emphasizing the necessity of rigorous scrutiny when evaluating the testimony of single witnesses within sexual assault cases ([paras 30]). The case underscored the judicial principles regarding the assessment of evidence in criminal trials, outlining the obligation of courts to uphold evidentiary standards and procedural safeguards, especially in protecting the rights of the accused when faced with allegations of serious crimes such as indecent assault.

This decision highlights the delicate balance between acknowledging and addressing the needs of vulnerable complainants while ensuring that fundamental rights to a fair trial and due process are preserved ([para 1, 2]). 

--- 

The implications of this judgment extend beyond the immediate outcome for the appellant; it serves as a clarion call for judicial panels to rigorously apply principles of law regarding witness credibility and evidentiary assessment in similar future cases.
