Supreme Court of Appeal

Silverback Technologies CC & Others v Commissioner for South African Revenue Service [2023] ZASCA 128

The appellants independently instituted legal proceedings against the Commissioner at different times throughout 2016 and 2017, seeking to have SARS's tariff determinations set aside ([5]–[6]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 128

F – Facts

The case at hand involves a legal dispute concerning the classification of bicycle parts imported by the appellants—Silverback Technologies CC, Omnico (Pty) Ltd, and Cytek Cycle Distributors CC—for customs duty purposes ([1]–[2]). The appellants sought to challenge the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS) concerning tariff determinations under the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964, specifically questioning whether the imported parts should be classified as bicycles under tariff heading 8712.00.10—which would attract a customs duty of 15%—or as mere parts under tariff heading 8714.9, which would exempt them from duty ([2]–[4]).

The appellants independently instituted legal proceedings against the Commissioner at different times throughout 2016 and 2017, seeking to have SARS's tariff determinations set aside ([5]–[6]). The overarching issue for all parties involved was whether the imported goods constituted complete bicycles or merely parts and accessories ([8]).

The High Court, presided over by Mokoena AJ, interpreted the relevant General Interpretation Rule (GIR) and ultimately determined that the essential character of the imported parts rendered them subject to customs duties as bicycles ([11]–[12]). The High Court's judgment, delivered in January 2022, led to an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa ([10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue centers on whether the imported bicycle parts bore the essential character of bicycles under tariff heading 8712.00.10, or whether they merely constituted parts exempt from customs duty under tariff heading 8714.9 ([8]–[9]). The court was tasked with determining the nature and characteristics of the goods in question and whether they could be classified under the relevant tariff headings as defined by the Customs and Excise Act ([14]).

In addition, procedural issues arose concerning the admissibility of expert evidence presented by both parties, particularly whether Mr. Du Toit's evidence for SARS and Mr. Stickells' evidence for the appellants should have been considered by the High Court ([9]–[10], [42]–[45]).

R – Rules

The legal rules relevant to this case stem from sections of the Customs and Excise Act, specifically section 47(1), which mandates the payment of duties on imported goods, and the interpretation rules regarding tariff headings as encapsulated in the General Interpretation Rules (GIR) ([2]–[4]). GIR 2(a) states that incomplete or unfinished articles may be classified as complete articles if they possess the essential character of such items.

Additionally, guidance from international conventions, particularly the Harmonized Commodity Description and Coding System, as well as Explanatory Notes issued by the World Customs Organization, were invoked to support the interpretation of tariff headings and their application in this context ([28]–[30]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the issue using a three-stage classification process outlined in prior cases, emphasizing the interpretation of statutory language, the nature and characteristics of the goods, and the selection of the most appropriate heading ([16]).

In addressing the essential character of a bicycle, the court gave weight to the characteristics and functionalities of the imported components collectively, emphasizing that a determination of essential character should consider all parts, even if some parts—such as wheels—were absent upon importation ([15], [23], [40]). The court dismissed the appellants' stance that wheels were an indispensable criterion and instead recognized that an assembly of parts could still carry the essential character of a bicycle, supported by GIR 2(a) and various interpretative precedents ([39], [40]).

With respect to expert evidence, the court upheld the High Court's reliance on Mr. Du Toit's expertise, concluding that his substantial experience adequately qualified him to provide relevant insights. The argument that expert testimony amounted to inadmissible opinion evidence was ultimately dismissed, reinforcing the court's assessment that such evidence aids in understanding the context and characteristics of the imported goods ([42]–[44]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal against the High Court's ruling, concluding that the imported bicycle parts bore the essential character of bicycles and, therefore, were correctly classified under tariff heading 8712.00.10, making them liable for the relevant customs duty ([46]). The court emphasized that the determination of essential character must be understood in light of all components of the consignment, reaffirming the principles of interpretation that strive for coherence with international standards as articulated in the Customs and Excise Act and GIR provisions ([40]).

The implications of this decision underscore the need for importers to evaluate carefully the collective characteristics of goods being imported to ensure accurate classification and compliance with customs regulations. The court's ruling ultimately affirms the need for a purposive interpretation of statutory provisions in the context of evolving trade practices and facilitates adherence to international customs standards.
