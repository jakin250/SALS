Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: *Makgatho v S (732/12) [2013] ZASCA 34*

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–8])

The case arises from the conviction of Nelson Sepuru Makgatho (the appellant) for murder, following an incident that occurred during the evening of 20 February 2004, at a tavern in Senobarana, Limpopo. The appellant, upon encountering his former girlfriend, Daphne, attempted to engage her but faced objections from other patrons, including Stanley Maloba and Nicholas Maloba, leading to an altercation. The appellant responded to physical aggression by drawing a firearm and firing it into the air, subsequently discharging a second shot, which ultimately led to the death of an individual, referred to as the deceased. 

The regional court magistrate in Polokwane found the appellant guilty of murder based on the legal standard of *dolus eventualis*, concluding that he acted with reckless disregard for the consequences of his actions ([para 2]). Given concerns regarding the appellant's representation, legal aid was sought, but it came late in the process, contributing to the procedural challenges manifested in the appeal ([para 3]). 

The appellant's conviction was affirmed by the Gauteng High Court, Pretoria, leading to the current appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal regarding both conviction and sentencing ([paras 1, 4]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 8–10])

The primary issues before the court included:

1. Whether the trial court correctly found that the appellant acted with intent in the form of *dolus eventualis*, which necessitates a subjective foresight of the potential for lethal consequences from his actions ([para 8]).
2. The reliability and credibility of the state witnesses' testimonies, specifically regarding the determination of who fired the fatal shot and whether the appellant's version of events merited belief ([para 4]).
3. Whether substantial and compelling circumstances existed to warrant a sentence lesser than the prescribed 15 years imprisonment, given the absence of premeditation and the surrounding contextual factors of the incident ([para 3]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 9–18])

The court framed its analysis around several key legal principles regarding the notion of *intention* in criminal law, particularly *dolus eventualis*. The legal definition requires that the accused must subjectively foresee the possibility that their actions might result in death but nevertheless persist in their conduct ([para 9]). This notion has been omnipresent in South African case law, underpinned by decisions such as *S v Qege* ([2012] 2 SACR 41), which elucidates the necessity for subjective foresight ([para 10]).

Other references to earlier judgments reinforced the rule that actions leading to murder cannot simply arise from neglect but must be grounded in a demonstrated subjective intent ([paras 9–11]). Important citations included *R v Difford* (1937) AD 370 and *S v van der Meyden* (1999) 1 SACR 447, both of which assert that the evidentiary burden lies with the state to prove the requisite mens rea beyond a reasonable doubt without misdirections from the trial court ([para 12]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 11–16])

In applying these principles, the Supreme Court of Appeal evaluated the reliability of the state witnesses against the appellant’s defensive claims. The trial court had accepted the testimonies of witnesses—who were largely consistent and corroborative—against the backdrop of the appellant's inconsistent account, including his claim of a struggle over the firearm and inability to specify how the shots were fired ([paras 11–12]).

The court deemed the appellant's assertions implausible, particularly the suggestion that he only fired into the ground and the absence of any credible evidence suggesting he acted under immediate threat ([paras 13–16]). Instead, the actions described by the witnesses indicated a clear initiation of violence by the appellant, framing his subsequent shooting as reckless conduct that demonstrated the requisite *dolus eventualis* ([paras 12–15]). 

Furthermore, the appellant’s behavior post-incident—fleeing the scene and not aiding the victim—failed to align with a narrative of accidental firing, further undermining his credibility ([para 16]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 18–20])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the lower court's decisions, concluding that the trial court's findings were not vitiated by material misdirections and that the evidence supported a finding of *dolus eventualis* ([para 18]). The ratio decidendi emphasized that the appellant's foresight of potential harm in discharging the firearm in a crowded environment sufficed to establish his guilt for murder, affirming the necessity for accountability in violent actions, particularly those involving firearms.

In terms of sentencing, the court reiterated the seriousness of the crime and reinforced the discretion afforded to trial courts in such matters. The absence of substantial and compelling circumstances justified the imposition of the minimum sentence prescribed by law, thereby dismissing the appellant’s appeal against both conviction and sentencing ([paras 19–20]). 

The implications of this judgment underscore the rigorous judicial inquiry into intent and the evidentiary standards that must be met to sustain a murder conviction in South Africa, shaping standards for future cases involving reckless conduct resulting in fatal outcomes.
