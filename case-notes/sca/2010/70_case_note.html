Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 70 #### FIRAC Method F – Facts: The appel

Case Note: Ngcamu v The State (665/09) [2010] ZASCA 70

FIRAC Method

F – Facts:
The appellants, Thulani Ngcamu and Sifiso Shange, were tried and convicted in the regional court for robbery with aggravating circumstances and attempted murder, among other charges. This case stems from an incident on 9 February 2004 at a BP filling station in Clare Estate, where armed individuals, later identified as the appellants, robbed employees of Coin Security while using Ngcamu's Honda Ballade as the getaway vehicle. During the robbery, Dingaan Mabuza and Tobias Mhlongo were accosted, with Mabuza giving chase in an armored vehicle, ultimately identifying Ngcamu as the driver of the getaway car ([1–3]). Ngcamu claimed a hijacking prior to the robbery, stating that his car was taken at 08:45 on the same morning. However, both the regional court and the KwaZulu-Natal High Court rejected this alibi as implausible ([7–16]). Additionally, after police efforts to reach him following the recovery of his vehicle, Ngcamu was arrested for the robbery on 15 February 2004 ([6]).

I – Issues:
The central issue in this appeal was whether there existed a reasonable possibility that Ngcamu's alibi of being hijacked was true, thereby undermining the prosecution's case that he was identified as the driver during the robbery ([8]). The appeal also concerned the convictions for robbery with aggravating circumstances and the attempted murder of Mabuza, while challenging the conviction for attempted murder concerning Mhlongo ([20]).

R – Rules:
The legal framework surrounding eyewitness testimony and identification is crucial in this case, especially the standards set forth in S v Mthetwa, where the court emphasized the reliability of observation must be tested against various factors such as lighting, proximity, prior knowledge, and the suggestibility of witnesses ([10]). The principle that an identification must withstand scrutiny, especially when it is based on a witness's observation during a rapidly evolving and potentially stressful situation, is key to the court's analysis ([10]). Furthermore, the firearm possession charges were examined under the newly effective provisions of the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000, which the court found did not apply to Ngcamu's conduct prior to its implementation ([2]).

A – Application:
The court undertook a rigorous examination of Mabuza's identification of Ngcamu as the driver, finding it credible. Mabuza’s familiarity with Ngcamu, having previously worked together, and the circumstances of the chase were deemed sufficient to dispel doubts regarding his identification, even considering the heat of the moment and the mobile nature of the events ([9], [11]). The court noted the clarity of observation given that the incident occurred in broad daylight, and Mabuza was positioned favorably to see both the car and its occupant ([11]). The timeline discrepancy and Ngcamu’s delayed reporting of the alleged hijacking were viewed critically, suggesting a lack of credibility in his defense ([13], [14]). The court concluded that the implausibility of the hijacking narrative diminished the likelihood of its truth and thereby upheld the convictions for robbery and attempted murder relating to Mabuza ([12], [18]).

On the other hand, the attempted murder conviction for Mhlongo was overturned due to a lack of evidence showing that shots were directed at him, as Mhlongo himself testified that he was not threatened during the incident ([20]).

C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Ngcamu's appeal against the conviction for robbery with aggravating circumstances and attempted murder concerning Mabuza, validating the lower court's assessments of eyewitness credibility and the evidence overall. Conversely, the appeal against the conviction for attempted murder concerning Mhlongo was upheld due to insufficient evidence supporting that charge ([21], [22]). This case underscores the judicial approach taken in matters of eyewitness identification and the requirements for substantiating claims made in a defense, alongside the judicial caution advised in evaluating such testimonies.
