Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

### Case Note: Tarr v S [2018] ZACC 35

#### F – Facts
In the Constitutional Court of South Africa, the case revolves around Ishaq Tarr, the applicant who faced conviction for the murder of Haremakale Selimo. The conviction was issued by Daffue J in the Free State Division of the High Court on 18 October 2013. On that occasion, Tarr was sentenced to 15 years' imprisonment following an incident that took place in November 2010 on the R57 road between Heilbron and Petrus Steyn. Mr. Lameck Mtagha, a former employee of Tarr, was co-accused yet acquitted. A significant issue arose from Mtagha's extra-curial statement made to a magistrate, which was admitted as hearsay evidence during trial ([paras 1–2]).

Mtagha recanted his statement at trial, claiming he had not made any inculpatory declarations against Tarr; however, the trial judge considered the statement relevant and supportive of the conviction. Subsequently, the Supreme Court of Appeal's judgment in the case of Nkosi had a retrospective effect that warranted reconsideration of the admissibility of evidence akin to Mtagha's statement, thereby raising questions about the fairness of Tarr's trial and conviction ([paras 3, 4]).

#### I – Issues
The primary legal issue in this case was whether the admission of Mtagha's extra-curial statement as evidence undermined the fairness of Tarr's trial. A secondary issue pertained to the requirement of the trial court to warn Tarr regarding the application of minimum sentencing legislation in light of the conviction for murder ([paras 6, 7]). 

Additionally, a procedural aspect arose regarding the applicant's delay in seeking leave to appeal, which required the court's consideration for possible condonation because of its exceptional circumstances ([paras 8–10]).

#### R – Rules
The applicable legal principles emerged from prior jurisprudence concerning hearsay evidence, particularly the rulings of the Supreme Court of Appeal in Ndhlovu, which asserted that extra-curial statements are generally inadmissible unless they correspond with certain exceptions. Furthermore, the Constitutional Court's ruling in Nkosi reinstated the common law position regarding such evidence against co-accused ([para 4]). The principle of fair trial rights under the South African Constitution, section 35, was also invoked to evaluate the implications of the trial court's evidentiary decisions ([para 4]).

#### A – Application
The court examined whether the inclusion of Mtagha's extra-curial statement affected the fairness of Tarr's trial. The judgment noted that the trial court had not solely depended on the hearsay evidence for conviction. Instead, the circumstantial evidence, notably the ballistics report and the discovery of the firearm at Tarr’s premises, was pivotal. The Court outlined three key facts that firmly established the prosecution's case: the firearm that killed the deceased was linked to Tarr, the evidence did not support any alternative explanation for the murder, and there existed a direct connection between the recovered weapon and the offense ([paras 14–15]).

The Court thus affirmed the trial court’s discretion in assessing the totality of evidence, concluding that even without the contested extra-curial statement, the remaining evidence was compelling enough to secure a conviction beyond a reasonable doubt. Furthermore, concerning the sentencing issue, it was determined that the trial judge had adequately considered the relevant legal frameworks, indicating that Tarr had adequate representation and was subject to fair trial requirements ([paras 12–17]).

#### C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the Constitutional Court held that the applicant, Ishaq Tarr, did not have prospects of success for his appeal against his previous conviction. The court established that the extra-curial statement did not render his trial unfair and the substantive evidence against him remained intact, justifying the original conviction. Additionally, due to exceptional circumstances regarding the delay in application, condonation was granted, but ultimately, leave to appeal was refused. This decision affirmed the necessity for acquittal standards to be stringently adhered to while also underscoring the weight of circumstantial evidence within the framework of procedural justice in South Africa ([paras 17–18]). The ruling underlines the precedential impact of Nkosi and the court's commitment to uphold fair trial rights, even in complex evidentiary cases.
