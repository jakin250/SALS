Public Protector and Others v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [2021] ZACC 19

Following a review application by the President, supported by the Speaker of the National Assembly and the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP), the High Court set aside the Public Protector's findings.

Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 19

F - Facts

This case arises from a direct appeal to the Constitutional Court regarding the findings and remedial actions taken by the Public Protector against President Cyril Ramaphosa following allegations of unethical conduct, specifically concerning donations made to the CR17 campaign, which aimed to support Ramaphosa's election as President of the African National Congress (ANC). The Public Protector's report found that Ramaphosa had misled Parliament regarding the nature and recipients of the donations and directed various governmental bodies to take remedial action, including investigations into the alleged conduct.

Following a review application by the President, supported by the Speaker of the National Assembly and the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP), the High Court set aside the Public Protector's findings. However, it left unanswered AmaBhungane Centre for Investigative Journalism's claim regarding the constitutionality of the Executive Ethics Code. The Public Protector, alongside the Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF) and AmaBhungane, sought to appeal this dismissal.

The High Court’s ruling found material errors in the Public Protector’s approach, particularly in defining "wilfully misleading" Parliament, which inaccurately included "inadvertent" misleading. This misunderstanding resulted in the conclusion that the President had indeed misled Parliament, without the requisite intent being established ([1]–[80]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issues before the Constitutional Court included:

  1. Whether President Ramaphosa had misled Parliament in violation of the Executive Ethics Code.
  2. Whether he had a duty to disclose donations made to the CR17 campaign.
  3. The Public Protector’s authority to investigate the affairs of the CR17 campaign.
  4. The applicability of the audi principle concerning remedial actions taken by the Public Protector.
  5. The legality of the Public Protector's remedial actions.
  6. The correctness of the High Court's refusal to adjudicate AmaBhungane’s constitutional challenge against the Code ([54]–[56]).

R - Rules

The case involved the interpretation and application of several significant legal frameworks:

  • The Executive Members’ Ethics Act 82 of 1998: This Act mandates compliance with an ethical code and provides the Public Protector with the authority to investigate breaches of that code.
  • Executive Ethics Code: Provides standards for the conduct of members of the Executive, including prohibitions against misleading Parliament and requirements for the disclosure of financial interests.
  • The Constitution of South Africa: Particularly sections 42(3), 86, 87, and 96, which establish the accountability of Parliament and the ethical obligations of executives ([1]–[31]).

Moreover, the case utilized principles from the case law surrounding the Public Protector's mandate, including discussions on legislative interpretation, procedural fairness, and the implications of misleading statements in parliamentary contexts ([56]–[60]).

A - Application

The Court's application of the law involved a close examination of the Public Protector’s findings against both the statutory and constitutional requirements associated with their roles.

  1. Misleading Parliament: The Court noted that the Public Protector conflated "wilfully" with "inadvertently," thereby failing to establish intent—a necessary criterion for violation of the Code. The High Court's finding that the Public Protector misapplied the legal standard was upheld.
  2. Duty to Disclose: The Court found that the Public Protector failed to establish that the President had personally benefited from the CR17 campaign donations, thus his duty to disclose such benefits was not triggered.
  3. Authority to Investigate: The Public Protector lacked jurisdiction to investigate the affairs of the CR17 campaign as none of the complaints explicitly necessitated such an investigation. The conclusions drawn regarding possible money laundering were also found to lack a sufficient factual basis.
  4. Audi Principle: The Court concluded that the remedial actions taken by the Public Protector lacked fairness as the President was not afforded an opportunity to respond to the potentially damaging findings based on emails she relied upon, which had not been disclosed to him prior to their inclusion in the report.
  5. Legality of Remedial Actions: The critical analysis centered around whether the Public Protector acted outside her authority in mandating actions requiring compliance from bodies like the Speaker of the National Assembly and the NDPP regarding non-existent breaches of conduct.
  6. AmaBhungane’s Challenge: Regarding the challenge to the Executive Ethics Code’s constitutionality, the Court found that the High Court erred in dismissing AmaBhungane’s claim, noting that critical constitutional issues regarding ethical governance demand scrutiny ([54]–[149]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court dismissed the appeal in large part, affirming the findings of the High Court that had set aside the Public Protector's report, primarily due to substantial legal and procedural deficiencies. However, the Court also vacated the High Court’s dismissal of AmaBhungane’s challenge, remitting the matter for further consideration, thus reinforcing the role of ethical accountability in governance.

The Court ordered the President to pay AmaBhungane's costs, establishing a precedent that emphasizes the necessity for transparency and ethical conduct by members of the Executive within the framework of South African constitutional law. There was no order for costs against the other parties involved, including the Public Protector ([1]–[149]).

This judgment serves to clarify the standards surrounding ethical conduct within the public sphere and highlights the importance of proper investigative procedures and transparency in maintaining constitutional integrity and accountability ([150]–[204]).
