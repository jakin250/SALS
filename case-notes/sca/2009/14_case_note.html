Supreme Court of Appeal

Mokoena v S (563/08) [2009] ZASCA 14

The appellant, Richard Mokoena, and his co-accused were charged with murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances in the Free State Provincial Division, presided over by Hattingh J and assessors ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 14

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The appellant, Richard Mokoena, and his co-accused were charged with murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances in the Free State Provincial Division, presided over by Hattingh J and assessors ([1]). The State asserted that on the night of February 27/28, 1995, Mokoena and his co-accused murdered Mrs. Catarina Johanna Koster in her home, stealing various items including her vehicle ([1]). Mokoena pleaded guilty to culpable homicide for the murder charge, which was rejected, while he pleaded guilty to robbery, which was accepted. Consequently, he was convicted of the charges as laid out ([1]).

The trial court sentenced Mokoena to 40 years for murder and 15 years for robbery, with half of the robbery sentence to run concurrently with the murder sentence ([2]). This effectively resulted in a total of 47.5 years’ imprisonment. The appellant sought leave to appeal against this sentence and also sought condonation for the late application for leave to appeal, which was denied by the trial court ([3]). Mokoena subsequently appealed this refusal in the Supreme Court of Appeal.

I – Issues

The primary issues raised in this case include:

  1. Whether the trial court erred in refusing to grant leave to appeal and condonation for the late appeal ([3]).
  2. The appropriateness of the sentences imposed, particularly the excessive nature of the combined sentence for the crimes of murder and robbery ([6]).

R – Rules

The legal principles applicable in this case predominantly involve sentencing guidelines established in previous authorities. The Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized that:

  • The function of a court in imposing a sentence is to determine the maximum period that a convicted person may serve, without attempting to delineate a minimum period, as detailed in S v Matlala ([6]).
  • A court is restricted from letting parole considerations interfere with its determination of the sentence; it must assess the severity of the crime independently of potential parole outcomes ([6]).
  • Sentencing must take into account various factors, including the nature and gravity of the crime, the personal circumstances of the accused, and the need for society's protection and deterrence in violent crime contexts ([11]).

A – Application

Upon review, the court identified a significant misdirection by the trial judge regarding the application of the law related to sentencing ([6]). The remarks made during the sentencing phase reflected a preoccupation with how long the appellant might serve before being considered for parole, rather than focusing solely on the maximum appropriate sentence ([5], [6]). The trial court's discretion, initially exercised narrowly, lacked a thorough consideration of the legal boundaries established in case law, primarily concerning the function of a sentencing court.

The Supreme Court of Appeal, after considering Mokoena's personal background—such as his age, minor previous convictions, and lack of violent history—ultimately assessed that the overall sentence was unduly harsh, especially in light of the appellant's relatively youthful age at the time of the offences ([8]). The gruesomeness of the crime necessitated a significant penalty, yet a consideration of his circumstances guided the court to reduce the overall sentence significantly.

In accordance with the precedents set forth, the court determined that a sentence of 25 years for murder and 15 years for robbery (with ten years of the robbery sentence running concurrently) would better align with the nature of the established crimes and the guidelines of appropriate sentencing practices ([12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the original sentences were excessively severe due to a misapplication of the sentencing principles ([12]). Consequently, the court set aside the sentence imposed by the trial court and replaced it with a new sentence of 25 years' imprisonment for murder, and 15 years for robbery, leading to an effective imprisonment of 30 years ([3]). The court further ruled that this sentence should be backdated to September 19, 1995, in line with s 282 of the Criminal Procedure Act ([3]).

This case underscores the importance of judicial restraint concerning sentencing and the necessity for courts to apply principles of law without external influences concerning administrative decisions like parole. It emphasizes that thorough consideration of the circumstances of both the crimes and the offender is crucial in achieving balanced and just sentencing outcomes in South African law.
