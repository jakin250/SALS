Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: De Klerk v The State (718/2022) [2023] ZASCA 172

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts ([1]–[10])
On 14 October 2019, the appellant, Pieter Cornelius de Klerk, was charged with murder in the Regional Court at Boksburg, Gauteng, after a violent altercation on 27 December 2018 led to the death of Wendell Pietersen. The incident began with a dispute over money, during which Pietersen threw stones at several individuals, including Darryl van Greunen and Theodore Hoffman. As tensions escalated, Pietersen armed himself with a half-brick and approached de Klerk, who had just returned home from work.

De Klerk contended that he attempted to prevent Pietersen from throwing further stones. During their confrontation, Pietersen swung the half-brick at de Klerk, prompting de Klerk to retrieve his firearm in self-defense. However, during a struggle for the weapon, de Klerk's finger inadvertently pressed the trigger, resulting in the fatal shooting of Pietersen.

On 16 March 2020, the regional court convicted de Klerk of murder with direct intent and sentenced him to 15 years’ imprisonment. The regional court denied leave for an appeal, prompting de Klerk to petition the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court for leave to appeal, which was similarly denied. Ultimately, he sought and obtained special leave from the Supreme Court of Appeal.

### I – Issues ([3], [4], [9])
The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

1. Whether the high court erred in refusing leave to appeal against both conviction and sentence.
2. The appropriateness of the conviction for murder with direct intent, considering the circumstances surrounding the shooting.
3. Whether the imposed sentence of 15 years' imprisonment was excessive and disproportionate, given the mitigating factors presented.

### R – Rules ([3], [8], [9])
The court applied the legal principles outlined in **S v Matshona** [2008] ZASCA 58, which established that the primary concern on such petitions is whether there exists a reasonable prospect of success in the envisaged appeal. The court emphasized that it could not address the merits of the original appeal but could determine if sufficient grounds existed to justify assessment by a higher court.

The statutory framework governing minimum sentencing in South Africa is articulated in the **Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997**, which stipulates that prescribed minimum sentences may only be deviated from where "substantial and compelling circumstances" are present.

### A – Application ([5], [6], [8], [9], [10])
In its deliberation, the Supreme Court of Appeal examined whether there were reasonable prospects of success on appeal. The court characterized the trial proceedings as problematic, with the magistrate displaying an overbearing demeanor towards witnesses, which contributed to a potentially prejudicial environment. The court noted numerous procedural irregularities that could have adversely affected the fairness of the trial.

The court also highlighted that the regional court had not explicitly justified its finding of direct intent, and the defense contended that based on the evidence, the incident could more accurately reflect culpable homicide rather than murder. Given the circumstances, the court found that Pietersen acted aggressively and was not simply an innocent victim, questioning the regional court's conclusion that de Klerk’s actions manifested a direct intention to kill.

The Supreme Court acknowledged the significance of the mitigating factors presented during the sentencing phase, including de Klerk's lack of prior criminal history, his responsibilities toward his dependents, and the nature of the confrontation itself.

### C – Conclusion ([9], [10], [11])
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the high court erred in refusing to grant leave to appeal, recognizing reasonable prospects of success in reconsidering both the conviction and the sentence. As such, it set aside the high court’s order, granting de Klerk leave to appeal against both conviction and sentence in the Gauteng Division of the High Court.

This case underscores the importance of due process and the necessity for trial courts to conduct proceedings impartially, ensuring that the rights of the accused are safeguarded. The ruling may have wider implications for future cases involving claims of self-defense and the application of minimum sentences under South African law, particularly where aggressive conduct and mitigating circumstances are present.
