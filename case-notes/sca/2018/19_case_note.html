Patmar Explorations (Pty) Ltd v Limpopo Development Tribunal (1250/2016) [2018] ZACC 19

After the Tribunal's decision, the appellants sought relief in the Gauteng High Court to review and set aside the Tribunal's ruling. N F Kgomo J dismissed the appellants' application, leading to the current appeal.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZACC 19

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-2])

The appeal in this case originated from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, where the appellants, comprising several companies, opposed a land development application made to the Limpopo Development Tribunal by the Gawie Labuschagne Trust. This application had its roots in provisions of the Development Facilitation Act (DFA) which had been previously declared unconstitutional. The South African Constitutional Court had suspended the order of invalidity of these provisions for a period to allow the legislature to amend them; however, this suspension expired on June 17, 2012. On this date, the Tribunal proceeded to approve the Trust's application on November 8, 2012, despite the appellants contending that the Tribunal had lost its power to do so post-expiration.

After the Tribunal's decision, the appellants sought relief in the Gauteng High Court to review and set aside the Tribunal's ruling. N F Kgomo J dismissed the appellants' application, leading to the current appeal.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2-3])

The primary legal issues presented for consideration by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) pertained to whether the High Court erred in dismissing the appellants' application by failing to recognize the Tribunal's lack of jurisdiction post the expiration of the suspension order. A secondary issue revolved around whether the SCA should reconsider its interpretation of precedents established in similar cases, particularly the reliance on the doctrine of stare decisis, which mandates courts to follow precedents established by higher courts or previous decisions within the same jurisdiction unless clearly erroneous.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 1-4])

The SCA based its decision on well-established principles of administrative law, particularly regarding the limits of statutory authority following the lapse of the suspension order declared by the Constitutional Court. The stare decisis principle, requiring that courts adhere to prior judgments unless they are demonstrably erroneous, was pivotal in this case. The decision in Shelton and another v Eastern Cape Development Tribunal and others [2016] ZASCA 125 was particularly relevant, as it underscored that following the lapse of the suspension order, relevant provisions of the DFA rendered tribunal decisions invalid.

Furthermore, the SCA cited prior cases that clarified the jurisprudential standards for overruling previous decisions, emphasizing that a departure from established legal precedent requires clear errors in past judgments. It cited Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v Gauteng Development Tribunal [2010] ZACC 11, which had originally declared the DFA’s provisions unconstitutional, and Coface South Africa Insurance Co Limited v East London Own Haven [2013] ZASCA 202, which provided insight into how the courts determine when to reconsider their prior judgments.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 5-9])

The SCA found that the Tribunal's approval of the development rights was invalid due to the court’s preceding decisions. The Tribunal had failed to demonstrate that its prior exercise of power was justified post the expiration of the suspension order. The High Court erred by not following the precedent set by Justice Mothle in the parallel case concerning the Mogalakwena Municipality, which had also ruled that Tribunal powers were divested upon expiration of the suspension.

The judgment underscored that the High Court did not meet the substantial burden required to deviate from Mothle J’s decision, as it failed to satisfy the tests for stare decisis. Furthermore, the SCA reiterated that mere disagreement does not suffice to overrule precedents. The appellants' arguments, grounded in constitutional principles, drew on the authority from their previous litigation efforts and the essence of legal certainty that stare decisis provides to safeguard proper governance and judicial integrity.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 8-10])

The SCA upheld the appeal, thereby setting aside the decision of the Gauteng High Court with costs, stating that the Tribunal's decision was ''null and void''. The court's finding reinforced the notion that statutory bodies must adhere strictly to their defined powers, especially in the context of constitutional compliance. The judgment highlighted the importance of legal predictability and respect for prior legal decisions, providing clarity on the necessity for courts to follow established precedent unless a significant error is demonstrated.

This case broadens the implications for future governance and tribunal decision-making within South Africa, marking a critical reaffirmation of the rule of law and the limitations of administrative authority in the wake of constitutional deficiencies. It also emphasizes the ongoing necessity for legislative bodies to remain vigilant and prompt in amending laws declared unconstitutional to maintain effective administrative operations in land development matters.

In conclusion, the SCA's affirmation of the principles of stare decisis and jurisdictional constraints not only reflects the judiciary's commitment to upholding constitutional norms but also serves as a cautionary tale for administrative bodies regarding compliance with judicial rulings.
