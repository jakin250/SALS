Traxys Africa Holdings Ltd and Another v Westbrook Resources Ltd (509/2020) [2021] ZASCA 122

The respondent, Westbrook Resources Ltd ("Westbrook"), operates a mineral refining business in Croatia, specifically focusing on manganese extraction from slag ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 Traxys Africa Holdings Ltd and Another v Westbrook Resources Ltd (509/2020) [2021] ZASCA 122.

F – Facts

The respondent, Westbrook Resources Ltd ("Westbrook"), operates a mineral refining business in Croatia, specifically focusing on manganese extraction from slag ([1]). Facing operational inefficiencies, Westbrook entered into three separate agreements with the second appellant, formerly known as Metmar (Pty) Ltd and currently Traxys Africa Holdings (Pty) Ltd ("Metmar"), for the sale of jigs used in the refining process ([2]).

The primary contention arose regarding who bore the responsibility for commissioning the jigs—whether it lay with Metmar, who provided the jigs, or with Westbrook ([2]). Following unsuccessful commissioning attempts, Westbrook initiated legal action against Metmar in the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court, claiming damages due to Metmar's failure to fulfill its obligations as per the agreements ([2]).

The trial court initially ruled in favor of Westbrook but failed to adequately address the specific issues posed for determination, leading to a series of appeals by both parties ([4]). In the subsequent full court judgment, the court affirmed that Metmar bore the obligation to commission the jigs but again did not resolve the specific legal questions raised ([5]).

I – Issues

The main legal questions before the court were as follows:

  1. Obligation to Commission: Was Metmar obliged to commission the jigs sold to Westbrook in Croatia, as alleged by Westbrook? ([4.2.1])
  2. Cost and Risk Responsibility: Were Westbrook and/or De Beer required to handle the commissioning of the jigs at their own cost and risk without assistance from Metmar? ([4.2.2])
  3. Actual Commissioning Responsibility: Did Westbrook and/or De Beer attend to the commissioning of the equipment independently, at their own expense and risk, without any involvement from Metmar? ([4.2.3])

These questions culminated in significant ramifications for both parties, determining liability for commissioning the jigs and establishing the proper interpretation of their agreements ([6]).

R – Rules

The court relied upon various legal principles and precedents to address the issues at hand. The key legal rules included:

  1. Existence and Nature of Agreements: The general principles of contract law govern oral and written agreements. The court examined the specifics of both the oral and written agreements to ascertain obligations of the parties ([3]), citing the established principle that if an agreement is partly oral and partly documented, the parol evidence rule may not necessarily preclude the admission of oral terms ([57]).
  2. Parol Evidence Rule: The rule prohibits the introduction of extrinsic evidence to modify or contradict clear written contracts unless exceptional circumstances are present ([58]). However, the court acknowledged a partial integration exception, allowing for understanding supplementary oral agreements that do not contradict the written terms ([59]).
  3. Credibility and Factual Findings: In reviewing the decision of the trial court, the appellate court emphasized the importance of credibility assessments made by the trial judge, as these were pivotal in determining the weight of testimonial evidence presented ([55]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the obligations arising from the agreements, drawing heavily on the evidence presented during the trial. Critically, the court examined the relationship and communication between Westbrook and Metmar, establishing that Metmar's role involved not only the sale of jigs but also extending to the commissioning of said equipment ([56]).

The court applied the principles of the parol evidence rule and set aside Metmar's argument that oral testimony regarding the commissioning obligation could not be considered. Instead, they determined that the oral agreements did not contradict the written invoices but instead clarified and supplemented them by establishing an obligation on Metmar’s part ([57]–[59]).

Furthermore, the documentary evidence, including emails and invoices, was indicative of Metmar’s intention and acknowledgment of its commissioning obligations. The persistent communication stressing commissioning responsibilities and payment agreements reinforced Westbrook’s claims ([39], [46]). The trial court had also made robust credibility determinations favoring Westbrook's witnesses, particularly Walton, while portraying Boshoff’s testimony as evasive and lacking reliability ([51], [52]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Metmar's appeal and upheld Westbrook's cross-appeal ([61]). The judgment clearly established that Metmar was indeed obliged to commission the jigs sold to Westbrook, directly contradicting Metmar's assertion of Westbrook's unilateral responsibility ([61]).

The court directed the resolutions of the unresolved questions in favor of Westbrook, thereby affirming its right to recover damages suffered due to Metmar’s breach of contractual obligations ([61]). This result underscores the principle that courts are meticulous in upholding contractual responsibilities and clarifying ambiguities arising from both oral and written agreements, thus providing greater certainty in commercial transactions ([61]).

In conclusion, this case serves as a pertinent example of the interplay between oral and written agreements in contract law, reaffirming the necessity for parties to clearly delineate their obligations within any agreement to avoid protracted legal disputes.
