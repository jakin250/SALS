Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL  COURT  OF  SOUTH  AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# FIRAC Case Note: **CCT 1/96: KwaZulu-Natal Amakhosi and Iziphakanyiswa Amendment Bill 1995 & CCT 6/96: Payment of Salaries, Allowances and other Privileges to the Ingonyama Bill 1995**

**Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa**  
**Heard on: 21 May 1996**  
**Delivered on: 05 July 1996**  
**Judgment: Chaskalson P**  

---

### F - Facts

[1] The case emerged from the KwaZulu-Natal provincial legislature concerning two Bills: the Payment of Salaries, Allowances, and Other Privileges to the Ingonyama Amendment Bill (the Ingonyama Amendment) and the KwaZulu-Natal Amakhosi and Iziphakanyiswa Amendment Bill (the Amakhosi Amendment), presented for consideration in October 1995. These Bills sought to amend legislation enacted by the KwaZulu legislature before the adoption of the 1993 Constitution. 

[2] Members of the African National Congress objected to the constitutionality of certain provisions within these Bills. The Speaker of the legislature referred these disputes to the Constitutional Court under section 98(9) of the Constitution. Arguments were made concurrently by Mr. Davis on behalf of the petitioning members and Mr. Richings representing the KwaZulu-Natal government.

[3-5] The Amakhosi Amendment aimed to amend the KwaZulu Amakhosi and Iziphakanyiswa Act of 1990, which detailed the recognition, appointment, and functions of traditional leaders. Similarly, the Ingonyama Amendment sought to modify the KwaZulu Act on the Payment of Salaries, which provided for the remuneration of the Ingonyama—a traditional title in the Zulu community. 

[6-7] Central to the consideration of competence was whether the provincial legislature had the authority to repeal or amend these existing acts. Section 229 of the Constitution recognized these acts as still relevant, pending their potential repeal or amendment by a competent authority. Further legal analysis focused on whether these amendments fell within the constitutional competences outlined in Schedule 6.

### I - Issues

[8] The principal legal questions revolved around the legislative competence of the KwaZulu-Natal provincial legislature to amend or repeal the two Acts. Specifically, it required determination whether the provisions of the Bills interacted with matters permitted under Schedule 6 of the Constitution.

[12] Additionally, concerns arose whether the new provisions introduced by the Bills exceeded provincial authority and whether they served to frustrate national legislation, specifically the Remuneration of Traditional Leaders Act 29 of 1995.

### R - Rules

[8-9] Section 126(1) of the Constitution empowers provincial legislatures to enact laws regarding functional areas defined in Schedule 6. In addition, section 181 governs traditional authorities, reinforcing that laws regulating these authorities are within provincial competence.

[10-11] Furthermore, Section 232(1)(a)(ii) reflects the notion that pre-existing laws may apply across provincial boundaries if enacted by a competent authority. Therefore, if the amendments did not infringe explicit national legislative authority, the provincial legislature retained substantial leeway in enacting related laws.

[25-27] The legal definition of a tax was explored, particularly in relation to the obligation imposed by the amendments on traditional leaders to deposit unauthorized remuneration into the provincial revenue fund, contrasting it with previous case law regarding taxation definitions.

### A - Application

[13-14] The Court examined the specific provisions prohibiting the Ingonyama and other traditional leaders from accepting any forms of remuneration outside those designated under the amendments. The implications of these provisions for the institutional independence of traditional leaders were evaluated, especially in light of arguments suggesting the amendments sought to undermine the authority of traditional leaders in favor of provincial interests.

[18-19] The intention behind the legislation was scrutinized, particularly claims suggesting it was designed to align with or oppose the national legislative framework concerning traditional leader remuneration. The Court determined that the true purpose of the amendments was pivotal in assessing their constitutional validity.

[21] The interrelationship between the Ingonyama and Amakhosi amendments and the broader context of provincial versus national legislative powers was a primary concern, particularly regarding remuneration of traditional leaders and the applicability of section 207 regarding salaries.

[32-33] Furthermore, the potential extraterritorial application of the prohibitions stipulated within the Bills was considered, especially concerning traditional leaders' remunerative engagements beyond the province of KwaZulu-Natal.

### C - Conclusion

[49] The Constitutional Court concluded that neither the Ingonyama Amendment nor the Amakhosi Amendment violated constitutional provisions. The Bills did not exceed the legislative competence of the KwaZulu-Natal provincial legislature; instead, they were deemed consistent with the framework established under the 1993 Constitution. The amendments were thus sanctioned and would remain lawful within the prescribed limitations set by existing national laws.

[50] The decision reflected a careful balance between the autonomy of provincial legislatures to govern traditional authorities and the overarching constitutional protections securing the independence of such leaders. This ruling carries significant implications for the local governance of traditional leadership structures and their relationship with established national legislation.
