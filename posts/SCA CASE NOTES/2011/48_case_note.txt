Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: The State v. Hendrik Lourens Jacob Boekhoud ([2011] ZASCA 48)

## F – Facts

The case concerns an application by the Director of Public Prosecutions, South Gauteng, for leave to appeal following the refusal of the South Gauteng High Court to reserve questions of law regarding the indictment against Hendrik Lourens Jacob Boekhoud ([1]). In 2006, Boekhoud was indicted alongside four co-accused for multiple charges, including racketeering and other crimes related to theft, fraud, and money laundering under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA) and the Mining Rights Act 20 of 1967 ([2]). 

These offences were linked to the theft of unwrought precious metals from South African mines, predominantly in the Rustenburg area, which were then exported to the United Kingdom ([6]). The extensive and voluminous charge sheet prompted Boekhoud to request further particulars, which consisted of an elaborate exchange of documents between the parties ([4]). Ultimately, Boekhoud raised a plea of lack of jurisdiction, noting that he had resided in the United Kingdom since renouncing his South African citizenship in the 1980s ([13]).

The High Court found that it lacked jurisdiction to hear counts 2-55 since the alleged criminal acts were committed outside South Africa ([13], [14]). This led to the High Court's refusal to reserve questions of law where the State had contended the applicability of the common purpose doctrine ([10], [16], [20]). The State's application for leave to appeal was thus rendered contingent upon the resolution of these jurisdictional questions.

## I – Issues

The principal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the High Court correctly determined its jurisdiction in relation to the charges against Boekhoud, specifically regarding acts committed outside South Africa ([12], [13]). This raised several sub-issues:

1. Whether the acts constituting the charges were solely performed by the co-accused within South African territory, thereby excluding the jurisdiction over Boekhoud ([13]).
2. The applicability of the doctrine of common purpose under the circumstances of extraterritorial actions ([16], [20]).
3. The implications of the High Court's interpretation of section 2 of POCA concerning the jurisdictional limits of South African courts ([15], [18]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case includes:

1. **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA)**: Specifically section 319, which provides for the reservation of questions of law for higher court consideration ([20]).
2. **Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA)**: Particularly section 2, which delineates the parameters defining racketeering activity and its jurisdictional implications ([37]).
3. **Principles of Common Purpose in South African Law**: As articulated in cases such as *S v Mgedezi* and *S v Thebus*, which elucidate the requirements for establishing common purpose and its legal consequences ([57]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal, led by Navsa JA, critically analyzed the factual matrix against the established legal principles. The court noted that the High Court had found it lacked jurisdiction based on two central reasons: that the alleged criminal conduct was carried out by co-accused in South Africa and that Boekhoud's involvement was entirely extraterritorial ([13], [14]).

In respect to the doctrine of common purpose, Borchers J had expressed that extending jurisdiction over an individual for acts committed outside South Africa—even in the presence of common purpose—was unprecedented unless expressly legislated ([16]). The SCA, however, postulated whether the actus reus of the co-accused could be imputed to Boekhoud based on their common purpose, emphasizing that jurisdiction need not solely rely on the location of the act but could also depend on participatory involvement in a broader criminal enterprise ([57]).

The SCA further underscored the need for clarity in the questions reserved for appeal, aligning with the principles set forth in *S v Basson*, which reiterated that certainty must exist regarding facts related to the legal questions posed ([34]). The ambiguity surrounding the State's claims of common purpose and the alleged extraterritorial actions contributed to the court's skepticism regarding the State’s case ([59]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the application for leave to appeal, concluding that the High Court's refusal to reserve questions of law was warranted given the uncertainty and ambiguity surrounding the jurisdictional issues ([30], [75]). The court emphasized the necessity for well-founded legal bases when asserting jurisdiction, especially concerning allegations of common purpose for extraterritorial acts, which were not adequately established in the State's submissions. This ruling signifies the stringent nature of jurisdictional requirements under South African law and underscores the need for precision in prosecutorial assertions, particularly in complex cases involving multiple parties and international dimensions ([75]).

---

The dismissal of the application for leave to appeal has implications for prosecutorial strategies in cases involving multi-jurisdictional offenses and may lead the State to reconsider its approach when drafting indictments or deciding how to proceed with charges. This ruling reinforces the principle that jurisdiction is a prerequisite that must be clearly articulated and substantively supported, particularly in the realm of serious crime such as organized crime and racketeering.
