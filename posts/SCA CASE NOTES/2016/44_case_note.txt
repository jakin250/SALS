Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Tyco International (Pty) Ltd v Golden Mile Trading 547 CC [2016] ZASCA 44

## FIRAC Case Notes

### F – Facts
On 7 November 2005, a collision occurred involving two trucks on the N1 national motorway, situated approximately 40 kilometers outside Richmond, South Africa ([1]). The appellants, Tyco International (Pty) Ltd and Fast ’n Fresh Transport (Pty) Ltd, owned the red truck driven by Samuel Fakude. The respondent, Golden Mile Trading 547 CC, owned the white truck driven by Daniel Groenewald Jr. The accident transpired as Fakude attempted to avoid an overtaking vehicle, resulting in the refrigeration unit dislodging from his truck and creating an obstruction on the roadway ([2]–[4]).

Later that night, after placing reflective triangles to warn approaching traffic and illuminating the obstruction with his headlights, Fakude left the scene to rest in the cabin of his truck, believing the situation was under control. Subsequently, Groenewald Jr drove southbound, and despite the warning from a third vehicle's headlights—it temporarily blinded him—he failed to reduce his speed significantly, leading to a collision with both the refrigeration box and the red truck ([5]–[9]). Following the incident, both Tyco and Golden Mile filed claims for damages against each other in the Western Cape Division of the High Court ([9]).

The trial court, finding both parties negligent, apportioned the damages disproportionately, attributing 80% liability to the appellants and 20% to the respondent ([11]). The appellants appealed against this decision.

### I – Issues
The primary issue on appeal was whether the trial court's apportionment of damages was appropriate given the negligence of both drivers ([12]). Specifically, the court needed to determine:
1. The causal relationship between Fakude's negligence and the collision, particularly relating to his departure from the scene and the placement of warning triangles.
2. The degree of fault attributed to Groenewald Jr in contrast to that of Fakude.
3. Whether the trial court had exercised its discretion correctly under the Apportionment of Damages Act 34 of 1956 ([12]).

### R – Rules
The appeal relied on Section 1(1)(a) of the Apportionment of Damages Act, which allows courts to reduce damages suffered by a claimant based on the claimant's fault ([12]). The legal framework surrounding negligence requires proof of a causal link between the negligent act and the resultant harm. The court cited precedents, including *Transnet Ltd t/a Metrorail & Another v Witter* and other relevant case law, which outlined the judicial standards for appeals concerning damages apportionment, emphasizing the need for judicial discretion to be exercised correctly ([12]).

### A – Application
In assessing the respective negligence of the parties, the court systematically examined their actions leading up to the collision. The trial court had found that Fakude's decision to leave the scene, despite having previously managed traffic effectively to prevent accidents, constituted negligence ([9]). However, the appeal court contended that if Fakude had remained on site and maintained alertness, he likely could have averted the incident by properly directing traffic ([17]).

Simultaneously, the apportionment of fault for Groenewald Jr's negligence was also scrutinized. His failure to decrease speed significantly when alerted to the potential danger of the obstruction connected to the flashing lights raised his degree of fault ([20]). The court posited that both drivers deviated equally from the standard of care observed by a reasonable person under similar circumstances ([19]). Hence a reevaluation of the apportionment suggested a more equitable approach—50:50 or 60:40—in favor of the appellants, rather than the trial court's initial 80:20 allocation ([13]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the trial court had misapplied the standards of fault and, therefore, erred in its apportionment of damages. The court determined an equal attribution of negligence to both Fakude and Groenewald Jr, resulting in a revised order of 50% liability for both parties. Consequently, each was instructed to bear its own costs ([22]). 

This case highlights the complex interactions of factual negligence, the importance of maintaining responsible actions in potentially hazardous situations, and the nuances involved in apportioning damages in accordance with established legal thresholds for negligence. The potential implications signal a shift towards more collaborative accountability in negligence matters on South African roads, reflecting an equitable stance on shared responsibility among negligent parties.
