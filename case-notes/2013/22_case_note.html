Case Note: Government of the Republic of Zimbabwe v Fick and Others [2013] ZACC 22

FIRAC Method

F – Facts:
The case originates from a land reform policy in Zimbabwe that expropriated agricultural land without compensation, placing affected farmers, including respondents Louis Karel Fick, Richard Etheredge, and William Michael Campbell, in a position where they could not seek legal redress domestically ([1], [12]). The farmers turned to the Southern African Development Community (SADC) Tribunal, which ruled in their favor and ordered Zimbabwe to protect their land rights and compensate for the expropriated farms ([2], [14]). Zimbabwe's failure to comply with these orders led the farmers to seek enforcement through the North Gauteng High Court in South Africa ([4], [16]). The High Court granted registration of the Tribunal’s costs order against Zimbabwe, prompting Zimbabwe to appeal, asserting a lack of jurisdiction ([18]).

I – Issues:

  1. Did the South African courts have jurisdiction to register and enforce the costs order issued by the SADC Tribunal?
  2. Was Zimbabwe entitled to claim sovereign immunity against enforcement of the Tribunal’s order in South Africa?
  3. Did the amending agreement of the SADC Treaty bind Zimbabwe, thereby granting jurisdiction to the SADC Tribunal?
  4. Were common law requirements for enforcing foreign judgments satisfied in the context of this case? ([23], [24], [32]).

R – Rules:
The court relied on several legal frameworks:

  • The Treaty of the Southern African Development Community establishing the SADC, which mandates member states to promote human rights and rule of law ([5], [6]).
  • Article 32 of the Tribunal Protocol, which obligates member states, including Zimbabwe, to take measures facilitating the enforcement of Tribunal decisions ([33]).
  • The Sovereign Immunities Act, which provides guidelines on foreign state immunity in South Africa ([32]).
  • The common law principles on the enforcement of foreign judgments, as established in cases like Purser v Sales ([38]).

A – Application:
The Application of Law to Facts was addressed in the following key respects:

  1. Jurisdiction: The court determined that South African courts possessed jurisdiction to enforce the Tribunal's costs order based on the binding nature of the amended SADC Treaty and Tribunal Protocol ([30], [47]). Zimbabwe, having ratified the Treaty, could not later assert that the court lacked jurisdiction ([46]).
  2. Sovereign Immunity: The court found that Zimbabwe had waived its sovereign immunity when it participated in the SADC Tribunal proceedings, thus allowing South African courts to exercise jurisdiction pursuant to Article 32 of the Tribunal Protocol ([32], [35]).
  3. Common Law Requirements: The court explored whether the common law criteria for the enforcement of foreign judgments, notably that the foreign tribunal had the requisite jurisdiction and the judgment was final and not contrary to public policy, were met. The judgment of the Tribunal was deemed final, and no public policy issues were identified since the subject matter involved human rights ([38], [48]).
  4. Development of Common Law: Given the absence of specific legislation regulating the enforcement of Tribunal decisions in South Africa, the court acknowledged the need to develop common law to recognize international tribunal judgments as enforceable ([53], [60]). This led to a ruling that the term "foreign court" covered international tribunals like the SADC Tribunal, thus allowing enforcement under domestic law ([64]).

C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court ruled in favor of the respondents, granting leave to appeal and dismissing Zimbabwe's appeal with costs ([74]). The judgment underscored the constitutionality of enforcing the SADC Tribunal's decisions in South Africa, reinforcing the principles of human rights and access to justice within the framework of regional law. It emphasized that South Africa had an obligation to uphold international treaty agreements, especially regarding human rights, thus paving the way for potential future collaborations and enforcement of international tribunal decisions on its soil ([71], [72], [73]). The development of common law to include recognition of international tribunal judgments was deemed essential to fulfill constitutional obligations and maintain adherence to the rule of law.
