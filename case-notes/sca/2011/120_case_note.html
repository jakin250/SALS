Supreme Court of Appeal

Netshituka v Netshituka [2011] ZASCA 120

The deceased had multiple customary marriages, the last being with Tshinakaho on 1 December 1956, which, like the others, was unregistered ([4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011 [2011] ZASCA 120

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The present case arises from a dispute regarding both the validity of a civil marriage and a will connected to the late Masewa Joseph Netshituka. The appellant, Rudzani Netshituka, sought to have declared null and void the civil marriage between the deceased and the first respondent, Joyce Munyadizwi Netshituka, that was contracted on 17 January 1997 ([1]). The appellant, along with co-applicants who are not a part of this appeal, based their contest on the existence of a customary marriage between the deceased and Tshinakaho Netshituka, an assertion that was initially made in a founding affidavit prior to Tshinakaho's passing ([3]).

The deceased had multiple customary marriages, the last being with Tshinakaho on 1 December 1956, which, like the others, was unregistered ([4]). After the deceased's death on 4 January 2008, the appellant and her co-applicants sought to challenge both the marriage to Joyce and the validity of the deceased's last will, executed on 20 November 2007, citing that he lacked the capacity due to chronic ill-health ([5]).

The respondents opposed the appeal, asserting that the deceased’s marriage to Joyce was lawful, referencing a claimed divorce from one of the previous customary wives, Martha Mosle Netshituka, prior to the civil marriage to Joyce ([7]). The matter was centered on the interpretation of Section 22(1) of the Black Administration Act 38 of 1927 and related legislative provisions ([6]).

I – Issues

  1. Marital Validity: Whether the civil marriage contracted between Mr. Netshituka and Joyce, during the subsistence of existing customary unions, is valid under South African law ([7]).
  2. Will Validity: Whether the deceased possessed the legal capacity to execute his last will given his state of health and mental capacity at the time ([16]).

R – Rules

The court relied upon several statutory provisions to resolve these issues:

  1. Black Administration Act 38 of 1927: Specifically, Section 22, which restricts a male partner in a customary union from contracting a marriage with another woman during the subsistence of that union ([9]). The statute permits a civil marriage under certain conditions but prohibits such if the man is still bound to a customary union.
  2. Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998: This Act acknowledges the legal standing of customary marriages and their implications concerning the contracting of civil marriages and property rights.
  3. Nkambula v Linda [1951] established that a man in a customary union cannot validly marry another while the first union is still recognized as valid ([8]).

A – Application

The court began by affirmatively recognizing that the customary marriages of the deceased remained intact despite the civil marriage to Joyce ([12]). The judgment highlighted that Section 22(1) required unequivocal termination of any existing customary union before a new civil marriage could be validly contracted. The testimony concerning the civil marriage occurring without proper legal clearance given the husband's status in customary unions therefore rendered it a nullity ([14]).

Regarding the validity of the will, the court assessed medical evidence that indicated the deceased was chronically ill but mentally competent at the time of the will's execution. Experts concluded from supporting documents that he was “mentally normal” despite his illnesses ([16]). The court found no grounds that would deem the will invalid based on allegations concerning ownership of property and ambiguities therein. The court underscored that mental capacity at the time of execution must be proven as impaired, which was not established by the evidence presented ([19]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the court upheld part of the appeal, declaring the marriage between the first respondent and the deceased null and void, while affirming the validity of the will on the basis of mental competence ([20]). The ruling reasserts the importance of adherence to existing laws governing customary unions in South Africa and emphasizes the necessity of clear evidence to disprove mental capacity regarding will execution. The implication of this decision is that practitioners in family and estate law must navigate the complex interplay between customary and civil law when advising clients and handling such cases, thereby ensuring compliance with legislative requirements while protecting the interests of all parties involved.

The court’s determination regarding costs indicated a fair approach, emphasizing the shared responsibility in legal proceedings, particularly where the appellant was partially successful ([20]).
