# Case Note: McGregor v Public Health and Social Development Sectoral Bargaining Council and Others [2021] ZACC 14

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])

The case involves Dr. Charles James McGregor, who was employed as the Head of Anaesthesiology at George Hospital, part of the Department of Health, Western Cape. Following an internal disciplinary inquiry in December 2016, Dr. McGregor was dismissed for four charges of misconduct, which included allegations of sexual harassment against an intern thirty years his junior. The specific charges included making unwelcome sexual suggestions and inappropriate physical contact while on duty ([paras 3–4]).

Dr. McGregor appealed the dismissal internally, but his appeal was dismissed. He subsequently claimed an unfair dismissal before the Public Health and Social Development Sectoral Bargaining Council, where the arbitrator found him guilty on three charges but concluded that the dismissal was substantively and procedurally unfair. Consequently, the arbitrator awarded him compensation equivalent to six months' remuneration, amounting to R924,679.92 ([paras 4–6]).

Both parties contested the arbitrator’s ruling, with Dr. McGregor seeking reinstatement and the Department of Health seeking a review of the compensation award, claiming the amount was excessive. The Labour Court later upheld the arbitrator’s findings but varied the decision regarding the substantive unfairness of the dismissal. On further appeal, the Labour Appeal Court agreed with the Labour Court’s finding and declined to revisit the compensation amount due to an oversight regarding the Department’s cross-appeal ([paras 6–8]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2, 11, 12])

The primary legal questions presented to the Constitutional Court revolved around:

1. Whether the Labour Appeal Court was correct in its conclusions regarding Dr. McGregor’s dismissal.
2. Whether the award of six months’ compensation was appropriate, considering the findings related to substantive and procedural unfairness.
3. Whether the Department’s cross-appeal against the Labour Court’s decision on compensation should be upheld ([paras 2, 11–12]).

Additionally, the case addresses broader constitutional implications regarding the right to fair labour practices under Section 23 of the Constitution, as well as the integrity of internal disciplinary processes in matters involving accusations of sexual misconduct ([para 9]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 21–24, 33–34, 46–48])

The court relied on several provisions from the Labour Relations Act (LRA) and applicable precedents:

1. **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995**:
   - Section 193(1)(c): Permits compensation awards for unfair dismissals.
   - Section 194(1): States that compensation must be just and equitable but cannot exceed twelve months’ remuneration.
  
2. **Relevant Case Law**:
   - *National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality v Minister of Home Affairs* acknowledged that an appellate court may only interfere with lower court discretion under specific circumstances.
   - *Kemp t/a Centralmed v Rawlins* distinguished between broad discretion in awarding relief for unfair dismissal and the narrower discretion in determining compensation amounts ([paras 24, 27–28, 33]).

The ratio of these statutes and judgments emphasizes the need for careful consideration and justification when altering compensation linked to findings of procedural and substantive fairness.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 45–48])

Justice Khampepe's opinion underlined the significance of power dynamics in cases of sexual harassment and acknowledged the serious nature of Dr. McGregor's misconduct. The court highlighted the following key considerations in its ruling:

1. **Discretion in Compensation**: The court noted that while the first two levels of ruling upheld the procedural unfairness of the dismissal, the dismissal was ruled substantively fair based on the nature of the misconduct. Therefore, an assumption of justified compensation based on procedural unfairness alone was incorrect ([paras 34–39, 46]).
  
2. **Implications of Misconduct**: The court emphasized that the nature of the alleged sexual harassment was severely detrimental not only to the victim but also to broader workplace equality. The court underscored the importance of signaling through penalties that sexual misconduct will not result in financial reward ([paras 42–44, 46–48]).

3. **Compensation Adjustments**: Ultimately, the court ruled that the six months' compensation awarded was excessive and reduced it to two months' remuneration. This adjustment stemmed from a belief that the procedural lapses did not merit such a significant financial award, especially in light of the seriousness of Dr. McGregor's proven misconduct ([paras 48]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 46–51])

The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the Department's cross-appeal and ruled to reduce the compensation awarded to Dr. McGregor to the equivalent of two months’ remuneration. The ratio decidendi highlighted that while procedural unfairness requires redress, it does not do so at the expense of justice, particularly in cases of serious misconduct such as sexual harassment ([paras 46–51]).

This decision sends a clear message about the gravity of workplace sexual harassment, framing it as unacceptable behavior that undermines workplace equality and dignity. The ruling affirms the necessity for proportional redress in cases involving serious misconduct and clarifies the standards for compensatory awards under the LRA amidst contentious labor disputes.

### Implications
The ruling reinforces the commitment to uphold victims' rights and suggests a potential pivot towards ensuring that disciplinary actions for sexual misconduct are met with appropriate sanctions, thus fostering a more equitable work environment.
