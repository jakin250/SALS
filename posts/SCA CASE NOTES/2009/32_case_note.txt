# Case Note: Mzizi v The State [2009] ZASCA 32 (30 March 2009) – FIRAC Analysis

## F – Facts

[1] The applicant, Edmund Gugulethu Mzizi, was tried in the Mmabatho High Court on charges of murder and unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition. He was convicted of murder on 6 February 2001 and sentenced to 30 years' imprisonment, while he was acquitted of the firearm charges. His application for leave to appeal was denied on 14 March 2003.

[2] Mzizi subsequently sought to apply for four special entries to be recorded in the initial proceedings under section 317 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the Act). This application was heard on 2 March 2007, and the judgment was delivered on 19 July 2007. The trial judge acknowledged only one special entry, which related to the admissibility of a hearsay statement made by the deceased.

[3] Mzizi's papers for further appeal were only lodged in the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) in February 2008, prompting a request for condonation due to late submission. The need for condonation arose because applications for appeal and special entries had to be lodged within 21 days of the lower court's decision.

[4] The critical facts surrounding the case include the events leading to the death of Captain Tatisi, who sustained a fatal gunshot wound during a police chase. Prior to his death, Tatisi identified Mzizi as “Gugu” while being transported to the hospital, a statement which was later contested as hearsay evidence in the trial.

## I – Issues

[5] The primary legal issues to be determined by the SCA included:

1. Whether Mzizi had established good cause for the delay in submitting his applications for condonation, given the stringent requirements set out in the Act.
2. Whether the admission of the hearsay statement “That’s Gugu” by the deceased constituted a legal irregularity sufficient to affect the conviction.
3. Whether the applicant had demonstrated any reasonable prospects of success in his appeal against conviction and sentence, which could justify the granting of condonation.
4. Whether the application to lead further evidence could be sustained under the prevailing evidentiary standards.

## R – Rules

[6] The rules governing condonation are set out in sections 316 and 317 of the Criminal Procedure Act, which stipulate that an application for appeal outside the prescribed timeframe requires a satisfactory explanation and demonstration of reasonable prospects of success ([paras 8-9]).

[7] The requirements for the introduction of further evidence on appeal were outlined in _S v De Jager_ as needing a sufficient rationale for the evidence not having been presented at trial, prima facie likelihood of the evidence's truth, and material relevance to the trial's outcome ([paras 17-18]).

[8] Additionally, the admissibility of hearsay within the context of legal proceedings is now regulated by section 3 of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988, which requires that hearsay evidence must meet specific conditions for admissibility ([paras 22]).


## A – Application

[9] Mzizi argued that the delay in his application resulted from his financial constraints and loss of confidence in his legal representation, thus justifying the late application for condonation. However, the SCA found the explanations insufficient, particularly given the lack of immediate efforts made to pursue an appeal when he had the financial means to do so ([paras 10-12]).

[10] The court also noted that some key delays were attributable to the applicant’s legal representatives, and the logistical complications presented did not amount to a viable excuse ([para 15]). 

[11] On the issue of the hearsay statement by Tatisi, the SCA assumed for the sake of argument that the admission of this evidence constituted an irregularity. Nonetheless, they concluded that such an irregularity did not lead to a miscarriage of justice. The remaining evidence, even without the hearsay statement, was deemed sufficient to support the conviction, as the identification of Mzizi was corroborated by multiple witnesses ([paras 23-24]).

[12] Regarding the application to lead further evidence, the SCA found that the proposed testimony of Mzizi's mother did not significantly change the weight of evidence that had already been presented in the trial. Moreover, the court reaffirmed that reopening criminal trials for further evidence is disfavored, as it could undermine the finality of criminal convictions ([para 18]).

## C – Conclusion

[13] The SCA ultimately held that Mzizi had not established grounds for condonation at the procedural level. The court underscored the importance of a timely appeal process, emphasizing the requisite showing of good cause and reasonable prospects of success ([para 25]).

[14] The judgment concluded with the refusal of the condonation application and the striking off of the matter from the court roll, reaffirming the importance of adhering to established procedural timelines in the criminal justice system ([para 26]). 

[15] This decision highlights the court's commitment to maintaining procedural safeguards while simultaneously ensuring that justice is served, illustrating the balance between individual rights in the face of rigorous procedural demands. The ratio decidendi reinforces the principle that a combination of satisfactory explanations for delays, alongside clear prospects of successful appeals, is foundational to the success of cautions in criminal procedure.
