Polovin v The Director of Public Prosecutions and Others (1230/2022)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 2019 ([para 4]). Green subsequently requested the issuance of a certificate of nolle prosequi from the DPP on December 4

F - Facts

The case Polovin v The Director of Public Prosecutions and Others (1230/2022) arises from a longstanding dispute between David Neville Polovin, a senior attorney (the applicant), and his neighbor Liesel Jane Green (the second respondent), regarding unauthorized access to confidential information. The events triggering this litigation occurred in May 2012, when Polovin accessed Green's confidential credit records without her consent while using a colleague's login details to the Law Data System of TransUnion, which is a violation of Section 86(1) of the Electronic Communications and Transactions Act 25 of 2002 (ECTA) ([para 4]).

Initially, Green laid a criminal charge against Polovin, prompting the Director of Public Prosecutions for the Western Cape (the first respondent) to decline prosecution on November 8, 2019 ([para 4]). Green subsequently requested the issuance of a certificate of nolle prosequi from the DPP on December 4, 2019. The certificate, which allows for private prosecution, was issued on May 20, 2020, but lapsed within three months as the prosecution was not instituted ([para 5]).

After complicating events, the Acting DPP re-issued the certificate on October 14, 2020, this time including additional charges. Polovin filed a frontal challenge against the private prosecution in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, where both parts of his application were dismissed with costs ([paras 7-8]). He then sought leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal regarding various issues, fundamentally questioning the legitimacy of the private prosecution and the sufficiency of the DPP's re-issuance of the nolle prosequi certificate ([paras 1-2]).

I - Issues

The pertinent legal issues considered by the Supreme Court of Appeal can be categorized into two categories ([para 8]):

  1. Leave to Appeal: Whether leave to appeal should be granted under sections 17(1)(a)(i) and (ii) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, focusing on the likelihood of success in appeal proceedings.
  2. Merits of the Appeal: Should the appeal succeed on:
  • The validity of the certificate issuance according to Section 7(1)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act 55 of 1977 (CPA), specifically whether jurisdictional requirements were satisfied.
  • The locus standi of the private prosecutor (Green) under Section 7(1)(a) of the CPA.
  • Whether the DPP had the authority to re-issue the nolle prosequi certificate, and if it was permissible to include additional charges beyond those initially delineated.
  • The overarching question of whether the private prosecution itself is vexatious and against public policy.

R - Rules

The major legal statutes and case precedents considered in this case include:

  1. Criminal Procedure Act 55 of 1977 (CPA):
  • Section 7 allows private individuals who suffer substantial and peculiar interests to initiate a private prosecution after the DPP has declined to prosecute ([paras 14-15]).
  1. Protection of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA):
  • The appeal raised whether the issuance of the nolle prosequi certificate constitutes administrative action subjected to the principles of legality and rationality ([para 18]).
  1. Relevant Case Law:
  • Singh v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development: Established initial interpretations of requirements for private prosecution.
  • Nundalal v Director of Public Prosecutions KZN: Provided conflicting views on the role and discretion of the DPP in issuing nolle prosequi certificates.
  • National Director of Public Prosecutions v Freedom Under Law: Explored the extent of judicial review applicable to DPP's decisions ([paras 12-18]).

A - Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled primarily in favor of the respondent on both the procedural and substantive aspects ([para 46]).

  1. Leave to Appeal: The Court found the appeal did involve questions of law of significant public interest and granted leave to appeal. It highlighted that conflicting interpretations of the CPA's provisions necessitated judicial clarification ([paras 8, 12]).
  2. Merits: The Court examined the validity of the nolle prosequi certificate and concluded:
  • Locus Standi: It ruled that Green had sufficiently demonstrated a "substantial and peculiar interest" arising from the injury inflicted by Polovin's unauthorized actions ([paras 22-28]).
  • Re-issuance of the Certificate: The argument that the Acting DPP acted beyond his powers in re-issuing the certificate was rejected. The Court confirmed that the DPP retains authority to issue such certificates, and additional charges could be included, particularly as they arise from the context of the case ([paras 30-38]).
  • Vexatious Nature of Prosecution: The Court dismissed Polovin's claims of the private prosecution being vexatious and against public policy, recognizing the private prosecutor’s right to seek redress through the courts ([paras 39-42]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal and confirmed the orders of the High Court, with costs ([paras 40-46]). The ruling clarifies the procedural boundaries of private prosecution within South African law, especially in the context of the DPP's role and the jurisdictional requirements underpinning private prosecutions. The decision underscores the balance between an individual’s right to seek justice through private prosecution and the requisite checks against potential misuse of that right. By affirming Green’s standing and the appropriateness of the DPP's actions, the Court reinforced the judicial framework within which private prosecutions operate, advocating for a judicious resolution to disputes brought before the legal system.

This case serves as a pivotal reference point concerning private prosecutions in South Africa, elucidating the delicate interplay between prosecutorial discretion, individual rights, and public policy considerations.
