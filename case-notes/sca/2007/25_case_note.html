Supreme Court of Appeal

City of Johannesburg v Rand Properties (Pty) Ltd 2007 SCA 25 (RSA)

The case centers on the City of Johannesburg (the Appellant) which sought to evict occupants from unsafe buildings under its jurisdiction, invoking the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977, specifically s 12(4)(b).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007

FIRAC Case Note

F - Facts

The case centers on the City of Johannesburg (the Appellant) which sought to evict occupants from unsafe buildings under its jurisdiction, invoking the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977, specifically s 12(4)(b). The Appellant contended that the buildings in question posed significant risks to the health and safety of the occupants due to their dilapidated state ([paras 1–5]).

Upon conducting site inspections, the Court found that the condition of the buildings was critical, highlighting issues such as inadequate sanitation, health hazards, and severe fire risks, creating an emergency situation for the inhabitants ([paras 2–10]). Many of the occupants were characterized as poor and vulnerable, lacking formal employment or income ([para 2]). The City issued notices under s 12(4)(b), requiring occupants to vacate immediately for their safety. However, the High Court initially dismissed the City's application for eviction and granted a counter-application in favor of the respondents, asserting that the City was mandated to provide adequate alternative housing before eviction could occur, citing constitutional obligations regarding the right to housing ([paras 14–16]).

I - Issues

The key legal issues addressed in this appeal revolved around:

  1. Whether the City of Johannesburg was precluded from exercising its powers to order eviction from unsafe buildings unless suitable alternative housing was provided to the occupants.
  2. Whether the provisions of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) applied to the proceedings initiated by the City against the respondents ([paras 4, 13, 17]).

The case also considered whether the City’s actions were consistent with its constitutional obligations, particularly under s 26 of the Constitution, which guarantees the right of access to adequate housing ([para 34]).

R - Rules

The legal framework governing this case includes:

  • National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977: Specifically, s 12(4)(b), which permits local authorities to issue notices requiring occupants to vacate unsafe buildings ([para 6]).
  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996: This includes s 26, which sets the groundwork for the right to housing and stipulates that evictions must not occur without a court order and must consider all relevant circumstances ([paras 35, 38]).
  • Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE): This Act is relevant when determining the processes required for evictions, especially in relation to unlawful occupiers ([para 57]).

Relevant case law includes Port Elizabeth Municipality v Various Occupiers ([2005] ZACC 7) which underscores the need for considerations of all relevant circumstances surrounding evictions ([paras 31, 40]).

A - Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the City of Johannesburg had the authority to issue evacuation notices based on safety concerns independently of providing alternative housing. The Court clarified that while the City could not be compelled to house residents in the inner city, it bore a constitutional obligation to provide minimum shelter to those without alternative accommodation after eviction ([paras 5, 68–70]).

The Court also navigated the relationship between the provisions of s 12(4)(b) and PIE, ultimately finding that PIE's provisions did not apply in scenarios where a local authority acted in the interest of public safety ([para 59]). The ruling emphasized that the City’s decision to evict was rational and legally valid under s 12 of the Act, as it acted out of necessity to preserve safety ([para 66]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court concluded by upholding the City of Johannesburg's appeal, asserting that the eviction of occupants from dangerous buildings under s 12(4)(b) was lawful. However, it also stressed that the City had a constitutional obligation to provide temporary accommodation for those who would be left homeless due to the eviction ([para 77]).

Consequently, the ruling underscored the delicate balance between the City’s mandate to ensure public safety and its obligations to those vulnerable residents needing housing assistance. The appeal was granted, with the understanding that while occupants could be evicted for safety reasons, the city must still address the housing needs of those affected through emergency housing solutions ([para 78]).
