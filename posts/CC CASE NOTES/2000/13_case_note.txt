Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2000

# Case Note: Sunnyboy Edward Saane v Susan R. Hulme NO & Minister of Justice (CCT 33/00)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

The case involves Sunnyboy Edward Saane, a law student at the University of South Africa, who sought leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court of South Africa from a conviction for corruption handed down by the Johannesburg Magistrates’ Court. On 27 September 1999, the trial court sentenced him to two years' imprisonment, granting him leave to appeal to the High Court the following day ([para 1]). 

As of 6 July 2000, nearly ten months post-sentencing, Saane's appeal had yet to be set down for hearing in the High Court. This delay became a point of contention for Saane, as he alleged violation of his constitutional right to a speedy trial, which is essential for the protection of fair trial rights enshrined in the Constitution of South Africa ([para 2]).

The Constitutional Court, upon request from its President, directed inquiries to the Clerk of the Magistrate’s Court to understand the cause of the significant delay in processing Saane's appeal. It was revealed that the holdup stemmed from difficulties in preparing a corrected record of the trial, which was extensive, comprising 1,296 pages ([para 3]). Ultimately, the record was lodged with the High Court, bringing the promise of a hearing date ([para 4]).

### I – Issues

The principal legal issues raised in this case are:
1. Whether the Constitutional Court should allow the direct appeal from a conviction in the Magistrates’ Court, given the pending High Court appeal ([para 1]);
2. Whether the prolonged delay in setting a date for Saane's appeal infringed upon his constitutional right to a speedy trial as protected under section 35(3)(d) of the Constitution of South Africa ([para 2]).

### R – Rules

The relevant constitutional provisions and precedents discussed in this judgment underscore the protections afforded to individuals in criminal proceedings, particularly regarding the right to a fair trial and the right to a speedy trial. Section 35 of the Constitution, particularly subsection 3(d), guarantees that every accused person has the right to a trial that is conducted without unreasonable delay. This provision has been interpreted in various cases to mean that undue procedural delays by the state can constitute a violation of constitutional rights ([para 2]). 

The court generally applies the principle that where procedural remedies, such as an appeal to the High Court, are available and pending, direct appeals to the Constitutional Court should not be entertained in order to preserve the hierarchy and efficiency of the judicial system ([para 4]).

### A – Application

In applying these rules to the facts of the case, the court assessed Saane's request for direct appeal against the framework established to manage appeals and ensure judicial efficiency. Despite recognizing the importance of safeguarding an accused individual's rights, the court emphasized that there were ongoing proceedings in the High Court where all the issues raised by Saane could be addressed adequately ([para 4]).

The court found that while the delays in the hearing of his appeal were regrettable, they were not a sufficient basis to bypass the traditional appellate structure. Instead, the appropriate remedy was for Saane to exhaust the available legal recourse in the High Court, which would necessitate a careful examination of both factual and legal issues that he raised ([para 4]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately refused Saane's application for leave to appeal directly to the Court, concluding that the High Court was the appropriate forum to resolve matters arising from his conviction and sentence ([para 4]). The Court asserted that the pending appeal in the High Court would provide Saane the opportunity to thoroughly contest the issues related to his conviction and alleged trial delays.

This decision affirms the importance of adhering to established judicial processes and upholds the rights enshrined in the Constitution, while also reinforcing the necessity of judicial efficiency in managing appeals. The ruling serves as a reminder of the balance that must be struck between individual rights and the functioning of the judicial system as a whole.
