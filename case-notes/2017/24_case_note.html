Baron and Others v Claytile (Pty) Limited and Another [2017] ZACC 24

The matter concerns an appeal against a judgment from the Land Claims Court (LCC) regarding the eviction of several occupiers from a brick manufacturing farm owned by Claytile (Pty) Limited.

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 24

F – Facts

The matter concerns an appeal against a judgment from the Land Claims Court (LCC) regarding the eviction of several occupiers from a brick manufacturing farm owned by Claytile (Pty) Limited. The applicants, consisting of former employees and their families, had resided on the farm under the terms of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act (ESTA) after their employment ceased between 2006 and 2011 ([1]–[6]).

On 3 November 2012, Claytile issued eviction notices, leading to eviction proceedings in the Magistrate's Court in June 2013, after the applicants failed to vacate ([8]–[9]). The Magistrate's Court found their eviction just and equitable, granting the order on 7 February 2014, which required the applicants to vacate by 30 October 2014 ([13]–[14]). The LCC later confirmed this order on review, rejecting the applicants' claims regarding the lack of suitable alternative accommodation and emphasizing the hardship to the first respondent ([15]–[16]).

The applicants contested their eviction on grounds involving compliance with sections 25 and 26 of the Constitution and provisions of ESTA, questioning whether their eviction was justifiable given the absence of suitable alternative accommodation ([10]–[18]). After unsuccessful appeals to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), they sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([22]–[23]).

I – Issues

The key constitutional and legal questions addressed by the Court included:

  1. Compliance with ESTA: Whether the eviction observed the requirements of section 10 of ESTA concerning suitable alternative accommodation for the occupiers in light of their constitutional rights under sections 25 (property rights) and 26 (right to adequate housing) ([10]–[11]).
  2. Duties of Private Landowners: The obligations placed upon private landowners in relation to mutually accommodating former employees and providing for suitable alternative accommodation during eviction ([29]).
  3. Constitutional Obligations: The extent of the City of Cape Town Municipality's duty to provide adequate housing and how far this obligation extends in cases of eviction initiated by private landowners ([30]–[47]).

R – Rules

The Court relied on an array of legal principles derived from:

  1. The Constitution of South Africa: Specifically, sections 25 and 26, which protect property rights and the right to housing ([10]–[11]).
  2. Extension of Security of Tenure Act (ESTA): Key provisions included section 10's requirements regarding eviction, and section 1's definition of "suitable alternative accommodation" ([11]–[17]).
  3. Precedents: The Court referenced several significant cases, including Daniels v Scribante [2017] ZACC 13, which clarifies tenant protections under ESTA, and Port Elizabeth Municipality v Peoples Dialogue on Land and Shelter concerning the balance between housing and property interests ([3]–[4], [18]).
  4. Legislative Framework: The preamble to ESTA emphasizes the Act's goal of providing security of tenure and regulating evictions ([10]).

A – Application

In applying the legal standards, the Court undertook a detailed examination of whether suitable alternative accommodation had been adequately provided. The City of Cape Town had proposed housing in Wolwerivier; however, the applicants rejected this due to the perceived inadequacy of conditions, including concerns about distance from work and school ([30]–[32]).

The Court noted that while the City must take reasonable legislative measures. The provision of housing must align with available resources, reiterating that the state has an ongoing obligation to provide housing alternatives ([36]–[46]). The available evidence suggested that the City made efforts to secure accommodation, yet it was the applicants’ refusal to accept the offer that ultimately led to the Court's finding in favor of the eviction, underscoring that delaying eviction based on housing dissatisfaction was untenable ([38]–[40]).

The Court further emphasized the need to balance the private landowner's rights with the constitutional obligations owed to the occupiers, indicating that landowners are endowed with responsibilities towards assisting in finding alternative accommodation but not to the extent of wholly replicating the State's duties ([35]–[49]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court dismissed the appeal, confirming the eviction order from the Magistrate's Court. It ruled that the first respondent's rights could not be indefinitely subordinated to accommodate the applicants, who had lived rent-free for several years following the termination of their employment. The Court recognized that while the evicted parties could not indefinitely resist eviction on the grounds of unwillingness to accept terms for alternative accommodation, the City had a constitutional duty to facilitate access to suitable housing for evicted individuals ([54]).

The judgment highlights the necessity to adhere strictly to the legislative provisions on eviction while ensuring that constitutional rights and duties, particularly regarding housing and property, are harmonized within South Africa's constitutional framework. The implications of this ruling extend to the delineation of responsibilities between private landowners and the State in eviction processes involving former employees or occupants.
