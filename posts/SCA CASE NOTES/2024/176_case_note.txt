Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Siyabonga Gugulethu Galela (Ex Parte Application) (1294/2023)

## F – Facts

The case involves Siyabonga Gugulethu Galela (Appellant), who applied for admission as a legal practitioner under the Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014 (LPA). Ms. Galela commenced her admission application in June 2023, supporting it with documents that included her academic transcript from the University of the Witwatersrand (Wits), indicating completion of a Bachelor of Arts on 19 December 2018 and an LLB on 14 December 2020; however, she did not attach her LLB degree certificate ([para 3]).

The Gauteng Provincial Office of the Legal Practice Council (LPC) noted two deficiencies in Ms. Galela's application: (1) the absence of her LLB certificate and (2) her failure to disclose her directorship in Varsigator Solutions (Pty) Ltd during her practical vocational training (PVT), which she believed had become defunct in 2017. Following an investigation, the LPC questioned Ms. Galela about her directorship, which she claimed had ceased after 2017, and her financial difficulties that prevented her from obtaining her degree certificate ([paras 4-10]).

Consequently, the high court dismissed her application, ruling that Ms. Galela was not a fit and proper person to be admitted, primarily due to her misrepresentation regarding her role at Varsigator and her outstanding university debts ([paras 11-13]).

## I – Issues

The critical legal questions before the court were:

1. What constitutes a fit and proper person for admission as a legal practitioner under the LPA?
2. What information is an applicant obliged to disclose in an ex parte application for such admission?
3. What are the legal consequences of failing to fully disclose pertinent information, particularly regarding directorship in a company and outstanding academic qualifications? ([paras 1-2, 12]).

## R – Rules

The admission of legal practitioners is governed by the LPA, particularly sections 24 and 26. Section 24(6) stipulates that the high court must admit a person who is duly qualified, a South African citizen or permanent resident, has submitted the required application information, and is a fit and proper person ([para 2]).

Rule 22.1.5 of the LPC establishes that a candidate attorney cannot have a pecuniary interest in an attorney's practice or engage in other business without prior written consent from the LPC. If violated, the contract is deemed void ab initio, unless good cause is shown ([para 5]).

The assessment of fit and proper status draws from established case law, including the precedent set in *Ex parte Tlotlego*, which affirms that courts should not act as gatekeepers limiting access based on candidates' financial statuses, so long as satisfactory explanations are provided ([paras 19-21]).

## A – Application

During the proceedings, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that Ms. Galela had demonstrated good cause for her prior violations of LPC rules after she submitted a supplementary affidavit addressing the LPC's queries. Her explanations included a detailed account of her financial hardships and the circumstances leading to her inability to settle her university fees, which she contended was a genuine issue rather than one of recalcitrance. Ms. Galela’s mother corroborated this narrative ([paras 18, 23]) while also clarifying the circumstances surrounding the deregistration of Varsigator and her daughter's assertion that it was inactive after 2017 ([paras 22, 23]).

The court emphasized that the LPC had a duty to substantively engage with the applicant's situation concerning her admission. It highlighted that a transparent and candid disclosure, although incomplete initially, reinforced Ms. Galela’s legitimacy and integrity regarding her application ([paras 14, 15]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Ms. Galela's appeal, overturning the high court's dismissal of her admission application. It found that her non-disclosures, while concerning, did not stem from a dishonest intent but rather from misunderstandings regarding her directorship and financial obligations. The Court concluded that sufficient grounds existed to condone her earlier non-compliance with the LPC rules. Ultimately, Ms. Galela was declared fit and proper for admission as a legal practitioner, highlighting the court’s recognition of the need for a fair balance between stringent regulatory compliance and equitable access to the legal profession ([paras 24-24]).

This case underscores the importance of transparency and integrity in the admissions process for legal practitioners within South Africa while advocating for a nuanced understanding of an applicant's circumstances, especially in terms of financial barriers. The court reaffirmed that individuals should not be denied access to the profession due solely to past financial issues, provided adequate explanations are offered and remedies are sought.
