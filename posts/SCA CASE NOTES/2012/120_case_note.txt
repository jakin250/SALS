Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Ngqula v South African Airways (701/11) [2012] ZASCA 120

## F – Facts

The Appellant, Khayakhulu Ngqula, was the former Chief Executive Officer of South African Airways (Pty) Ltd (the Respondent). The Respondent had initiated two separate civil proceedings against the Appellant in the South Gauteng High Court (SGHC). In **Case No. 27955/2010**, the Respondent claimed approximately R26,581,794.77 from the Appellant, attributing this to alleged breaches of fiduciary duties based on his employment contract. In **Case No. 30920/2010**, the Respondent sought recovery of US $3,400,000 associated with a sponsorship agreement and R229,170 in unauthorized disbursements, arguing that the Appellant acted beyond his authority.

The Appellant contended that the SGHC lacked jurisdiction to hear these claims, asserting that jurisdiction was exclusively conferred to the North Gauteng High Court (NGHC) by a jurisdiction clause in his employment contract. Before the Appellant's defenses could be adjudicated, the Respondent applied for the removal of both actions to the NGHC, claiming such a move was more convenient and appropriate for addressing the disputes properly.

The SGHC, after hearing arguments from both parties, authorized the removal of the proceedings to the NGHC, ruling that the application was to bear the costs of the trial. The Appellant subsequently contested the SGHC's order, leading to the current appeal ([paras 1–10]).

## I – Issues

The central legal issue in this case is whether the order made by the SGHC to transfer proceedings to the NGHC is appealable. The Appellant argued that the removal order affected his right to defend himself based on the claim of prescription and thus should be subject to appeal. In contrast, the Respondent argued that the order was an interlocutory order, which typically does not qualify for appeal. The court’s examination of appealability focused on whether the order significantly impacted the resolution of the main dispute and the potential consequences of permitting the appeal to go forward ([paras 11–20]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework for this case is found under the **Interim Rationalisation of the Jurisdiction of the High Courts Act 41 of 2002**, specifically Section 3, which pertains to the removal of civil proceedings between different High Courts. Key principles of appealability were derived from established cases, including **Zweni v Minister of Law and Order** 1993 (1) SA 523 (A) and **Health Professions Council of South Africa v Emergency Medical Supplies and Training CC t/a EMS** 2010 (6) SA 457 (SCA), which articulate that appealability is contingent upon whether an order disposes of a significant portion of the relief sought, and whether it promotes an expeditious resolution of the main dispute ([paras 12–20]).

## A – Application

On assessing the nature of the SGHC's order authorizing the transfer of proceedings, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) noted that such orders typically function as procedural decisions aimed at facilitating a more effective resolution of disputes. The SCA articulated that the primary focus should be on the effect of the order rather than its label; since the order required the transfer of jurisdiction without resolving the core issues of liability or the merits of the claims, it did not dispose of any part of the relief sought in the primary action ([paras 14–16]).

In discussing the Appellant's argument regarding potential prescription claims, the court emphasized that any arguments related to prescription must be raised through proper pleadings in the NGHC, rather than being speculated upon in the context of the removal order. This perspective underscored the view that the SGHC's order did not infringe upon the substantive legal rights of the Appellant but rather facilitated the efficient management of the litigation ([paras 17–20]).

Thus, the court concluded that the removal order possessed none of the characteristics of an appealable order as discussed in **Zweni**, focusing on expeditious justice and resolving disputes effectively rather than delaying litigative processes ([paras 20–22]).

## C – Conclusion

The SCA ruled that the appeal against the order for removal of proceedings to the NGHC was not appealable and, hence, struck it off the roll. Furthermore, it ordered the Appellant to bear the costs of the appeal, including the costs incurred due to the engagement of two counsel, reinforcing the principle that parties who pursue appeals without a suitable legal foundation may face financial consequences for their actions. This decision underscores the importance of adhering to proper procedural avenues and maintaining an efficient judicial process ([paras 22–23]).

In summary, this case highlights significant principles regarding the appealability of interlocutory orders and procedural efficiencies in South African civil procedural law, establishing a precedent regarding the handling of jurisdictional transfers among courts.
