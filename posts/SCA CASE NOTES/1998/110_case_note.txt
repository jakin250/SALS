Source Folder: 1998

# Case Note: Republic of South Africa: Multilaterale Motorvoertuigongelukkefonds v Jan Johannes Van Wyk

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
On the night of December 24, 1991, the respondent, Jan Johannes Van Wyk, sustained serious bodily injuries when he was struck by a minibus driven by one Williams outside the Gordonia Hospital in Upington ([paras 2-3]). Following this incident, Van Wyk initiated a claim for damages against the appellant, the Multilaterale Motorvoertuigongelukkefonds, seeking compensation for the injuries he had suffered in accordance with the provisions of the Multilaterale Motorvoertuigongelukkefonds Act 93 of 1989 ([para 2]).  

At the onset of the trial, the presiding judge, Swart R, ordered that the issue of causation regarding the collision be determined separately before addressing the extent of the respondent's damages ([para 3]). Following the hearing of evidence and arguments, the court found that the appellant was liable for 40% of the damages, leading to a subsequent costs order which required the appellant to cover costs up to a specified date ([paras 3-4]). 

The trial court's ruling was subject to an application for leave to appeal, following which both parties sought to appeal the costs order and the primary liability findings ([paras 4-8]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues raised in the appeal include:
1. Whether the trial court erred in its assessment of the evidence regarding the cause of the collision ([paras 7-8]).
2. Whether the court's conclusion that both parties displayed negligence was justified ([paras 9-10]).
3. The appropriateness of the costs order imposed by the trial court in relation to the settlement offer made by the appellant ([paras 12-14]).

### R – Rules
Key legal principles and statutes pertinent to this case include:
- **Negligence**: The standard for establishing liability in tort requires proving (1) a duty of care; (2) breach of that duty; and (3) causation of damages ([para 9]).
- **The Multilaterale Motorvoertuigongelukkefonds Act**: This legislation governs claims and compensations involving road traffic accidents in South Africa ([para 2]).
- **Costs Discretion**: The discretionary powers of courts in awarding costs are governed by legal precedents, notably the case of *Rondalia Assurance Corporation of South Africa Ltd v Page and Others* 1975 (1) SA 708 (A), which emphasizes fairness in cost orders and the circumstances surrounding their imposition ([para 12]).

### A – Application
In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal examined the conflicting testimonies of the key witnesses. The trial court favored the account of Williams, the driver, over that of Bosman, a witness for the respondent, reflecting significant skepticism about the reliability of Bosman's recollection regarding the circumstances of the collision ([paras 7-8]). The court's conclusion was supported by a finding that Williams' version, which depicted the occurrence of the accident on the access road rather than the parking area, was more credible based on the evidence presented ([paras 6-8]).

The court also assessed both parties' alleged negligence. It found that while Van Wyk had crossed the road unsafely, Williams had failed to take sufficient care to avoid an accident by not actively scanning for pedestrians ([paras 9-10]). This led to a finding of concurrent negligence, with the trial court attributing 60% of the fault to Van Wyk and 40% to Williams.

Regarding the issue of costs, the appeal court upheld the discretion exercised by Swart R, highlighting that the offer made by the appellant was relayed late and did not afford the respondent a reasonable opportunity to consider it adequately. The court emphasized that both parties exhibited unreasonable conduct, which justified the ruling that each party would bear their own costs ([paras 12-14]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed both the appeal against liability and the cross-appeal concerning the costs order ([para 14]). The rationale underpinning the court’s decision was rooted in a thorough examination of the circumstantial evidence and the credibility of the witnesses. This ruling underscores the legal principle that findings of negligence must be based on a careful and unbiased evaluation of conflicting evidence ([para 9-10]). The implications of this case highlight the challenges in proving liability in negligent driving cases, especially in light of conflicting testimonies, and reaffirm the discretion exercised by courts in determining costs that promote fairness. Each party was ordered to bear their own costs, thus upholding the principle of equity in judicial costs awards ([para 14]).
