Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Monde v Viljoen NO & Others (1162/17)

### F – Facts

The case revolves around the eviction of Mzimkhulu Martiens Monde (the Appellant) from Millhurst Farm, located in De Doorns, Western Cape. Monde was employed as a farm worker since January 6, 1995, and was granted accommodation on the farm as part of his employment. His right to reside on the farm was stipulated to continue only as long as he was employed by the first respondent, Jacobus Johannes Viljoen NO (the First Respondent) and the Stonefield Trust (the First Respondent’s employer).

In March 2013, the appellant was dismissed due to a series of unauthorized absences, which he failed to contest through the labour dispute mechanisms of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA). Following his dismissal, the respondents, through their attorneys, communicated that Monde’s right of residence had ended and demanded he vacate the premises within 30 days ([paras 2–4]).

The appellant contested the eviction order that was subsequently granted by the Worcester Magistrate’s Court and confirmed by the Land Claims Court (LCC). He asserted that his dismissal was unfair, and, significantly, he claimed a right to remain on the property based on prior family connections and occupancy rights established before his formal employment. 

The LCC issued a confirmation of the eviction order, leading Monde to appeal the matter to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 1, 3, 22]). The appeal revolved around whether the respondents had lawfully terminated his right of residence and subsequently evicted him.

### I – Issues

The core legal questions in this appeal included:

1. Did the First and Second Respondents adequately satisfy the legal requirements for terminating the appellant's right of residence under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA), specifically regarding the lawfulness and the just and equitable nature of the termination ([paras 12, 10, 23])?
   
2. Was the eviction of Monde legally justified, particularly given that it was executed without a mandatory report from a probation officer as required by ESTA?

### R – Rules

The key statutory provisions relevant to this case included:

1. **Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA)**: 
   - **Section 8**: Outlines lawful grounds for terminating an occupier's right of residence, specifically requiring that such termination be just and equitable ([para 10]).
   - **Section 9(2)**: States that an eviction of an occupier may proceed only if their right to residence has been properly terminated under Section 8 ([para 23]).
   - **Section 9(3)**: Requires a probation officer's report when eviction occurs, addressing matters such as availability of alternative accommodation and any undue hardships that the eviction may cause ([paras 24–30]).

2. Judicial precedents also played a significant role, particularly the **Snyders & Others v De Jager & Others** case, which highlighted the importance of fair procedure in terminations related to employment under ESTA ([paras 12, 13]).

### A – Application

The Court undertook an analysis of whether the respondents correctly applied the prescribed legal standards from ESTA to the circumstances of Monde:

1. **Termination Justification**: The respondents claimed that Monde’s right of residence terminated automatically upon his dismissal. The Court found, however, that while the dismissal was lawful, the eviction did not exclusively arise from the employment contract. The employment contract clause asserting the exclusivity of residence during employment was contradicted by other evidence indicating that Monde may have established residency rights through family connections. The Court particularly noted that the magistrate had erred in disregarding this familial relationship and the definition of "occupier" under ESTA ([paras 21, 22]).

2. **Procedural Fairness**: The Court assessed whether the eviction order complied with the necessary procedural safeguards outlined in ESTA. Citing prior judgments, the Court emphasized the mandatory nature of obtaining a probation officer’s report and noted that this omission deprived the proceedings of necessary context regarding the impact on Monde and the justness of the eviction. Both the Land Claims Court and Magistrate’s Court had erred in giving insufficient regard to this requirement ([paras 26–31]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that both the termination of Monde’s right of residence and the subsequent eviction were unlawful. The respondents failed to establish that the termination was just and equitable under ESTA, nor did they provide the mandatory probation report necessary to support their eviction claim. Consequently, the appeal was upheld, and the eviction order issued by the Magistrate's Court was set aside. The judgment reinforced the protective nature of ESTA, underscoring that eviction processes must be conducted with due consideration to established rights and protections afforded to occupiers under the act.

The ratio decidendi thus established speaks to the importance of procedural compliance and fairness when engaging in eviction actions involving tenants or occupiers who may have a complex legal relationship with their landlords or employers. In light of the rulings, the case emphasizes a judicial commitment to uphold rights and ensure that evictions occur within the framework of established law and fairness principles ([paras 31]).
