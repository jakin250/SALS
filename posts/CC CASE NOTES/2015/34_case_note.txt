Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: 
**Mighty Solutions CC t/a Orlando Service Station v Engen Petroleum Ltd and Another** [2015] ZACC 34

---

## F - Facts
The applicant, **Mighty Solutions CC** trading as **Orlando Service Station** ("Mighty Solutions"), is a licensed petroleum retailer under the Petroleum Products Act 120 of 1977. The first respondent, **Engen Petroleum Limited** ("Engen"), is a licensed wholesaler of petroleum products. The second respondent is the **Controller of Petroleum Products**, who was cited for potentially having an interest in the matter but did not actively participate in the proceedings ([3]–[4]).

Engen leased property in Soweto, where it created a branded service station and subsequently entered into an operating lease with Mighty Solutions in September 2005. This lease, valid until March 2008 with a one-month cancellation period, continued month-to-month after expiration. However, it was validly terminated in July 2009. Post-termination, Mighty Solutions unlawfully continued to occupy the premises without paying rent ([4]–[5]).

In 2013, Engen sought an order from the **Gauteng Local Division of the High Court, Johannesburg** to evict Mighty Solutions on the grounds that it had no legal right to remain on the property after the lease's termination. Mighty Solutions contended that Engen lacked standing for eviction, relying on section 2A(5)(a) of the Act to argue that it had acquired possessory rights through its retail license, which could only be revoked by the Controller ([8]–[10]).

The High Court ruled in favor of Engen, positing that Mighty Solutions could not challenge Engen’s rights and had no entitlement to continue occupying the premises. Mighty Solutions then appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which dismissed the appeal ([12]–[14]).

## I - Issues
1. **Did Engen have standing to seek the eviction of Mighty Solutions?**
2. **Should the common law rule allowing a lessee to be evicted regardless of the lessor's title be developed in line with constitutional principles?**
3. **Does Mighty Solutions possess an enrichment lien providing it with a right to retain possession of the premises?** ([18]–[19]).

## R - Rules
The central legal principles involved include:
1. The common law principle that a lessee cannot contest the lessor's right to occupy the property when the lease is terminated, as elucidated in **Boompret Investments (Pty) Ltd v Paardekraal Concession Store (Pty) Ltd** 1990 (1) SA 347 (A) ([28]–[30]).
2. Section 39(2) of the South African Constitution, which mandates the promotion of the spirit, purport, and objects of the Bill of Rights when developing common law ([1], [35]).
3. The implications of **Rule 33 of the Uniform Rules of Court**, which disallows arguments beyond agreed statements of facts ([60]).

## A - Application
The Court affirmed that the common law position remains unaltered; as per traditional legal principles, Mighty Solutions, as a sub-lessee, cannot challenge Engen’s right to evict it merely based on the argument that Engen lacks title. Even though Mighty Solutions attempted to substantiate its claims by referencing an alleged enrichment lien, the Court found this argument to be either outside the agreed-upon facts or unsupported by the necessary legal framework ([30], [57]).

The Court further stated that while there may be a case for the common law to evolve in accordance with the Constitution, the specifics of Mighty Solutions' arguments did not compellingly demonstrate a need for such evolution in this instance. The judgment outlines that the established common law rules serve essential roles in ensuring commercial certainty and balance of rights and obligations within contractual relationships ([33]–[52]).

The enrichment claim was deemed inadmissible as it contradicted the stipulated agreement and the principles guiding eviction proceedings. Mighty Solutions failed to prove that it possessed any form of independent title or right that would frustrate Engen's eviction claim ([64]–[66]).

## C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court, by a unanimous decision, concluded that Engen has the standing to evict Mighty Solutions under common law. The Court also affirmed that no constitutional issues were present that necessitated the development of the common law regarding lease agreements in this instance. Mighty Solutions' application for leave to appeal was dismissed based on the lack of prospects for success. As a result, the order included that Mighty Solutions be liable for costs, including the costs of two counsel ([70]).

This case underscores the resilience of established common law principles in property and contractual relations and highlights the challenges faced when attempting to revise deeply entrenched legal doctrines.
