Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Putco (Pty) Ltd v City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality and Others

**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Citation:** Putco (Pty) Ltd v City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality and Others (Case No 252/22) [2023] ZASCA 31 (30 March 2023)  

### F – Facts
Putco (Pty) Ltd (referred to as "Putco") is a public bus service operator in Gauteng, while the City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality (the "City") serves as a planning authority under the National Land Transport Act 5 of 2009 (the "NLTA") ([1]). The City has been working on an Integrated Public Transport Operational Plan, which primarily aims to merge all forms of public transport within Johannesburg, notably through the Rea Vaya Rapid Bus System ([3]). Putco claimed a 26% ownership stake in the bus operating company associated with the Rea Vaya Phase 1B, which was established to facilitate this integration ([4]).

The central controversy arose during negotiations for the implementation of Phase 1C(a) of the Rea Vaya system, which affected Putco's existing services. The negotiations, which began in 2017 through a Negotiation Framework Agreement (the "NFA"), aimed to establish the criteria for determining which operators were considered ‘affected’ by the new bus routes proposed in the Rea Vaya system ([6],[8]). Disagreements regarding these criteria led to a breakdown in negotiations, with Putco asserting that the City was offering inadequate shares ([7]).

On 27 October 2020, Putco initiated legal proceedings in the Gauteng Division of the High Court seeking to compel the City to adhere to the dispute resolution mechanisms outlined in the NFA and, subsequently, sought an interdict to prevent the City from proceeding with its operational plans for Phase 1C(a) ([9],[10]). The high court dismissed the application, concluding that Putco had not established a prima facie right and held that the dispute was governed by section 41 of the NLTA rather than section 46, which pertains to existing contracts ([10]).

### I – Issues
The case presents several legal issues centered primarily on the interpretation of sections 41 and 46 of the NLTA and their applicability to negotiations between Putco and the City:

1. Does the dispute resolution mechanism in section 46(2) of the NLTA apply to ongoing negotiations for contracts under section 41, particularly regarding Putco's claims about Phase 1C(a)?
2. Did the high court err in ruling that section 46 was inapplicable to the negotiations at hand, given that Putco operates under an interim contract defined by the now-repealed National Land Transport Transition Act 22 of 2000 ([2],[10])?

### R – Rules
The legal framework underpinning the case hinges on the provisions of the NLTA, specifically:

- **Section 41:** Governs the establishment of negotiated contracts to integrate public transport services and does not permit disputes arising from these negotiations to be referred to mediation or arbitration. It is focused on enabling municipalities to engage in negotiations for new service contracts rather than existing ones ([19],[20]).
  
- **Section 46:** Addresses existing contracting arrangements and delineates the procedures for amending such contracts when integrating new transport networks. Notably, it includes provisions for mediation or arbitration when there are disagreements on existing contracts ([25],[30]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal in a previous case, *Golden Arrow Bus Services (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town and Others*, clarified the distinctions between sections 41 and 46, establishing that section 46 applies solely to existing contracts and not to those negotiated under section 41 ([10],[19],[20]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal examined Putco's arguments regarding its claim for an interdict based on section 46 of the NLTA. Putco posited that its interim contracts were relevant to section 46, and thus any disputes concerning its negotiations with the City should allow for mediation or arbitration per section 46(2) ([12],[29]).

However, the court affirmatively rejected Putco's interpretations for multiple reasons. Firstly, it contextualized the negotiations as inherently tied to the new contracts under section 41, noting that Putco had not invoked any existing contractual rights during these discussions ([27]). Furthermore, the court highlighted that the explicit goals of section 41 necessitated a streamlined negotiation process that could not be encumbered by potential disputes leading to arbitration or mediation, as lengthy delays would hinder timely implementation ([24],[30]).

The court also addressed Putco's contention regarding the duties imposed by an intergovernmental agreement, affirming that such agreements did not alter the statutory meanings of sections 41 and 46 ([12],[17]) and concluded that Putco's reading was not supported by the factual circumstances nor was it aligned with the intent and design of the NLTA ([28]). 

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the high court's dismissal of Putco's application for an interdict and refused to allow the introduction of new evidence. The court reinforced that the negotiations fell squarely under section 41, thus ruling out the applicability of section 46's provisions regarding mediation and arbitration. This decision underscores the court's strict interpretation of the statutory framework governing public transport contracts in South Africa and emphasizes municipalities' responsibilities in efficiently integrating public transport systems ([22],[30]).

The implications of this ruling are significant for transport operators and municipalities alike, reinforcing adherence to statutory separations between new and existing contractual arrangements, and asserting the immediacy of legislative timelines in public transport integration efforts. The case delineates the boundaries of negotiation authority within the context of the NLTA, establishing a precedent that operational and business arrangements are bound by clear legislative direction, curbing any broad interpretation that may hinder municipal governance and planning responsibilities.
