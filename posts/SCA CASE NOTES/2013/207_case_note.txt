Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Reward Ventures 01 CC v Walker (946/12) [2013] ZASCA 207

## F – Facts
The matter under review stems from a dispute relating to a written sale agreement dated 4 June 2008, where Reward Ventures 01 CC (the appellant) sold the Paquita restaurant in Knysna to Roland Guy Walker and Pietro Luigi Guiseppe Carrara (the respondents) for R2,64 million, purportedly excluding Value Added Tax (VAT) ([1]–[2]). The respondents contended that the purchase price included VAT, asserting that prior drafts and a cancelled agreement supported their view. They alleged that the appellant had altered the agreement to remove the inclusion of VAT, leading them to refuse payment of the claimed VAT portion of R369,000 ([2]).

After failing to resolve their dispute, the appellant initiated arbitration proceedings in January 2009, claiming the VAT and alleging that the respondents owed him a lesser amount based on profits from the restaurant ([3]). The arbitrator resolved the matter in favor of the appellant, awarding him the claimed amount without providing reasons or explicitly addressing the respondents' counterclaims, which led to a complaint from the respondents regarding the incompleteness of the arbitrator's decision ([4]).

The respondents subsequently launched review proceedings under sections 33(1)(a) and (b) of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965, challenging the arbitrator's conduct and the finality of the award. Tsoka J ruled against them, affirming the arbitrator's discretion not to provide reasons ([5]). However, the full court later overturned this ruling, leading to the present appeal ([6]).

## I – Issues
The key legal questions considered by the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

1. Whether the full court exceeded its jurisdiction in its findings regarding the arbitrator's award and counterclaims ([7]).
2. Whether the arbitrator's decision was final, particularly regarding the status of the respondents' counterclaims ([7]).
3. If the award was not final, whether the arbitrator was guilty of gross misconduct in the arbitration process ([7]).

## R – Rules
The rules relevant to this case center around the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965, specifically:

- **Section 28**: States that for an arbitration award to bind the parties, it must be final unless the arbitration agreement specifies otherwise.
- **Section 33(1)**: Provides grounds for a court to review an arbitration award if there has been a gross irregularity or misconduct in the arbitrator's conduct or judgment ([9]).

Moreover, the arbitration agreement between the parties included a provision that allowed the arbitrator to consider matters just and equitable without strict adherence to procedural formalities ([10]).

## A – Application
The court analyzed the proceedings to determine whether the arbitrator had committed an error, particularly regarding the perceived dismissal of the respondents' counterclaims and the requirement to provide reasons ([12]). 

Maya JA scrutinized the arbitrator's actions and found that he had, indeed, recognized the respondents' counterclaims in the award by referencing them before issuing a ruling. The absence of expressly dismissing these claims did not equate to them being ignored; instead, it suggested that the arbitrator had assessed them and found them lacking ([12]). The court further held that the respondents had not sufficiently established that the lack of reasons constituted gross misconduct on the part of the arbitrator ([13]).

The court noted that while parties expressed dissatisfaction with the merits of the award, proper principles of review concerning finality and the conduct of arbitration needed to be addressed. Since the award's finality is mandated by the arbitration agreement and the necessary regulations, the court determined that the arbitrator had executed his task adequately and within the scope of his authority according to section 28 of the Arbitration Act ([9]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the full court had erred in its assessment of the arbitrator's conduct, particularly in determining whether the award was final or addressed the counterclaims ([14]). The court upheld the appeal, concluding that the arbitrator did not commit gross misconduct nor fail to fulfill his duties, and thus, the award should stand. The court’s final ruling included the award of costs to the appellant, reinforcing the significance of ensuring that arbitration awards are treated with due deference unless a clear misconduct is proven ([14]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the judiciary's role in maintaining the integrity of the arbitration process and reinforcing the binding nature of final arbitration awards, shaping how parties approach disputes and their reliance on arbitration agreements in future transactional contexts.
