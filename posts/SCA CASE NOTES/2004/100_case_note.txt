# Case Note: Foodcorp (Pty) Ltd v Deputy Director General Environmental Affairs and Tourism

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The matter relates to the allocation of commercial fishing rights for pelagic fish (specifically pilchard and Cape anchovy) during the 2002-2005 fishing seasons under the Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998 (hereinafter ‘the Act’) ([1]). The Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism (the second respondent), or a delegate (the first respondent), is responsible for determining the total allowable catch (TAC), which is then allocated among commercial fishers based on a points system that prioritizes various objectives of the Act ([1]).

The allocation formula used for this purpose was contested by the appellant, Foodcorp (Pty) Ltd, which claimed that the formula infringed its rights to just administrative action under Section 33 of the Bill of Rights and the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([1]). The lower court (Van Zyl J) dismissed the initial application but granted leave to appeal, determining that judicial deference should be exercised toward policy-heavy administrative acts ([2]). The Act aims for sustainable utilisation of marine resources and equitable access, with various principles outlined in section 2 of the Act ([3]–[4]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue surrounds whether the administrative actions taken by the Minister and the Department concerning the allocation of fishing rights were so unreasonable that they breached the standards set by PAJA, particularly Section 6(2)(h), which addresses actions deemed inconsistent with the law ([11]). The case also involved examining the transition from separate quotas for pilchard and anchovy to a collective quota and whether the formula applied was a reasonable administrative action ([12]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework consists of the Marine Living Resources Act, particularly sections regarding the determination of TAC and allocation of rights (sections 14 and 21), and PAJA, which codifies grounds for judicial review of administrative actions ([1], [11]). The implication of the constitutional right to just administrative action means that administrative decisions must be both reasonable and rational, and judicial deference toward administrative expertise must be balanced with the expectation of accountability ([12]).

Relevant jurisprudence that guides the court's analysis includes cases such as *Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Environmental Affairs* and *Computer Investors Group Inc v Minister of Finance*, where the courts stressed the importance of not fixing administrative discretion and ensuring decisions are made on the merits of each case ([10], [12]).

### A – Application
The allocation results produced by the application of the contested formula raised substantial anomalies. Foodcorp's share was reduced significantly, while competitors Lamberts Bay and SASP received disproportionate increases in their allocations ([15], [19]). The discrepancies highlighted that the formula might not account for specific circumstances, such as historical performance and capacity, which contributed to a conclusion that the administrative action was irrational and unjustifiable.

The court assessed that a reasonable decision-maker would have sought to ensure allocations resulting from the formula were justifiable and reflective of the actual circumstances, as the raw data fed into the formula appeared skewed and led to irrational outcomes ([19]). The lack of clarification or reasonable explanations from the respondents regarding these discrepancies further justified the review ([18]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, setting aside the previous decision and ordering a fresh determination of the pelagic TAC allocations for the 2005 season ([21]). The court underscored the importance of applying rational, transparent criteria in administrative decision-making, especially concerning substantial economic impacts on industry players. This case reinforces the need for regulatory bodies to be held accountable for adherence to the principles of administrative justice and the implications of their decisions on affected parties ([21]). 

The ruling serves as a pivotal reminder that excessive reliance on formulaic approaches in administrative allocations can undermine the intent of legislation aimed at ensuring equitable distribution and sustainable resource management. The decision invites future scrutiny of similar practices to ensure they align with established legal standards.
