Case Note: THE CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# Case Note: Annette Brink v Andre Kitshoff NO [1996] ZACC 3 (CCT 15/95)

## F - Facts

The present case arises from a referral concerning the constitutionality of section 44 of the Insurance Act, 1944. The applicant, Annette Brink, was involved in litigation following her husband, Mr. Brink's, death on 9 April 1994 ([paras 19–24]). Prior to his death, Mr. Brink had taken out a life insurance policy valued at approximately R2 million in 1989, which he ceded to his wife in 1990. However, a claim against the insurance proceeds arose when Mr. A Kitshoff, appointed as executior of Mr. Brink's estate, issued a notice to creditors regarding the estate's insolvency.

Under section 44 of the Insurance Act, the executor demanded that the insurer pay the proceeds into the estate, arguing that the policy's cession to Mrs. Brink was subject to legislative provisions that limit her rights. In response, Mrs. Brink initiated a counter-application seeking rectification of the insurance policy and raised a constitutional challenge against section 44 on the grounds of discrimination against married women.

The Transvaal Provincial Division granted a consent order referring the constitutional question to the Constitutional Court, with the parties acknowledging the importance and potential decisiveness of the issue ([paras 25–26]).

## I - Issues

The pivotal issues in this case are:
1. Whether sections 44(1) and (2) of the Insurance Act constitute a violation of the right to equality as entrenched in section 8 of the Constitution ([para 19]).
2. Whether the referral of the constitutional question met the prerequisites of section 102(1) of the Constitution, namely that the issue is within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court, may be decisive in the outcome of the case, and is in the interest of justice to be referred ([paras 30–31]).

## R - Rules

The legal framework pertinent to this case primarily derives from:
- **Section 8 of the 1993 Constitution**, which guarantees the right to equality and protection from unfair discrimination on various grounds, including gender.
- **Sections 44 of the Insurance Act**, which differentiates the treatment of married men and women regarding the proceeds of life insurance policies, particularly in the context of insolvency.
- **Section 102(1)** of the Constitution, which governs the referral process for constitutional issues to the Constitutional Court, stipulating that a provincial or local division must consider the significance of the constitutional question and whether it may be decisive for the case ([paras 30–34]).

Notable precedents cited include *S v Mhlungu* and *S v Zuma*, which elucidate the parameters around referrals of constitutional matters and emphasize the necessity of a reasonable prospect of success for a matter to warrant referral ([paras 29–31]).

## A - Application

The Court assessed whether the provisions of sections 44(1) and (2) of the Insurance Act discriminate against married women and violate their right to equality under section 8. The Court noted that the respondent conceded that the sections do indeed breach the constitutional provision ([para 32]).

In applying the rules, the Court emphasized that the provisions treat married women unfavorably compared to their male counterparts, effectively limiting their rights as beneficiaries of insurance policies while safeguarding the interests of creditors ([paras 43–49]). The Court established that the historical context of these provisions, which purportedly aimed to benefit married women, was rendered irrelevant following the abolition of laws prohibiting gifts between spouses in the mid-1980s, thus implying the provisions were now unjustly detrimental ([para 47]).

The question of whether the referral met the requirements of section 102(1) was also critically analyzed. The Court determined that key factual clarification regarding the vesting of rights under the insurance policy needed to be settled by the provincial division before referral could occur, a step missing in this case, leading to the referral being deemed improper ([paras 14–16, 30–31]).

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled that sections 44(1) and (2) of the Insurance Act were unconstitutional and thus invalid, as they discriminated against married women in violation of section 8 of the Constitution. The Court highlighted the absence of any reasonable justification for the discriminatory treatment and noted that the provisions could not be deemed necessary for protecting creditors, given existing legislative safeguards ([paras 50–58]).

The ruling declared the provisions invalid effective from 27 April 1994, but exempted any transactions completed before this date from invalidation. This determination was aimed at balancing justice and good governance, considering that many estates may have been settled under these provisions ([paras 60–61]).

This case contributes significantly to the jurisprudence on equality rights in South Africa, affirming the constitutional injunction against discrimination and clarifying the procedural necessity for properly substantiating the basis of constitutional referrals.
