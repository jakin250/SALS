Case Note: Nthabiseng Pheko v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality [2011] ZACC 34 (CCT 19/11)

Case Note: Nthabiseng Pheko v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality [2011] ZACC 34 (CCT 19/11)

F – Facts

The case arises from the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality's (the Municipality) decision to declare the Bapsfontein Informal Settlement a local disaster area due to concerns regarding dolomite instability ([1]–[10]). Following assessments by civil engineers, the Municipality concluded that substantial risks to life and health warranted the evacuation of residents. By December 2010, the Municipality enacted measures to forcibly relocate residents, citing the Disaster Management Act (DMA) as the authority for this action.

The applicants, led by Nthabiseng Pheko, were mere occupants of the Bapsfontein area, challenging the legality of the eviction and demolition of their homes without a court order. They argued that this action violated their constitutional rights to housing and dignity ([16]–[20]), as guaranteed by sections 26 and 10 of the South African Constitution. The North Gauteng High Court dismissed their application for urgent interdictory relief, justifying the Municipality’s actions based on the declared emergency ([12]–[15]).

Following the dismissal, the applicants sought leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court, seeking to overturn the High Court's ruling and address broader issues regarding unlawful eviction without due process and the legality of the DMA's application in this scenario ([16]–[33]).

I – Issues

The main legal questions arising in this case include:

  1. Whether the court should grant leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court ([19]–[28]).
  2. Whether the removal of the applicants from their homes constituted permissible evacuation under section 55 of the DMA, as opposed to unlawful eviction pursuant to section 26(3) of the Constitution ([24]–[34]).
  3. The adequacy of the Municipality's engagement and consultation with residents surrounding the eviction ([16]–[18]).

R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case includes:

  • Constitution of South Africa: Sections 26 (right to adequate housing) and 10 (right to dignity) ([34]–[36]).
  • Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002: Section 55(2)(d) permits evacuation during local disasters for life preservation ([36]–[38]).
  • Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE): Establishes requirements for lawful eviction, including court orders and consideration of relevant circumstances.

The court referenced several precedents, including the substantial interpretation of constitutional rights regarding eviction and the responsibilities of municipalities to provide adequate housing ([45]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court applied a strict interpretation of both the DMA and the Constitution to assess the legality of the Municipality's actions. It emphasized that section 55(2)(d) of the DMA was intended solely for emergency evacuations to save lives and did not encompass the power for municipalities to undertake demolitions or evictions without judicial oversight ([36]–[40]).

The Court found that the serious risks posed by the dolomite instability, while concerning, did not meet the exigent standards for emergency action as outlined in the DMA, especially given the prior knowledge of risks and lack of immediate danger demonstrated ([41]–[43]).

The Court noted an absence of imminent threat to life and the Municipality's failure to pursue timely action after past reports concerning the area, thus invalidating claims of urgency that would justify bypassing judicial approval ([44]). Ultimately, the Municipality’s actions were deemed unlawful both in terms of the DMA's provisions and the constitutional protections against arbitrary eviction ([45]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, overturning the High Court's ruling and declaring the Municipality's actions as unlawful ([52]–[53]). It mandated that the Municipality provide adequate alternative accommodation, engage with the displaced residents, and report back to the Court on compliance within specified timelines. This ruling underscored the importance of adhering to constitutional rights and lawful processes in the context of evictions and disaster management, reinforcing the rule of law and protecting the vulnerable against unlawful dispossession ([53]).

The judgment represents a significant contribution to the development of constitutional jurisprudence in South Africa, affirming the rights to housing and dignity while clarifying the appropriate applications of emergency legislation in relation to established constitutional protections. This case holds far-reaching implications for how municipalities manage disaster responses, particularly regarding informal settlements and the engagement of residents during such processes.
