10 Case Note

Case Title: South African Commercial Catering and Allied Workers Union and Others v Irvin & Johnson Limited Seafoods Division Fish Processing Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa Case No: CCT 2/00 Heard on: 18 May 2000 Decided on: 09 June 2000 Judgment by: Cameron AJ

Constitutional Court 2000 CCT 2/00

Case Title: South African Commercial Catering and Allied Workers Union and Others v Irvin & Johnson Limited Seafoods Division Fish Processing

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case No: CCT 2/00
Heard on: 18 May 2000
Decided on: 09 June 2000
Judgment by: Cameron AJ

F – Facts

[1] The origins of this matter lie in an appeal by the applicants—a trade union and several dismissed workers—against the Labour Appeal Court’s refusal to grant recusal of two judges. This refusal was based on the applicants' apprehension of bias arising from a previous case involving similar facts ([1]–[2]).

[2] The dismissed workers were terminated from their employment on 2 August 1995 following their participation in a march at the workplace, initiated as a protest against their dismissals ([8]). The dismissals were linked to an ongoing rivalry between the union and another workers' union, culminating in a prolonged dispute involving multiple dismissals after a series of protests ([9]).

[3] The judicial procedure involved several key phases, with earlier dismissals occurring in a case identified as “Nomoyi,” where the Labour Appeal Court upheld some of the dismissals by a significant ruling on 24 May 1999 ([10]). The present appeal followed subsequent unfavorable outcomes for the applicants regarding their recusal application ([11]).

[4] The core argument for recusal hinged on the premise that the judges had previously delivered opinions on related circumstances during the Nomoyi appeal. Ms. Holland, representing the applicants, argued that this created a reasonable apprehension of bias due to overlapping issues and witness credibility ([20]).

I – Issues

[5] The critical legal question before the Court was whether the previous statements and findings made by the Labour Appeal Court regarding the June 1995 events constituted valid grounds for apprehending bias that warranted the recusal of the judges in the current proceedings ([18]).

[6] A secondary issue concerned whether an applicant for recusal has an automatic right to appeal against a refusal to recuse judges, or if such requests are subject to judicial discretion given the procedural contexts of appellate courts ([5]).

R – Rules

[7] The legal framework for assessing recusal applications is grounded in principles established within South African jurisprudence, notably articulated in President of the Republic of South Africa and Others v South African Rugby Football Union ([3]). A judge must recuse themselves if a reasonable, informed person might apprehend that they would not bring an impartial mind to adjudicate the case ([12]–[14]).

[8] The applicants are burdened with the responsibility to provide convincing evidence to rebut the presumption of judicial impartiality ([13]–[14]). The tests for bias or apprehended bias are particularly stringent, emphasizing that friendly interpretations must not undermine judicial integrity ([15]).

A – Application

[9] The Court critically assessed the details surrounding the applicants' arguments, concluding that their apprehension of bias lacked the necessary cogency. The Labour Appeal Court had provided a nuanced distinction between the events assessed in Nomoyi and those in Nkatu, indicating that they regarded the issues as fundamentally different, even though the context may exhibit certain commonalities ([22], [29]).

[10] Furthermore, the Court noted that the judicial officers' statements in Nomoyi regarding the conduct of the union were made under unchallenged factual conditions, negating the likelihood of inherent bias in subsequent rulings. The judges were not perceived to have made any pre-emptive judgments regarding the credibility of witnesses significant to the Nkatu appeal ([34], [37]), thereby maintaining a necessary element of judicial impartiality.

[11] The Court applied the principles of appellate review to emphasize that the judiciary, particularly in appeals, operates under a presumption of impartiality, strengthened by the procedural nature that limits personal bias due to collegial decision-making ([38]–[44]).

C – Conclusion

[12] Ultimately, the Constitutional Court granted the applicants leave to appeal against the Labour Appeal Court’s refusal but dismissed the substantive recusal appeal. The Court determined that the applicants failed to meet the high threshold needed to demonstrate a reasonable apprehension of judicial bias sufficient to compel recusal ([51]).

[13] The dismissal of the appeal, despite granting leave, underscores the critical importance of maintaining judicial integrity and public confidence in judicial processes, particularly in labor relations contexts during a period marked by intense organizational disputes. The decision highlighted the delicate balance between protecting judicial impartiality and addressing legitimate concerns of bias within the ambit of labor law ([53]).

[14] Therefore, while the applicants' concerns were perceived as understandable, they did not possess the requisite foundation to disrupt the proceedings, resulting in no order as to costs against the unsuccessful applicants, acknowledging the nature of the constitutional issues engaged in the litigation ([54]).
