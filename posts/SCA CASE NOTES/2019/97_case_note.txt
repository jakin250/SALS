# FIRAC Case Note: P M obo T M v Road Accident Fund (1175/2017) [2019] ZASCA 97

## F – Facts

This case revolves around a settlement agreement reached on the first day of a trial regarding a damages claim against the Road Accident Fund (RAF). The appellant, Mrs. M, brought forth the case on behalf of her minor child, claiming damages of R1 million after the child's father was killed in a car accident attributed to the insured driver's negligence ([2]). The RAF defended the claim, which included past and future loss of support ([2]).

On September 14, 2016, upon the commencement of the trial, the parties reached a settlement whereby the RAF agreed to pay R561,314.63 to the appellant ([1]–[2]). However, the trial judge, Mokgohloa DJP, refused to make the settlement an order of court, citing concerns about the apparent lack of negligence based on witness statements and pleadings ([3]–[5]). The judge required the trial to continue, wanting evidence regarding the accident's circumstances to be heard ([5]).

Subsequently, the appellant sought to halt the trial and declare it a nullity by arguing that the lis between the parties had been resolved through the settlement agreement ([6]–[8]). Despite the RAF not opposing the application, both the high court and the full court dismissed her appeal, concluding that the trial had not been completed, thus leaving the judge's initial decision unchallenged ([8]).

The case reached the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) for a final decision ([1]). 

## I – Issues

The case primarily raised two legal questions:

1. **Permissibility of Challenge**: Was it permissible for the appellant to challenge the judge's refusal to make the settlement agreement an order of court through an interlocutory application?
2. **Judge's Approach**: Was the judge's method of handling the settlement agreement permissible, considering that the parties had reached an agreement and the understanding that the trial need not continue? ([9]–[10]).

These issues invite an examination of judicial discretion, procedural regularity, and the inherent jurisdiction of the court, especially in cases involving settlement agreements and damages claims against state entities like the RAF.

## R – Rules

The SCA considered several applicable legal principles and precedents:

1. **Judicial Authority**: The court's authority to adjudicate cases is defined by the litigation's issues (the lis) as presented by the parties. Once a settlement agreement is reached, the court's jurisdiction can be deemed extinguished only if the parties effectively withdraw the case ([12]–[16]).

2. **Settlement Approvals**: In PL v YL, it was established that a court's mandate can remain intact even when a settlement is reached. The court retains the authority to make such settlements orders, as demonstrated in Eke v Parsons and supported by Fagan v Business Partners, which highlight the need for court scrutiny in divorce and damages settlements ([11], [27], [34]–[60]).

3. **Public Funds and Court Scrutiny**: The RAF, as an organ of the state, has a duty to adhere to constitutional and ethical standards in resource use. Hence, judicial scrutiny is necessary to ensure fair disbursement of public funds ([34]).

4. **Jurisdiction of Courts**: The SCA relied on established authority indicating that decisions of high courts are not subject to review while trials are ongoing ([17]–[20]).

## A – Application 

In analyzing the application, the SCA found that:

1. **Challenge to Judicial Decision**: The court rejected the appellant's argument that her interlocutory application was appropriate. The trial had not concluded, as the judge had not rendered a final judgment or dismissed the case. Given that the proceedings were still active, appealing the decision through the mechanism selected (an interlocutory application) was inappropriate ([21]–[23]).

2. **Judge's Determination on the Settlement**: The SCA disagreed with the notion that the trial judge's refusal to make the settlement an order constituted an overreach of jurisdiction. The judge took proper account of the evidence and witness statements indicated that there might not have been negligence on the part of the insured driver. Therefore, the refusal was made in good faith, based on the sufficiency of the evidence and the need for judicial oversight ([22]–[36]). 

Furthermore, the court acknowledged that concerns regarding the settlement's compatibility with public policy and the safeguarding of the minor child provided sufficient grounds for the judge to exercise discretion to refuse making it an order of court ([34]–[37]).

## C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the SCA dismissed the appeal, affirming the full court's original decision that the application was incompetent, and criticizing the appellant for incorrectly framing her application ([40], [62]). The judgment underscored the trial court's responsibility to scrutinize settlements, particularly where public funds are involved, hence asserting that judicial oversight is vital in such circumstances.

This case reinforces the principle that judicial discretion is fundamental in determining whether a settlement agreement should receive the court’s endorsement as an order, particularly in the context of public policy and the protection of minors. The implications of this ruling extend to future cases involving the RAF and similar public entities, highlighting the necessity of comprehensive judicial oversight in the settlement process to prevent potential abuse or irregularities in settlements made under equations of state accountability.
