Constitutional Court 2004 CCT 63/03

FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Finance v Van Heerden (CCT 63/03)

F - Facts:
The case involves a constitutional challenge to the rules of the Political Office-Bearers Pension Fund (the Fund), raised by Fredrik Jacobus van Heerden, a former member of the National Party who served in Parliament both pre- and post-1994. His challenge is twofold: firstly, that certain provisions of the Fund are discriminatory under the equality clause of the Constitution of South Africa; and secondly, that the Fund itself was improperly established as it did not comply with provisions of the interim Constitution ([1]–[3]).

Van Heerden served as a member from 1987 to 1994 and was then re-elected in 1994 until 1999. He, along with 145 others, argued that rules governing pension contributions discriminated unfairly against members, especially those classified as Category C, who received lower employer contributions compared to new Members of Parliament and those in Category A and B. This differentiation, he claimed, was arbitrary and violated equality rights under section 9 of the Constitution ([2], [11]).

The High Court found in favor of Van Heerden, declaring the differential contribution scheme unconstitutional. The Minister of Finance and the Fund appealed this decision, contesting both the findings of discrimination and the assertion that the Fund was improperly established ([3], [12]).

I - Issues:
The main legal issues at play include:

  1. Whether the pension contribution rules of the Fund constitute unfair discrimination against the old members of Parliament under section 9 of the Constitution.
  2. If these rules are indeed invalid, does this invalidation render the entire Fund a nullity due to improper establishment under constitutional provisions, specifically sections 190A and 219 of the interim and final Constitutions ([2]–[3]).

R - Rules:
The applicable legal framework rests primarily on:

  • Section 9 of the Constitution of South Africa – stipulating the right to equality before the law and prohibitions against unfair discrimination, which encompasses:
  • Subsection 1: Right to equality and equal protection.
  • Subsection 2: Legislative measures to promote equality for disadvantaged groups.
  • Subsections 3 and 5: Prohibiting discrimination based on specified grounds unless justified as fair ([22]).

The Court also considered precedents from previous cases which established criteria for determining unfair discrimination such as the necessary rational connection to a legitimate governmental purpose and the context of differentiation ([110]).

A - Application:
The Court examined the differentiated contributions to determine whether they constituted unfair discrimination. The High Court held that the differentiation was not merely categorization but resulted in significant disparities in pension benefits that unfairly harmed the members in Category C. Additionally, the High Court asserted that the burden of proof rested on the state to justify such discrimination under section 9(2), which focuses on affirmative measures, and that it had failed to prove that the disparity was rationally connected to the aims of equality ([12]–[14]).

The Supreme Court's majority judgment, authored by Moseneke J, shifted the focus, arguing that measures meant to promote members who had been historically disadvantaged should not automatically attract a presumption of unfairness under section 9(2) ([32]–[35]). The Court considered the long-term objectives of equality and observed that a finite and transitional five-year measure was justified given the historical context of exclusion under apartheid legislation.

The court concluded that the differentiation did meet the criteria under section 9(2); it targeted previously disadvantaged groups, aimed to advance them, and promoted overall equality within Parliament ([49]–[57]).

C - Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court reversed the High Court's decision, holding that the differentiation in pension contributions was constitutional and served a legitimate purpose of redressing historical inequalities. The provisions of rule 4.2.1 of the Fund were not unconstitutional, and the appeal was upheld. The case reaffirmed the Court's commitment to substantive equality, distinguishing between necessary affirmative measures and outright unfair discrimination, thus framing a nuanced understanding of equality within the South African constitutional context ([57]–[66]).
