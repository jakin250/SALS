Constitutional Court 2003

FIRAC Case Note on Swartbooi v Brink and Others

F – Facts:

The appellants, Gabriel Ntleli Swartbooi and seventeen others, are elected councillors of the Nala Local Municipality (the council). In April 2001, they participated in and voted in favor of two controversial decisions affecting the rights of the first and second respondents, Lilian Ray Brink and Gerrit Niewoudt, also members of the council. The respondents subsequently applied to the Free State High Court for orders to set aside these decisions and for a special costs order against the appellants.

The High Court granted the application, setting aside the decisions and concluding that the appellants should personally bear the costs of the proceedings on the scale as between attorney and client. A rule nisi was issued against them, requiring them to explain why they should not be held personally liable for costs de bonis propriis. The appellants were unpersuasive in their defense, resulting in the High Court mandating that they pay costs personally ([1]).

After being refused leave to appeal by the Supreme Court of Appeal, the appellants sought to appeal the costs order to the Constitutional Court. The Council was joined as a party to the appeal, and several other governmental entities submitted arguments. The crux of the appeal rested on the legal basis for the High Court's costs order ([2]).

I – Issues:

The primary legal questions that arose during the proceedings were framed as follows:

  1. Whether it falls within the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court to determine the circumstances under which elected municipal councillors may be ordered to pay costs of court proceedings personally (de bonis propriis).
  2. If so, what are the appropriate circumstances for such an order, and in the specific case, whether the appellants ought to have been ordered by the High Court to bear costs personally ([3]).

R – Rules:

The key legal provisions referenced include:

  • Section 161 of the Constitution of South Africa, which facilitates the enactment of provincial legislation providing for the privileges and immunities of municipal councils and their members ([8]).
  • Section 28 of the Local Government: Municipal Structures Act No 117 of 1998, which outlines that councillors are entitled to immunity from civil and criminal liability for actions undertaken in the course of their duties within the council ([9]).

The rules governing the award of costs, particularly as they relate to actions undertaken in a representative capacity and under severe circumstances (such as malice or improper conduct) were also referenced, though the respondents could not identify case law demonstrating municipal councillor liability under such standards ([7]).

A – Application:

The Constitutional Court meticulously analyzed whether the decision of the High Court complied with the protections afforded under Section 28. The Court noted that the High Court's reliance on common law principles rather than the statutory provisions led to a misapplication of the law ([23]). It held that conduct associated with participating in council decisions should have warranted the protections outlined in Section 28.

Yacoob J asserted that the High Court unjustifiably attributed personal liability to the councillors based on the decisions and their implications, rather than recognizing the outlined constitutional protections which extended to the council's deliberative contexts ([12], [18]). The Court emphasized the necessity for robust debate in legislative functions, asserting that the protective scope of Section 28 ensured councillors could engage freely in council matters without fearing personal liability from resultant controversies ([20]).

The Court also addressed the improper considerations of the High Court, including the notion that imposing costs would foster better decision-making among councillors in the future. This rationale was viewed as punitive and encroaching upon the separation of powers, as the judiciary should not punish legislative conduct in order to influence future behaviors ([25]).

C – Conclusion:

The Constitutional Court ultimately concluded that the High Court had erred in its application of the law concerning costs liability. The appeal was allowed, nullifying the order for costs against the appellants and mandating that the Nala Local Municipality, rather than the individual councillors, be held liable for litigation costs ([29]).

The ruling underscored the function of Section 28 as a vital component of local governance in South Africa. It affirmed the importance of safeguarding councillors from personal liability for actions taken during the execution of their responsibilities, thereby facilitating a more dynamic and engaged local government structure that is foundational for democracy ([26], [28]). This decision not only corrects the judicial misdirection of the High Court but also serves as a reaffirmation of legislative immunity principles critical to the effective functioning of local governance.
