Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 162

F – Facts

The case centers around the appellant, S M, who was convicted of murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances in the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, Pietermaritzburg ([1]). The criminal activities in question were perpetrated on 20 February 2002, when the appellant, alongside an accomplice, robbed and killed an occupant of a farmhouse, stealing money, firearms, and other valuables from the victim while he was asleep ([2]).

At the time of these offenses, the appellant was a minor. On 25 July 2003, he was sentenced to life imprisonment for murder and fifteen years for robbery ([1], [4]). Subsequently, on 14 February 2013, a Full Court of the High Court reduced the murder sentence to twenty-five years and the robbery sentence to fifteen years, allowing ten years of the robbery sentence to run concurrently with the murder sentence ([4]). This resulted in an effective term of thirty years ([4]).

The Full Court also fixed a non-parole period of twenty years under section 276B(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ("the Act") ([5]). The appellant subsequently sought leave to appeal the imposition of this non-parole period, which was granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal on 27 January 2016 ([6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case concerns whether the court had the authority to impose a non-parole period under section 276B of the Criminal Procedure Act, given that the offenses occurred and the appellant was sentenced before this section came into operation on 1 October 2004 ([7],[9]). Specifically, the question is whether the imposition of a non-parole period constituted a misdirection by the court below.

R – Rules

The legal principle at stake involves the interpretation of section 276B of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. This section allows courts to stipulate a non-parole period when sentencing individuals convicted of offenses, applicable only to crimes committed after its commencement ([7]). It is established that statutes are presumed to apply prospectively unless there is clear legislative intent to apply them retroactively ([8]). In prior cases such as S v Mpetha and R v Silas, the courts have held similar views regarding the non-retroactive application of statutory provisions ([8]).

Additionally, at the time of the appellant’s sentencing, parole decisions were governed by the Correctional Services Act 8 of 1959, which delineates the criteria under which a prisoner may earn credits toward potential parole ([10]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal examined the circumstances surrounding the imposition of the non-parole period. By emphasizing that the appellant's crime occurred prior to the operation of section 276B, the court noted that the Full Court’s ruling did not consider the legislative intent concerning retroactivity ([9]). The judgment thus reasoned that the imposition of a non-parole period was not permissible under the law at that time ([9],[12]).

The court also recognized that, as the appellant had already served nearly fifteen years of his sentence, reinstating the matter for a decision regarding the non-parole period would unduly prejudice him. Given that he was eligible for parole based on the time served and potential credits under the Correctional Services Act, the court ruled that it would be inappropriate to subject him to a further delay and uncertainty regarding his parole ([10],[11]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, finding that the imposition of a non-parole period constituted a misdirection by the Full Court. The court decisively ruled to set aside the non-parole order, thereby allowing the appellant to be considered for parole based on established statutory provisions applicable at the time of his original sentencing ([12]). This judgment reinforces the principle that legislative provisions regarding sentencing must not be applied retroactively, safeguarding individual rights and ensuring fairness in the judicial process.

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the specific case, clarifying the operational boundaries of statutory provisions in relation to timelines of criminal conduct and sentencing, and they reinforce the notion of fairness and justice within the penal system.
