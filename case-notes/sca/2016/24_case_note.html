South African Municipal Workers’ Union v Mokgatla (20810/2014) [2016] ZASCA 24

In April 2014, amidst these allegations, a Central Executive Committee meeting was convened; however, no resolution to remove any members was passed.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 24

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) case revolves around a dispute involving the South African Municipal Workers’ Union (SAMWU) and its members, specifically the suspension and expulsion of certain members from the union. The appellants, who were national office bearers of SAMWU, were accused by the respondents, the provincial office bearers, of financial mismanagement and corruption. The allegations arose after the finance committee failed to provide transparent financial reports for 2013, triggering a loss of confidence among union members.

In April 2014, amidst these allegations, a Central Executive Committee meeting was convened; however, no resolution to remove any members was passed. Subsequently, the appellants suspended and expelled several respondents, citing their propagation of "malicious information" and disruption of union affairs ([1]–[2]).

The respondents challenged this decision in the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court, claiming that the SAMWU constitution's disciplinary procedures were violated in the process of their removal ([3]). The High Court rejected a plea by the appellants asserting a lack of jurisdiction, determining that they had jurisdiction over the application ([4]). The appellants then appealed the High Court's decision ([1]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue for determination in this case was whether the High Court and the Labour Court have concurrent jurisdiction concerning disputes arising from section 158(1)(e) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) ([1]). The focus was on assessing the applicable jurisdiction in light of the procedural adherence to the disciplinary procedures delineated in SAMWU's constitution ([3], [4]).

Additionally, the court had to consider whether the representatives of SAMWU could bypass the Labour Court, citing common law rights as the basis of the dispute, as the respondents contended ([4]).

R – Rules

The SCA relied on specific provisions in the Labour Relations Act, particularly sections 157 and 158, which delineate the jurisdictions of the Labour Court and High Court. Section 157(1) grants exclusive jurisdiction to the Labour Court over matters arising under the LRA, while section 157(2) provides for concurrent jurisdiction regarding fundamental rights arising from employment relations ([9]).

Section 158 further empowers the Labour Court to remedy disputes arising from violations of the constitution of trade unions ([10]). The court also considered the decisions from previous cases such as Macun, which emphasized the necessity for a narrow interpretation of section 157(2) in light of the LRA's objectives to maintain a cohesive framework for labor disputes ([12], [14]).

A – Application

In its reasoning, the SCA highlighted the importance of the pleadings in determining jurisdiction. Although the respondents had initially disavowed reliance on the LRA, the court concluded that their claims were inherently linked to the enforcement of union constitution provisions, which fell under section 158(1)(e)(i) of the LRA ([15]).

The court found that the High Court's determination, which allowed a parallel appeal under common law, contradicted the legislative framework provided by the LRA. The SCA emphasized that allowing litigants to choose their forum undermined the specialized dispute resolution established under labor law. In this regard, it noted that the Constitutional Court had previously underscored the need for specificity and specialization in addressing labor-related disputes, affirming that jurisdiction should remain with the Labour Court when matters pertain directly to the LRA ([13], [14]).

The SCA ruled that the lack of adherence to the established disciplinary procedures constituted a matter of exclusive jurisdiction for the Labour Court under section 158 ([14], [15]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the SCA found in favor of the appellants, overturning the decision of the High Court. The appeal was allowed, the High Court's order was set aside, and the application of the respondents was dismissed with costs. This decision solidified the jurisdictional authority of the Labour Court in disputes relating to the enforcement of labor laws, thereby reinforcing the LRA’s intended framework for adjudicating employment matters ([17]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the necessity for union members and office bearers to adhere to constitutional and procedural mandates within their governing documents, while also clarifying the boundaries of jurisdiction between the Labour Court and the High Court in labor-related disputes.
