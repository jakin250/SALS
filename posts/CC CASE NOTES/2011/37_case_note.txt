# Case Note: FIRAC Method for *Minister of Safety and Security v F* [2011] ZACC 37

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–17])
The case at hand involves Ms F, who, at the age of 13, was brutally raped by Mr Allister Claude van Wyk, a policeman on standby duty ([para 1]). On 14 October 1998, Ms F accepted a lift home from Mr van Wyk after leaving a nightclub. Despite her initial discomfort due to his behavior, she trusted him, thinking he was a police officer ([paras 8–10]). After driving away from her home ostensibly for a detour, Mr van Wyk stopped at a secluded area where he assaulted and raped her, threatening harm if she reported the incident ([paras 11–14]).

Following the attack, Ms F reported the crime, leading to Mr van Wyk's conviction for assault and rape, resulting in a 12-year prison sentence ([para 15]). Upon reaching adulthood in December 2005, Ms F initiated a civil suit against the Minister of Safety and Security and Mr van Wyk for damages, asserting the Minister's vicarious liability for Mr van Wyk's conduct while on duty ([para 16]). The High Court found for Ms F, holding the Minister vicariously liable under the test established in *K v Minister of Safety and Security* ([para 18]). The Minister subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which ruled against her, asserting that Mr van Wyk's actions were outside the scope of his employment ([paras 19–23]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 26–39])
The substantive issue arising from this appeal is whether the state—specifically the Minister of Safety and Security—can be held vicariously liable for the wrongful acts of a police officer committed while off duty. The preliminary issues presented relate to the timeliness of filings and procedural matters regarding condonation for late submissions and applications ([paras 26–28]). 

The central question for determination is whether a sufficiently close connection exists between Mr van Wyk's delictual conduct (the rape of Ms F) and the Minister's vicarious liability as his employer ([paras 26–27]). The approach must also consider the constitutional obligations to uphold the rights to dignity and security of individuals under sections 10 and 12 of the South African Constitution ([para 36]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 40–50])
The foundation for evaluating vicarious liability in South African law is the common law principle that an employer can be held liable for the wrongful acts of an employee executed in the course of employment. This premise extends to deviation cases where the employee’s actions, though contrary to employment duties, may still establish liability if there exists a sufficiently close connection between the wrongful act and the duties of the employee ([paras 40–41]).

The precedent set in *K v Minister of Safety and Security* provides a two-question test: first, whether the acts were undertaken solely for the purposes of the employee, and second, if a sufficiently close link exists between the employee's personal acts and the business of the employer ([para 49]). The test necessitates consideration of factors such as the employer’s constitutional obligations to protect individuals, the trust placed in police personnel, and the nature of the employee's conduct ([paras 53–66]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 51–82])
The court meticulously analyzed Mr van Wyk's conduct in light of the legal principles governing vicarious liability. It initially assessed the subjective component of the *K* test, determining that Mr van Wyk's actions were driven by personal motives, thus failing the first leg of the test. However, it then explored whether a sufficiently close connection could still establish liability ([para 51]).

Several normative factors were examined, particularly the state's constitutional duties to protect citizens and the trust citizens place in police officers ([paras 53–68]). The court noted that Mr van Wyk’s use of a police vehicle and the trust Ms F placed in him as a police officer were significant. The majority judgment differentiated between the acts of commission (the rape) and omissions (failure to protect), determining that both aspects held relevance in establishing liability, particularly through the lens of the victim’s perspective ([paras 71–82]).

The argument for vicarious liability was further reinforced by the understanding that Ms F's trust was invoked precisely due to Mr van Wyk's position as a police officer, underscoring the need for accountability within the police service ([para 82]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 86–179])
The Constitutional Court ultimately determined that the state (the Minister) was vicariously liable for the rape perpetrated by Mr van Wyk. The Court emphasized the importance of public trust in police officers and the responsibilities that come with state power to protect citizens, especially vulnerable individuals like Ms F ([para 86]).

The judges posited that the trust Ms F placed in Mr van Wyk, fueled by his official position, created a clear and significant connection between his wrongful act and the business of the police service ([para 82]). The judgment reaffirmed that the police have a constitutional obligation to protect the community from harm, and societal expectations of their conduct bind the state to account for acts of misconduct when such acts betray public trust ([para 83]), reflecting the constitutional ethos of protecting human rights.

In conclusion, this case serves to delineate the boundaries of state liability in delict, particularly within the context of police officers acting inappropriately or unlawfully while holding positions of authority. The ruling underscores the imperative for the police to uphold their duty in safeguarding public trust and the constitutional rights of individuals against violations by state actors.
