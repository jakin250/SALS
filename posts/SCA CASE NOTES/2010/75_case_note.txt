Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

## FIRAC Case Note: Bredenkamp v Standard Bank (599/09) [2010] ZASCA 75

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–20])
This appeal concerns the right of a banker to terminate a client’s account, brought before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) of South Africa. The appellants, comprising John Arnold Bredenkamp, his two companies, and a trust, sought to halt the closing of their banking accounts by Standard Bank of South Africa Ltd. The appellants, engaged in international commodity trading, had maintained multiple accounts with the bank since 2002, including credit and foreign currency accounts. 

On December 8, 2008, the bank informed the appellants of its intention to suspend and subsequently close these accounts due to Bredenkamp’s designation as a "specially designated national" (SDN) by the US Department of Treasury's Office of Foreign Asset Control (OFAC) because of his alleged ties to the Zimbabwean regime. The appellants sought an interim interdict in the South Gauteng High Court but were deprived of relief after the court dismissed their application, leading to this appeal.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1–10])
The central issue raised in this appeal is whether the Bank’s closure of the appellants' bank accounts was constitutionally defensible. The case primarily revolved around two interconnected questions derived from the principles established in *Barkhuizen v Napier*: first, whether the termination clause in the banking contract was fair; and second, whether the enforcement of such a clause must also adhere to standards of fairness, particularly in light of the provisions in the Bill of Rights—specifically sections concerning human dignity, freedom, and trade.

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 11–34])
The court referenced various legal precedents and legislative provisions throughout the ruling. Key among them was the case of *Barkhuizen v Napier*, which established that contractual terms should be scrutinized for fairness under the Constitution ([para 1]). The court also noted principles from prior interpretations of the Bill of Rights, including sections on equality, human dignity, privacy, freedom of trade, and property rights ([para 9]). 

Additionally, the court examined the implications of implied terms in law that govern indefinite contractual relationships, asserting that these may be terminated with reasonable notice: a principle evident in *Putco Ltd v TV & Radio Guarantee Co (Pty) Ltd* ([para 6]) and *Amalgamated Beverage Industries Ltd v Rond Vista Wholesalers* ([para 6]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 25–66])
In applying the aforementioned rules to the instant case, the SCA first established that the contracts between the parties contained both express and implied terms allowing for termination with reasonable notice, which the appellants accepted and did not contest ([paras 22–24]).

The court determined that the Bank's reasons for closing the accounts—arising from reputational risks linked to regulatory sanctions against Bredenkamp—were legitimate, irrespective of the accuracy of the allegations surrounding his status as an SDN ([paras 12–18]). The decision to terminate was thus classified by the SCA as a bona fide business choice, one made to mitigate further reputational damage and operational risk without implicating any constitutional violation or proxy public policy issues ([para 64]). The appellants’ failure to provide evidence of any objective unfairness tied to the Bank's tactics further undermined their argument ([para 65]).

While the appellants argued that their status as critical international businessmen warranted protections under the common-law framework, the court reiterated that fairness is not a standalone requirement dictating contractual conduct, and adherence to purely legal obligations suffices under these circumstances ([para 53]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 66–66])
Ultimately, the SCA dismissed the appeal with costs, thereby affirming the decision of the High Court. The ruling underscored that the contractual rights held by the Bank to terminate accounts could be executed without the obligation of additional considerations for fairness absent a demonstrable public policy breach or identifiable constitutional infringement ([para 66]).

This judgment indicates a robust reinforcement of contractual autonomy and the restrictions on judicial interference in commercial banking practices, bolstering the legal principle that banks possess the right to sever ties with clients under the conditions of reasonable notice, largely circumventing broader accountability under the Bill of Rights when no abuse of rights is demonstrated. The implications of the ruling serve to clarify the balance between contractual freedom and constitutional protections, particularly in commercial transactions within the banking sector.
