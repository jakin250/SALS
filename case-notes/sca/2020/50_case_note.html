Supreme Court of Appeal

The Minister of Police v K (Case no 403/2019)

In the case of The Minister of Police v K ([1]–[12]), the respondent, Ms K, was subjected to a traumatic event on 9 December 2010 when she was abducted, robbed, and repeatedly raped at King's Beach, Port Elizabeth.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 50 (6 May 2020) Coram: Maya P, Zondi and Dlo

Neutral citation: The Minister of Police v K (Case no 403/2019) [2020] ZASCA 50 (6 May 2020)

Coram: Maya P, Zondi and Dlodlo JJA, Koen and Gorven AJJA

F – Facts

In the case of The Minister of Police v K ([1]–[12]), the respondent, Ms K, was subjected to a traumatic event on 9 December 2010 when she was abducted, robbed, and repeatedly raped at King's Beach, Port Elizabeth. Following her escape, Ms K reported the incident to the South African Police Service (SAPS) at Humewood police station.

Concerned about her absence, Ms K's cousin reported her missing at around 19h00 on the same day, leading to the police discovering her abandoned vehicle at approximately 23h30. A search was initiated, involving a trained canine unit and aerial support, but ultimately yielded no results; her assailant was not apprehended.

On 14 November 2013, Ms K filed a civil claim against the Minister of Safety and Security and three individual police officers, alleging they negligently failed to conduct an effective search and investigation, resulting in her prolonged trauma. The High Court established that the SAPS had breached their duty to conduct a reasonable search and investigation, determining a 40% liability on the part of the Minister ([5]–[6]). The SAPS was held responsible for contributing to the aggravation of Ms K's psychological distress resulting from the incident.

I – Issues

The core legal questions posed in this case involved:

  1. Did the police officers breach their duties to conduct a reasonable search and investigation which caused Ms K further psychological harm? ([14]–[17])
  2. Were the elements required to establish a delict—namely conduct, wrongfulness, fault, damage, and causation—met in the context of police conduct during the search and investigation? ([18]–[60])

R – Rules

The legal principles relevant to this case are grounded in delictual liability and the Constitution of South Africa, particularly:

  • Negligence Standard: As per Kruger v Coetzee 1966 (2) SA 428 (A) at 430, negligence arises when a reasonable person would foresee injury and fails to take appropriate preventive measures ([19]).
  • State Responsibility: The state has an obligation to protect the constitutional rights of its citizens, including the right to dignity and personal security (s. 12(1) and s. 10 of the Constitution) ([15]–[16]).
  • Wrongfulness: Negligent omissions are actionable only if they occur in circumstances that the law recognizes as giving rise to a legal duty to avoid causing harm ([51]).
  • Causation: To establish liability, Ms K was required to demonstrate that the allegedly negligent conduct of the police was a factual and legal cause of her alleged damages ([56]–[62]).

A – Application

Negligence

The court examined whether the SAPS’s actions fell short of the expected standard of care during the search for Ms K and the subsequent investigation ([18]–[21]). While multiple resources were deployed, the court found that police officers did not conduct adequate foot searches and did not utilize their canine unit effectively.

Furthermore, the SAPS's failure to search specific areas where Ms K was held captive was highlighted as a critical oversight. The High Court had determined that the police’s actions were negligent, concluding that had a more thorough search been conducted, Ms K could have been found sooner ([34]–[36]).

Wrongfulness

The High Court found that the police's actions constituted a breach of their legal duty to protect Ms K's rights, thereby deeming their conduct “wrongful” ([51]–[54]). However, the appeal court scrutinized this conclusion, positing that the standard of liability applied might be overly burdensome on the police, entangling them in potential civil litigation for every instance of negligence ([55]).

Causation

The elements of factual causation were critically examined. The court noted that the experts had indicated that Ms K’s severe psychological trauma was primarily a consequence of the assault, rather than the police's failures ([62]–[63]). The court concluded that causation had not been established effectively, which undermined Ms K's claim.

C – Conclusion

In the appellate judgement delivered by Zondi JA, it was concluded that the High Court's findings on negligence, wrongfulness, and causation could not be supported by the evidence ([64]–[66]). Therefore, the appeal was upheld, leading to the dismissal of Ms K's claim with costs, including expert witness fees and other related expenses ([67]).

This case underscores the complex interplay between ensuring police accountability and the legal expectations placed upon public entities. The judgement affirms the necessity for clear standards in assessing police conduct while simultaneously addressing the potential implications such standards may have on the operational efficacy of law enforcement.
