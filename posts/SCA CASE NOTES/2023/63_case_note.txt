Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

## Case Note: TWK Agriculture Holdings (Pty) Ltd v Hoogveld Boerderybeleggings (Pty) Ltd and Others [2023] ZASCA 63

### F – Facts
The matter before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) arose from an action instituted by Hoogveld Boerderybeleggings (Pty) Ltd and its shareholders against TWK Agriculture Holdings (Pty) Ltd ([1]). The plaintiffs alleged that, following the adoption of amendments to the company’s Memorandum of Incorporation (MOI) during an annual general meeting on 5 February 2019, they were adversely affected due to their new categorization as "related parties" therein. This change allegedly diminished the preferences, rights, and limitations associated with their shares. Consequently, invoking the appraisal rights under s 164 of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, they demanded payment for the fair value of their shares, which TWK declined ([1]-[2]).

The plaintiffs’ particulars of claim were amended following a notice from the defendant to remove the cause of complaint. However, TWK's exceptions were presented against the amended particulars of claim ([3]) for two primary reasons: (1) the assertion that the appraisal remedy is only valid if there are multiple classes of shares, and (2) the claim that the purported "deemed relatedness" did not materially affect share terms but rather the individuals holding them ([4]-[5]).

The initial exceptions were upheld by Van Rensburg AJ in the High Court, prompting the plaintiffs to appeal to the full court of the Mpumalanga Division, which reversed the lower court’s ruling ([6]). TWK sought leave to appeal to the SCA on the basis of whether the full court's dismissal of the exceptions was itself appealable ([7]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue was whether the full court’s order dismissing the exceptions filed by TWK was appealable to the SCA ([8]). This question involved a consideration of:
1. Whether the dismissal of an exception is deemed final and thus appealable, or whether it allows for reconsideration during trial ([9]-[10]).
2. The implications of recent judgments concerning the appealability of interlocutory orders, especially in light of the constitutional principles surrounding the interests of justice ([14]-[20]).

### R – Rules
The court elaborated on the long-standing principle identifying that the dismissal of an exception is typically not appealable, as it does not constitute a final determination of rights ([9]-[10]). This doctrine is rooted in case law, notably from *Blaauwbosch Diamonds Ltd v Union Government* and later affirmed in *Maize Board v Tiger Oats Ltd*. The latter case confirmed that exceptions merely delayed further proceedings without finalizing the legal issue ([10]).

The court also acknowledged the shift in interpretation post-2013 with the introduction of the Superior Courts Act, which recognizes the need for flexibility based on interests of justice, allowing appellate scrutiny of interlocutory decisions in limited circumstances ([14]-[27]). Additionally, precedents have recognized that jurisdictional challenges are exceptions to this rule due to their definitive nature ([43]).

### A – Application
Applying established principles of appealability, the court decided that the dismissal of the exceptions by the full court did not meet the requirements for appeal ([51]). The court emphasized the necessity of finality, cautioning against piecemeal litigation, and reinforced that the High Court should first provide a comprehensive resolution ([21]-[22]).

The SCA highlighted that the dismissal of the exceptions is not a final decision, as it does not impede the plaintiff's ability to pursue their claim in trial ([31]-[32]). Furthermore, the court noted that even if the class exception were compelling, the notion of upfront appeal contradicts the procedural integrity of first allowing the trial court to provide a final judgement, including potential corrections of its earlier stances ([45]-[50]). 

Notably, the court acknowledged concerns about precedential issues raised by the parties but concluded that they do not create an exception to the Maize Board ruling ([48]-[49]). The SCA upheld the established rule that until the trial court renders its last word, the appeal from the dismissal of exceptions will not be worthy of judicial review.

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the SCA ruled that the appeal was not properly before the court, resulting in the appeal being struck off the roll ([52]). The implications of this decision reaffirm a commitment to respecting the framework of finality within judicial proceedings, ensuring that issues of law are best determined in the context of a full trial. This ruling emphasizes that without a final decision from the trial court, litigants should first exhaust all remedies available in the court from which the appeal arises.
