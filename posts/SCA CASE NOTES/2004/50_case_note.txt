Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2004

### Case Note: Republic of South Africa (MEC for Department of Public Works v Thamsanqa Faltein) 

**FIRAC Method**

---

#### F – Facts: 
The case centers around an incident on 16 November 1996, where the respondent, an employee of the Department of Public Works in the Eastern Cape, sustained serious injuries in a vehicular collision involving a department-owned bus ([1]). The respondent, along with others, was a passenger on the bus, which was meant to convey employees to a funeral of a deceased colleague ([2]). 

The legal action was initiated against the appellant, the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) responsible for Public Works, for damages totaling R1,364,000, claiming that the collision was due to the negligence of the driver, Mr. Owen Belwana, another departmental employee who was allegedly acting within the course of his employment at the time ([1]). The appellant contended that Belwana was not acting within the scope of his employment since the bus was under the control of the passengers pursuant to a private loan agreement with the department ([2]).

The trial court upheld the respondent's claims substantiating that Belwana's negligence had caused the accident. The appeal examined whether Belwana was acting in the scope of his employment, and further issues arose regarding indemnification agreements and the effect of Section 40 of the Public Service Act, 1994 ([4]).

#### I – Issues:
The pivotal legal questions for consideration included:
1. Whether Mr. Belwana was acting within the course and scope of his employment at the time of the collision ([4]).
2. The nature of the agreement between the appellant and the passengers regarding the bus use ([4]).
3. Whether the respondent had entered into any indemnification agreement that would prevent him from claiming damages ([4]).
4. Whether the respondent was barred from claiming compensation under Section 40 of the Public Service Act, 1994 ([4]).

#### R – Rules: 
The court relied on principles of vicarious liability, particularly regarding the scope of employment. The leading cases include:
- **Van der Byl v Swanepoel** (1927) AD 141, establishing that a master is liable for a servant's negligence only if the servant acts within the scope of their authority.
- **Minister of Law and Order v Ngobo** (1992) (4) SA 822 (A), which reiterated the considerations of whether the employee was engaged in the employer's business ([10]).
- **Rodrigues and Others v Alves and Others** (1978) (4) SA 834 (A), addressing instances where employees, despite not being authorized at the time, could still be acting within their employer's scope due to the nature of the responsibilities they were fulfilling ([12]).

Furthermore, Section 40 of the Public Service Act, 1994, stipulates limited liability for the state concerning employees and the conveyance of persons, unless the circumstances align with serving state functions ([18]).

#### A – Application: 
The court examined whether Belwana acted within the course and scope of his employment despite not having explicit authorization for that trip ([10]). It noted that both drivers—Magadla (the authorized driver) and Belwana—were engaged per management’s loose framework of authority regarding the bus’s use for employee funerals ([13]).

Evidence suggested that while the employees were on their own time, the management actively facilitated their transportation as a goodwill gesture to support morale and union relations ([19]). The court opined that this policy highlighted a broader operational purpose in furthering workplace harmony, which indirectly served the state’s interests ([20]). Thus, the refusal to allow employees to manage the bus freely indicated that, although unauthorized, Belwana was performing an employment-related task ([15]).

On the issue of indemnification, the pushback was centered on whether an arrangement that required indemnity had genuinely been formed. It was brought forth that the respondent never signed an indemnity form, raising doubts about the appellant's claims ([16],[17]).

The applicability of Section 40 was analyzed, suggesting that since the departmental bus's use was meant to serve employee relations rather than state operations, the respondent was not precluded from bringing a claim for tort ([19]).

#### C – Conclusion: 
The Supreme Court of Appeal found that Belwana was indeed acting within the course of his employment at the time of the accident, as he was fulfilling the employer’s objectives through the use of the bus during a funeral ([20]). Consequently, the appeal was dismissed, affirming the trial court’s decision to hold the appellant liable for the injuries sustained by the respondent.

The judgment underscored the principles of vicarious liability in the context of informal transportation arrangements and clarified the operational scope under which state employees interact. Mutually affirming the legal relevance of employee relations as part of an employer's duties and responsibilities, the ruling reinforced the importance of clarity in indemnification protocols in departmental policies and highlighted the limitations of Section 40 in protecting state interests under specific operational contexts. 

**Final order:** The appeal was dismissed with costs ([20]).
