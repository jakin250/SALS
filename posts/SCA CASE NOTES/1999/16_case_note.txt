# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** T Moosajee v The State  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Judgment Date:** 23 March 1999  
**Case No:** 29/98  
**Coram:** Smalberger, Vivier, and Streicher JJA  

---

## F – Facts

The appellant, T Moosajee, was convicted on two counts of theft after pleading guilty in the Magistrate's Court in Johannesburg ([1]). The convictions stemmed from the theft of R1699.22 on 20 October 1995 and R3320.12 on 8 November 1995, both amounts stolen from her employer, Tiger Wheel and Tyre, where she worked as a credit controller for approximately one year ([2]). Following her conviction, she was sentenced to eight and twelve months' imprisonment, respectively ([1]).

At trial, Moosajee was represented by counsel but chose not to testify. The defence called witnesses including social worker Ms Adams and probation officer Ms Fouche, who had prepared pre-sentencing reports recommending correctional supervision as a suitable sanction due to the appellant's personal and financial circumstances, including family obligations and remorse expressed for her actions ([3], [4], [6]).

The appellant had two previous convictions for fraud, raising doubts about her character. The first was for fraud involving R41.00 in 1982, and the second for R5600.00 in 1991, where she received a fine or a default prison sentence ([5]). In her current case, the circumstances included her being the primary support for five siblings and having repaid the stolen amounts within two months ([4], [6]). The magistrate, however, determined that due to the seriousness of the offences and Moosajee's history, imprisonment was the only appropriate sentence, stating that correctional supervision would have been suitable only for a first-time offender ([7]).

On appeal against the sentence, Moosajee also sought condonation for the late submission of appeal documentation, which was denied due to a perceived lack of reasonable prospects of success ([1]). This led to a direct appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, as permitted under sections 21(1) and 22 of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 ([1]).

---

## I – Issues

The main legal issues in this case arise from:

1. Whether the magistrate misdirected himself in assessing the appellant's financial difficulties and the appropriateness of the sentences imposed ([9], [12]).
2. Whether the appellant had a reasonable prospect of success on her appeal against the denial of condonation for her late filings, affecting the court's ability to consider her appeal on its merits ([1], [14]).

---

## R – Rules

The court cited established principles regarding sentencing and the appellate process:

1. **Discretion in Sentencing:** The trial court has the discretion to determine an appropriate sentence and interference on appeal is only warranted in cases of material misdirection or where the sentence shocks the conscience ([8]).
   
2. **Legitimacy of Doubts in Facts:** The court referenced *S v Pillay* 1977 (4) SA 531 (A) concerning the need for a material misdirection to warrant interference and *S v Dreyer* 1990 (2) SACR 445 (A) regarding the effectiveness of previous sentences in light of the appellant's awareness of possible penalties ([12]).

3. **Condonation Applications:** The conditions under which a court may grant or deny condonation for late filings were analyzed in the context of prior decisions such as *S v Gopal* 1993 (2) SACR 584 (A), indicating the ambiguity in statutory provisions affecting appeal processes ([15]).

---

## A – Application

In addressing the first issue, the court examined whether the magistrate had exercised his discretion properly in sentencing. The magistrate acknowledged the appellant's personal circumstances, but concluded that given her previous convictions and the nature of her crimes, imprisonment was warranted ([8], [12]). It was noted that the appellant did not provide a satisfactory explanation for the thefts, particularly why she had committed them prior to the asserted financial difficulties related to her sister’s health issues ([10]).

The second issue concerning the denial of condonation was approached by assessing the reasonable prospects of success in the appeal against the original sentence. The court affirmed that the refusal of condonation did not represent a misdirection meriting appellate intervention, given the comprehensive explanation provided by the lower court regarding the appellant's case ([14]). The court emphasized that since the appeal against the sentence was unlikely to succeed, the application for condonation was also dismissed.

Ultimately, the Supreme Court articulated that the magistrate's discretion was properly exercised, reinforcing the notion that past convictions and the need for deterrent sentences in cases of theft, particularly those involving abuse of trust, must be considered ([11], [14]).

---

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the appellant, T Moosajee, did not possess reasonable prospects of success in contesting either the denial of condonation or the sentence itself. The court concluded that the sentences imposed were not shocking in light of the nature of the thefts and the appellant’s repeat offences. The decision emphasizes the need for courts to balance individual circumstances with broader societal interests in instances of theft. Furthermore, the judgment highlights a legislative anomaly concerning appeal processes, suggesting an urgency for review in the relevant statutory provisions ([15], [16]). 

In sum, the appeal was dismissed with costs ([17]).
