Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: National Stadium South Africa (Pty) Limited v Firstrand Bank Limited [2010] ZASCA 164

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts ([paras 1–50])

The case revolves around a legal dispute concerning the naming rights of the FNB Stadium, originally erected in Soweto and financed by Firstrand Bank Limited (the "Bank"). The stadium was rebuilt in preparation for the 2010 FIFA World Cup. The Bank contended that it retained exclusive naming rights under a 1988 loan facility agreement, which stipulated that the stadium should be designated as "FNB Stadium" or a name chosen by the Bank from time to time ([para 7]). 

As the reconstruction progressed, conflicts arose regarding the naming rights between the Bank, the City of Johannesburg (a tenant), and the stadium management companies—National Stadium South Africa (Pty) Limited and Stadium Management South Africa (Pty) Limited (collectively referred to as the "managers"). The managers claimed that the naming rights had lapsed and that they were entitled to market the naming rights ([paras 2–3]). Consequently, the Bank initiated an urgent application for an interdict to prevent the managers from naming the stadium anything other than "FNB Stadium" ([para 3]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the Bank, granting an interdict and a declaratory order confirming the Bank's sole right to name the stadium until the expiration of the servitude agreement ([paras 4–5]). The managers and the City appealed the decision, prompting the Supreme Court of Appeal to review the matter.

### I – Issues ([paras 22–34])

The key legal issues at hand included:

1. What were the nature and scope of the naming rights granted to Firstrand Bank under the servitude agreement?
2. Did the Bank's naming rights expire when the original stadium was demolished and replaced during reconstruction?
3. Were the naming rights personal or real rights capable of binding third parties, including the current managers and the City?
4. Whether the failure to specify direct obligations regarding the promotion of the stadium undermined the efficacy of the servitude agreement.

### R – Rules ([paras 27–34, 37–39])

The Court primarily relied upon the principles of property law regarding real rights and personal rights to determine the nature of the naming rights. A servitude is defined as a limited real right in respect of the property of another ([para 32]). The distinction between real rights (which bind third parties) and personal rights (obligations against specific parties) was pivotal in the Court's analysis. 

Furthermore, procedural rules from the Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937 were invoked, specifically Section 63(1), which dictates that personal rights not restricting ownership cannot be registered as servitudes ([para 33]). This set the foundation for evaluating the enforceability of naming rights against third parties.

### A – Application ([paras 36–46])

In rendering its decision, the Court examined whether the naming rights established through the servitude agreement constituted real rights. It interpreted the terms of the servitude and determined that the rights conferred to the Bank were indeed real rights, enforceable against third parties ([para 34]). The Court found that the servitude allowed the Bank exclusive rights to name the stadium, thereby excluding the possibility of conflicting naming boards being displayed ([para 35]).

On the question of whether the rights lapsed with the demolition of the original stadium, the Court reasoned that the servitude agreement was designed to accommodate the new stadium, given that it was fundamentally a reconstruction for FIFA's World Cup event ([para 42]). The intention behind the agreement indicated that the naming rights were meant to extend to any new structure built on the property, regardless of the original's physicality.

Addressing the managers' argument regarding business efficacy, which posited that the lack of obligations on the government to promote the name rendered the servitude ineffective, the Court disagreed. It highlighted that the enforceability of the servitude was separate from contractual obligations, asserting that the naming rights were valid and enforceable ([para 39]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 47–50])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the Bank retained the sole naming rights to the stadium as outlined in the servitude agreement. It concluded that the servitude constitutes a real right, affirming that the rights survived the demolition of the original structure and extended to any new construction. The appeal by the managers and the City was upheld with respect to specific amendments, while being dismissed overall. 

This judgment underscores the significance of property rights and contractual stipulations within servitude agreements. The implications of this case not only reinforce the enforceability of naming rights in stadiums but also may influence future transactions where naming rights are at stake in similar contexts.
