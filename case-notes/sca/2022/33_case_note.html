Supreme Court of Appeal 2022

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

[1] In the present case, the appellant, Waylan Abdullah, was convicted by the Western Cape Division of the High Court, Cape Town on 15 September 2020, alongside a co-accused, of murder, robbery with aggravating circumstances, possession of an unlicensed firearm, and unlawful possession of ammunition. The appellant was sentenced to 29 years of imprisonment but sought leave to appeal against his conviction, initially denied by the high court but later granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA).

[2] The key aspect of the appeal centers on the identification of Abdullah as one of the gunmen involved in the murder of the deceased, Gregory Carelse. Moreover, the high court denied the appellant's application to recall the sole eyewitness, Dale Carelse, after an inspection in loco had been completed.

[3] The events in question occurred on 18 October 2018 between 17:00 and 18:00 in Bishop Lavis, Cape Town, where Gregory Carelse and another individual were gunned down by two assailants. The deceased, who had long been active in community safety and had previously testified against gang members, was shot with multiple gunshots, with significant evidence indicating the use of two different firearms.

[4] The atmosphere of gang violence in the region, particularly surrounding Valhalla Park, underpins the context of the murders. The deceased was actively involved in community safety and had even previously witnessed a gang-related shooting, making him a target due to his cooperation with law enforcement.

[5] Dale Carelse, the eyewitness and son of the deceased, attested to the events, claiming he saw the appellant firing a gun before running away. His initial fear of retaliatory violence delayed his statement to the police by 19 days, during which time he expressed concern regarding corrupt police involvement related to gang activities.

[6] The defense contested the reliability of Mr. Carelse's identification of the appellant, suggesting insufficient opportunity for observation amid chaotic circumstances. However, Mr. Carelse's familiarity with the appellant, having seen him regularly around Valhalla Park, was a pivotal point in the prosecution's case.

I – Issues

[7] Central to this appeal are two pivotal legal issues:

  1. Reliability and Credibility of Identification Evidence: Whether the identification of the appellant as one of the shooters by the sole eyewitness was credible and reliable given the circumstances surrounding the incident ([paras 2–3]).
  2. Constitutional Right to a Fair Trial: Whether the refusal of the high court to grant the application to recall Mr. Carelse infringed upon Abdullah's constitutional right to a fair trial, particularly concerning the right to challenge evidence ([paras 23–24]).

R – Rules

[8] The principles surrounding evidence from single witnesses are well established in South African law. Courts are required to treat such evidence with caution and ensure it meets a standard of reliability that considers factors such as lighting conditions, the proximity of the witness, and their opportunity for observation (S v Mthethwa 1972 (3) SA 766 (A); R v Mokoena 1932 OPD 79; S v Sauls 1981(3) SA 172 (A)).

[9] Regarding the right to a fair trial, the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa explicitly grants the accused the right "to adduce and challenge evidence" (s 35(3)(i) of the Constitution). This right includes an opportunity for cross-examination of witnesses.

[10] Furthermore, case law indicates that previous knowledge of the individual being identified plays a critical role in the reliability of such identification. The threshold for accurate identification is markedly different when the witness is familiar with the accused (Arendse v S [2015] ZASCA 131; Machi v The State [2021] ZASCA 106).

A – Application

Identification Evidence

[11] The court undertook a detailed examination of Mr. Carelse's identification of the appellant. It was established that despite the brief observation time (2 to 4 seconds), Mr. Carelse was not a stranger to the appellant; rather, their regular encounters rendered the identification a matter of recognition rather than mere observation ([paras 7–13]).

[12] The court acknowledged that the accuracy of identification tends to increase when the witness has prior knowledge of the accused. In this instance, Mr. Carelse's familiarity with the appellant and the recognition of the context—including the appellant’s nickname, “Wena”—provided a credible basis for identification ([paras 13–16]).

[13] The overall circumstances—the proximity of Mr. Carelse and the chaos of the scene—did not undermine the reliability of his identification. The court highlighted that Mr. Carelse explicitly stated how he had been focusing on the appellant’s face while identifying him ([paras 17–20]).

Fair Trial Right

[14] Concerning the high court's refusal to allow further cross-examination of Mr. Carelse post-inspection in loco, the appeal argued this denied Abdullah the right to fully challenge the evidence against him, potentially compromising the fairness of the trial ([paras 23–26]).

[15] However, the court found no compelling evidence that the inspection caused significant discrepancies that necessitated Mr. Carelse’s recall. Counsel had ample opportunity to challenge Mr. Carelse’s evidence during the initial trial, and the observations made during the inspection did not materially impact the essence of his testimony ([paras 28–30]).

[16] The SCA concluded that the refusal to recall Mr. Carelse was justified; the identification evidence remained credible and coherent despite any inconsistences mentioned by the appellant concerning the distances measured during the inspection.

C – Conclusion

[17] The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal and upheld the findings of the high court, affirming that the witness's evidence was reliable and credible ([paras 30–31]). This reinforces the principle that familiarity plays a significant role in identification cases and underscores the stringent standards required for admitting eyewitness testimony in criminal trials.

[18] The judgment also solidifies the balance courts must maintain between an accused’s rights to a fair trial and the necessity to ensure that the testimonies of witnesses can be duly recorded and respected, particularly in cases significantly marked by community violence and intimidation.

[19] Consequently, this case contributes to the ongoing discourse surrounding identification evidence and the application of constitutional rights within the framework of South African law, particularly in high-stakes criminal trials involving violent crime and gang activity.
