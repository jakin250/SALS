Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Golden Dividend 339 (Pty) Ltd v Absa Bank Ltd - FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts

In *Golden Dividend 339 (Pty) Ltd v Absa Bank Ltd* (569/2015) [2016] ZASCA 78 ([2]), the first appellant, Golden Dividend 339 (Pty) Ltd ("the company"), entered into a loan agreement with the respondent, Absa Bank Ltd ("the bank"), on April 5, 2016. The bank financed approximately eight million rand for the acquisition of immovable property, secured by a first mortgage bond ([2]). The company defaulted on its payments beginning January 2012, leading to an outstanding debt of approximately six million rand by July 7, 2013.

On July 25, 2013, the bank served a letter of demand as per section 345 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973, read with clause 9 of schedule 5 of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 ([2]). The company, on August 27, 2013, placed itself under business rescue proceedings as it was financially distressed, appointing Mr. Etienne Naude as the business rescue practitioner ([3]). A business rescue plan was published on October 4, 2013, but it faced procedural complications, notably inadequate notice for a creditors’ meeting. A subsequent meeting on November 22, 2013, resulted in a majority adoption of the rescue plan ([3]).

The bank filed an application to declare the adopted business rescue plan unlawful on November 21, 2013 ([4]). The company’s defense included a non-joinder argument, asserting that other creditors with substantial interests in the plan had not been joined in the proceedings ([5]). The bank countered that such joinder was unnecessary given that notification to creditors had been properly executed according to the Act ([6]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this appeal concerns whether the non-joinder of creditors in an application aimed at setting aside a business rescue plan renders such an application untenable ([1]). Specifically, the court was asked to determine if creditors, who possess a substantial interest in such proceedings, should have formally participated and, if their absence is fatal to the bank's application ([6]).

### R – Rules

The Court relied on several key legal principles derived from the Companies Act 71 of 2008. Section 152(4) explicitly states that an adopted business rescue plan is binding on the company and all its creditors, thereby establishing a collective interest among them ([5]). The issue of non-joinder relates to established principles of procedural law, which state that a party may not be joined in proceedings when such a party possesses a direct and substantial interest in the litigation's outcome if failing to include them jeopardizes their rights ([8]).

The court also referenced previous case law, underscoring that the obligation to join affected parties originates from common law and is amplified by the statutory framework of the Companies Act. The ratio from *Gordon v Department of Health, KwaZulu-Natal* ([10]) establishes that if the outcome of a case could adversely impact a party's interest, that party must be included in the proceedings.

### A – Application

The court's analysis showed that the non-joinder of creditors would significantly prejudice their rights. In its decision, it noted that a creditors’ vote had resulted in benefits outlined in the business rescue plan, which could be dismantled by the court's decision ([10]). The bank's argument that notification procedures sufficed to mitigate the need for formal joinder did not hold. The court held that ensuring proper notice does not replace the need for actual participation by the creditors, as mere notification does not protect their interests in the same manner as joinder would ([8]).

The court referenced a prior ruling by Ismail J, which asserted that creditors must be joined in such applications to ensure their rights are protected ([9]). It concluded that the failure to join the creditors was indeed fatally flawed, as their exclusion directly contravened the principles of fairness and the statutory mandates that aim to safeguard the interests of all affected parties ([10]).

Ultimately, the court indicated that while the bank had filed a notice of withdrawal regarding its opposition to the appeal, this did not negate the legitimate claim of the company against the initial ruling of the lower court, which had facilitated non-joinder ([11]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the non-joinder of creditors was fatal to the bank's application intended to set aside the business rescue plan. Thus, the appeal was upheld, and the court ordered the dismissal of the application with costs ([12]). This ruling reinforces the importance of creditor participation in business rescue proceedings under South African company law, cementing the view that creditors’ interests should be central to such applications. The decision serves as a critical reminder of the need for comprehensive procedural adherence in managing business rescue efforts and litigation involving creditors.

This case sets a precedent highlighting the necessity for strict compliance with the rules concerning the joinder of parties with vested interests in litigation. It reinforces the legal principle that procedural rigour is essential to uphold the integrity of the judicial process, particularly in matters impacting the financial interests of multiple stakeholders.
