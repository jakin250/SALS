Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 1999

# Case Note: The Commissioner for Customs and Excise v. Kemtek Imaging Systems Ltd

### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts:**
The case involves an appeal concerning the classification of imported goods under tariff headings 37.01 and 37.02 of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964. The parties involved are the Commissioner for Customs and Excise (First Appellant) and First Graphics (Proprietary) Limited (Second Appellant) as appellants against Kemtek Imaging Systems Limited (Respondent) ([para 1]). Both parties supply sensitised aluminium lithographic printing plates, differing primarily in their manufacturing processes—where Kemtek’s plates are cut from imported materials and First Graphics’ plates are locally produced ([para 1]). 

The Commissioner had classified the imported lithographic plates as subject to a 15% customs duty under tariff heading 37.01, while Mc Creath J of the Transvaal Provincial Division determined that the product should be classified under tariff heading 37.02, attracting no duty. This judgment was appealed ([para 1]).

The imported product is described as rolls of photosensitised emulsion on aluminium, used in the lithographic printing industry after being processed into individual plates ([para 2]). The question of classification arose primarily from the definitions and interpretations of "photographic plates" and "photographic film," both of which play a key role in whether the imported goods fall within the ambit of tariff headings provided in the Schedule ([paras 3–4]).

**I – Issues:**
The central issue pertains to the appropriate tariff classification of the imported product within the framework of the Customs and Excise Act, specifically whether it should be classified under tariff heading 37.01, which covers "photographic plates," or under tariff heading 37.02, which covers "photographic film in rolls" ([paras 5–6]). 

Sub-issues include:
1. The ordinary meanings and implications of "plates" versus "film" in the context of the relevant tariff headings.
2. The legislative intent underlying the distinctions in each tariff heading ([paras 9–10]).
3. Whether the imported goods could be considered "photographic plates" even in their rolled form ([paras 7–8]).

**R – Rules:**
The legal framework governing tariff classification is primarily laid out in the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964. In particular:
- Section 47(1) stipulates that duty is payable on all imported goods as specified in Schedule 1 ([para 3]).
- The interpretation of tariff headings is guided by the definitions provided in the Act and the Explanatory Notes to the Harmonized System issued by the Customs Co-operation Council ([para 3]).
- Tariff heading 37.01 specifically refers to "photographic plates," while tariff heading 37.02 covers "photographic film in rolls" ([para 5]).
- The distinction between "plates" and "film" in a photographic context, as supported by standard dictionary definitions, underscores the importance of understanding their distinguishing features, namely, rigidity ([para 8]).

**A – Application:**
The court examined the nature of the imported goods and determined that after processing, the product qualifies as a photographic plate under tariff heading 37.01. It established that since the product was intended to be transformed into individual lithographic plates, it retained its classification as a plate despite being imported in roll form. The court rejected the idea that the only distinction between the tariff headings was the form of the product at the time of import ([paras 10–11]).

The legislative intent was assessed, particularly the structured differentiation between "photographic plates" and "film in rolls." The court noted that the lack of specific mention of photographic plates in tariff heading 37.02 indicated that the legislature did not intend for plates, in any form, to fall under the category of film in rolls. This was bolstered by the definition found in the Explanatory Notes which further classified the varied uses of both photographic plates and films, suggesting that they were meant to describe different products entirely ([paras 12–16]).

The court relied heavily on the principle of statutory interpretation, upholding that the legislature uses specific terminology for a purpose—an omission of relevant terms in the tariff schedule further indicated that the imported product did not fit under the less specific term of "film" ([para 15]). The factual background showed consensus that the product, once processed, emerged as a lithographic plate rather than film, despite its form during transport ([para 9]).

**C – Conclusion:**
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the imported goods should be classified as photographic plates under tariff heading 37.01, thus attracting the relevant customs duties as previously determined by the Commissioner for Customs and Excise. The court overturned the prior ruling by the Transvaal Provincial Division on the grounds that the classification process had not adhered to the legislative intent and definitions provided in the Customs and Excise Act ([para 18]). 

The court's decision not only reinforced the importance of precise statutory language but also clarified the interpretation of competing tariff headings, leading to significant implications for customs duties on the relevant imported goods. The appeal was upheld with costs, emphasizing the court's support for the original classification by the Commissioner ([para 18]).
