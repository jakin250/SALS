# Case Note: FIRAC Method
## THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
### Republic of South Africa
**MERVYN DENDY (Appellant) v UNIVERSITY OF THE WITWATERSRAND (First Respondent), NEIL GARROD (Second Respondent), ANDREW ST QUINTIN SKEEN (Third Respondent)**  
**Court Quotes**: [2007] SCA 30 (RSA)  
**Judgment Delivered**: 28 March 2007  

### F – Facts
The appeal stems from a judgment rendered by Boruchowitz J in the Johannesburg High Court, which upheld exceptions raised against two claims made by the appellant, Mervyn Dendy, concerning alleged infringements of his right to dignity under Section 10 of the South African Constitution ([1–2]). The claims were predicated on procedural irregularities in the handling of Dendy's application for a position as a chair of law at the University of the Witwatersrand. The appellant argued that these irregularities violated his constitutional rights, causing him feelings of insult and humiliation.

In the first claim (Claim A), Dendy alleged that procedural flaws occurred during his application process ([2]). The second claim (Claim B) centered on the University's failure to provide him with reasons for his non-appointment and the minutes of the selection committee meeting that decided his application ([2]). The exception was founded on the claim that Dendy's pleadings did not sufficiently disclose a cause of action capable of injuring his dignity or justifying a remedy in damages ([3]).

The High Court, in its judgment, applied the established common law principles governing claims of "injuria," citing the necessity of objective and subjective criteria to evaluate dignity infringements. The court concluded that the alleged conduct did not rise to an actionable level, holding the procedural issues raised by Dendy had not been sufficiently grave to constitute a violation of dignity under both the common law and constitutional standards ([4–8]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal questions arising from this case are:
1. Whether the procedural irregularities alleged by Dendy amounted to a violation of his constitutional right to dignity ([2]).
2. Whether the common law needed to be developed in light of the constitutional mandates under Section 39(2) to allow for claims arising from the violations of constitutionally entrenched rights, and thus whether these past decisions required changes to align them with human dignity's increased importance in the modern legal framework ([13–16]).

### R – Rules
The main legal principles considered in the judgment derive from established common law regarding "injuria," particularly articulated in the case of **Delange v Costa**. The rules state that to establish a cause of action for injury to dignity:
1. There must be an intention on the part of the offender to produce the effect of the act.
2. There must be an overt act which is not legally permissible, and 
3. This act must constitute an aggression against another’s dignity or reputation ([5–6]).

The court emphasized the necessity for objective criteria to assess dignity infringements, cautioning against overly subjective interpretations that could open the floodgates to trivial claims ([6]). The constitutional framework, especially Section 39(2), mandates that common law must evolve to promote the spirit and objects of the Bill of Rights, ensuring the protection of rights entrenched therein ([22]).

### A – Application
In applying the law to the facts of this case, the court assessed both claims based on the legal standards established in **Delange v Costa**. Claim A was scrutinized for whether the alleged defects could be classified as offensive or insulting. The judgment concluded that the procedural irregularities, even if proven, did not possess an inherently offensive character when measured against societal norms of reasonableness ([8–12]).

Moreover, the court remarked that the appellant's claims were not based on the denial of the position itself, but rather the manner in which the selection process unfolded. However, upon review, the reasonable person's perspective indicated that knowledge of available review remedies would likely elicit feelings of relief rather than insult or humiliation from the procedural mishaps—thus failing to establish actionable claims for damages ([17–19]).

In relation to Claim B, the court reinforced that the refusal to provide reasons or minutes did not constitute an offensive act and further supported the alternate remedies available to Dendy which adequately addressed his grievances without needing to resort to claims for damages ([10–11]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Dendy's appeal, concurring with the views of the High Court that the claims under consideration failed to establish a cause of action requisite for a remedy in damages. The judgment affirmed that both claims were inadequately pled and that the common law principles regarding injury to dignity remained relevant and adequate under the constitutional framework, not necessitating further development in this context ([19–20]). 

With costs awarded to the respondents, this case illustrates the continuing tension between the evolution of common law and the imperatives of constitutional rights, while affirming the robustness of existing legal frameworks against claims deemed to arise from subjective feelings of insult or humiliation without accompanying objective merit.
