# Case Note: Avonmore Supermarket CC v Venter (211/13) [2014] ZASCA 42

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts ([1]–[7])
The case revolves around an incident that transpired on 30 June 2006, when Christina Venter (the respondent) slipped and fell on a damp floor while shopping at a supermarket operated by Avonmore Supermarket CC (the appellant). Following her fall, which resulted in bodily injuries, Venter instituted a delictual action seeking damages against the appellant in the KwaZulu-Natal High Court.

In her particulars of claim, Venter contended that the appellant was negligent for its failure to adequately supervise the cleaning of the supermarket floor, to warn customers of the wet floor, and to implement proper systems for cleaning. The appellant, on the other hand, denied any form of negligence, asserting that it had employed an independent cleaning contractor, DBU Cleaning Services CC (DBU), to maintain the cleanliness of the store. 

The trial in the High Court was bifurcated, focusing first on the issue of liability ([5]). The High Court found that the condition of the wet floor was the sole cause of the fall, and determined that the appellant was liable for the damages incurred by the respondent, subsequently ordering that the issue of quantum be postponed ([6]–[7]).

### I – Issues ([8]–[9])
The primary legal issues addressed in this appeal included:

1. Whether the appellant was absolved from liability through its alleged contract with the independent contractor DBU.
2. Whether the appellant was negligent in the management of the cleaning operations which led to the damp floor and consequent injury to the respondent.

The court evaluated whether the appellant had sufficiently demonstrated the existence of a valid contract with DBU, which would absolve it of liability for the actions of the independent contractor's employees.

### R – Rules ([10]–[19])
In evaluating negligence, the court employed the standard articulated in *Kruger v Coetzee*, where negligence (culpa) is established if:

- A reasonable person in the defendant's position would foresee the potential for harm and would take reasonable steps to prevent it.
- The defendant failed to take such precautions ([10]).

The court also referenced existing jurisprudence, notably the decision in *Ngubane v South African Transport Services*, to detail the considerations influencing a reasonable person's actions pertaining to foreseeable risks, including the degree of risk, gravity of potential consequences, and the burden of prevention ([15]).

The appellant's legal duty to ensure their premises were safe for patrons was well established, including references to cases such as *Probst v Pick ‘n Pay* and *Brauns v Shoprite Checkers*, which emphasized the need for adequate systems to address the risks presented by cleaning operations ([16]–[19]).

### A – Application ([20]–[21])
In applying the established legal principles to the facts, the court noted that the appellant's failure to provide adequate oversight on the cleaning operations resulted in a dangerous situation for customers. It found that:

- The cleaning operation occurred during peak shopping times without adequate warning signage placed near the damp area.
- The cleaner, having failed to ensure that the area was dry before moving on, created a foreseeable risk of harm ([20]).

The court determined that the facilities and processes employed by the appellant were inadequate given the foreseeable nature of the risk posed by a damp floor. The negligence of the appellant was established through the lack of preventative measures and effective supervision of the cleaning staff. Consequently, this negligence was directly linked to Venter's fall and her resulting injuries ([21]).

### C – Conclusion ([22])
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court's finding of liability against the appellant. The judgment underscored the principle that businesses must take reasonable precautions to ensure the safety of their premises, particularly when engaging in activities such as cleaning that could pose risks to customers. The court amended the High Court’s order to clarify that the appellant was liable for damages to be determined and agreed upon later. This case reaffirms the responsibility of business owners to effectively manage safety protocols and highlights the legal implications of negligence in commercial premises.

This ruling supports the broader legal standards concerning delictual liability in South Africa, by reinforcing the obligation of premises owners to protect their patrons from foreseeable harms that arise from their operations.
