Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: 

**Mirchandani v Unica Iron & Steel (Pty) Ltd and Unica Iron & Steel (Pty) Ltd v Mirchandani**  
(Case nos: 802/2020 and 813/2020) [2022] ZASCA 58 (22 April 2022)  
Coram: Saldulker, Mocumie, Mbatha JJA, Tsoka and Weiner AJJA

---

### F – Facts:

The case involves two appeals originating from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, concerning disputes between Suresh Chanderbhan Mirchandani (the appellant in SCA case no. 813/2020) and Unica Iron & Steel (Pty) Ltd (the appellant in SCA case no. 802/2020). Both parties contested the orders made by the High Court on 3 March 2020 which were subsequently amended on 4 September 2020 ([1]).

The pertinent facts are as follows: Mirchandani entered a Profit Share Agreement with Unica on May 21, 2007, dating back to December 4, 2006, wherein he was to serve as the technical director ([2]). His role included overseeing the commissioning and operation of Unica's first plant in Hammanskraal, known as Unica 1 ([2]). Following his tenure culminating on September 30, 2010, Mirchandani reported Unica to the Gauteng Department of Rural Development for alleged breaches of environmental laws under the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA) ([4]).

Unica contended that Mirchandani had failed to ensure compliance with environmental regulations, constituting a breach of their agreement. Ultimately, Unica was fined due to non-compliance with NEMA ([5], [6]). Unica further asserted that Mirchandani, as a director, breached fiduciary duties, leading to financial damages ([12]).

In addition, Mirchandani claimed he was incorrectly charged utilities and bond costs on his loan account under lease agreements, which were supposed to impose that the utilities be covered by Unica, adversely affecting his profit-sharing arrangement ([22], [24]). These intricacies led to litigation concerning the lease agreements amidst allegations of their legitimacy ([29]).

---

### I – Issues:

1. Whether Mirchandani breached the terms of the Profit Share Agreement leading to Unica’s penalties for environmental infractions.
2. Whether the lease agreements under which Mirchandani claimed for utilities and costs were indeed enforceable contracts or mere shams.
3. Whether fiduciary duties allegedly breached by Mirchandani can be substantiated under the common law and the Companies Act 71 of 2008, specifically regarding sections 218 and 76(2)(a) ([16]).

---

### R – Rules:

1. **Breach of Contract**: The valuation of the breach hinged on the explicit or tacit terms of the Profit Share Agreement. Common law principles apply concerning fiduciary duties as outlined in *Master of the High Court Western Cape Division, Cape Town v Van Zyl* ([13]).
  
2. **Fiduciary Duties**: Standard definitions include duties of loyalty and fidelity, distinguishing between mere incompetence and deliberate disloyalty ([13]). A breach must demonstrate disloyalty rather than negligence.

3. **Companies Act 71 of 2008**: Section 218 permits claims for damages resulting from contraventions of the Act without specifying requisite pleadings, while Section 76(2)(a) addresses directors' obligations regarding duties and care ([16]).

4. **Construction of Contracts**: The interpretation of contractual terms requires a reasonable assessment, avoiding judicial imposition of terms inconsistent with the intentions of the parties, as per *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality* ([32]).

---

### A – Application:

In analyzing the breach of the Profit Share Agreement, the court found that Unica's allegations against Mirchandani regarding non-compliance with NEMA were fundamentally flawed ([6], [10]). The evidence indicated that the decision to disregard compliance was collectively taken by all directors with full knowledge of the implications. The court noted that no explicit term in the agreement required Mirchandani to ensure compliance with environmental regulations ([7], [10]).

The court's assessment revealed that Mirchandani had acted diligently, and if any breach occurred, it was likely due to systemic failures of the organization rather than individual misconduct ([12], [20]). It refrained from attributing liability solely to Mirchandani when the responsibility was shared among the directors.

Regarding the lease agreements, the court emphasized the obligation of Unica to prove any claims that these agreements were sham contracts. Evidence demonstrated that the agreements were indeed legitimate and that Mirchandani was to pay rent while Unica was responsible for utilities ([23], [31]). Unica’s claim that the discrepancies in addresses and amounts rendered the agreements ineffective was rejected, affirming the validity of the contractual terms ([30], [34]).

On allegations of fiduciary breach under the Companies Act, the court ruled that Unica had failed to properly plead the circumstances surrounding the breach of fiduciary duty. It emphasized the importance of pleading sufficient facts as to allow the other party to understand the claims against them ([16], [18]). Consequently, the court dismissed Unica’s reliance on fiduciary breaches, reiterating that Mirchandani had acted in the company’s interests throughout his tenure ([21]).

---

### C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld Mirchandani's appeal against Unica while dismissing Unica's appeal concerning its own claims ([36]). This decision underscored the necessity for clear evidence in claims of fiduciary breaches and emphasized that directors should not be scapegoated in circumstances where the governance and operational decisions were collectively made. Mirchandani was affirmed in not being liable for Unica’s breaches of environmental law, as well as for the accounting claims pertaining to the lease agreements they had entered into. 

The outcomes of these appeals clarify the obligations imposed on corporate directors, the nature of fiduciary duties, and the critical importance of contractual clarity in business arrangements, thereby setting a relevant precedent in South African corporate law ([18], [21]).
