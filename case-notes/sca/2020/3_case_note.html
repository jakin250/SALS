Meyers v MEC, Department of Health, Eastern Cape (1010/2018) [2020] ZASCA 3

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 3

F – Facts

The present case emerges from the surgical treatment undergone by the appellant, Felicia Meyers, on March 2, 2010, at Livingstone Hospital, Port Elizabeth, where she underwent a laparoscopic cholecystectomy performed by Dr. Richard Vogel, a surgeon employed by the Eastern Cape Department of Health. During the operation, two small injuries approximately two millimeters in diameter were inflicted upon Ms. Meyers' common bile duct, leading to bile leakage and subsequent infection ([2]–[3]). Following the discovery of these injuries, which necessitated a second surgery to rectify the complications, Ms. Meyers filed a claim for damages against the MEC for Health, alleging that the injuries were the result of negligence on part of Dr. Vogel and/or his medical team.

The plaintiff's allegations of negligence centered on four specific claims:

  1. Failure to convert the laparoscopic procedure to an open cholecystectomy,
  2. Failure to perform the procedure with the requisite care, diligence, and skill,
  3. Failure to safeguard against injury to the bile duct during the operation,
  4. Failure to ensure the electro-cautery device employed was properly insulated ([3]).

Initially, the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court ruled in favor of the respondent, asserting that the plaintiff had not discharged the burden of proof regarding negligence ([4]). Despite this ruling, Ms. Meyers was ultimately granted leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([4]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues presented in this case revolve around:

  1. Negligence: Whether the surgeon, Dr. Vogel, fell short of the standard of care expected in performing laparoscopic cholecystectomy, thereby establishing liability for the injuries incurred by the plaintiff.
  2. Burden of Proof: Whether the plaintiff successfully discharged the onus of establishing negligence through the evidence presented, especially when an expert witness rendered contradictory opinions ([3], [49], [54]).

R – Rules

The law of delict, particularly with reference to negligence, is applicable in the present case. Negligence requires proof of a duty of care, breach of that duty, causation, and resultant damages. The following precedent and authority serve as key legal principles within the judgment:

  1. Standard of Care: A medical practitioner is expected to exercise reasonable skill and care, not the highest degree of skill (Mitchell v Dixon 1914 AD 519 at 525) ([1]).
  2. Onus of Proving Negligence: The plaintiff must prove negligence and cannot succeed if the evidence is evenly balanced (Van Wyk v Lewis 1924 AD 438 at 444) ([54]).
  3. Expert Testimony: The expertise in professional negligence cases requires evaluation based on the actions of a reasonable practitioner within that particular field (Goliath v MEC for Health, Eastern Cape [2014] ZASCA 182; 2015 (2) SA 97 (SCA) para 8) ([67]).

In matters involving specialized skill, the standard of care is accordingly adjusted to reflect the competencies possessed by those in the applicable medical field ([52]).

A – Application

In assessing the evidence, the court examined the testimonies of both expert witnesses: Dr. B.H. Pienaar, who testified on behalf of the plaintiff, and Prof. P.C. Bornman, who represented the MEC. The court favored the evidence provided by Prof. Bornman, who contended that a reasonable surgeon performing under similar conditions would not necessarily have been negligent. He drew a distinction between minor perforations, which did not inherently imply negligence, and major injuries that might warrant an inference of negligence ([8], [42]).

Revelas J's initial ruling noted that the injuries sustained by Ms. Meyers seemed to be consistent with human error rather than a failure to exercise due care. The judgment pointed to the complexities of surgical practices and the inherent risks involved in laparoscopic procedures where precision is critical, contending that the surgeon's actions did not deviate significantly from accepted standards ([9]).

Despite the different views of the expert witnesses, the court concluded that while Dr. Pienaar maintained that any injury to the bile duct indicated negligence, such a standard was excessively high and unrealistic. The court additionally factored in that Dr. Vogel had performed the procedure competently in line with the accepted guidelines for laparoscopic cholecystectomies, including the application of the so-called "ten commandments" of gallbladder surgery ([62], [63]).

It was determined that Ms. Meyers had not sufficiently discharged her burden of proving that the alleged negligence led to the injuries inflicted upon her, especially considering the standard set forth in Kruger v Coetzee, which guards against deciding negligence based solely on hindsight ([52]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that Ms. Meyers had not discharged the onus of proof regarding the alleged negligence on the part of Dr. Vogel. The court upheld the decisions of the lower courts, concluding that the evidence did not substantiate a finding of negligence. The application of expert testimony coupled with the assessment of the standard of care led to the ruling of the court, ultimately resulting in the dismissal of the appeal with costs ([65]–[66]).

The judgment reasserts the need for clear evidence of negligence in medical malpractice cases, emphasizing the complexities and inherent risks associated with surgical procedures. Furthermore, it highlights the critical role of expert testimony in establishing the standards of care expected within the medical profession ([62], [80]).

Order:

  1. The appeal is upheld with costs.
  2. The prior orders of the full court are set aside and substituted, holding the defendant liable for damages resulting from the injury caused by Dr. Vogel during the laparoscopic cholecystectomy on March 2, 2010 ([66]).
