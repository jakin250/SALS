# FIRAC Case Note: Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v United Manganese of Kalahari (Pty) Ltd

**Neutral Citation:** C:SARS v United Manganese of Kalahari (Pty) Ltd (264/2019) [2020] ZASCA 16 (25 March 2020)

**Coram:** Cachalia, Wallis, Mbha, Dambuza, and Schippers JJA

**Heard:** 13 March 2020

**Delivered:** 25 March 2020

--- 

### F – Facts
[1] The case concerns the South African mineral and petroleum royalty obligations of mining companies as provided in the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Royalty Act 28 of 2008 (hereafter "the Royalty Act"). The Royalty Act mandates that mining companies, such as United Manganese of Kalahari (Pty) Ltd ("UMK"), pay royalties calculated on their gross sales from the extraction and sale of minerals. 

[2] UMK is identified as the fourth largest manganese producer in South Africa, engaging in domestic and international sales of manganese. For international sales, UMK operates on both Free on Board (FOB) and Cost, Insurance, and Freight (CIF) terms. A discrepancy arose between UMK and the South African Revenue Service ("SARS") regarding the calculation of gross sales for the 2010 and 2011 tax years, specifically related to the exclusion of specific costs from the gross sales figure.

[3] Following a tax audit initiated by SARS, which revealed differing interpretations regarding gross sales calculations, UMK sought declaratory relief in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, asserting its right to deduct expenditures incurred for transport, insurance, and handling from its gross sales. The High Court granted a declaratory order, leading to the appeal by SARS.

### I – Issues
[4] The main legal issue revolves around the interpretation of the phrase "gross sales" as defined in sections 6(2) and 6(3) of the Royalty Act, particularly the meaning of "without regard to any expenditure incurred in respect of transport, insurance[,] and handling" of the mineral resources post-extraction.

[5] The court was tasked with resolving the question of whether UMK could deduct transportation, insurance, and handling expenses from its gross sales calculation regardless of whether those costs were separately itemized in sales contracts.

### R – Rules
[6] The relevant statutory provisions are contained within the Royalty Act, specifically sections 6(2) and 6(3)(b). Section 6(3)(b) explicitly states that gross sales are to be determined "without regard to any expenditure incurred" relating to transport, insurance, and handling after the mineral resource was prepared as specified in Schedule 2.

[7] The court referenced established canons of statutory interpretation, such as those articulated in *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality* and others, emphasizing an objective approach that considers the language used, the context, and the legislative purpose behind the provisions.

[8] Precedents and legal maxims were also cited, including previous tax law cases that have shaped the understanding of terms such as "gross income" and related deductions, highlighting the need for clarity in statute interpretation in areas concerning taxation.

### A – Application
[9] The court initially recognized the agreement between the parties regarding the meaning of receipts from sales as being synonymous with the term "gross income" under the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962. The matter was focused on the nature of the expenditures and their treatment in gross sales calculations.

[10] The court examined the argument posited by SARS that UMK should only be allowed to deduct TIH costs from gross sales where these costs had been explicitly stated as separate items in the pricing structure. UMK countered that the actual incurred expenditure, regardless of specification in pricing agreements, should be eligible for deduction.

[11] Delving into the statutory language, the court noted that the phrase "without regard to" necessitated a clear understanding that any expenditure incurred inherently must be disregarded in the context of determining gross sales. It was highlighted that the statutory language did not compel the need for those costs to be separated in pricing agreements for them to be considered deductible.

[12] The interpretation was further supported by examining the legislative history and the context within which the Royalty Act operates, especially concerning the mining industry's norms regarding pricing for bulk commodities on FOB and CIF terms.

[13] The context illustrated that a majority of transactions do not separate TIH costs, and as a matter of practicality, the language of section 6(3)(b) was constructed with the legislative intent to prevent over-taxation on expenses incurred to facilitate the sale of natural resources.

### C – Conclusion
[14] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court’s declaratory order should be amended. UMK is entitled to calculate its gross sales in respect of its manganese sales by deducting relevant expenses for transport, insurance, and handling, irrespective of whether those costs were itemized separately in the pricing offered to buyers.

[15] The appeal was dismissed with costs which included those stemming from the employment of two counsel. This decision underscores the importance of clear statutory interpretation and reinforces the principle that mining operators should not owe royalties on the costs incurred to sell mineral resources.

[16] The outcome may have significant implications for how royalties are calculated for mining companies in South Africa, ensuring that taxation reflects the true revenue generated from mineral extraction and sale while considering operational costs incurred therein.
