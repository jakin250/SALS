Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Minister of Mineral Resources v Mawetse (SA) Mining Corporation (Pty) Ltd [2015] ZASCA 82

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case concerns the Minister of Mineral Resources (first appellant) and Dilokong Chrome Mine (Pty) Ltd (fifth appellant), against Mawetse (SA) Mining Corporation (Pty) Ltd (respondent). Dilokong applied for a prospecting right under the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA) on November 24, 2006, for chrome ore on the farm Driekop 253 KT ([3]).

On December 6, 2006, the Regional Manager requested that Dilokong submit a signed shareholder agreement demonstrating compliance with the Broad-Based Socio-Economic Empowerment (BEE) requirements by February 4, 2007 ([4]). The Director-General (DDG) formally approved the prospecting right on June 21, 2007, for four years, but conditioned it upon compliance with the BEE requirements, necessitating a shareholding of at least 26% by an empowered entity ([4]-[5]).

By November 14, 2007, the prospecting right could not be notarially executed due to Dilokong's failure to comply with BEE requirements, which were further complicated by a government moratorium on the sale of relevant shares ([5]-[6]). Mawetse subsequently lodged an application for a prospecting right in September 2009, which was rejected on the grounds that Dilokong already held a valid prospecting right for Driekop.

This led Mawetse to challenge the award of the prospecting right in the North Gauteng High Court on January 20, 2012, ultimately culminating in a review application that reached the Supreme Court of Appeal after the High Court dismissed Dilokong's countermotion to compel the DMR to execute the prospecting right ([2], [6]).

### I – Issues
The primary issues for determination were:
1. Whether Dilokong had been lawfully granted a prospecting right and whether it could exercise that right, particularly in light of BEE compliance ([1]).
2. If the prospecting right had lapsed due to expiration or abandonment ([1], [18]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework pertinent to this case is derived primarily from the MPRDA, specifically sections 16 and 17, which detail the application and conditions for prospecting rights. Section 17(1) mandates that the Minister must grant a prospecting right if certain conditions are met, including financial capability and environmental compliance ([9]). Section 17(4) specifically empowers the Minister to impose conditions related to BEE compliance in accordance with the objectives outlined in section 2(d) of the MPRDA, which seeks to enhance the participation of historically disadvantaged individuals in the mining sector ([9], [12]).

The established legal doctrine from previous case law, particularly the decisions in **Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd v Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd** [2011] ZACC 26, emphasizes the state's affirmative action goals within the mineral resource sector ([8], [16]).

### A – Application
The court, led by Majiedt JA, examined whether BEE compliance was a condition precedent to granting the prospecting right. It was established that the DDG had expressly made BEE compliance a condition of granting the prospecting right ([12]-[13]). The court noted that Dilokong acknowledged this requirement and endeavored to comply but was ultimately thwarted by the aforementioned moratorium on share transfers, which impaired its ability to meet the BEE compliance requirement necessary for the issuance of the right ([13]-[15]). 

On the issue of lapse, the court recognized the importance of distinguishing between the granting, execution, and becoming effective of the prospecting right. It determined that the prospecting right was granted effective from the communication of that decision on July 18, 2007, and not merely upon notarial execution ([19], [21]). The court thus computed the duration of the right from this notification and found that it had expired four years later due to non-compliance and failure to execute, effectively concluding that the prospecting right had lapsed ([21]).

### C – Conclusion
The appeal by Dilokong was dismissed, affirming the lower court's ruling that the prospecting right had lapsed due to Dilokong's failure to comply with BEE requirements and the timeframe for execution. The court underscored the statutory imperative for compliance with BEE as a condition for the operation of mining rights and emphasized the need for timely exploitation of resources, consistent with the MPRDA's objectives ([28]). The decision holds substantial implications for future applications under the MPRDA, establishing that compliance with BEE requirements is essential and that lapsing of rights can occur due to failure to fulfill necessary conditions. The appeal is dismissed with costs, including those of two counsel ([29]). 

This case highlights the ongoing relevance of BEE and the statutory frameworks governing mineral resources in South Africa, reinforcing the judiciary's commitment to addressing historical inequalities through law.
