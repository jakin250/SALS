Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: *Public Servants Association of South Africa and Others v Government Employees Pension Fund and Others* (Case no. 886/2019) [2020] ZASCA 126

## F – Facts
The matter concerns the appeal filed by the Public Servants Association of South Africa (PSA) and two individual members against the decision of the Government Employees Pension Fund (GEPF) regarding the calculation of actuarial interest for members whose service ended after 1 April 2015 ([1]–[2]). The GEPF, established to manage pension funds for government employees, decided on 3 December 2014 to amend the F(Z) and A(X) factors used in this calculation, which directly impacts members’ benefits under Rule 14.4.2 of the GEPF Rules. The amendment took effect on 1 April 2015 without prior consultation with the PSA or designated employee organisations, contrary to the prescribed requirement ([6]–[7], [14]).

The first awareness of this amendment by the appellants came in July 2015, when complaints emerged regarding the significant reductions in benefits after the changes ([15]–[16]). Following unsuccessful attempts to engage the GEPF and the Minister of Finance in a meaningful consultation process, the PSA sought judicial review of the GEPF’s decision ([18]–[19]). The High Court dismissed the application, asserting that consultation occurred through the Public Service Co-ordinating Bargaining Council (PSCBC) ([41])—a conclusion disputed by the appellants.

## I – Issues
The primary legal question revolves around whether the GEPF's decision to amend the actuarial interest factors, enacted without prior consultation with the PSA and other employee organisations, contravened the principles of legality and adhered to the procedural requirements laid out in the GEPF Rules ([6]–[7], [41], [62]). Key considerations include:
1. Did the GEPF satisfy the consultation requirements outlined in Rule 14.4.2?
2. Is the requirement for consultation before a decision is made legally binding, and if so, what remedies are available for non-compliance?

## R – Rules
The legal authorities and interpretations relevant to this case include:
1. **Government Employees Pension Law, 1996**: Establishes the GEPF and mandates consultation before variations to benefit structures ([2]).
2. **Rule 14.4.2 of GEPF Rules**: Specifies that actuarial interest factors are determined "after consultation" with the Minister and employee organisations. The court interpreted the necessity of consultation as a prerequisite for legitimate decision-making ([39]–[41]).
3. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Provides for the review of administrative actions, although the appellants contended this was not strictly necessary if a legality challenge was sufficient ([41]).

Precedents, such as *Premier, Western Cape v President of the Republic of South Africa* [1999] ZACC 17 and *Unlawful Occupiers, School Site v City of Johannesburg* [2005] ZASCA 7, emphasized the duty to consult and recognize that consultation must precede decision-making, requiring good faith consideration of submissions made during the consultative process ([57]–[65]).

## A – Application
The court found that the GEPF did not comply with the requirement for prior consultation as mandated by Rule 14.4.2. The decision-making process undertaken by the GEPF fell short of the legal standards prescribed, as it was based solely on an actuarial report without engaging the affected employees or their representatives, leading to a conclusion that the decision was unlawful and reviewable ([38]–[41], [67]).

The High Court's reasoning, which suggested that subsequent consultations with the PSCBC validated the initial decision, was rejected by the appeal court. The proper sequence of events demands that consultation precedes any decision, sharing the rationale that a meaningful interpretation of the word "after" in this context implies there should be consideration of consultation outcomes prior to concluding any amendments ([55]–[66]). 

The appeal court concluded that the procedural flaw could not be validated by later attempts at consultation, reaffirming that the GEPF had fundamentally failed to adhere to the legal requirements governing its operations ([67]). 

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the GEPF’s decision to amend the actuarial interest factors was invalid due to non-compliance with the required consultation process. The appeal was upheld, and the GEPF was ordered to consult with the PSA and designated employee organisations regarding the actuarial interest calculations as stipulated in Rule 14.4.2 ([68]).

The implications of this decision underscore the necessity for statutory compliance within public governance, reinforcing the principle of legality and the value placed on stakeholder consultation within labour relations. This ruling not only provides clarity on procedural obligations under pension fund rules but also enshrines the rights of employees to be actively involved in the management of their benefits, thereby promoting equitable treatment within public service employment ([68]).
