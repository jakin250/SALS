Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Normandien Farms (Pty) Limited v South African Agency for Promotion of Petroleum Exportation and Exploitation SOC Limited and Others [2020] ZACC 5

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts:
In **Normandien Farms (Pty) Limited v South African Agency for Promotion of Petroleum Exportation and Exploitation SOC Limited and Others**, the case arose from an application by Rhino Oil and Gas Exploration South Africa (Pty) Limited for an exploration right under the **Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002** (MPRDA) on properties owned by Normandien Farms in Northern KwaZulu-Natal, where timber farming operations were conducted ([paras 2–3]). Normandien contested the acceptance of Rhino's application, citing procedural defects in both the acceptance and subsequent publication processes carried out by the South African Agency for Promotion of Petroleum Exportation and Exploitation (PASA), arguing they suffered legal prejudice as a result ([paras 5–6]).

The High Court found in favor of Normandien, ruling that PASA's acceptance of Rhino's application and the associated notifications were invalid due to non-compliance with the requirements of the MPRDA and its regulations ([para 22]). However, the Supreme Court of Appeal overturned this decision, stating that Normandien had not demonstrated any actual prejudice and that the matter was not ripe for adjudication ([para 24]).

Subsequently, Rhino withdrew its exploration application on July 24, 2019, further complicating the issue of mootness in the ongoing appeal process ([paras 27–30]).

### I – Issues:
The primary legal questions before the Constitutional Court included:
1. **Mootness of the Application**: Whether the withdrawal of Rhino's exploration right application rendered the appeal moot, thereby affecting Normandien's standing to challenge the judgment of the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 41, 46]).
2. **Interests of Justice**: If the case was deemed moot, whether it was nonetheless in the interests of justice for the Court to hear the appeal considering the implications for future applications under the MPRDA and the costs order issued against Normandien ([paras 41–56]).

### R – Rules:
The Court considered several statutory provisions from the **MPRDA** and its associated regulations. Key legal principles included:
- **Procedural Requirements**: The necessity for compliance with section 79(1) of the MPRDA and the accompanying regulations regarding applications for exploration rights ([paras 8–10]).
- **Administrative Action**: The criteria for determining what constitutes administrative action under the **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**, and the implications of failure to comply with procedural mandates ([para 20]).
- **Ripeness and Prejudice**: Legal standards related to ripeness of administrative challenges and the necessity for a party to demonstrate actual prejudice when contesting administrative actions ([para 33–34]).

### A – Application:
In its judgment, the Constitutional Court analyzed the impact of Rhino’s withdrawal of the application on the ongoing matter. It determined the key focus was whether the issues raised by Normandien were merely academic following the withdrawal. The Court acknowledged that while the Supreme Court of Appeal had reached a premature conclusion based on perceived lack of prejudice, the essential elements of public participation and procedural compliance required substantive examination ([para 54]).

The Court also evaluated the significance of the case against the backdrop of potential future applications under the MPRDA, recognizing that clarity on procedural compliance and public rights was necessary for future cases. However, it ultimately found that the primary relief sought—an order for the setting aside of the disputed acceptance—was rendered moot by the withdrawal of the application. Furthermore, it concluded that resolving the dispute would not have a practical effect on the parties or the regulatory framework ([para 52]).

### C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court dismissed Normandien's application for leave to appeal, citing the moot nature of the issues presented. It ruled that while the matter fell within its jurisdiction, it was not in the interests of justice to grant leave due to the absence of a live controversy and the lack of practical effects stemming from a judicial resolution ([paras 58, 73]).

In addition, the Court issued a punitive costs order against Normandien, citing its conduct in prolonging litigation despite the recognition that the case was moot and suggesting that Normandien's actions appeared to be a tactic for recovering costs rather than a legitimate legal grievance ([para 60]). The dismissal of the leave to appeal and the costs order underline a crucial emphasis on proper compliance with procedural legal frameworks in public resource management and a caution against litigation tactics that abuse judicial processes.
