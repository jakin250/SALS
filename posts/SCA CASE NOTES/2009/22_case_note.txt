Source Folder: 2009

# Case Note: Hoërskool Ermelo v The Head of Department of Education: Mpumalanga (219/08) [2009] ZASCA 22

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
Hoërskool Ermelo, a public school governed by its school governing body, faced a protracted legal dispute with the Mpumalanga Department of Education concerning its language policy. The conflict traces back to 2001, culminating in significant administrative interventions by the department, including suspending the school's principal and disbanding its governing body ([paras 1–2]). 

In January 2007, in response to a growing demand for English language educational services, the Head of the Department issued directives that directly contradicted the established Afrikaans medium policy, mandating the admission of learners for English instruction ([paras 10, 11]). Following this, an interim committee was appointed, which revised the language policy from Afrikaans to a parallel medium but did so without proper consultation ([paras 12, 13]). 

The governing body sought judicial review of the department's actions and decisions, leading to the initial hearing held by Ngoepe JP, Seriti J, and Ranchod AJ, which dismissed their application ([para 2]). The appellants were granted leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 2]).

### I – Issues
The legal questions before the Court primarily involved: 
1. Whether the Department of Education acted unlawfully in withdrawing the governing body's right to determine the language policy of the school ([para 20]).
2. The validity of the appointment of the interim committee to determine and implement the language policy ([paras 33, 34]).
3. Whether the proper procedure was followed in withdrawing the governing body's functions, specifically concerning the applicable sections of the South African Schools Act 84 of 1996 (the Act) and the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([para 31]).

### R – Rules
The applicable legal framework includes:
- **Section 20 of the Schools Act**: Confers upon the governing body the authority to determine the school's language policy ([paras 15–16]).
- **Section 22(1)**: Authorizes the head of the department to withdraw functions from a governing body on reasonable grounds, but necessitates compliance with prescribed procedures ([para 19]).
- **Section 25**: Provides for the appointment of persons to perform functions if a governing body has ceased to execute its duties effectively ([para 25]).
- **PAJA**: Ensures that administrative action is lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair ([para 31]).

### A – Application
The Court found that the Head of the Department unlawfully withdrew the governing body’s function to determine the language policy of Hoërskool Ermelo ([para 33]). Importantly, the Court emphasized that the Department’s action circumvented the legal requirements outlined in the relevant sections of the Act and violated basic principles of administrative justice as established by PAJA.

The Court critically analyzed the interpretation of sections 22 and 25 from the previous judgment in *Minister of Education, Western Cape v Governing Body, Mikro Primary School*. It concluded that the head of department did not provide sufficient grounds for the drastic step of appointing an interim committee in the absence of a situation where the governing body had ceased to perform its functions under section 25 ([para 26]). Moreover, the changes to the language policy by the interim committee lacked ample consultation and adherence to procedural fairness, rendering them invalid ([paras 31, 32]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appellants’ appeal should be upheld, setting aside the decisions to withdraw the governing body's functions, appoint an interim committee, and alter the language policy ([ para 34]). The ruling emphasized the necessity of following legal protocols in administrative actions and reinforced the governing body's exclusive authority to determine a school’s language policy under the Schools Act. This decision has significant implications for the autonomy of school governing bodies and the lawful exercise of administrative power, highlighting the critical oversight required to protect the rights and autonomy of educational institutions. 

This case serves as a reminder of the importance of lawful processes in educational governance and may influence future administrative actions undertaken by education departments across South Africa.
