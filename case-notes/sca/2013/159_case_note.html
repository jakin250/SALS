FIRAC Case Note: Cowan v Hathorn (176/2013) [2013] ZASCA 159

F – Facts

The present appeal stems from a decision made by the Western Cape High Court regarding an application by Mr. Michael Alexander Cowan ([1]) to set aside an action instituted against him by the joint liquidators of Africa Plastics Holdings (Pty) Ltd, specifically Mr. Craig Hathorn N.O., Mr. Christopher van Zyl N.O., and Mr. Dudley Davids N.O. ([1]). The liquidators sought to set aside certain notarial general covering bonds registered by Cowan over the company's movable property, claiming these constituted prohibited dispositions under sections 26, 29, 30, and 31 of the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936 ([2]).

In February 2012, a creditor, Olampa (Pty) Ltd, expressed the intent to initiate these proceedings against Cowan, with a letter to the liquidators offering an indemnity for any costs stemming from such litigation ([3a]). This indemnity was delayed due to negotiations over its terms and the extent of security required, eventually being provided post-institution of the proceedings ([3e]).

Cowan's rule 30 application contended that without an indemnity prior to the initiation of proceedings, the summons served against him was a nullity, thus invalidating the action ([5]). The High Court ruled that the subsequent provision of indemnity fulfilled the purpose of section 32(1)(b) of the Insolvency Act, as neither the liquidators nor creditors suffered prejudice due to the delay ([6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question addressed in this appeal concerns whether the High Court erred in dismissing Cowan’s application to set aside the summons as an irregular step under rule 30 of the Uniform Rules of Court, based on the timing of the indemnity provision ([7]). A secondary issue arose regarding whether Cowan would suffer substantial injustice due to the interruption of prescription if the summons was not declared a nullity ([14]).

R – Rules

The legal foundation for this case revolves around the construction and interpretation of sections 32(1)(a) and (b) of the Insolvency Act ([8]). Essentially, these provisions stipulate that a creditor may take legal action in the name of the trustee only if the trustee has failed to do so and only after providing indemnity against costs incurred ([8]).

The relevant legal principle holds that deviations from statutory requirements do not render proceedings necessarily invalid unless they result in a substantive injustice that cannot be remedied ([10]). Additionally, section 157(1) of the Act states that irregularities or formal defects do not render actions invalid unless they cause substantial injustice ([7]).

Further precedents, like Lane & another NNO v Dabelstein & others and Patel v Paruk's Trustee, elucidate the purpose of indemnity in protecting liquidators from adverse costs thereby preventing asset dissipation during litigation ([11], [12]).

A – Application

The Court assessed whether the liquidators were protected by the indemnity as intended under section 32(1)(b) of the Insolvency Act. Critical to the Court's analysis was the sequence of communications and actions taken by Cowan and Olampa leading up to the initiation of the lawsuit. Notably, the liquidators expressed concerns regarding Olampa’s financial capacity to fulfil the indemnity, necessitating assurances before proceeding ([12a]-[12b]).

The Court concluded that the liquidators were adequately protected against potential adverse costs as the indemnity effectively covered all litigation costs, thus allowing for the realization of the statute's core objectives—primarily those of asset protection from litigation expenses ([12d], [13]). It was determined that the object of section 32(1)(b) was achieved, as protecting the liquidators from financial risk was paramount ([13]).

With regards to Cowan's argument on substantial injustice due to the interruption of prescription, the Court noted that the object of the statute had been met, negating the necessity to delve into further inquiries regarding section 157(1) ([15]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled that the High Court's decision to dismiss Cowan's application was correct, affirming that the lack of pre-emptive indemnity did not invalidate the proceedings, as the objectives of the Insolvency Act were satisfied due to the subsequent provision of indemnity post-institution. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs ([17]).

This case reinforces the notion that judicial scrutiny may validate procedural deviations when the substantive objectives of legislation, particularly protections for creditors and trustees, are fulfilled. Furthermore, it highlights how legal mechanisms, such as indemnities, play a crucial role in structuring the responsibilities and protections afforded to liquidators during insolvency proceedings.
