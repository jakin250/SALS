President of the Republic of South Africa v Democratic Alliance and Others [2019] ZACC 35

On April 3, 2017, the Democratic Alliance (DA) initiated an urgent review application in the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria.

Constitutional Court 2019 [2019] ZACC 35

F – Facts

This matter originates from a decision made by the former President Jacob Zuma on March 30, 2017, when he reshuffled his Cabinet, resulting in the dismissal of several key ministers, including Minister Pravin Gordhan and Deputy Minister Mcebisi Jonas ([4]). In the aftermath of the reshuffle, the newly appointed officials included Minister Malusi Gigaba and Deputy Minister Sifiso Buthelezi. A statement was issued by the Presidency outlining the reasons for these changes ([4]).

On April 3, 2017, the Democratic Alliance (DA) initiated an urgent review application in the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria. They sought to set aside the President's decision, claiming it was unlawful, unconstitutional, and invalid, with irrationality being the core argument ([5]). The application was framed under Rule 53 of the Uniform Rules of Court, which governs the procedural requirements for such reviews ([7]).

The DA encountered difficulties obtaining the record of proceedings and the reasons for the President's decision, leading them to file an interlocutory application compelling the President to comply ([6]). The High Court, applying a purposive interpretation of Rule 53, ruled in favor of the DA, requiring the President to submit the requested documentation within five days ([7]).

Aggrieved by this decision, the President appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). During the pending appeal, Jacob Zuma resigned, and Cyril Ramaphosa took office, effectively rendering the main application moot and leading to the DA's decision to withdraw the review application ([8]–[9]).

The SCA recognized the mootness but maintained that the question regarding Rule 53’s application to executive decisions warranted consideration. They ultimately dismissed the appeal, concluding it lacked practical effect ([12]). The President then sought leave to appeal from the Constitutional Court ([13]).

I – Issues

The primary issues arising from this case are:

  1. Was the President's decision to appoint or dismiss a Minister justiciable under Rule 53 of the Uniform Rules of Court?
  2. Should the Constitutional Court determine the appeal despite the mootness of the underlying review application?
  3. What are the implications of extending Rule 53 to navigate executive functions?

The mootness of the matter raised significant questions about whether the Constitutional Court should exercise its discretion to hear a case that no longer presented a live controversy ([3], [17]). Additionally, the concerns over the appropriateness of judicial review regarding executive powers highlight constitutional principles related to separation of powers ([24]).

R – Rules

The rules relevant to this case included:

  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996: Sections 91(2) and 93(1), which delineate the powers of the President regarding executive appointments and dismissals.
  • Rule 53 of the Uniform Rules of Court: Governs procedures in review applications of decisions made by inferior courts and certain public bodies, requiring them to furnish records of proceedings and reasons for their decisions ([1]).

The Constitutional Court has established precedents regarding the discretionary power to hear moot matters in the interest of justice ([19]). In Langeberg Municipality v Independent Electoral Commission and Shuttleworth v South African Reserve Bank, the court outlined the conditions under which moot matters may be entertained, emphasizing judicial discretion informed by the interests of justice ([18]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court, led by Chief Justice Mogoeng, concluded that it was not in the interests of justice to entertain the moot application ([36]). The majority emphasized that the primary question—the application of Rule 53 to executive decisions—could not be resolved without recounting the merits of the original review application, which was no longer extant ([32], [34]).

The Court discussed how the justiciability of executive decisions has traditionally been confined by the separation of powers doctrine and highlighted that extending Rule 53 could pose significant separation of powers issues ([24], [33]). The interpretation of Rule 53 as applicable to executive actions had not been established and warranted thorough examination, especially given the complex political dynamics involved ([20], [33]).

The Justices noted that while there may be a desire for clarity surrounding legislative duties regarding cabinet reshuffles, such inquiries require robust factual context to engage effectively with the law ([32]). The Court determined that it could not decide on the applicability of Rule 53 without traversing the merits that had not been adequately considered previously ([38]).

In dissent, Justice Jafta emphasized that a ruling on the applicability of Rule 53 would serve as binding precedential guidance for future presidential decisions, highlighting the necessity of addressing the moot appeal for future litigants ([60]). He indicated that the interpretation of Rule 53 would hold value for similar disputes moving forward, outlining that wrongful interpretations by lower courts needed rectification to clarify obligations upon the President’s future executive actions ([66]–[70]).

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court dismissed the President's application for leave to appeal, ruling that it lacked practical effect following the mootness of the underlying application ([40]). The judgment underscored that while the interests of justice may sometimes support adjudicating moot matters, the resolution of the issues needed to be grounded in a live controversy to be principled judicial action ([25], [36]).

The case exemplifies the tension between judicial oversight and executive discretion, illuminating the need for clearer statutory frameworks governing executive actions. The ruling implies that future attempts to challenge executive decisions may result in similar questions surrounding the interpretation of judicial review norms, potentially altering the landscape of political accountability and governance in South Africa.
