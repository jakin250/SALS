# FIRAC Case Note: François Stephanus Cloete v The State (317/11)

**Case Title:** François Stephanus Cloete v The State  
**Neutral Citation:** [2011] ZASCA 02  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram:** Mthiyane DP, Maya, Malan, Wallis JJA, and Petse AJA  
**Heard:** 23 November 2011  
**Delivered:** 2 March 2012

---

## F – Facts

On 11 May 2007, **François Stephanus Cloete** (the Appellant) was convicted in the Regional Court at Schweizer-Reneke for the murder of **Thulasizwe Hlatswayo** (the Deceased) and sentenced to ten years' imprisonment ([1]). Unhappy with this outcome, the Appellant sought leave to appeal to the North Gauteng High Court, which was granted on 12 November 2010. This court dismissed his appeal ([2]). 

The Deceased died on 13 February 2006 from a **subdural haemorrhage**, but the evidence indicated that he had suffered multiple assaults prior to his death, including by the Appellant ([3]). The Appellant's conviction was based on the assertion that he had a legal duty to protect the Deceased from harm by others after previously having conducted a citizen's arrest ([4]).

Notably, the charge sheet did not allege that the Appellant had failed in this duty nor did it claim that he had acted in common purpose with the other assailants ([5]). At trial, the Appellant admitted substantial factual elements, including that he had assaulted the Deceased and dragged him across a road ([6]).

Witness testimonies included accounts from individuals present at the scene, with one stating he overheard the Appellant threatening the Deceased ([9]). Citations from the trial court indicated that the medical evidence did not definitively establish the cause of death but could not rule out contributions from the Appellant's conduct ([16]).

## I – Issues

The **central legal issues** presented to the court were:

1. Whether the Appellant was under a legal duty to prevent harm to the Deceased by the assault from others ([24]).
2. Whether the Appellant's actions causally contributed to the Deceased's death ([25]).
3. Whether the Appellant was acting in common purpose with the four assailants responsible for the prior assault ([26]).

## R – Rules

The court examined various legal rules and precedents:

- The **Common Law** concerning murder and its requisite elements—which requires proof of intention and causation ([17]).
- The legal duty akin to that in cases such as **S v Musingadi & Others** ([20]) as well as the necessity for the State to prove each element of the charge beyond a reasonable doubt, specifically in relation to established precedent surrounding common purpose and culpability ([26]).
- The **Criminal Procedure Act** provisions, particularly §258(b) regarding the possibility of a verdict of assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm when murder is not proven ([32]).

## A – Application

Upon examination, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) found the Appellant's conviction unsustainable based on several grounds:

1. **Legal Duty:** The Court held that the Appellant had articulated that any intervention to prevent the Deceased's assault was futile given the other assailants' numerical superiority and his own exhaustion. Thus, the claim of legal duty was not substantiated by evidence ([24]).

2. **Causation:** The medical testimony established that the Deceased did not die instantaneously from the prior assault and highlighted the significant role of the assault by the four individuals in the Toyota Tazz, which left open the possibility that the prior assaults caused fatal injuries. The SCA noted the insufficiency of proof that the Appellant's actions caused the death ([25], [26]).

3. **Common Purpose:** The argument that the Appellant acted in furtherance of a common purpose was explicitly unsupported by the charge sheet, which solely accosted him for the direct act of murder. The court concluded that there was no evidence indicating a pre-arranged concert of action with the other assailants ([27]).

## C – Conclusion

The SCA ruled in favor of the Appellant, concluding that:

1. His conviction for murder was incorrect and should be amended to assault with intent to cause grievous bodily harm, given that the aggravating factors and admissions established an element of assault ([39]).
2. The Appellant's conviction was set aside as well as the original ten-year sentence, and replaced with a four-year term of imprisonment. The court found this sentence proportional, considering both mitigating and aggravating factors, including the Appellant's prior convictions and the nature of the assault ([35], [38]).

This ruling underscores the necessity for the State to substantiate claims of legal duty and causality with concrete evidence transcending mere allegations, affirming principles central to South African criminal jurisprudence.
