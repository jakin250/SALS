Supreme Court of Appeal

South African Municipal Workers’ Union National Provident Fund v Dihlabeng Local Municipality and Others (65/2022) [2023] ZASCA 55

Following their dismissal, the affected employees and the Municipality entered into a settlement agreement on October 8, 2009, which stipulated that they would be "employed" by the Municipality from that date.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 55

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case centers on the South African Municipal Workers’ Union National Provident Fund (the Fund) which filed an application in the Free State Division of the High Court against the Dihlabeng Local Municipality (the Municipality) for payment of alleged arrear pension fund contributions and associated statutory interest. The Fund claimed that the Municipality had not fulfilled its obligations under the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 (the Act). The contentious backdrop involved the dismissal of 75 Municipality employees on July 31, 2009, after an unprotected strike on April 6, 2009.

Following their dismissal, the affected employees and the Municipality entered into a settlement agreement on October 8, 2009, which stipulated that they would be "employed" by the Municipality from that date. Various terms included final written warnings for participating in the strike, and the condition that no retrospective salaries or benefits would be granted for the period of unemployment (July 30 to October 7, 2009).

Two years post-agreement, the affected employees approached the Fund for their withdrawal benefits resulting from their initial dismissal. The Fund declined payment, asserting that the employees were reinstated and thus remained members of the Fund. The employees then petitioned the Pension Funds Adjudicator, who concluded in December 2012 that the employees were in continuous employment and thus members of the Fund.

Subsequently, the Fund sought enforcement of alleged contributions from the Municipality, which contended that the employees validly elected to switch their pension fund affiliation to the Municipal Employees Pension Fund (MEPF) upon re-employment, claiming they had not been reinstated but rather re-employed.

This appeal arises from the dismissal of the Fund's claims, with a focus on whether the employees were reinstated or re-employed and if the matter was res judicata based on prior adjudications.

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the court can be delineated as follows:

  1. Res Judicata: Did the determination by the Pension Funds Adjudicator create a res judicata effect, preventing the Municipality and MEPF from contesting the employees’ pension fund membership status?
  2. Employment Status: Were the affected employees reinstated or re-employed per the settlement agreement?
  3. Prescription: Has the Fund's claim for contributions become prescribed as per applicable law?

R – Rules

Several legal rules and statutory provisions governed the court’s determinations, notably:

  1. Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956:
  • Section 13A mandates that employers must pay contributions to pension funds for all employees in service.
  • Section 30O(1) stipulates that the Adjudicator's determinations carry the weight of a civil judgment, essentially equating them with court judgments.
  1. Principles of Res Judicata and Issue Estoppel:
  • A matter is considered res judicata if the same parties, issues, and relief are present as in prior judgments (Royal Sechaba Holdings (Pty) Ltd v Coote and Another [2014] ZASCA 85).
  • Issue estoppel allows for avoiding relitigation of an issue that has been definitively resolved between the same parties (Smith v Porritt and Others [2007] ZASCA 19).
  1. Interpretation of Contracts:
  • Established in Endumeni Municipality [2012] ZASCA 13, interpretation hinges on the ordinary meaning of terms and contextual understanding of the overall contract purpose.

A – Application

In addressing res judicata, the court found that the Adjudicator's determination did not include the Municipality as a party to the complaint. The court emphasized that the issue of contributions was not adjudicated, thus precluding any application of res judicata or issue estoppel in this matter. The Municipality’s exclusion limited the applicability of previous determinations to bind it.

On the issue of employment status, the court engaged with the language of the settlement agreement. It analyzed whether the settlement constituted reinstatement or re-employment. The court concluded that the affected employees' new employee numbers, benefits elections, and the absence of retrospective salary obligations indicative of a new employment contract rather than reinstatement were determinative facts. Relying on precedence in similar cases (Equity Aviation Services [2008] ZACC 16), the court underscored that reinstatement entails reestablishing the prior employment terms while re-employment reflects a new hire under potentially varying terms.

Lastly, on the matter of prescription, the court noted that, owing to the primary determinations surrounding the nature of employment, it was not necessary to delve into this defense.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the high court's ruling, dismissing the Fund's appeal with costs. The court reaffirmed that the affected employees were re-employed, not reinstated, leading to the determination that their membership in the Fund had lawfully ceased post-dismissal. The res judicata and prescription claims were effectively rendered moot due to this characterization, signaling clear implications for future cases concerning pension fund membership and the rights of employees in transition from dismissal to re-employment under settlement agreements.

This case solidifies the boundaries between employment status types under South African labor legislation, emphasizing the need for clarity in settlement agreements and the implications for associated employee benefits.
