Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Bakane v The State (1180/2016) [2017] ZASCA 182

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–2])

On the morning of 23 June 2004, the body of Mr Johannes Albertus Maré was discovered in his home, having been strangled with a piece of curtain. The police found items believed to have been stolen from the deceased near the crime scene and arrested the appellant, Lefu Jantjie Bakane, along with four co-accused individuals. The appellant and his co-accused were charged with murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria. The trial court convicted Bakane, sentencing him to life imprisonment for murder and 15 years for robbery, running concurrently. Bakane appealed the convictions and sentences to a Full Court, which dismissed the appeal, leading to Bakane's further appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) with special leave ([paras 1–2]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 8–9])

The primary legal issue on appeal was whether the trial court correctly admitted the appellant’s statement (Exhibit H) into evidence, considering that the appellant contended it was obtained involuntarily due to police coercion. The appeal raised constitutional questions about the validity of obtaining confessions and admissions under Section 35 of the South African Constitution, particularly concerning the right to remain silent and the right to legal representation. Furthermore, it questioned whether a fair trial was rendered impossible due to the alleged violations of constitutional rights during the police investigation ([paras 8–9]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 10–12])

The SCA referred to several key legal principles and statutory provisions regarding the admissibility of confessions and admissions. According to Section 35(5) of the Constitution, evidence obtained in violation of any rights in the Bill of Rights must be excluded if admitting it would render the trial unfair. The provisions of the Criminal Procedure Act, particularly Section 219A, stipulate that evidence related to a confession must be voluntarily made; involuntary admissions are inadmissible. Additionally, the State bears the onus to prove, on a balance of probabilities, that any statement was made voluntarily ([paras 10–12]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 13–25])

In its judgment, the Court systematically examined the circumstances surrounding the taking of the appellant’s statement, concluding that the police had violated the appellant’s rights. The Court highlighted the appellant's claim that he was assaulted by police and coerced into providing a statement, with evidence from the trial that confirmed some rough handling and that the appellant was denied proper legal representation (Exhibit H was produced following a night of interrogation). 

The Court scrutinized the contents of Exhibit H, noting significant discrepancies and a lack of particulars regarding the commission of the alleged offences. It found that the admissions contained in the statement were vague, failing to establish the necessary details about the crimes. The SCA rejected the trial court's finding that the statement was made voluntarily, considering the evidenced coercive circumstances ([paras 17–20]). It also noted that, without the contested Exhibit H, there existed no substantial evidence upon which Bakane could be convicted. The Court reiterated that the trial court had a duty to uphold constitutional rights and dismiss any evidence obtained through infringement of these rights ([paras 21–24]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 25–27])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the trial court erred in admitting Exhibit H and consequently in convicting the appellant based on that evidence. The judgment emphasized the severe implications of disregarding constitutional rights and highlighted the need for law enforcement agencies to adhere strictly to legal and ethical standards during investigations. The Court underscored the message that failing to respect the Bill of Rights damages the integrity of the judicial process, reinforcing the need for a fair trial. The SCA upheld the appeal, set aside the convictions and sentences, and declared the appellant not guilty ([paras 25–27]).

The ruling is pivotal, reiterating the importance of constitutional protections, particularly in criminal law, and the obligations of the state to uphold these rights, thus reinforcing the rule of law in South Africa.
