Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note 

## Case Title: 
**Petrus Johannes Bestbier and Others v Nedbank Limited**  
[2024] ZACC 2 
Coram: Maya DCJ, Kollapen J, Mathopo J, Mhlantla J, Rogers J, Schippers AJ, Theron J, Tshiqi J, Van Zyl AJ

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts (Paras [1]–[6], [3]–[6], [15])
This case arose from a dispute regarding the applicability of Rule 46A of the Uniform Rules of Court concerning the execution against residential immovable property. The first applicant, Petrus Johannes Bestbier, along with several others acting as trustees of the Goede Hoop Trust, engaged in litigation with the respondent, Nedbank Limited, after failing to repay debts exceeding R8 million secured by nine mortgage bonds over the Trust's property, Goede Hoop Wine Estate.

The High Court initially granted judgment in favor of Nedbank, declaring the property specially executable after determining that Rule 46A did not apply. The applicants contended that the property served as both a commercial enterprise and a residence for several individuals, including farmworkers, thus engaging Rule 46A protections intended to safeguard the right to housing under the South African Constitution.

After the High Court's ruling, the applicants appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which dismissed the appeal while holding that, although Rule 46A technically applied, the circumstances did not warrant its invocation as the applicants had consented to the judgment. Hence, the decision to declare the property executably was justified.

### I – Issues (Paras [2], [10], [51])
The central legal question revolved around whether Rule 46A should apply to the residential property owned by the Goede Hoop Trust, especially in the context of the rights of the farmworkers residing there and the interpretation of 'residential immovable property' under South African law.

1. **Does Rule 46A apply when the property is owned by a trust, and is used for both residential and commercial purposes?**
2. **Should the beneficiaries and occupiers of the property, who may face potential displacement, be regarded as parties affected by any sale in execution, thereby entitling them to notice under Rule 46A(3)(b)?**
3. **Was the prior consent granted by the applicants to the execution process enforceable, thereby negating the need for judicial oversight under Rule 46A?**

### R – Rules (Paras [18], [68], [70])
The court primarily relied upon **Rule 46A** of the Uniform Rules of Court, which governs execution against residential immovable property. This rule emphasizes the need for judicial oversight to ensure that the constitutional right to adequate housing, safeguarded under **Section 26(1)** of the South African Constitution, is respected during execution procedures.

Section 26(1) states, "Everyone has the right to have access to adequate housing." This principle has been shaped by relevant case law, including **Jaftha v Schoeman** and **Gundwana v Steko Development CC**, which recognize the necessity of ensuring that execution does not lead to homelessness.

Importantly, the court noted that **Practice Directive 33A** is relevant alongside Rule 46A in cases where the property appears to function as the primary residence for the judgment debtor or others.

### A – Application (Paras [10], [41], [68], [81])
The Constitutional Court adopted a broad interpretation of "residential immovable property" to include properties owned by trusts occupied by individuals as their primary residence. The court highlighted that such interpretation aligns with the intent behind Rule 46A, which is to protect those vulnerable to losing their homes due to financial distress.

1. **Ownership versus Occupancy**: The court emphasized that ownership by a trust should not negate the applicability of Rule 46A, especially when natural persons occupy the property, as the purpose of Rule 46A is to safeguard against potential violations of housing rights.

2. **Notification to Affected Parties**: The court further examined the necessity that parties who may be affected by the execution (in this case, the farmworkers) must be notified. The court opined that the interests of these secondary parties fall within the ambit of "any other party who may be affected" by a potential sale.

3. **Judicial Oversight**: While the applicants had consented to the judgment and execution, the court maintained that the circumstances surrounding the property required thorough judicial review to ascertain whether adequate alternatives existed for the judgment debtor and those who live on the property.

### C – Conclusion (Paras [2], [93])
The Constitutional Court concluded that:
1. **Leave to appeal** was granted, but the appeal was dismissed with costs, reflecting the court's affirmation of the interpretation and application of Rule 46A as the norm for disputes involving residential properties linked to trusts.
  
2. The **cross-appeal** from Nedbank was struck from the roll, corroborating that procedural integrity and the importance of upholding housing rights remain paramount in execution matters involving dependent parties like farmworkers.

3. This case sets a significant precedent regarding the applicability of Rule 46A in situations where residential properties are owned by trusts but occupied by individuals. It emphasizes the protection of those who may be indirectly affected by legal proceedings designed to enforce debts secured against their homes, reinforcing a broad interpretation of housing rights under the Constitution.

This ruling underscores the necessity for courts to meticulously evaluate the circumstances of those residing in properties subjected to execution and affirms the continuing relevance of judicial oversight in protecting vulnerable communities against potential evictions and homelessness through execution processes.
