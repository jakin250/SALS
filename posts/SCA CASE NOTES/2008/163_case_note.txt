Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note: Lebowa Platinum Mines Ltd v Viljoen (733/2007) [2008] ZASCA 163

## F - Facts 
This case arises from the interpretation of the term “occupier” defined in the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA) ([1]). The appellant, Lebowa Platinum Mines Ltd, is a registered mining company operating in the platinum mining sector. It holds a permit from the Department of Minerals and Energy to use land on the farm Zeekoegat No 421 KS in the Northern Province for residential purposes, having constructed approximately 160 residential houses to house employees for a nominal rental ([2]).

The respondent, Gerhard Viljoen, commenced employment as an Operations Supervisor with the appellant on 3 August 2004. He was allocated a family home at a nominal rent as part of his employment benefits ([3]). However, following disciplinary proceedings that led to his dismissal on 22 June 2006 for dishonesty, the respondent refused to vacate the premises, citing ongoing review proceedings in the Labour Court concerning his dismissal ([4]). The appellant filed for eviction in the Pretoria High Court under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE), arguing that the respondent had become an unlawful occupier as he no longer had consent to occupy the premises ([5]).

The court transferred the case to the Land Claims Court, where it was determined that the respondent was still an “occupier” under ESTA, as his right to reside was not terminated automatically by his dismissal. Instead, he retained the right to occupy until the resolution of the labour dispute ([6]).

## I - Issues 
The central issue in this appeal was whether the respondent qualified as an “occupier” under the provisions of ESTA post-dismissal, and whether the appellant's claim for his eviction was valid under the circumstances ([6]).

## R - Rules
The legal principles in question revolve around the definitions and conditions of “occupier” as articulated in ESTA. According to section 1 of ESTA, an “occupier” is defined as a person residing on land with the owner’s consent, but excludes individuals earning above a prescribed monthly income of R5,000 ([10]). Section 3(1) stipulates that consent to occupy may only be terminated in accordance with the provisions of section 8, which outlines specific conditions under which an occupier’s residence, particularly if it arises from an employment contract, may be terminated ([11]).

The interpretation of what constitutes an 'occupier' also draws upon the aims of ESTA, which seek to protect vulnerable occupiers from arbitrary evictions, reflecting the broader constitutional principles enshrined in Section 25(6) of the Constitution ([9]), which addresses the insecure tenure of land as a result of past discriminatory practices.

In its ruling, the court referenced the judgment in *Mkangeli v Joubert*, emphasizing that while ESTA primarily protects vulnerable individuals, it also requires courts to interpret its provisions without regard to race or past discrimination ([12]).

## A - Application 
The court analyzed whether the respondent could be classified as an “occupier” after his employment was terminated. The appellant’s argument rested on the claim that the respondent’s right to occupy the premises was strictly conditional on his employment contract and terminated automatically with his dismissal ([7]). However, the court contended that upon his dismissal, the respondent retained the appellant's consent to remain in occupation for an additional 30-day period, following the housing policy of the company ([8]).

This transitional period was critical, as the court found that the respondent's circumstance changed during this time—he became unemployed and did not earn an income that surpassed the R5,000 threshold stipulated under ESTA ([18]). Thus, at the time eviction proceedings were initiated, the respondent's status as an occupier was substantiated under ESTA's criteria, as he occupied the premises with legitimate basis and under the concept of continuing occupancy ([19]).

Furthermore, the court refuted the appellant's literal interpretation of the term, asserting that to deny the respondent's status based on the form of his earlier occupation would yield unreasonable results. The appellant’s own housing policy inadvertently provided the basis for the respondent to claim his right to reside, highlighting a critical aspect of waiver of rights in eviction proceedings ([16], [18]).

## C - Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the respondent was indeed an “occupier” under ESTA at the time the eviction application was made. The court noted that ESTA serves to protect vulnerable individuals from unfair evictions, and while it acknowledged that the respondent may not fit the profile of the impoverished farm worker initially targeted by the legislation, it emphasized the necessity of considering each individual's circumstances carefully ([19]). 

The judgment reinforced the principle that eviction proceedings must respect the framework established by ESTA, and underscored the significance of the 30-day grace period provided by the appellant after dismissal as a crucial factor in determining the respondent’s occupier status. Therefore, the court ultimately concluded that the appellant must follow the proper protocols outlined in ESTA for any further actions concerning the respondent’s occupation of the premises. The appeal was dismissed with costs to the respondent.
