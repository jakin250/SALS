# Case Note: Eyssen v S (746/2007) [2008] ZASCA 97

### F - Facts

The appellant, Shamiel Eyssen, was charged alongside 17 others in the Cape High Court with 75 counts related to various statutory contraventions and common law crimes. The State alleged that Eyssen was the leader of a gang known as the Fancy Boys, which operated primarily in housebreaking and robbery from 2001 to 2003 in Salt River ([1]). He was convicted of multiple offenses including racketeering under sections 2(1)(e) and 2(1)(f) of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998, as well as charges related to robbery and housebreaking, leading to an initial sentence of 20 years' imprisonment for racketeering and aggregated sentences on other counts creating an effective term of 20 years ([2]).

After being denied leave to appeal by the trial judge, permission was later granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal to challenge only the two racketeering convictions ([3]). The State's case heavily relied on the testimony of Mishal Donough, an accomplice who had entered a plea bargain with the State in exchange for his cooperation, raising concerns about the reliability of his testimony due to his motivation to ensure Eyssen's conviction ([12]).

### I - Issues

The primary legal questions before the court were whether Eyssen's actions constituted contraventions of sections 2(1)(e) and 2(1)(f) of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act, which deal with participation in and management of the affairs of an enterprise involved in racketeering activities ([3]).

### R - Rules

The provisions of sections 2(1)(e) and 2(1)(f) of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act are critical to this case. Section 2(1)(e) requires a person to conduct or participate in an enterprise's affairs through a "pattern of racketeering activity," while section 2(1)(f) holds a person liable if they manage an enterprise and know or ought to have known about another’s participation in racketeering ([4]). The statutory definitions of "enterprise" and "pattern of racketeering activity" set out broad parameters for what constitutes involvement in organized crime ([6]).

**Key Precedents:**
1. *United States v Turkette* (452 US 576 (1981)) establishes the necessity for a structure in a criminal organization where the criminal actions must align with the enterprise's purpose ([7]).
2. *United States v Bledsoe* (674 F.2d 647 (1982)) delineates the need for an ascertainable structure within an enterprise as part of proving racketeering [7].

### A - Application

Upon analysis of the evidence presented in the lower court, the Supreme Court found several critical flaws:

1. **Lack of Participation in Affairs**: The court noted that the State failed to establish that Eyssen’s criminal acts were part of the affairs of the Fancy Boys gang. The evidence from Donough was insufficiently corroborated and did not adequately demonstrate that the robberies Eyssen was convicted for were part of a structured criminal enterprise ([14]).

2. **Direct Participation Requirement**: For section 2(1)(e), actual participation in the enterprise's affairs is required. The Appellate Court found that although the acts could fit the crimes committed, it was not shown that these acts were part of an enterprise's operations ([14]).

3. **Management Requirement**: The State's argument that Eyssen managed the gang or its activities did not hold, as Donough's testimony about Eyssen’s role in directing operations was weak and uncorroborated ([15]–[19]).

In light of these findings, the Court ruled that the State's case did not satisfactorily support the conviction on the grounds of racketeering, and therefore, Eyssen's convictions under sections 2(1)(e) and 2(1)(f) were not substantiated by the evidence presented ([21]).

### C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the evidence did not support Eyssen's convictions for racketeering, thus the appeals were successful. The convictions on counts 1 and 2 were set aside, and it was ordered that the sentences for other convictions (counts 4, 29, and 46) would run concurrently with count 5, resulting in an effective sentence of 15 years ([21]). The bringing of this appeal highlighted the importance of stringent evidential standards in convictions related to organized crime, exemplifying the balance that must be maintained between prosecutorial zeal and fair trial provisions. 

This judgment thus serves as not only a corrective measure for Eyssen but also a reaffirmation of the judicial standards required for convictions under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act, emphasizing the necessity of clearly defined enterprises and roles in establishing criminal liability for racketeering activities.
