Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Lourens v Speaker of the National Assembly of Parliament

**Legal Citation:** Lourens v Speaker of the National Assembly of Parliament (20827/2014) [2016] ZASCA 11.

## F – Facts

The appellant, Mr. Cornelius Johannes Alexander Lourens, an Afrikaans-speaking attorney, contended that the practices of the Parliament of South Africa violated his rights by unfairly discriminating against him and other non-English speaking individuals. His grievances were rooted in the belief that legislation is predominantly introduced, published, and assented to in English only, which he claimed violated the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (the "Equality Act"). Mr. Lourens initiated proceedings in the Equality Court, seeking a declaration of conduct amounting to unfair language discrimination due to this failure to publish statutes in all 11 official languages of South Africa, as recognized in Section 6 of the Constitution ([paras 1–3]).

The Equality Court dismissed his application, concluding that while the practice might disadvantage non-English speakers, it did not amount to unfair discrimination. The court also noted the necessity of hearing the appeal due to its significant constitutional implications ([paras 4–5]). 

## I – Issues

The core legal issues revolved around:

1. Whether Parliament's failure to publish all legislation in all 11 official languages constitutes unfair discrimination against non-English speaking individuals under the Equality Act.
2. The proper interpretation of “official language” within the context of government action as provided under Section 6 of the Constitution.
3. Whether Mr. Lourens was entitled to an order compelling Parliament to fulfill an alleged obligation to publish legislation in all official languages ([paras 22–23]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case included:

1. **Section 6 of the Constitution**, which stipulates the official languages of South Africa and obliges the government to take positive measures to elevate the status and promote the use of historically diminished languages ([para 8]).
   
2. **Section 9 of the Constitution**, enshrining the right to equality, which prohibits unfair discrimination on various grounds, including language ([para 23]).
   
3. **Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000**, which defines discrimination including acts or omissions that disadvantage individuals based on prohibited grounds ([paras 24-25]).

4. **Joint Rules of Parliament (Rule 220)**, outlining the procedural language requirements for bills introduced to Parliament, specifying that bills must be available in at least one official language other than the language of introduction ([para 17]).

## A – Application

The court first assessed the nature of "discrimination" under the Equality Act, emphasizing that while it acknowledged the disadvantage faced by non-English speakers, it ruled that not all disadvantages qualify as "unfair" as contemplated under Section 9 of the Constitution and the Equality Act. The legislation’s constitutional compliance – illustrated through the explicit stipulations of Section 6(3) that allow for government action in a minimum of two official languages – further invalidated Lourens's claims ([paras 12–30]).

The court critically evaluated the claims for structural orders requiring Parliament to fulfill language obligations. It stated that such orders could not compel the government to perform tasks that are not legally mandated, reflecting that the duty to translate or publish statutes does not extend universally to all official languages ([paras 31–34]).

Additionally, the court noted that the “equitable treatment” reflected in the Constitution does not necessitate equal treatment, underpinning the argument that resources and practical constraints might prioritize certain official languages over others when executing state functions. The judgment referred to expert testimony indicating a shortage of qualified translators, which informed Parliament’s operational realities ([paras 19, 28]).

## C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the ruling of the Equality Court, concluding that the conduct of Parliament in legislating primarily in English does not amount to unfair discrimination against non-English speakers. The court pointedly noted the practicalities and constitutional interpretations leading to this decision, distancing itself from an imposition of obligations that would exceed legislative and executive powers as presently framed ([paras 31–35]). 

The decision underscores the nuanced balance between linguistic equity and the realities of governance, reiterating the principle that the Constitution does not impose an absolute duty on Parliament to publish all laws in every official language, but rather ensures that all languages are treated equitably under existing frameworks.
