Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Condy Mawela & Another v The State (377/2021) [2022] ZASCA 18

**F – Facts**  

The case arises from a tragic and violent incident that occurred on the evening of 9 May 2017, in Magukubjane village, Limpopo, where community members became enraged over an alleged rape committed by Jackie Mashiyane. This led to a mob gathering, numbering around 100, intent on finding and punishing the accused. Jackie subsequently committed suicide, and in their pursuit of justice, the mob confronted unconnected family members, leading to the deaths of two brothers from the Mashiyane family, Kleinbooi and another brother [1-2].

At trial in the Limpopo Division of the High Court, both appellants, Condy Mawela and Paulos Mathibela, were convicted of murder and other charges. Mawela received a 12-year penalty for murder and five years for malicious injury to property, while Mathibela received a 12-year sentence for murder and respective sentences for other charges, with concurrent time orders [4-5]. 

Witness testimonies for the State were largely drawn from accomplices who had been granted immunity and their credibility became a central issue for the appellate court. The court noted material contradictions among the witnesses, particularly regarding the sequence of events and the degree of involvement attributed to the appellants, ultimately leading to their appeal [8-10].

**I – Issues**  

The principal legal issues under scrutiny by the Supreme Court of Appeal were:
1. Whether the High Court correctly applied the legal principle of *dolus eventualis* in finding the appellants guilty of murder [16].
2. Whether the various State witnesses (s 204 witnesses) were adequately assessed for credibility given their contradictory testimonies and the implications of a conspiracy to falsely implicate the appellants [7].
3. Whether the State had adequately proven the element of common purpose regarding the actions of the mob [24].

**R – Rules**  

1. **Dolus Eventualis**: The legal standard requires the State to prove that the accused foresaw the possibility of death resulting from their actions and accepted this outcome. This was scrutinized in the context of the High Court's reasoning that the appellants ought to have foreseen the fatal outcomes from their actions during the mob assault [16-18].
  
2. **Credibility of Witnesses and Section 204**: Under s 204 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, accomplice evidence must be approached with caution, particularly when there are indications of a conspiracy among witnesses to falsely implicate defendants (Mojapelo v S and other precedents) [14][15].

3. **Common Purpose Doctrine**: Established in *S v Mgedezi* and clarified in *S v Thebus*, it dictates that for a conviction of common purpose, all participants must share a prior agreement to commit the crime [24].

**A – Application**  

The Court meticulously examined the credibility of the State witnesses, noting significant discrepancies. For instance, Ms Mnguni's testimony, which statеd that Mawela attacked the deceased with a golf stick, was contradicted by other witnesses who placed both appellants closer to the car when the violent incident began [10-12]. 

The evidence that the High Court relied upon to establish *dolus eventualis* was found insufficient as it failed to demonstrate that the appellants acted with intent or foresight regarding the fatal blow to Kleinbooi. The State's assertion that a stone thrown from the mob caused the death lacked supportive evidence establishing specific culpability on the part of the appellants [17-19]. 

Furthermore, the appeals court scrutinized the handling of the autopsy report and determined that it had not been adequately considered during the trial, which directly impacted its findings of guilt regarding murder [20]. The contradictions in witness testimony diminished the reliability of the State's case as there was no solid link established between the appellants' actions and the cause of death beyond doubt [16].

Concerning count 3, while Mr Mathibela was implicated in the assault of Mr Masilela, there was sufficient evidence to reduce the charge to common assault, given the absence of grievous bodily harm as defined by law [22].

**C – Conclusion**  

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeals against murder and malicious injury convictions for both appellants, illustrating a firm stance on the necessity for reliable evidence and the danger of overly relying on the testimonies of compromised witnesses. It was concluded that the prosecution had not met the requisite burden of proving guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. 

Thus, the appellants were found not guilty on counts 1 and 5 and discharged, whilst on count 3, Mathibela was convicted of common assault with a sentence wholly suspended for three years [25]. The implications of this judgment emphasize the importance of credible witness testimony and the strict adherence to evidentiary standards in criminal proceedings.
