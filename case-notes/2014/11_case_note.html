Botha and Another v Rich NO and Others [2014] ZACC 11

The Trust subsequently issued summons in the Magistrates’ Court for the cancellation of the contract and for Botha’s eviction.

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 11

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The applicants, Lorraine Sophie Botha and Khululekani Laundry CC, sought leave to appeal against a decision from the Full Court of the Northern Cape High Court, which had dismissed their appeal regarding the cancellation of a property sale agreement with the respondents, trustees of the JJW Hendriks Trust. The dispute centered on an instalment sale agreement dated 19 November 2003 involving the property Erf 4128, De Aar. The purchase price was set at R240,000, payable in monthly instalments. Botha had occupied the property and made payments until October 2007, ultimately having paid over half of the purchase price before defaulting.

The Trust subsequently issued summons in the Magistrates’ Court for the cancellation of the contract and for Botha’s eviction. Although Botha was briefly reinstated to the property by a High Court interdict, the Trust sought to formalize the cancellation after a prolonged period without payment, prompting legal engagements culminating in the contested appeal ([1]-[12]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues for determination included:

  1. Whether the respondents were obliged to register the property transfer in the name of the applicant after payment of over 50% of the purchase price, as per section 27(1) of the Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981.
  2. The constitutionality of enforcing a cancellation clause under circumstances where the applicant had paid more than 50% of the purchase price.
  3. Whether the forfeiture of already paid amounts upon cancellation was an unfair and unconstitutional penalty against public policy ([21]-[24]).

R – Rules

The case predominantly revolved around the interpretation of section 27 of the Alienation of Land Act, which stipulates that a purchaser who has paid not less than 50% of the purchase price has the right to demand transfer, provided that a mortgage is registered to secure the balance. The court previously affirmed principles from the Barkhuizen case regarding the enforceability of cancellation clauses relative to public policy and conspicuous unfairness ([19],[20],[33]).

Moreover, further provisions of common law and statutory interpretations emphasize the necessity of equity, good faith, and reciprocity in contractual relationships, reflecting constitutive values inherent in the South African constitutional framework ([28]-[46]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court analyzed both the objectives of section 27 and the nature of the instalment sale agreement. The court articulated that the statute aims to protect purchasers in precarious positions. By interpreting section 27(1), it was apparent that Botha was entitled to demand the transfer of property upon fulfilling her obligation to pay the discounted purchase price, taking into account any reciprocal obligations to remedy her default in payments ([33], [40]).

The court also concluded that while Botha was in arrears, the rigid application of the principle of reciprocity would result in undue hardship, and any forfeiture or penalty imposed on her should not be disproportionately burdensome, especially with respect to her substantial payments already made. This necessitated a condition that she must remedy her outstanding arrears and municipal charges to enable the transfer of the property into her name ([44]-[49]).

C – Conclusion

The court held that the appeal was warranted, concluding that Botha was indeed entitled to demand the transfer of the property given her substantial compliance with payment obligations. Additionally, enforcing the cancellation clause without restitution for the amounts paid was deemed constitutionally indefensible, equating to an unreasonable and disproportionate reaction to her breach of the agreement. As such, the Constitutional Court set aside the previous orders of the lower courts and mandated the trustees to register the property in Botha’s name, contingent upon payment of arrears and the registration of a mortgage bond. Costs were awarded in favour of the applicants across all levels of court, reflecting the overarching principle that justice must be affixed to fairness and equality among contracting parties ([52]-[53]).

This case illustrates vital tenets of constitutional law intersecting with property rights, emphasizing the critical balance between contractual freedoms and equitable treatment inherent in South Africa’s transformative constitutional landscape.
