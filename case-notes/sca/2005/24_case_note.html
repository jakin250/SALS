Supreme Court of Appeal

Export Harness Supplies (Pty) Ltd v Pasdec Automotive Technologies (Pty) Ltd

Citation: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, Case Number 97/04, delivered on March 29, 2005.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2005

Citation: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, Case Number 97/04, delivered on March 29, 2005.

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The present appeal concerns liquidation proceedings initiated by Pasdec Automotive Technologies (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "the applicant") against Export Harness Supplies (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "the second respondent"). The application was originally lodged in the High Court, Johannesburg, where it was dismissed concerning the second respondent. However, a majority of the full court overturned this decision, granting a final liquidation order against the second respondent (see [1]–[2]).

The primary issue stemmed from the applicant's claim that the second respondent was unable to pay its debts—an assertion pivotal under section 345(1)(c) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973. The original demand for payment had not been addressed to the second respondent but to another entity, leading to the initial dismissal due to lack of compliance with statutory requirements ([5]–[6]).

I – Issues

The legal issues raised in this appeal include:

  1. Whether the applicant established a prima facie case that the second respondent was indeed unable to pay its debts as per section 345 of the Companies Act ([5]–[8]).
  2. Whether the final liquidation order was justifiable based on the alternative ground of it being "just and equitable" to wind up the second respondent ([12]–[14]).
  3. The admissibility of further evidence proffered by the second respondent in the current proceedings [16].

R – Rules

The following legal principles govern the case:

  • According to section 345(1)(a) of the Companies Act, a company can be declared unable to pay its debts if it neglects to pay a sum due for two weeks after a valid demand ([5]).
  • For a final liquidation order, the court requires proof on a balance of probabilities, as outlined in previous cases including Paarwater v South Sahara Investments (Pty) Ltd ([4]).
  • The Plascon-Evans rule states that where factual disputes exist in motion proceedings, a final interdict should only be granted if the applicant's papers and the admitted facts justify such an order ([4]).

A – Application

First, the court assessed whether the applicant had sufficiently demonstrated that the second respondent was unable to pay its debts, given the flawed demand notice. Despite claiming a debt against the second respondent, the applicant's lack of evidence regarding the second respondent's financial status rendered the claim weak ([7]–[11]). The affidavit from the second respondent, particularly from its director, indicated the opposite: that the second respondent was owed money from the applicant ([7]).

Secondly, it was noted that the applicant’s allegations concerning the second respondent's equitable winding up, while potentially grave, lacked sufficient documentation and were contested effectively by the respondent ([13]–[15]). The absence of concrete evidence supporting allegations of fraud and misconduct indicated that a liquidation order on equitable grounds was unfounded.

Lastly, regarding the admissibility of supplementary evidence submitted by the second respondent, the application was dismissed based on previous refusal of the evidence in the lower court, in line with section 22 of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 ([16]).

C – Conclusion

In concluding the judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the High Court, determining that the applicant failed to adequately prove the second respondent's inability to pay its debts as required by law. The deficiencies in the allegations made by the applicant, coupled with the refusal to permit further evidence, led to the dismissal of the liquidation application. The order of the full court was thus set aside, reaffirming that a company in liquidation must be unable to pay its debts as per statutory requirements ([18]).

This case serves as a vital reference point for understanding the boundaries of proving insolvency within South African company law, particularly highlighting the standards required for establishing a case for liquidation.
