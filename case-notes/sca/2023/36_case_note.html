Thomas and Another v Thomas (1223/2021) [2023] ZASCA 36

When the respondent failed to pay costs from this eviction, the appellants sought his sequestration, which was granted on 17 March 2006.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 36

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case revolves around a prolonged family dispute between brothers James William Thomas (the first appellant) and Barend Johannes Thomas (the respondent) concerning property and financial liabilities. The respondent owned two farms, which he sold in 1999 due to financial difficulties. On 13 March 2000, the second appellant, Middelplaas-Suid Landgoed Bpk—co-owned by the first appellant—purchased one of these farms, known as Middelplaats-South. Following this transaction, a series of disputes erupted, leading to multiple litigations, including an eviction action which the appellants won.

When the respondent failed to pay costs from this eviction, the appellants sought his sequestration, which was granted on 17 March 2006. The respondent subsequently underwent rehabilitation on 28 May 2010. In 2018, he applied to the High Court for a declaratory order seeking recognition of his right, title, and interest in a previous action (case number 202/2003) initiated against the appellants, claiming this interest had not formed part of his insolvent estate and asserting that his creditors and trustees had abandoned their claim to it ([1–8]).

The appellants, however, contended that the right became extinguished due to the trustees’ abandonment of the right of action during the insolvency process ([10], [20]). The High Court initially ruled in favor of the respondent, leading the appellants to appeal the decision ([11–12]).

I – Issues

The central legal question in this case was whether the right, title, and interest in the action (the right of action) was extinguished upon the trustees’ abandonment of it, and whether the respondent, after rehabilitation from his insolvent status, could pursue this right in light of the abandonment by the trustees ([17], [20]).

R – Rules

The legal authorities relevant to this case include sections of the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936, which outlines the processes and consequences of sequestration and rehabilitation of insolvent estates. The Act provides that upon sequestration, the estate vests in the Master of the Court and the appointed trustees ([14]). The nature of the right of action, defined under South African law as incorporeal property, is significant in this context, as established by precedents like De Villiers NO v Delta Cables (Pty) Ltd and Stratgro Capital (SA) Ltd v Lombard NO ([14], [15]).

Further, legal scholarship pertinent to waiver and abandonment supports understanding these principles. As per Christie’s Law of Contract and Van Huyssteen et al, waiver of rights could occur unilaterally, although the authority of trustees to abandon a right of action is also critical to the determination of its status ([12–13]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the implications of the trustees' abandonment of the right of action, citing that such abandonment effectively extinguished the right. The court drew on established principles in South African law that indicated when a right of action is abandoned, it ceases to exist. This was seen as analogous to a compromise of a claim, where the right is extinguished ([18]).

The respondent's argument that the abandonment of rights by trustees allows an erstwhile insolvent to pursue the right post-rehabilitation was based on his assertion that the claim remained extant due to lack of monetization and failure of the creditors to lay a competing claim. However, the court clarified that the character of the right of action as movable property, which by law vests with the trustees upon sequestration, diminished any franchise the respondent might hold over the claim after its abandonment by the trustees ([18–20]).

The judicial interpretation of prior cases, such as Van Der Merwe referenced by the respondent, where immovable property was involved, was shown to be distinct due to the tangible nature of the property compared to the incorporeal right of action in this matter ([19]).

C – Conclusion

The appeal was upheld, concluding that the right of action had been extinguished with the trustees' abandonment. The respondent was not entitled to pursue the action, as the trustees possessed the authority to act on behalf of the insolvent estate and their decision to abandon the claim removed any potential claim the respondent might assert after rehabilitation. The High Court’s order in favor of the respondent was set aside, and costs were awarded to the appellants ([22]).

The ruling signifies a crucial clarification on the interplay between insolvency proceedings and the rights of action, emphasizing that rights within an insolvent estate cannot be pursued post-abandonment, thereby reaffirming the legislative intent behind the Insolvency Act ([18–20]).

This case serves as a pivotal reference for future matters involving abandoned rights of action post-sequestration, outlining obligations of trustees and the finality of their decisions on the status of claims within insolvent estates.
