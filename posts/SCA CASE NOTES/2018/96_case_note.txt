# Case Note: Flanagan v Minister of Safety and Security (497/2017) [2018] ZASCA 96

## F - Facts

The case of **Flanagan v Minister of Safety and Security** involves Mr. Earl Flanagan, who was arrested on 10 October 2009 for driving under the influence and related offenses. Following his arrest, he was detained at Mount Road police station, where he was in police custody pending his release on bail ([1–2]). 

Inspector Erasmus, an officer involved in the case, recommended that Flanagan be released on bail due to his stable circumstances, including a permanent residence and employment ([4–5]). However, a failure in communication between police officers resulted in Flanagan remaining in custody over the weekend. During this period, he was sodomised by fellow detainees, an incident he reported only later out of fear ([5-6]).

In response to this traumatic event, Flanagan filed a claim for damages against the Minister of Safety and Security, contending that the police breached their duty to protect him while in custody ([1]). The lower court initially dismissed his claim, asserting that the police acted reasonably under the circumstances ([10–12]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal issues involved in this appeal are:

1. Did the police officers owe a legal duty to ensure the safety of Flanagan while in custody?
2. Was there a breach of this duty due to the negligence of the police, particularly regarding their failure to release Flanagan on bail and keep him separated from violent offenders? ([6–7], [16]).

## R - Rules

The applicable laws and principles include:

1. **Delictual Liability Standards**: As per **Kruger v Coetzee** 1966 (2) SA 428 (A), the test for negligence involves three questions: foreseeability of harm, the reasonable measures that should have been taken to avoid that harm, and whether those measures were indeed taken ([16]).
2. **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA)**, Section 59 outlines the authority of police officers to grant bail in non-violent cases ([17]).
3. **Standing Order** 361, which mandates specific protocols for the treatment of detainees, especially separating those accused of violent crimes from other detainees ([21]).

## A - Application

In assessing the negligence claim, the court determined that the police failed to fulfill their duties in two crucial respects:

1. **Failure to Release on Bail**: The court noted that Flanagan was eligible for bail under the CPA. Given the recommendation for his release by Inspector Erasmus, the police should have acted to release him ([18–19]). The lack of follow-through on this recommendation was deemed a significant oversight that directly contributed to Flanagan's prolonged detention and subsequent victimization ([20–21]).

2. **Failure to Separate Detainees**: The court recognized the police's obligation under the Standing Order to ensure that Flanagan was detained separately from individuals charged with violent crimes. This separation was both warranted and necessary to avert foreseeable risks to Flanagan’s safety ([22–25]).

In light of the testimony and evidence presented, including the lack of cell inspections and inappropriate handling of detainees, the appellant's claims regarding police negligence were substantiated ([26]). The court addressed the causational link between the police's negligence and the harm suffered by Flanagan, asserting that the police's omissions contributed directly to the conditions that allowed the assault to occur ([27–31]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that Flanagan had established all elements of a delictual claim against the Minister. The failure to release him on bail, combined with the failure to ensure appropriate detainee management, demonstrated a lack of reasonable care that resulted in foreseeable harm to Flanagan. The lower court's ruling was thus overturned, and the appellant was awarded damages amounting to R200,000, plus interest and costs of the suit ([39]).

This decision underscores the responsibilities of law enforcement in ensuring the safety and rights of individuals in their custody, reflecting broader implications for police conduct and constitutional obligations regarding the treatment of detainees within South Africa ([18], [22]).
