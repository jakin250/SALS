Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: Member of the Executive Council for Health, KwaZulu-Natal v Premier of KwaZulu-Natal

## F – Facts
This case arises from an appeal application by the Member of the Executive Council for Health (hereinafter “MEC”) in KwaZulu-Natal (KZN), who was previously a respondent in the Treatment Action Campaign (TAC) case before the High Court ([1]). The prominent dispute revolves around representations made on behalf of the province concerning a matter where the MEC initially contested the application for leave to appeal but faced opposition from the Premier, who intervened just before the hearing ([2]–[4]). 

The Premier, also represented by a distinct legal team, declared that he wished to pursue a different approach by supporting the TAC’s position while opposing the MEC ([4]). The High Court subsequently ruled that the Premier’s involvement was valid, resulting in the substitution of the MEC for the Premier as the representative respondent for KZN government interests in the TAC dispute ([5]–[6]). Thus, the MEC sought leave to appeal this High Court decision directly to the Constitutional Court ([6]).

## I – Issues
The legal questions at the forefront of this case include:
1. Is the MEC entitled to appeal the High Court's decision granting the Premier the right to represent the interests of the KZN government in the TAC case?
2. Does the court possess the power to directly appoint representatives in government matters?
3. Is the nature of this dispute justiciable, considering it is primarily political in nature? ([7]–[9]).

## R – Rules
The court's decision on whether to grant leave to appeal is guided by several statutory provisions and principles:
1. The governing framework of cooperative governance laid out in Chapter 3 of the Constitution, particularly Section 41(1)(h), which mandates cooperativeness among state organs ([9]).
2. The criteria established under Section 167(6)(b) of the Constitution regarding the interests of justice as a standard for granting direct appeals to the Constitutional Court ([6]).
3. Relevant precedents, particularly the ruling in _National Gambling Board v Premier KwaZulu-Natal and Others_, which articulated the importance of compliance with cooperative governance obligations ([9]–[10]).

## A – Application
The court emphasized that the primary conflict was a political one, indicating that political disputes should ideally be resolved outside the courtroom. It highlighted the apparent lack of justiciability in the conflict, as neither the MEC nor the Premier had complied with the requirements for cooperative governance ([7]–[9]). The court expressed concern over the practicality of the relief sought by the MEC, noting that even if successful, this would have no substantive impact on the outcomes of the TAC case, as both the MEC and Premier would remain bound by the High Court ruling ([10]–[11]). 

Furthermore, the failure to resolve these matters politically, along with the lack of justiciability and the mutual obligations of cooperative governance, led the court to conclude that it was not in the interests of justice to grant the requested leave ([12]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed both the MEC's application for leave to appeal and the Premier’s request to present further evidence, reaffirming that the matter was inappropriate for judicial resolution and should have been handled politically ([14]). The court clarified that the public interests aligned with cooperative governance practices, and without compliance with these principles, it would refrain from intervening in such disputes. The ruling signifies the court's commitment to prioritizing cooperative engagement among state officials before resorting to judicial intervention, emphasizing the inherent political nature of the conflict.

This ruling serves to reinforce the obligations of governmental entities to cooperate, thereby discouraging the judicial pursuit of essentially political disputes. The dismissal has implications for how similar matters may be approached in the future, signaling a preference for political resolution over judicial intervention in intra-governmental conflicts.
