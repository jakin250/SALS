Kimberley Jeanne Kett v Afro Ventures (Pty) Ltd and Christopher Leigh Kemp

On 30 August 1996, the Supreme Court of South Africa heard the appeal in the case of Kimberley Jeanne Kett (Appellant) versus Afro Ventures (Pty) Ltd (First Respondent) and Christopher Leigh Kemp (Second Respondent) ([paras 1-2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 1996

F – Facts

On 30 August 1996, the Supreme Court of South Africa heard the appeal in the case of Kimberley Jeanne Kett (Appellant) versus Afro Ventures (Pty) Ltd (First Respondent) and Christopher Leigh Kemp (Second Respondent) ([paras 1-2]). The appellant, a Swiss resident, sustained injuries in Botswana when a Land Rover truck, in which she was a passenger, overturned. The vehicle was driven by the second respondent, Kemp, who was employed by the first respondent, a tour operator in Randburg organizing safaris to the Okavango swamps ([para 2]).

The contract for the trip, which included a waiver of rights and an exemption from liability clause, was entered into by the appellant's husband, who acted on both his own and the appellant's behalf ([paras 1-2]). Following the accident, the appellant initiated a lawsuit against both respondents for damages in delict, alleging negligence on the part of Kemp. In their defense, the respondents filed a special plea, emphasizing the waiver clause, alongside a conditional third-party notice seeking indemnification from the appellant’s husband ([para 3]).

The plaintiff challenged the validity of the special plea, asserting it lacked necessary averments regarding the waiver clause’s applicability to actions for damages stemming from negligent driving. The plaintiff sought a court order dismissing the special plea with costs. Eloff JP of the Witwatersrand Local Division dismissed the exception, deeming the exemption clause clear and unambiguous, thereby allowing the respondents to invoke it ([paras 3-4]).

Later, the court was requested to deliberate on the appealability of the dismissal, which led to an acknowledgment by both parties that the order was not appealable, and as such, the appeal was struck off the roll. However, the court reserved judgment on the issue of costs ([para 4]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues in this case included:

  1. Appealability of the Order: Whether the order dismissing the exception based on the special plea constituted an appealable judgment or order under section 20 of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 ([paras 4-5]).
  2. Liability for Costs: The determination of which party should bear the costs associated with the abortive appeal, particularly focusing on the procedural responsibilities and the factors affecting the court’s discretion in adjudicating costs ([paras 6-9]).

R – Rules

The court relied on established legal principles regarding appealability and costs, including:

  1. Appealability Standards: The dismissals of exceptions based on contract interpretation are generally reconsiderable by the trial court, thus not constituting a final judgment that could be appealed ([paras 4-5]). Relevant case law such as Blaauwbosch Diamonds Ltd v Union Government (Minister of Finance) 1915 AD 599 and Wellington Court Shareblock v Johannesburg City Council 1995 (3) SA 827 (A) was cited to illustrate that orders' appealability depends on whether they represent the final say in a matter ([para 4]).
  2. Discretionary Costs Principles: The court noted that costs are typically awarded against the losing party, but exceptional circumstances warrant discretionary considerations ([paras 6-9]). Previous cases, such as Stevenson v MacIver 1922 AD 413, and principles of "dominus litis" were discussed to underscore the plaintiff’s responsibility in initiating the proceedings and the resulting costs ([para 6]).

A – Application

Upon evaluating the appeal, the court found:

  1. On Appealability: The unanimous agreement between both counsels regarding the lack of appealability demonstrated the futility of the appeal. The court cited that no final resolution on the merits had been reached, and thus the special plea's dismissal could be revisited by the trial court, establishing that the appellate jurisdiction was not invoked correctly ([paras 4-5]).
  2. On Costs: The appellant, being the initiator of the proceedings, was primarily responsible for the costs incurred due to the abortive appeal. However, the court acknowledged the shared culpability of both parties' legal counsel for failing to recognize and address the non-appealability of the order earlier in the proceedings. Consequently, while the appellant would be liable for the majority of the costs, the court decided that the respondents should bear a portion (one-third) of the wasted costs due to their oversight ([paras 6-9]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court concluded that the appeal was struck off the roll due to lack of appealability, as the dismissal of an exception based on contract interpretation was not a final judgement ([paras 4-5]). The court ordered that the lower court's order granting leave to appeal, along with its order on costs, be set aside. The appellant was instructed to pay the costs related to the application for leave to appeal, along with two-thirds of the respondents' wasted costs arising from the appeal proceedings ([paras 9-10]).

This case solidified the need for legal practitioners to remain cognizant of procedural nuances regarding appealability and the allocation of costs in civil actions. The court’s decision exemplified its discretionary authority to ensure that costs were allocated in a manner that reflected the responsibilities of both parties.
