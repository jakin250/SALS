Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2000

# FIRAC Case Note: Dormehl v Minister of Justice [2000] CCT 10/2000

## F – Facts ([1]–[3])

In **Dormehl v Minister of Justice**, the applicant, Johan Georg Dormehl, sought direct access to the Constitutional Court of South Africa under Rule 17. The application was not in the required format of a notice of motion as specified by Rule 17(1) and was presented in a disorganized manner comprising a mix of arguments and factual assertions ([1]). Despite these procedural imperfections, the court chose to consider the application on its merits.

The applicant's primary claims were multifaceted: he contended that several rules and practices related to the administration of justice were unconstitutional. Specifically, he challenged the requirement for direct access to the Constitutional Court to be approved by the Court, the necessity for legal representation for corporations in court, the stipulation of security for costs, and the need for leave to appeal to higher courts ([2]). Additionally, he sought a mandamus against the fourth respondent, a High Court judge, demanding a formal written judgment following an oral ruling made two years prior, which had never been finalized with a written judgment or the judge’s signature ([3]).

## I – Issues ([4]–[9])

The case principally revolves around the following constitutional issues:
1. Whether Rule 17 of the Constitutional Court’s rules is unconstitutional for infringing upon the right to access the courts under section 34 of the Constitution.
2. Whether Rule 7(1), which restricts corporate representation to qualified attorneys, is constitutional.
3. The constitutionality of the security for costs requirement as it pertains to litigation.
4. The legitimacy of the requirement for a party to obtain leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([4]-[9]).

## R – Rules ([4]–[9])

The Constitution of South Africa outlines the requirements for direct access to the Constitutional Court under section 167(6). This provision allows the court to impose conditions, including obtaining leave for direct access, which is further detailed in Rule 17 ([4]). The court emphasized that section 34 grants no absolute right for direct access to any court but ensures access to a competent court for relief.

Additionally, the Court confirmed that the stipulation for corporations to be represented by a legal practitioner with a right of audience is permissible and serves the public interest in ensuring competent representation ([6]). Regarding security for costs, the court indicated that various existing legal provisions governing this issue remain reflective of the law’s demands for accountability and fairness in litigation ([8]). Lastly, the constitutionality of requiring leave to appeal was addressed as sanctioned by both the Constitution and previous judgments, affirming that such requirements are essential for judicial efficiency and substantive justice ([9]).

## A – Application ([5]–[10])

In its analysis, the Court rejected the applicant's claim that the requirement for obtaining leave under Rule 17 infringes upon the rights set out in section 34. The Court noted that the Constitution allows for such procedural rules to ensure proper management of cases, recognizing that it is in the interests of justice for cases to not be directly presented to the highest court without review by lower courts ([4]).

The Court also noted the importance of legal representation for corporations. Since case law, including judgments predating the new Constitution, established the necessity for qualified attorneys to ensure effective representation, the court found that challenging such a rule without direct relevance to a specific legal dispute was misplaced ([6]-[7]).

The applicant's blanket challenge to the security for costs provisions was dismissed for its vagueness and lack of connection to his interests as a litigant. The Court held that any grievance regarding security for costs must be anchored in practical litigation contexts rather than theoretical assertions ([8]). Similarly, the requirement for obtaining leave to appeal was underscored as crucial for maintaining order and efficiency within the judicial process, thereby reinforcing the Court's earlier holdings regarding this procedural necessity ([9]).

As for the mandamus seeking a written judgment from the High Court judge, the Court pointed out the procedural bar against initiating proceedings against a judge without consent from the court in which the judge serves. The Court underscored that the applicant did not demonstrate a compelling reason for the late request for a revised judgment ([10]).

## C – Conclusion ([11])

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court ruled against the applicant's application for direct access. The Court found that none of the constitutional challenges brought forth by Dormehl met the established criteria for granting such access. This decision affirmed the procedural integrity outlined in the Court’s rules, underscoring the importance of hierarchical judicial processes and the necessity of legal representation in court proceedings.

By rejecting the application, the Court not only preserved the procedural safeguards embedded in the rules but also emphasized the necessity of legal order and the importance of concrete disputes in upholding constitutional principles. The ruling serves as a significant reinforcement of the procedural requirements mandated by the Constitution, reinforcing the legitimate boundaries of judicial access in South Africa ([11]).
