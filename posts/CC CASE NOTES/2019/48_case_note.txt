Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Tshabalala v The State; Ntuli v The State [2019] ZACC 48

**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa

**Coram:** Mogoeng CJ, Froneman J, Jafta J, Khampepe J, Madlanga J, Mathopo AJ, Mhlantla J, Theron J, and Victor AJ

---

## F - Facts

On 20 September 1998, a violent incident occurred in the Umthambeka section of Tembisa, Gauteng, involving a group of young men who invaded multiple homes. They ransacked, looted, assaulted, and repeatedly raped eight female occupants, with the youngest being just 14 years old. The group surrounded houses, creating chaos by impersonating police, and utilized one or more individuals as lookouts while others committed the rapes. [1-6]. 

The geological and procedural history shows that the accused, Jabulane Alpheus Tshabalala and Annanius Ntuli, were charged alongside others shortly after the incident. Initially convicted of multiple counts of rape, the application of the common purpose doctrine under common law was pivotal to their conviction, leading to significant sentences. [7-12]. 

The High Court's conviction was subsequently appealed, with differing opinions regarding the applicability of the common purpose doctrine to the crime of rape. The Supreme Court of Appeal previously overturned a co-accused's conviction on similar grounds, causing the current applicants to articulate their cases anew in front of the Constitutional Court [13-17]. 

---

## I - Issues

The case raised several pertinent issues:
1. **Applicability of Common Purpose to Rape:** Whether the doctrine of common purpose can encompasses the common law crime of rape.
2. **Rational Basis for Distinction:** Whether there is any rational basis for distinguishing the crime of rape from other crimes where the doctrine has been held applicable. Both applicants posited that the nature of rape as an instrumentality offense prohibits accountability through the doctrine when no actual penetration occurred by a co-accused [2-3, 18-19].

---

## R - Rules

The Constitutional Court relied upon various precedents and legal principles:
- **Common Law Doctrine of Common Purpose:** This doctrine holds that individuals involved in a joint unlawful venture can be collectively held responsible for offenses committed by one of their number in furtherance of that venture [46].
- **Instrumentality Argument:** The applicants relied heavily on legal doctrine asserting that rape uniquely requires the physical act of penetration without any implication of shared liability [53].
- **International Standards on Gender-Based Violence:** Reference was made to international criminal law principles relating to sexual violence—highlighting how modern legal frameworks have increasingly recognized the culpability of accomplices in joint enterprises [40-41]. 

In South African law, the applicability of the common purpose doctrine is assessed based on the existence of a prior agreement to commit the crime, with each participant’s conduct imputed to others where such an agreement is established [48-49].

---

## A - Application

The Court analyzed various angles throughout the hearing. Regarding the initial issue of common purpose, applicants presented their argument, emphasizing the inability of the doctrine to apply since penetration inherently involves the physical body of the offender [33-37]. The argument was deemed as perpetuating gender inequality and an outdated patriarchal view of sexual violence [53-54]. 

Contrarily, the Court underscored that the prosecution presented a compelling case showing a "cohesive whole" acted with a prior agreement to commit multiple acts of violence, including rape [50-54]. It further noted that the criminality of the actions was not solely confined to penetration but viewed through the collective impact on victims’ dignity and bodily integrity over the long term [56-59]. 

The Court also considered international legal principles and comparisons, noting that a failure to apply the common purpose doctrine would perpetuate outcomes that are incongruent with the ethos of women's rights embedded in the South African Constitution and international law [93-99].

---

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the common purpose doctrine is indeed applicable to the common law crime of rape, affirming the High Court’s conviction of Tshabalala and Ntuli. The judgment reinforced the principle that collective criminal conduct against victims utilizing gender and power dynamics cannot be abrogated simply due to the nature of the offense, thus upholding the convictions under the established doctrine of common purpose.[65-67].

This ruling has significant implications for South African jurisprudence, restoring certainty relating to liability for joint criminal enterprises, particularly in cases of gender-based violence. The Court's decision not only advances protective measures for victims but also seeks to counter the pervasive culture of impunity for perpetrators by ensuring accountability irrespective of individual roles defined by traditional interpretations of sex crimes.
