Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 188 Coram: Mpati P and Mhlantla, Pillay, Sw

Case Title: The South African Music Performance Rights Association v Foschini Retail Group (Pty) Ltd and 9 Others
Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Neutral Citation: SAMPRA v Foschini Retail Group (Pty) Ltd (50/2015) [2015] ZASCA 188
Coram: Mpati P and Mhlantla, Pillay, Swain, and Zondi JJA
Heard: 17 November 2015
Delivered: 30 November 2015

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])

The case involves an appeal concerning the royalty amount payable under section 9A of the Copyright Act 98 of 1978, as the retail group (respondents) and the South African Music Performance Rights Association (SAMPRA, appellant) could not reach a mutual agreement regarding the tariff for playing sound recordings in retail stores ([para 1]). The dispute was escalated to the Copyright Tribunal due to the absence of a consensual resolution ([para 3]).

SAMPRA, representing the rights of recording companies and sound owners, contended that the tariffs set by them reflected a reasonable charge, while the retailers claimed the proposed amounts were unreasonable and sought a determination via the Tribunal. The Tribunal, presided over by Phatudi J, set a tariff which was less than that proposed by SAMPRA but greater than that suggested by the retailers. SAMPRA sought leave to appeal this decision.

The principal issues for the appellate court revolved around the jurisdiction of the Tribunal to establish a reasonable tariff, the onus of proof regarding tariff reasonableness, and the adequacy of the evidence provided by the retailers ([paras 5–6]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 7–10])

The court was tasked with resolving several interrelated legal questions, including:

  1. Jurisdiction of the Tribunal: Did the Tribunal's jurisdiction arise simply from the inability to reach an agreement on the tariff, or did it require the retailers to demonstrate that the SAMPRA tariff was unreasonable ([para 11(a)])?
  2. Onus of Proof: Was the burden of proof on the retailers to establish the unreasonableness of the tariff, or should it simply be a matter of presenting evidence for the Tribunal's consideration to deem the claim well-founded ([para 11(b)])?
  3. Sufficiency of Evidence: Did the Tribunal have adequate evidence to conclude that the retailers' claims regarding the unreasonableness of the tariff were well-founded, and if not, should the matter be remitted for further inquiry ([para 11(c)])?
  4. Determining Reasonableness of the Tariff: If evidence was sufficient, was the tariff set by the Tribunal reasonable under the prevailing circumstances ([para 11(d)])?

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 12–17])

The court referenced critical provisions within the Copyright Act that govern the licensing and royalty determination procedures:

  • Section 9A(1)(a) explicitly prohibits broadcasting protected works without payment unless agreed otherwise ([para 14]).
  • Sections 30 and 33 clarify the Tribunal's jurisdiction and application processes regarding disputes over unagreed licensing terms, reinforcing that Tribunal determinations must be "reasonable in the circumstances" ([paras 15–16]).
  • The National Association of Broadcasters v SAMPRA case provided a pertinent precedent regarding the statutory interpretation necessary for the Tribunal to exercise its powers effectively ([para 12]).

Moreover, the court discussed the inductive requirement that any applicant's claims be grounded in sufficient factual evidence for the Tribunal to act upon in determining the reasonableness of tariffs ([para 24]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 18–51])

Jurisdiction of the Tribunal

The court determined that the Tribunal's jurisdiction was established simply by the lack of a mutual agreement, reflecting that the formal onus of proving the unreasonableness of the tariff did not rest solely on the retailers. The Tribunal was obliged to consider the evidence presented comprehensively to assess if the claim was well-founded ([paras 18–22]).

Assessment of Evidence

In evaluating the underlying evidence, the court scrutinized testimonies surrounding the economic value derived from music played in stores. The court observed that the lack of existing studies quantifying this value rendered SAMPRA’s claims inadequate. Experts pointed out the impracticalities of obtaining precise figures for such values and acknowledged the speculative nature of estimations of economic benefit ([paras 31–36]).

Additionally, the retailers successfully advanced arguments referring to international benchmarking practices, demonstrating that comparisons with similar jurisdictions (like Australia) revealed substantial inequity in SAMPRA’s proposed tariffs - further backing claims that testified against the reasonableness of the tariff initially set by SAMPRA ([paras 38–46]).

Determining Reasonableness of Tariff

The Tribunal's significant reductions from SAMPRA’s tariffs were deemed arbitrary, lacking coherent justification, particularly concerning fixed rates for larger premises without escalating charges relative to size ([para 53]). A recalibrated approach was proposed, blending elements of benchmarking from foreign jurisdictions alongside local variables ([para 51]).

The court ultimately favoured the retailers' proposed tariffs, guided by the evidence elucidated regarding fair comparison and necessity, which evidently exceeded the SAMPRA tariff without accordance to prevailing economic realities ([paras 42–51]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 56])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded by adjusting the order of the Copyright Tribunal, setting a new reasonable tariff structure that balanced retail needs against fair compensation for music rights holders, effective from 1 January 2008 ([para 56]).

  1. The original tariff proposed by SAMPRA was set aside due to lack of evidence supporting its reasonableness and high comparative to international standards.
  2. The appellate nature of the resolution underscores the importance of adequately substantiated economic claims in adjudicating copyright disputes, promoting a balance between creative rights compensation and commercial viability in the retail sector.
  3. The decision signifies a clarion call for clearer and more reasonable benchmarks in assessing artist royalties across territories while fostering dialogues between copyright collecting societies and users - ultimately advancing the interests of all stakeholders engaged in the music industry.
