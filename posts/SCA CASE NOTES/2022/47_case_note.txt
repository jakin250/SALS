Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Johannes G Coetzee & Seun and Another v Le Roux and Another (969/2020) [2022] ZASCA 47

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
[1] This appeal concerns the doctrine of extinctive prescription, particularly whether a creditor must be fully aware of its rights before prescription commences. The suit originated from a dispute between the plaintiffs, Pieter Paul le Roux and Johanna Catharina le Roux (the respondents), and the defendants, Johannes G Coetzee & Seun and Daniel Cornelius Coetzee (the appellants), who were formerly the respondents’ attorneys.

[2] The plaintiffs claimed damages for alleged negligence by the defendants regarding a mandate to exercise an option to purchase a farm in Calvinia from the deceased, Mr. Jan Harmse Steyn. Unknown to the plaintiffs, the deceased had sold the property to a third party, Mr. Paul Nel, prior to the plaintiffs exercising their option. The transfer of property occurred shortly after the deceased’s death on September 13, 2003.

[3] The plaintiffs issued summons against Mr. Nel and the estate’s attorney on October 14, 2004, but their action was dismissed due to the non-compliance with Section 2(1) of the Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981. The plaintiffs subsequently sought to hold the defendants accountable for damages resulting from their alleged failure to properly manage the mandate. A special plea of prescription was raised by the defendants, asserting that the plaintiffs’ claim had prescribed.

[4] The High Court initially found in favor of the plaintiffs, determining they only became aware of the facts giving rise to their debt in November 2007. The defendants appealed against this judgment, leading to the case reaching the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA).

### I – Issues
[5] The primary legal question at hand was whether the plaintiffs had the requisite knowledge of the facts from which their claim arose, such that the prescription period began running before their claim was initiated. Specifically, did the plaintiffs' awareness or lack thereof regarding the legal implications of the defendants' breach affect the commencement of the prescription period under Section 12(3) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969?

### R – Rules
[6] The relevant statutory provision is Section 12(3) of the Prescription Act, which states that a debt is only deemed due when the creditor has knowledge of the debtor's identity and the facts from which the debt arises. The section differentiates between factual knowledge and legal knowledge, clarifying that the latter is not necessary for the running of prescription.

[7] Key case law principles were considered, including:
- **Mtokonya v Minister of Police**, which clarified that knowledge of legal conclusions is not required to establish that the prescription period has commenced.
- **Minister of Finance and Others v Gore NO**, affirming that prescription begins when a creditor has the necessary factual knowledge to initiate a claim, not when the debtor's full legal rights are understood.
- **Yellow Star Properties 1020 (Pty) Ltd v MEC**, reiterating that a claimant cannot delay the commencement of prescription by awaiting legal certainty concerning their rights.

### A – Application
[8] In applying the law to the facts, the SCA evaluated the plaintiffs' claim based on the timeline established. The court found that the plaintiffs had the requisite knowledge of essential facts as of September 26, 2003, when they authorized Mr. Coetzee to exercise the option to purchase. Their knowledge of the detrimental situation occurred when the option lapsed on November 13, 2003. This deemed the claim complete.

[9] Furthermore, the court examined the plaintiffs' assertion that they only became aware of their claim's specifics during cross-examination in 2007. This assertion was found insufficient because the legal conclusion regarding the option's invalidity due to non-compliance with Section 2(1) was not necessary for the running of prescription.

[10] The court emphasized that the plaintiffs’ awareness of the legal implications, or lack thereof, could not impede the start of the prescription period. Thus, prescription commenced before the plaintiffs filed their summons in October 2009, and the claim was deemed prescribed.

### C – Conclusion
[11] The SCA upheld the appeal, overturning the High Court's judgment. The court declared that the plaintiffs’ action was dismissed on the grounds of prescription, rendering their claim unenforceable as it was deemed to have prescribed prior to the issuance of summons.

[12] The case reinforces the principle that knowledge of factual circumstances, rather than legal consequences, dictates the onset of the prescription period. Should creditors be unaware of potential claims stemming from a factual situation—such as a breach of contract—this ignorance does not prevent prescription from commencing in accordance with Section 12(3) of the Prescription Act. 

This case is pivotal for practitioners as it delineates the threshold of awareness required for claims subject to prescription, providing clarity on the separation of factual and legal knowledge within the framework of South African law.
