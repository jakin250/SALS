Savoi and Others v National Prosecuting Authority and Another [2023] ZACC 38

In anticipation of these proceedings, the applicants alleged that 69 documents were improperly seized by state authorities, claiming that this seizure violated their legal professional privilege.

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 38

F - Facts

The applicants, Gaston Savoi, Intaka Holdings (Pty) Limited, and Fernando Praderi, are engaged in ongoing criminal proceedings before the High Court of South Africa, KwaZulu-Natal Division, Pietermaritzburg. They face charges including bribery, racketeering, money laundering, fraud, and corruption related to the provisioning of water purification plants to provincial health departments in KwaZulu-Natal and the Northern Cape ([paras 3–4]).

In anticipation of these proceedings, the applicants alleged that 69 documents were improperly seized by state authorities, claiming that this seizure violated their legal professional privilege. Consequently, they sought an interlocutory order to have the contested documents reviewed in camera (privately) under Section 32 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, arguing that the disclosures would tarnish the administration of justice and compromise their right to a fair trial ([paras 4–6]).

The High Court denied their request, maintaining that the applicants had not sufficiently proven the privilege of the documents. This decision was subsequently appealed, with leave sought from both the Full Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal, both of which denied their applications ([paras 5–7]).

I - Issues

The core issues presented before the Constitutional Court included:

  1. Whether the applicants must establish the privileged status of the documents before a deviation from the principle of open justice could be justified ([para 9]).
  2. The implications of the Nkosi AJ order on the authority of the Pietermaritzburg High Court to make determinations regarding the documents ([paras 12–16]).

These issues traversed the interplay between the right to legal professional privilege and the constitutional demand for open justice, raising broader questions about judicial process and the rights of accused parties ([para 8]).

R - Rules

The following legal authorities and precedents were significant in the court’s deliberation:

  1. Constitutional Framework: Section 34 of the Constitution guarantees the right to a fair public hearing, while the principle of open justice emphasizes transparency in judicial proceedings ([para 1]).
  2. Legal Professional Privilege: Recognized in jurisprudence as fundamental to fostering trust in legal counsel, it is critical to upholding fair trial rights as per Section 35 of the Constitution ([para 19]).
  3. Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013: Section 32 stipulates that all proceedings in Superior Courts must be open unless a special case is demonstrated ([paras 1–2]).
  4. Case Law:
  • Thint (Pty) Ltd v National Director of Public Prosecutions: This case provided a precedent for considering the balance between open justice and privilege ([para 19]).
  • South African Broadcasting Corporation Ltd v National Director of Public Prosecutions: This judgment underscored the need for public confidence in judicial processes and highlighted the importance of open hearings ([para 1]).

A - Application

The Constitutional Court, in addressing the issues presented, considered the nature of legal professional privilege and its role within a fair judicial process. The Court found that while the principle of open justice generally mandates public hearings, exceptions could be justified when essential interests, such as legal professional privilege, were at stake ([paras 19–26]).

  1. Establishing Privilege: The Court ruled that determining whether documents were privileged should not require strict prior establishment of their status at the interlocutory stage, as this could jeopardize the applicants' overarching claims for a permanent stay of prosecution ([paras 10–19]).
  2. In Camera Procedure: The Court endorsed an in-camera review process as a suitable mechanism for assessing the contested documents without compromising the principle of open justice. This included provisions for confidentiality, ensuring that while the documents could be subject to private review, their status could eventually transition to public disclosure if found non-privileged ([paras 24–26]).
  3. Nkosi AJ Order: The Court found that the Nkosi AJ order, which had implications for the access rights to certain contested documents, did not preclude the Pietermaritzburg High Court from establishing the procedural framework necessary to ensure that the privilege claim was examined adequately ([para 27]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court unanimously granted the applicants' appeal, setting aside the High Court's order and endorsing the establishment of an in-camera review process for the contested documents as requested by the applicants. The judgment emphasized the necessity of balancing legal professional privilege rights against the principle of open justice, underscoring that the latter could be deviated from to accommodate essential fairness in judicial proceedings ([paras 28]).

The ruling bears significant implications for future cases involving legal professional privilege, asserting that a considered procedural approach could safeguard rights to fair trial while still respecting the open administration of justice. The Court’s decision thus not only provides clarity on the matter at hand but reinforces the importance of maintaining judicial integrity in complex litigation contexts.
