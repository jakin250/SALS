Case Title: The Director of Public Prosecutions: Gauteng Division, Pretoria v Portia Thulisile Tsotetsi

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 (170/2017) [2017] ZASCA 083 (02 June 2017)

Case Title: The Director of Public Prosecutions: Gauteng Division, Pretoria v Portia Thulisile Tsotetsi

Neutral Citation: (170/2017) [2017] ZASCA 083 (02 June 2017)

Bench:

Leach, Saldulker, Zondi and Mathopo JJA and Coppin AJA

F – Facts

The appeal originated from the Gauteng Division of the High Court where Portia Thulisile Tsotetsi (the respondent) was convicted for the premeditated murders of her husband, Mr. Nzimeni Bednock Sithatu, on 21 February 2012 (count 1), and Mr. Dumisani David Ngubeni, on 16 May 2012 (count 2) ([1]–[2]). The respondent was arrested following interventions prompted by a neighbor, Mr. Bongani Nhlapo, who witnessed the latter murder and subsequently reported the incidents to the police ([10]–[11]).

Initially, the trial court sentenced the respondent to 20 years' imprisonment for each count, to run concurrently, citing the existence of "substantial and compelling circumstances" to justify a lesser sentence than the prescribed minimum of life imprisonment, which applies under Section 51 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([3]).

The DPP contended that the trial court erred in its findings regarding these circumstances, arguing that it failed to adequately consider the premeditated nature of the crimes and the aggravating factors surrounding the murders, particularly the respondent’s active involvement in planning both killings ([4]–[5]). The respondent, maintaining her innocence throughout, attempted to portray herself as a victim in challenging personal circumstances that shaped her actions ([12–16]).

I – Issues

The primary issue for the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the trial court had erred in finding substantial and compelling circumstances that justified imposing a lesser sentence than the minimum of life imprisonment for both counts of murder ([1]).

Sub-Issues Include:

  1. The adequacy of the trial court's consideration of aggravating factors surrounding the murders ([19]);
  2. The misinterpretation of the respondent's motives for the murders and the absence of remorse ([22][25]);
  3. Whether the cumulative effect of the respondent’s background constituted a proper basis for deviating from the minimum sentence threshold established by law ([30][33]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing the imposition of sentences for serious crimes, particularly murder, is situated within the framework of the Criminal Law Amendment Act, 105 of 1997, which mandates life imprisonment as the minimum penalty for certain serious offenses unless substantial and compelling circumstances necessitate a deviation ([3][27]).

The rules applicable to sentencing were framed in prior judgments including S v Malgas [2001] ZASCA 19, which holds that such circumstances must be truly compelling—a determination that courts should approach cautiously and with due consideration of the societal implications of the punishments imposed ([27][29]).

Further, the absence of remorse, as discussed in cases like S v Matyityi [2010] ZASCA 127, is a significant factor in evaluating an offender’s rehabilitation prospects and the overall approach sentencing courts should take ([26]).

A – Application

The SCA determined that the trial court had erred in multiple respects regarding the assessment of both mitigating and aggravating factors surrounding the murders ([20]–[24]). It underscored the premeditated nature of the offenses, emphasizing the calculated planning involved in recruiting individuals to carry out the murders for financial gain ([19][21]).

Moreover, the SCA found that the trial court's conclusion regarding the respondent's motives, specifically that her HIV status was a justification for murder, was unfounded and based on conjecture, rather than on the evidenced claims put forth by the respondent herself. This mischaracterization undermined the integrity of the sentencing options available and disregarded the extensive planning that had gone into executing the murders ([19][22]).

The argument posited by the DPP that the trial court had insufficiently accounted for the aggravating circumstances—particularly the planning of the murders—was upheld, demonstrating the need for a more nuanced and rigorous consideration of these evidences in establishing the appropriate sentence ([19][20][25]).

The disparities in sentencing between the respondent and her co-accused suggested a misalignment regarding the seriousness of their respective roles, reinforcing the notion that a singularly lenient sentence could not be justified in lieu of the collective severity of the crimes ([29][30]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA concluded that the trial court had misdirected itself by failing to appreciate the seriousness of the crimes committed by the respondent, concluding the same "substantial and compelling circumstances" could apply to both counts despite the clear legal principles established in prior case law ([30]). The result was an inappropriate and insufficiently punitive sentence for the crimes committed.

The appeal was upheld: the SCA set aside the sentences imposed by the trial court, replacing them with life sentences for both counts of murder, to run concurrently and antedated to 1 February 2016. This outcome not only rectified the inadequacies of the original sentencing but also emphasized to the public that the courts maintain a firm stance against serious crimes like contract killing—labeling such actions as “corrosive of justice” ([34]).

In conclusion, the SCA’s ruling reflects the pivotal role of maintaining proportionality in sentencing, ensuring that the penalties align consistently with the societal and moral imperatives of justice.
