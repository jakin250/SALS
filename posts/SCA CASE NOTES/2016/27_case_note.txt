# Case Note: Mothupi v MEC, Department of Health Free State (20598/2014) [2016] ZASCA 27

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7])

In **Mothupi v MEC, Department of Health Free State**, the appellant, Khomoeng Jane Mothupi, filed an application for condonation for her failure to comply with the notice requirements outlined in **s 3 of the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002** (hereinafter referred to as "the Act"). The procedural history reveals that the appellant had been admitted to Pelonomi Hospital in Bloemfontein for childbirth on the evening of 27 February 2002 and subsequently underwent a Caesarean section on 28 February 2002. Post-surgery, the appellant experienced significant medical complications, including paralysis, which she attributed to alleged negligence in the administration of a spinal anaesthetic ([paras 2–3]).

Following her discharge in early March 2002, Mothupi sought legal counsel nearly a year later, engaging in correspondence with the national Department of Health. Notably, misaddressed notifications were initially served on the national Minister of Health instead of the appropriate organ, the MEC of the Free State Department of Health. Despite clear guidance that the notice should have been directed to the MEC, her attorney served a summons on the national Minister of Health in October 2008 and ultimately sought to amend the claim to join the MEC as a defendant ([paras 4–6]).

The Free State Division of the High Court initially granted condonation for the late notice; however, this decision was later overturned by a full appellate court, prompting the present appeal ([para 1]). The court's examination focused particularly on whether the appellant established "good cause" for the late submission ([para 10–11]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–10])

The principal legal issue in this appeal revolves around whether the appellant demonstrated "good cause" as required by **s 3(4)(b)** of the Act to justify the condonation for her failure to provide timely notice to the respondent. The court considered three key dimensions for this inquiry: 

1. Whether the debt (claim for damages) had not been extinguished by prescription;
2. Whether good cause existed for the failure to comply with the notice requirement; and
3. Whether the respondent incurred unreasonable prejudice as a result of this failure ([paras 9–10]).

The court identified that the first and third elements were straightforward: the claim had not prescribed, and there was no significant prejudice claimed by the respondent ([para 12]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 8–9], [13–17])

The relevant statutory framework is encapsulated in **s 3 of the Act**, which stipulates that no legal proceedings against an organ of state may commence unless written notice has been issued within six months of the debt becoming due. Additionally, **s 3(4)(b)** outlines conditions under which condonation may be granted, emphasizing "good cause" criteria which courts interpret with flexibility considering the broader interests of justice.

In its deliberation, the court referenced the principle established in **Madinda v Minister of Safety and Security** 2008 (4) SA 312 (SCA) regarding the assessment of good cause. This principle entails a holistic examination of various factors, including:

- Prospects of success in the proposed action;
- The justification for any delays;
- The applicant's bona fides;
- Contributions from other parties to the delays ([para 14]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 11–17])

The Supreme Court of Appeal evaluated whether sufficient "good cause" existed for the appellant's late notice. The court concluded that while the appellant's attorney exhibited significant shortcomings—including delays and lack of professional rigor—the absence of severe prejudice to the respondent should weigh heavily in favor of granting condonation ([para 14]).

The potential merits of the underlying claim were pronounced relevant; the medical evidence suggesting possible negligence in the anaesthetic administration lent credence to the appellant's case. Thus, it was contended that given the substantial nature of her claim and the absence of reasonably demonstrable prejudice to the state, the requirement for good cause was sufficiently met to allow access to the courts ([paras 15–17]).

Ultimately, the court expressed that a rigid adherence to procedural technicalities should not override an individual's ability to seek remedy, particularly in serious claims affecting personal liberties and health ([para 17]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 18–25])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, thereby reversing the High Court's ruling and reinstating the appellant’s application for condonation. The judgment emphasizes the balance between procedural rigor and the need for equitable access to justice, particularly in claims of personal injury due to alleged negligence. The court's ruling underscores that requiring rigorous adherence to procedural norms must be tempered by considerations of fairness and the substantial nature of the underlying claims ([paras 24–25]).

Additionally, the court allocated costs in a manner that holds the appellant's negligent attorney responsible for certain expenses incurred, thereby setting a precedent for attorney accountability in procedural matters. This case serves as a critical reminder of the importance of diligence and preparedness in legal representation, as failures therein can impact the client's access to justice ([paras 19–23]).
