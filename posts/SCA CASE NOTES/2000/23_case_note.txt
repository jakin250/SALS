Case Note: DIE HOOGSTE HOF VAN APPèL
Source Folder: 2000

# FIRAC Case Note: Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl v T Makwanyane and M Mchunu

## F – Facts
In the case of *Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl* (Saaknommer: 233/92), the two appellants, T Makwanyane and M Mchunu, were convicted in the Witwatersrand Local Division on multiple counts, including four murders, one attempted murder, and one attempted robbery with aggravating circumstances. The trial court imposed the death penalty for each murder count, alongside sentences of twelve and ten years for attempted murder and robbery, respectively. Ultimately, each appellant faced a cumulative sentence of seventeen years imprisonment ([para 1]).

Following their conviction, the appellants filed an appeal against the judgments and sentences, with the Appellate Division initially dismissing these appeals on May 27, 1994, thus upholding the sentences of attempted murder and robbery. However, due to constitutional concerns over the legality of the death penalty, the appeals concerning death sentences were deferred for constitutional clarification by the Constitutional Court ([paras 2-3]). The judgment of the Constitutional Court, delivered on June 6, 1995, found the death penalty unconstitutional, leaving the appellants without a death sentence ([paras 3-4]).

As a result, the appellants’ case returned to the Appellate Division to propose appropriate replacement sentences in accordance with the new legal landscape regarding the death penalty ([para 4]).

## I – Issues
The principal issues arose concerning the constitutionality of life imprisonment as a sentence, particularly in light of the appellants' arguments that such a sentence, like the death penalty, would infringe upon their rights. Specifically, they claimed that life imprisonment violated their constitutional rights, namely the right to freedom of movement (Article 21), the right to dignity (Article 10), and the right to life (Article 11) ([para 10]). Additionally, two critical legal questions were posed:

1. Whether life imprisonment is an acceptable form of punishment within the ambit of the Constitution, considering the appellants’ assertions of its unconstitutionality ([para 6]).
2. The implications of legislative provisions concerning parole and the potential for reconsideration of life sentences under South African law ([para 14]).

## R – Rules
The legal context was informed by several significant documents and precedents. The Constitution of South Africa, particularly Chapter 2 (Bill of Rights), provides the framework for evaluating constitutional rights affected by sentencing. Article 36 specifically addresses limitations on rights, stating they may only be restricted under general laws that are reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society based on human dignity, equality, and freedom ([para 11]).

Additionally, reference was made to two notable judgments: the German Constitutional Court's ruling in *BVerfGE 45, 187* and the Namibian Supreme Court decision in *S v Tcoeib 1996 (1) SASV 390* ([para 12]). Both cases were utilized by the appellants to argue their position against life imprisonment, emphasizing that it fundamentally undermines the essence of human dignity and the enjoyment of life within a civilized society.

South African statutory provisions, as outlined in the Correctional Services Acts of 1959 and 1998, form the legislative basis for assessing the conditions surrounding life imprisonment, particularly concerning the potential for parole or other forms of early release ([para 13]).

## A – Application
In addressing the issue of whether life imprisonment infringes constitutional rights, the Court deliberated on the balance of factors surrounding the seriousness of the offense and the need for deterrence versus the potential infringement on individual rights. The Court noted that life imprisonment would, indeed, limit the rights to dignity and movement, but questioned whether such limitations could be justified under Article 36 of the Constitution ([para 11]) as reasonable and justifiable in the context of the gravity of the crimes committed.

In referencing *Makwanyane2*, the Court acknowledged the previous ruling's findings on the death penalty's deterring effect and the role life imprisonment can also play in crime prevention, citing the inherent dignity violation that capital punishment entails ([paras 6-9]). The Court further observed that life imprisonment does offer a potential avenue for rehabilitation and parole, which are vital elements in considering its constitutionality ([paras 14-15]).

The availability of parole, while indirectly subject to legislative efficacy, served as a counter to absolute life sentences, allowing for the possibility of rehabilitation and eventual release, which is not afforded by the death penalty. The Court emphasized that the legislative provisions for corrective supervision and parole indicated a systematic approach to reintegrating offenders into society ([para 13]).

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Court concluded that life imprisonment, under the circumstances and provisions of the legislative framework, does not contradict the Constitution ([paras 18-19]). The earlier death sentences were set aside, replaced with life imprisonment sentences, which were backdated to commence on the date of the original sentencing (April 28, 1992).

The ruling held profound implications, illustrating a critical shift in South African jurisprudence regarding capital punishment and the treatment of serious offenders. In light of the Court’s ruling, life imprisonment was deemed appropriate, reflecting the serious nature of the crimes committed while remaining aligned with constitutional mandates.

The case not only fortified the principle of human dignity within the context of punishment but also reaffirmed the judiciary's role in upholding constitutional values against punitive measures that could infringe on individual rights.
