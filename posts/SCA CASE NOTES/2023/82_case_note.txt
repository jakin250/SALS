# Case Note: Siqalo Foods (Pty) Ltd v Clover SA (Pty) Ltd (425/2022) [2023] ZASCA 82

## F – Facts

The case, **Siqalo Foods (Pty) Ltd v Clover SA (Pty) Ltd**, concerns the lawfulness of the labeling of Siqalo’s **STORK BUTTER SPREAD** product ([1]). Siqalo Foods, founded in 2018 following the acquisition of Unilever South Africa’s margarine business, offers a range of products, including the **STORK** line ([2]). Clover SA, a subsidiary of the Clover Group, produces what it describes as a modified butter product under the **BUTRO** trademark, dating back to 1985 ([3]).

Clover SA brought a complaint against Siqalo Foods regarding the misleading labeling of **STORK BUTTER SPREAD**, which Clover argued misrepresented the product as a butter product, while it is, in fact, a modified butter product. Clover alleged that the product label breached the **Agricultural Product Standards Act 119 of 1990** (the Act) and associated regulations ([4]). These rules stipulate that the size of any word or expression on a product label must not exceed the class designation, unless it is a registered trademark. Additionally, any mark that is likely to mislead about the product's characteristics is prohibited ([5]).

Clover applied for an interdict in the Gauteng Division of the High Court against Siqalo, seeking to restrain it from labeling and selling the modified butter product under the contested label ([6]). The High Court ruled in favor of Clover on 12 November 2021, granting the interdict ([6]). 

Siqalo Foods appealed the High Court’s decision ([6]). The essence of the appeal revolved around the alleged breaches of statutory labeling requirements and the resultant implications for competition and consumer perception ([7]).

## I – Issues

The appeal raised several pertinent legal questions:

1. **Did Siqalo Foods' product labeling contravene statutory requirements under the Act and relevant regulations?** ([11])
2. **Did the labeling misrepresent or create a misleading impression regarding the product?** ([11])
3. **If so, did such conduct constitute unlawful competition under common law?** ([11])
4. **Was there an alternative remedy available to Clover that precluded its application in court?** ([11])
5. **Was the Minister of Agriculture, Land Reform and Rural Development a necessary party to the suit, necessitating her inclusion?** ([11])

## R – Rules

The court relied upon several statutory provisions and case law in its analysis:

1. **Agricultural Product Standards Act 119 of 1990**: 
   - Section 3 empowers the Minister to regulate product labeling to ensure compliance with prescribed standards ([12]).
   - Regulation 2(1)(d) mandates compliance with specific labeling requirements for dairy products ([12]).
   - Regulation 26(7)(a) prohibits labels where words exceed the size of the class designation unless part of a registered trademark ([12]).
   - Section 6 and Regulations 32(3)(a) and 32(4) restrict the use of any mark that creates a false or misleading impression about a product’s nature or class ([18]).
  
2. **Case Law**: 
   - The ruling in **William Grant and Sons Ltd v Cape Wine and Distillers Ltd** supported the principle that a commonsensical and first-impression approach is central in assessing likelihood of confusion and misrepresentation ([20]).
   - Precedents establishing unlawful competition as actionable even in cases of innocent misrepresentation were referenced ([36]).

## A – Application

### Statutory Labeling Requirements

The court found clear evidence that Siqalo’s labeling did not comply with the statutory requirements. There was consensus that the product was misleadingly labeled as “butter” while being a **modified butter** blend, adulterated with a significant percentage of plant oils ([7]-[7]). The court examined the visual prominence of the word “BUTTER” on the label, which was oversized and effectively overshadowed other descriptors indicating that the product was modified butter ([9]). 

### Misrepresentation and Likelihood of Confusion

The court's application of the relevant principles led to the conclusion that the labeling would likely mislead consumers into believing they were purchasing pure butter rather than modified butter. Evidence such as consumer feedback from market research substantiated claims that consumers frequently associated the product with pure butter ([22], [24], [33]). 

### Unlawful Competition

Since the appeal recognized that Siqalo Foods was effectively trading contrary to statutory prohibition, it also accepted that this constituted unlawful competition. The court acknowledged that the respondent had adequately demonstrated a case for unlawful competition despite the appellant’s contention that alternative remedies were available ([36]).

### Alternative Remedies and Necessity of the Minister

The appellant’s argument regarding the availability of alternatives—specifically, that the Minister could have intervened—was dismissed. The court clarified that such governmental compliance mechanisms would not suffice as a substitute for Clover’s need for immediate injunctive relief ([38]). 

Finally, the assertion regarding the Minister's necessary involvement was also rejected, as the case centered on the private dispute between Clover and Siqalo without broader implications for regulatory interpretations that would necessitate her participation ([40]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Siqalo Foods' appeal, affirming the High Court's ruling with costs ([6]). The court underscored the importance of accurate product labeling for consumer protection and fair competition, establishing that the deceptive labeling practice by Siqalo Foods harmed not only Clover’s business reputation but also consumer welfare. The judgment clarifies the stringent requirements imposed by the **Agricultural Product Standards Act 119 of 1990** regarding dairy product labeling and sets a precedent for similar future disputes concerning product misrepresentation in the market. 

This case emphasizes the critical balance between competitive marketing practices and statutory compliance, reinforcing that labeling which misleads consumers constitutes both a public harm and an unfair competitive strategy.
