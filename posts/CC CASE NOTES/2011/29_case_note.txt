Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Judge President Mandlakayise John Hlophe v Premier of the Western Cape Province and Others [2011] ZACC 29

## F - Facts
The cases CCT 41/11 and CCT 46/11 were set down for oral argument on September 22, 2011 ([para 1]). The matters arose from earlier directives issued on May 30, 2011, and June 6, 2011, requiring written arguments only. The Chief Justice issued further directions on August 8, 2011, setting them for oral arguments ([para 1]). 

Significantly, seven of the eleven judges, including Chief Justice Ngcobo and Deputy Chief Justice Moseneke, were complainants in matters against Judge Hlophe ([para 2]). Initially, Justices Jafta and Nkabinde recused themselves earlier in the proceedings ([para 5]). This prompted the Chief Justice to remove the cases from the roll due to the lack of a quorum of eight judges necessary under section 167(2) of the Constitution ([para 10]). Thus, the hearing scheduled for September 22, 2011, was postponed to November 29, 2011 ([para 10]).

Concerns were raised by the respondent regarding potential quoracy on the new date set for hearing. Ultimately, the Court's inability to convene with an adequate number of judges prompted the postponement, citing exceptional circumstances ([para 4]).

## I - Issues
The principal legal issues in this case included:

1. Whether the Constitutional Court could determine the merits of the disputes in light of the judges recusing themselves ([para 2(a)]).
2. The interpretation of section 175(1) of the Constitution concerning the appointment of acting judges when a majority of judges consider themselves disqualified from hearing matters ([para 2(c)(i)]).
3. The application of the doctrine of necessity in circumstances where judges have recused themselves ([para 2(e)]).
4. The implications of granting the application for leave to appeal if the Court lacked the requisite quorum to adjudicate ([para 2(d)]).

## R - Rules
The rules relevant to this case predominantly include:

1. The South African Constitution, particularly sections 167(2) and 175(1), which govern the composition and operations of the Constitutional Court ([para 8]).
2. The directives outlined in Rule 11(4) concerning the handling of applications before the Chief Justice ([para 6]).
3. The doctrine of necessity, which posits that a court must resolve a matter, even when it may face recusal issues, if it can do so without compromising the integrity of justice ([para 2(e)]).

## A - Application
In addressing the issues, the Court noted that the majority of judges had recused themselves due to concerns about partiality stemming from their involvement as complainants against the applicant, Judge Hlophe ([para 7]). The recusal of Chief Justice Ngcobo, who did not participate in the later directions, led to a significant decline in available judges ([para 6]). 

The Court examined whether the Constitution allowed for the appointment of acting judges in scenarios where a substantial number would be disqualified. Since section 175 speaks to the appointment of acting judges, the Court pondered its interpretation in the context of a quorum ([para 2(c)]). The Court's analysis concluded that the absence of a quorum precluded the possibility of resolving the matter effectively. 

Moreover, the application of the doctrine of necessity was scrutinized to determine if it would apply in such circumstantial recusal. However, the Court found that the integrity and impartiality of the judicial process would be at risk if the matter proceeded with a significantly diminished bench ([para 2(e)]). Ultimately, the Court's inability to secure sufficient adjudicators rendered any application layer ineffectual, affirming that no purpose would be served by being heard devoid of a full bench ([para 10]).

## C - Conclusion
The Court ultimately decided to postpone the matters until November 29, 2011, due to the lack of quorum necessary to adjudicate ([para 10]). The ruling emphasizes the crucial role that judicial integrity and impartiality play in the function of the Constitutional Court, highlighting the necessity for a full bench to maintain constitutional standards in the adjudication of judicial reviews. The implications of this postponement accentuate the delicate balance between ensuring the availability of competent judges and the need for adherence to due process in judicial governance. 

In conclusion, this case illustrates significant issues concerning judicial integrity, the practical application of constitutional rules regarding judicial appointments, and the importance of maintaining a quorate court for due judicial process under South African law.
