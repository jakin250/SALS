Supreme Court of Appeal

Machi v The State (256/2020) [2021] ZASCA 106

Subsequently, Machi sought leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, which was granted.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 106

F – Facts

The case revolves around the murder of Mr. Walter Mandla Thusi, who was shot and killed at the Engen Diesel Depot in Johannesburg on January 19, 2007. Zola Cedric Machi (the appellant), an employee at the depot, was later arrested and charged with murder. The trial court, led by Mayat AJ, convicted him on December 11, 2008, sentencing him to life imprisonment. Following this, Machi appealed his conviction to the full court of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which upheld the conviction but reduced the sentence to 20 years' imprisonment.

Subsequently, Machi sought leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, which was granted. The basis of his appeal was twofold: firstly, the claim that the evidence presented was insufficient to prove his presence at the scene of the crime; and secondly, the assertion that the trial court failed to properly assess the credibility of witnesses, affecting the conviction ([paras 1–2], [12–14]).

I – Issues

The Supreme Court of Appeal was tasked with determining several key issues:

  1. Whether the State had proven beyond reasonable doubt that Machi was present at the scene of the crime.
  2. Whether Machi had actively associated himself with the unknown assailant who shot the deceased.
  3. Whether the evidence presented was sufficient to establish a 'common purpose' doctrine against Machi.
  4. Whether the sentence imposed was appropriate given the circumstances of the case ([paras 3, 16]).

R – Rules

The court relied on established legal principles regarding the evaluation of evidence and the assessment of credibility. The presumption exists that a trial court's factual findings are correct unless demonstrated otherwise. This principle is based on Rex v Dhlumayo, which establishes that evidence should only be disregarded if clearly erroneous ([paras 17–18], note 1).

Furthermore, the court evaluated the application of the common purpose doctrine as articulated in S v Mgedezi, stipulating that participation requires evidence of presence at the scene, awareness of the crime, active participation, and shared intent to commit the crime ([paras 34–35]).

A – Application

1. Presence at the Scene:
The court examined the testimonies of two key witnesses, Messrs. Ntunja and Makholisa, who provided circumstantial evidence placing Machi at the crime scene. They testified to seeing him arrive in a Mercedes Benz with an unknown man, whom they later identified as the shooter. The appellant's defense, which presented an alibi stating he was at home, was contradicted by witness accounts, leading the court to favor the testimony of the State’s witnesses ([paras 5–9], [30]).

2. Active Association and Common Purpose:
On the issue of whether Machi acted in common purpose with the shooter, the court analyzed the testimonies indicating that the witnesses could see the unknown shooter wearing clothing similar to a work uniform and recognized him as a person associated with the depot. The court found no compelling evidence that an alternate shooter or vehicle could undermine their identification, affirming that Machi's actions were consistent with active participation in the crime ([paras 36–39]).

3. Credibility of Witnesses:
While various contradictions and discrepancies were noted in the testimonies of the State witnesses, the court held that these did not significantly impact their credibility. The trial court’s finding that discrepancies were not material was upheld, as the overall testimony provided a coherent narrative ([paras 20–26]).

4. Sentencing:
The court concurred that although the seriousness of the crime warranted significant penalties, the mitigating circumstances acknowledged by the full court justified a reduction in sentencing. These factors included Machi's behavior while on bail and his potential for rehabilitation. Despite some confusion in the reasoning behind the sentence reduction, the lack of a cross-appeal from the State negated further alterations ([paras 41–44]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Machi's appeal against both conviction and sentence. It held that there was sufficient evidence to establish Machi's presence at the crime scene and his active participation in the murder through the common purpose doctrine. The court further upheld the reduced sentence of 20 years' imprisonment as appropriate under the circumstances. The findings underscored the complexities of evaluating witness credibility in the context of circumstantial evidence and the principles governing common purpose in criminal law ([paras 43–44]).

This case illustrates the rigorous standard of proof in criminal law and the critical role of witness testimony in establishing guilt, highlighting the delicate balance between individual rights and societal justice.
