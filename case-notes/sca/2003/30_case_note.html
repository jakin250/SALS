Wagener v Pharmacare Ltd & Cuttings v Pharmacare Ltd

Supreme Court of Appeal 2003

F – Facts

The case involves consolidated appeals by Anna Elizabeth Jacomina Wagener and Rita Elizabeth Cuttings against Pharmacare Ltd for product liability. Both appellants experienced severe medical complications following the administration of Regibloc Injection, a local anesthetic manufactured by the respondent ([1–3]). Wagener underwent shoulder surgery and subsequently suffered tissue necrosis and arm paralysis ([2]). Both claimants sued Pharmacare and the trustees of the trust that operated the private hospital, alleging that the damages arose from the defective nature of the Regibloc ([3–4]).

Initially, the High Court excused the manufacturer's liability claim due to the absence of fault, stating that the claim did not disclose a cause of action based on the principle of strict liability in the absence of a contractual relationship between the manufacturer and the injured parties ([5–6]). Despite this, it was accepted that the respondent had a legal duty to avoid harm resulting from proverbial defective manufacture, a condition met when the product left the manufacturer’s control ([7]).

I – Issues

The central legal question in this case is whether a manufacturer can be held strictly liable for damages caused by a defective product in the absence of contractual privity to the injured individual ([1]). The case raises concerns about the adequacy of existing common law remedies to achieve protection for the constitutional right to bodily integrity ([9–10]).

R – Rules

The primary rule at stake relates to the Aquilian action in South African law, which traditionally requires proof of fault for recovery of damages due to negligence in tort ([17]). The appellants argued for an extension of existing liability principles, previously established in Kroonstad Westelike Boere Ko-operatiewe Vereniging, Bpk v Botha, where strict liability for defects in sold goods was clarified ([9]). The case cites numerous legal texts and opinions supporting the notion of strict liability without proof of fault, alongside comparing South African principles with U.S. legal frameworks, particularly the Restatement (Second) of Torts and its approach to product liability ([23]).

A – Application

The court affirmed that while arising from constitutional rights, the Aquilian remedy still adequately addresses the claims of the appellants. The requirement for proving fault was a significant factor, with the ruling indicating that establishing a standard of strict liability would require profound alterations in established law without sufficient legislative authority or societal consensus on the required framework ([29–38]).

In analyzing the arguments, the court highlighted that introducing strict liability could create new complexities and inconsistencies in existing law without adequate groundwork or legislative oversight ([28–32]). The factors surrounding the control and safety of medical products were emphasized, indicating that proving defectiveness often parallels proving negligence, therefore not rendering the Aquilian action inadequate ([19–22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed both appeals, ruling that the plaintiffs' claims were adequately covered under Aquilian liability. The Court emphasized that changing liability standards requires legislative action rather than judicial activism, as it could potentially disrupt longstanding legal principles concerning fault and negligence in South African law. Thereby, the court reinforced the notion that the constitution does not create new liabilities but emphasizes the evolution and incremental development of existing principles ([39]). The judgment underscores the distinction between judicial and legislative powers in the realm of product liability law, leaving the ordering of strict liability to statutory frameworks rather than immediate judicial mandate.
