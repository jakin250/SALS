Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

### FIRAC Case Note: Naude & Another v S (488/10) [2010] ZASCA 138

#### F – Facts
The case originated from a tragic shooting incident that took place around 03h30 on the morning of 7 May 2004, at a residence in Bishop Lavis, an area marked by high rates of crime. A total of 39 gunshots were fired at the house, resulting in the deaths of four occupants: Ms. Beverley Van Heerden, her son Leon, her boyfriend Mr. Henry Martin, and his friend Mr. Lucius McKenzie. A fifth occupant, Ms. Liezel Van Heerden, miraculously survived despite being shot 25 times. The identification made by Ms. Van Heerden of Mr. Marco Moosa as one of the shooters significantly initiated the legal proceedings against Mr. Moosa and the appellants, Mr. Kashief Naude and Mr. Garreth Solomons, who were also implicated in the shooting ([paras 1–4]).

The sequence leading to the shooting involved a relationship dispute, wherein Mr. Moosa allegedly informed the appellants that Ms. Faranaaz Naude, Kashief's sister, was involved with Leon Van Heerden, leading to a confrontation. Accused with counts of murder and attempted murder, Mr. Moosa was sentenced to life imprisonment, while Kashief was sentenced to 20 years and Garreth to life imprisonment ([paras 4, 29]).

Kashief and Garreth appealed their convictions, arguing insufficient evidence to support the convictions, and Kashief further appealed his sentence on similar grounds. 

#### I – Issues
The primary legal issues in this case involved:
1. Whether the evidence presented by the State was sufficient to support a conviction.
2. The implications of the failure of the appellants to testify in their defence and how this impacted the assessment of the credibility of witness testimony.
3. The appropriateness of the sentences handed down, particularly the claim of diminished moral culpability by Kashief based on his role as the driver of the getaway vehicle.

These issues prompted a comprehensive evaluation of both direct and circumstantial evidence presented during the trial ([paras 4, 36]).

#### R – Rules
The legal framework applied by the court included the standard of proof in criminal cases, which requires that guilt be established beyond a reasonable doubt. The court referred to precedent cases setting the tone for evidence evaluation, including *S v Van der Meyden* 1999 (1) SACR 447 (W), which outlined that the conclusion must account for all evidence presented ([paras 28, 29]). Furthermore, the court reiterated that the failure of the accused to testify in the face of compelling evidence is unlikely to result in anything favorable for the defence, as seen in *S v Chabalala* 2003 (1) SACR 134 (SCA) ([para 37]).

The provisions of section 204 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the Act) were also discussed, which relate to the admissibility of evidence from witnesses who may face self-incrimination due to their testimonies. The court was obliged to evaluate the truthfulness and reliability of such witness accounts critically ([paras 28, 34]).

#### A – Application
The court meticulously analyzed the evidence presented during trial. Key witnesses included:
- Ms. Liezel Van Heerden, whose identification of Mr. Moosa was pivotal.
- Ms. Rugaya Solomons, who confirmed the timeline of events leading to the shooting and presented evidence of phone calls made that morning.
- Evidence from Mr. Abdoel Karriem Orrie, who claimed Garreth stated he had committed a "massacre" shortly after the event.

The court found inconsistencies in the alibi provided by Ms. Faranaaz Naude, which included prior contradictory statements regarding the night of the shooting. The trial court ultimately determined that Ms. Solomons was a credible witness, and her testimony substantially supported the State's case, leading to the rejection of Faranaaz's conflicting evidence ([paras 19, 24, 30, 31]).

Despite the appellants' appeals, which centered on the conflict of testimonies and claims of unreliability, the Supreme Court of Appeal corroborated the lower court's findings. They recognized that much of the identified evidence was compelling enough to warrant a conviction, particularly in light of the appellants' failure to present their side of the story ([paras 36–38]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court’s convictions for both Kashief and Garreth, emphasizing the weight of evidence presented and the implications of their decision not to testify. Specifically, the court stated that their silence in light of substantial evidence went against them, reinforcing the prosecution's case.

The court further dismissed Kashief's appeal regarding the sentencing, concluding that he had fully participated in the criminal act and had not established any mitigating factors that would lessen his culpability ([paras 38–40]). 

In summary, the ruling reinforced the principles of reliability in witness testimony, the impact of an accused's decision not to testify, and the judicial approach to evaluating contradictions within evidence. The case serves as a significant reference point for discussions on the weight of circumstantial evidence and the crucial role of witness credibility in criminal convictions.

---

**Order**:
1. The appeals by the appellants against their convictions are dismissed.
2. The appeal by the first appellant against all the sentences imposed is dismissed. [para 43]
