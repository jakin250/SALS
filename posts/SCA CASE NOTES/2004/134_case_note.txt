# FIRAC Case Note: Republic of South Africa (Times Media Limited v Niselow)

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal (Case No. 195/03)  
**Date Heard:** 26 August 2004  
**Date Delivered:** 1 December 2004  
**Judges:** Mpati AP, Streichers, Conradie JJA, Erasmus, Patel AJJA

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–8])

The case arises from an incident in which approximately 600 children experienced food poisoning after consuming meals prepared by the second respondent, Nise Caterers CC, during the opening ceremony of the All Africa Games in Johannesburg. Barry Niselow, the owner of Nise Caterers, was the first respondent. The appellants consisted of Times Media Limited (the publisher of the Sunday Times), its editor Michael William Robertson, and several journalists.

On September 10, 1999, the Foundation engaged these children for a performance. Meals prepared by Nise Caterers, including beef stew, were served late, resulting in numerous children falling ill shortly thereafter. Medical personnel attended to the children at the scene, with reports of severe symptoms necessitating hospital transport.

Subsequently, the Sunday Times published an article titled "Cook that spoiled Games broth," which included allegations that Niselow was responsible for the food poisoning. It further stated that despite the incident, Niselow continued to serve the food in question. The respondents filed for defamation, asserting that the article carried defamatory meanings regarding Niselow's negligence and the quality of the food that led to the children's illness.

The High Court found in favor of the respondents, deeming the statements published by the appellants to be defamatory, leading to the appeal by the appellants against this ruling ([paras 1–8]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–12])

The primary issue on appeal was whether the statements made in the article and accompanying placard conveyed one or more of the defamatory meanings attributed to them by the respondents. A further sub-issue revolved around the standard used to define whether the words used constituted defamation, particularly regarding the interpretation by a reasonable reader of the Sunday Times ([paras 9–12]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 8–23])

The court applied established principles of defamation law, particularly focusing on the meanings capable of being attributed to the statements made in the article. It referred to the ordinary reasonable reader standard derived from previous cases, such as **Demmers v Wyllie** [1980] (1) SA 835, which stipulates that the attribution of meaning cannot be from a morbid or suspicious mindset but should reflect the understanding of an average reader.

The judgments also referenced **Mthembi-Mahanyele v Mail and Guardian Ltd** [2004] (3) All SA 511 where it was held that the determinative meaning of allegations must derive from the context and content of the publication as a whole. Another significant case cited was **Johnson v Rand Daily Mails** [1980] (1) SA 762, addressing the need for objective proof of the alleged defamatory meanings and maintaining that a plaintiff’s claim is restricted to positions outlined in the pleadings.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 9–30])

The court analyzed the contents of the article and placard in totality rather than in isolated exerts. It noted that the allegations, conveyed within an overall context that included both statements from Niselow and expert opinions, reflected a reasonable suspicion regarding the beef stew's quality.

While recognizing that the word "suspected" implied a lack of certainty, the court held that this does not preclude the presence of defamatory meaning where the reasonable reader may conclude that there were grounds for suspecting the caterers' culpability. Further, the court rejected the appellants' arguments on basis that the respondents had not sufficiently proved their case, affirming that the appellants indeed bore the burden of proving the truth of the asserted allegations given the seriousness of the claims ([paras 10–30]).

The court noted that the allegations—particularly those addressing the food’s appearance and smell—were unsubstantiated by the evidence presented at trial. A key contention was whether Dr. Boffard's denial of having stated that the food "smelled awful" and "looked appalling" necessarily hurt the respondents' defamation claim. Ultimately it was reasoned that the truth of the article’s descriptions of the food’s condition should stand alone from the assertions regarding responsibility ([paras 23–30]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 31–49])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal by the appellants was successful, concluding that the respondents failed to restrict the defamatory meanings they sought to prove within the pled commitments. Specifically, the court determined that the stings as pleaded could not be said to uphold a defamatory interpretation based upon the definitions asserted.

The court clarified that allowing new meanings outside the pleadings would undermine fair trial rights, emphasizing the limitation of reliance on unpleaded meanings in a defamation suit. This conclusion resulted in the reduction of the appellants' liability and the dismissal of the defamation claims against them.

As a wider implication, this case reasserted the necessity for precise definitions within pleadings in defamation cases, reinforcing the legal principle that a plaintiff's success hinges upon the meanings explicitly articulated in their claim ([paras 31–49]). 

#### Final Order: The appeal was upheld, and the court a quo's ruling was set aside with costs, reflecting a substantive victory for the appellants and significant legal precedent regarding defamation claims and pleadings in South Africa.
