Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Lebogang Peter Mashilo v The State (1129/2019) [2022] ZASCA 81

### F - Facts

The case involves **Lebogang Peter Mashilo** (*the applicant*) and **Mzwakhe Moagi**, who were charged in a regional court with robbery with aggravating circumstances, kidnapping, and unlawful possession of a firearm, arising from an incident on **14 January 2014** where they robbed **Hajoon Khan** of his motor vehicle and two cell phones ([1]). The charges included aggravating circumstances due to the use of a firearm and the deprivation of Khan's liberty. Both accused had prior convictions for similar offences, leading to mandatory minimum sentencing under section 51(2) of the *Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997* (the Minimum Sentence Act) ([2]).

On **14 October 2014**, the trial court convicted both defendants, imposing a minimum sentence of **20 years** for robbery and **5 years** for kidnapping, albeit the sentences were not ordered to run concurrently, resulting in the applicant receiving a total effective sentence of **25 years** ([3]). After a refused application for leave to appeal by a regional magistrate, the applicant sought special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which was also dismissed ([4]-[5]).

Subsequently, the applicant petitioned the President of the Supreme Court of Appeal under section 17(2)(f) of the *Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013*, arguing that the denial of his appeal was unjust, especially in light of Moagi’s successful appeal which resulted in a reduced sentence ([6]-[10]). The President referred the matter back to the court ([7]).

### I - Issues

1. **Did the failure to forewarn the applicant about the minimum sentencing implications constitute grounds for reconsideration of his sentencing?**
2. **Were there substantial and compelling circumstances justifying a deviation from the standard minimum sentence imposed under the Minimum Sentence Act?**
3. **Did the court err in dismissing the application for special leave to appeal, affecting the applicant's right to a fair appeal?** ([8]-[18]).

### R - Rules

The court relied upon multiple legal principles and statutory provisions, particularly:

1. Section 51(2) of the *Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997*, which stipulates mandatory minimum sentences for certain offences, including robbery ([2]).

2. Section 17(2)(f) of the *Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013*, interpreted in light of the precedent set in *Avnit v First Rand Bank Ltd* [2014] ZASCA 132 ([8]), which outlines that exceptional circumstances must involve more than mere prospects of success but must also demonstrate a substantial legal point or a risk of grave injustice.

3. Case authority concerning minimum sentencing and the importance of forewarning defendants about sentence implications is pivotal, as highlighted in the appellate proceedings regarding Moagi ([10]).

### A - Application

In analyzing the applicant's appeal, the court reviewed the facts and highlights from the Moagi case, where it was found that the charge sheet’s silence on minimum sentencing and the trial court's failure to forewarn constituted substantial and compelling circumstances ([10]-[11]). However, the applicant’s case significantly diverged from Moagi's; the court noted that the charge sheet had clearly referenced the minimum sentence provisions, and both defendants were aware of the potential consequences as they presented their cases with legal representation ([13]-[14]).

Furthermore, the court found that the applicant’s appeal statement did not initially argue a failure of justice due to a lack of proper forewarning—the focus was rather on the imposition of a sentence considered unduly harsh given the circumstances of concurrent sentencing ([15]).

The crux of the ruling highlighted that the applicant's new arguments presented during the section 17(2)(f) appeal did not substantiate claims of judicial misdirection. The trial proceedings had adhered to the required legal standards, which included adequately forewarning both the applicant and co-accused regarding the minimum sentence implications. Consequently, claims of injustice were unfounded ([18]).

### C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the lower court's dismissal of the applicant's appeal. The court concluded the application lacked merit as it found no substantial legal grounds or exceptional circumstances to justify revisiting the original sentence issued. The ruling underscored the principle that a mere discontent with a sentence, without compelling justification, does not suffice to warrant an appeal's reconsideration ([19]).

This decision is significant as it reinforces the stringent nature of mandatory sentencing under the Minimum Sentence Act and clarifies the procedural expectations and rights of defendants concerning appeal processes in the South African legal context. The court’s insistence on documented legal principles regarding minimum sentencing serves to uphold the integrity of judicial processes while ensuring consistent application of law for offences involving aggravating circumstances.
