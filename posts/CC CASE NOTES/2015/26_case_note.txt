Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Minister of Defence and Military Veterans v Thomas [2015] ZACC 26

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The respondent, Dr. Liesl-Lenore Thomas, is a medical doctor employed by the Western Cape Provincial Government in its health department. On May 28, 2009, while seconded to the 2 Military Hospital in Wynberg, Cape Town, she sustained significant injuries after falling down a stairwell ([1]–[3]). Following her injury, Dr. Thomas lodged a claim for delictual damages against the Minister of Defence and Military Veterans (the applicant) and the hygiene service provider at the hospital ([4]). Concurrently, she sought occupational injury benefits under the Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act 130 of 1993 (the Compensation Act), which she was entitled to receive ([5]–[6]).

The Minister of Defence filed a special plea arguing that Dr. Thomas was barred from claiming damages against her as the employer by virtue of section 35(1) of the Compensation Act, which prohibits employees from pursuing civil claims for occupational injuries against their employers ([5]–[6]). The High Court upheld the Minister's special plea, affirming that Dr. Thomas was an employee of the State, thus consolidating her claims against all governmental entities ([8]).

However, the Supreme Court of Appeal overturned this decision, finding that the provincial government was individually liable under the Compensation Act and directing that the matter proceed to trial ([9]). The Minister thereafter sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, which deemed the constitutional implications significant enough to warrant intervention ([10]).

### I – Issues
The critical legal question in this case revolves around the definition of "employer" as per the Compensation Act, specifically whether the State functions as a singular employer or if its provincial components are regarded as separate entities ([11]). The Court was tasked with determining who constituted Dr. Thomas's employer for the purposes of her claim under both statutory and common law ([7]).

### R – Rules
The primary legal principle governing this case is found within the Compensation Act, particularly section 35(1), which states: 
> "No action shall lie by an employee... for the recovery of damages... against such employee’s employer..." This provision protects employers from civil claims related to occupational injuries, thereby defining the liability structure between employees and employers ([5]).

The Constitutional framework, particularly section 39(2) of the Constitution, mandates that legislation be interpreted in a manner promoting the Bill of Rights, underpinning the right to bodily integrity and security of person ([10]). Furthermore, precedents such as *Mankayi v Anglogold Ashanti* clarify the constitutional underpinning of workplace safety as integral to employee rights ([12]).

### A – Application
In interpreting section 35(1) of the Compensation Act, the Constitutional Court outlined that the term "employer" extends beyond conventional understandings. The definition mentioned in section 1 of the Act indicates that the State only fulfills specific employer functions when controlling particular governmental activities, thus suggesting a complexity in ascribing liability ([22]). 

The Court critically evaluated the Minister’s stance that the State should be viewed as a single entity. The judge held that interpreting the State as a singular employer overlooks the distinct structures established by South Africa's Constitution, which outlines separate governmental spheres with distinct legal identities and responsibilities ([27]). 

Moreover, doing so would effectively restrict Dr. Thomas's right to pursue a delictual claim, which contradicts the spirit of the Bill of Rights ([39]). The Court concluded that the interpretation supporting the Supreme Court of Appeal should be preferred, as it does not diminish the rights extended to employees under common law, thereby preserving the entitlement to seek recovery for damages owing to workplace injuries ([39]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court upheld the Supreme Court of Appeal's decision, affirming that Dr. Thomas’s employer was the provincial government, thus allowing her to maintain her delictual claim for damages in addition to the occupational injury benefits under the Compensation Act. The Court held that to deny her this right based on the argument of a singular State employer would be unconstitutional and infringe upon her right to bodily integrity and security of person ([40]).

The implications of this ruling signify a protective approach towards employee rights within the framework of governmental liability. The judgment emphasizes the importance of preserving avenues for employees to seek recourse against negligent conduct in the workplace, thus ensuring regulatory compliance with constitutional protections ([41]).

This FIRAC analysis encapsulates the essential legal principles and judgments of the Constitutional Court, demonstrating a careful balance between statutory interpretation and constitutional rights, with far-reaching implications for workplace injury claims and the scope of employer liability in South Africa.
