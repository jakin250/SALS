FIRAC Case Note on Rikhotso v Premier, Limpopo Province and Others [2021] ZACC 1

Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 1 #### F – Facts: Relevant background and proced

FIRAC Case Note on Rikhotso v Premier, Limpopo Province and Others [2021] ZACC 1

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history

[2] The case concerns an application for leave to appeal against the judgment of the High Court of South Africa, Limpopo Local Division, Thohoyandou (High Court), which dismissed an application by Mr. Masenyani Thompson Rikhotso to review and set aside the decision of the Premier of Limpopo to withdraw his certificates of recognition and appointment as the headman of Nsavulani. The Premier, empowered by the Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005 (the Act), had removed Rikhotso due to allegations of misconduct, which he denied. Following several disciplinary inquiries, the fifth respondent—the Mahumani Royal Family—resolved to dismiss Rikhotso effective from 16 January 2012. The first respondent formally withdrew the certificates on 8 March 2013.

[9] Due to significant delays, the applicant lodged a review application on 18 September 2017, claiming violation of his right to just administrative action under section 33 of the Constitution. His application was initially granted for late filing; however, the High Court later ruled against him on grounds of prescription, stating that more than three years had elapsed since the withdrawal of his certificates.

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions

[12] The principal issues to resolve were whether the High Court could rule on prescription given its earlier condonation of the applicant's late application, whether it could dismiss the matter based on delay after granting condonation, and whether the principles from Biowatch v Registrar, Genetic Resources [2009] ZACC 14 [2009] 6 SA 232 (CC), regarding costs in constitutional matters, applied in this case.

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon

[6] Section 33 of the Constitution provides everyone the right to just administrative action, while section 34 ensures the right to access courts to resolve disputes. The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) governs the review of administrative actions, mandating that applications be made without unreasonable delay, typically within 180 days as stated in section 7(1)(b).

[18] The relevant case law included Zondi v MEC for Traditional and Local Government Affairs [2004] ZACC 19, which underscored the importance of the right to access courts in the context of the rule of law, and Gqwetha v Transkei Development Corporation Ltd [2005] ZASCA 51, which established criteria for assessing undue delay in bringing a review application.

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law

[16] Upon review, the Constitutional Court found that the High Court erred in treating prescription as a valid defense. The High Court had previously granted condonation for the delay, which negated the relevance of prescription as a barrier to the applicant's application. The court stressed that procedural missteps should not obstruct the pursuit of justice, especially when constitutional rights are at stake.

[19] Highlighting the functus officio doctrine, the Constitutional Court held that the High Court could not alter its earlier decision once it had taken final action by granting condonation. The court reiterated that delays in review applications should not automatically lead to dismissals without consideration of the underlying rights being asserted. Thus, a holistic view of circumstances surrounding delays and the importance of upholding constitutional rights led the court to conclude that the High Court failed to exercise its discretion appropriately.

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications

[26] The Constitutional Court set aside the order of the High Court, thereby remitting the matter for consideration of the merits of the review application. The court ordered the costs of litigation from the initial High Court proceedings to be borne by the Premier of Limpopo and associated respondents, acknowledging the inappropriate invocation of the prescription argument given earlier condonation.

The ruling underscores the directive for courts to uphold the fundamental rights of access to justice and administrative fairness within the South African constitutional framework. By remitting the case for consideration of merits, the court preserves the avenue for individuals like Rikhotso to challenge administrative decisions affecting their rights, reinforcing the principle of legality and accountability of the state in administrative actions.

This case thus serves as a critical precedent promoting the interconnection of constitutional rights, judicial oversight of administrative actions, and legislative provisions, ultimately advancing the rule of law in South Africa.
