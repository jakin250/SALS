Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Economic Freedom Fighters and Others v Speaker of the National Assembly and Another [2017] ZACC 47  
**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Neutral Citation:** CCT 76/17  
**Heard on:** 5 September 2017  
**Decided on:** 29 December 2017  
**Coram:** Mogoeng CJ, Zondo DCJ, Cameron J, Froneman J, Jafta J, Kathree-Setiloane AJ, Kollapen AJ, Madlanga J, Mhlantla J, Theron J, Zondi AJ  

### F – Facts
The matter before the Constitutional Court involved a complaint by political parties represented in the National Assembly against the National Assembly's alleged failure to fulfill its constitutional obligations in holding President Jacob Zuma accountable for not implementing the Public Protector's corrective action from March 19, 2014, regarding the upgrades to his Nkandla residence ([1]–[4]).

The applicants included the Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF), the United Democratic Movement (UDM), and the Congress of the People (COPE). They sought relief against the Speaker of the National Assembly (first respondent) and President Zuma (second respondent). The Democratic Alliance (DA) intervened, supporting the application ([2]–[4]).

The background of the complaint involved the President failing to comply with a report from the Public Protector, which mandated him to repay a portion of the costs for non-security upgrades to Nkandla and reprimand relevant ministers for the handle of the project ([4]–[8]). Despite the Court ruling against the President in EFF 1 that his actions were inconsistent with the Constitution, the National Assembly did not pursue the matter further, particularly in respect to the provisions of section 89(1) of the Constitution, which provides grounds for the President's removal ([6]–[10]). 

### I – Issues
The key legal issues presented to the Court included:
1. Whether the National Assembly has failed to put in place mechanisms and processes for holding the President accountable under section 89 of the Constitution ([14]–[28]).
2. Whether the National Assembly has a constitutional obligation to hold the President accountable and whether it has indeed held him accountable for his failure to implement the Public Protector's remedial action ([32]–[40]).
3. The appropriateness of the mechanisms currently in place within the National Assembly for addressing potential grounds for impeachment ([69]–[75]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal authorities and constitutional provisions discussed in the judgment included:
- **Section 89(1)** of the Constitution, which provides the grounds for the removal of the President, requiring a two-thirds majority vote in the National Assembly.
- **Section 42(3)**, which outlines the National Assembly's obligation to represent people and ensure oversight of executive actions.
- **Section 55(2)**, which mandates the National Assembly to ensure accountability and maintain oversight ([28]–[29]).
- The constitutional duty of the National Assembly to uphold accountability through the mechanisms it establishes, as provided for in sections 57(1) and 59 of the Constitution ([30]–[32]).

Precedents discussed included the earlier judgment in *Economic Freedom Fighters v Speaker of the National Assembly* ([2016] ZACC 11) which established critical interpretations of the obligations of the National Assembly in respect of the executive ([6], [14]).

### A – Application
In its analysis, the majority judgment, authored by Jafta J, found that:
- The National Assembly had not established specific rules or procedures for the impeachment process under section 89, failing to execute its constitutional obligation. The absence of these rules constituted a breach of section 89, invalidating its prior actions ([67], [72]).
- The National Assembly did engage with the President via questions and three motions of accountability, yet these mechanisms did not meet the specific requirements for impeachment as outlined in section 89 ([85]–[90]).
- The Court concluded that for proper accountability mechanisms to be in place, especially concerning impeachment processes, the National Assembly must have well-defined procedures that facilitate swift responses to potential presidential misconduct ([201]–[207]).

In dissent, Zondo DCJ emphasized the flexibility of the National Assembly's rules and criticized the need for rigid procedures, arguing that the mechanisms already in place were adequate for handling accountability ([117]–[246]). 

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately ordered that:
1. The National Assembly had exclusive jurisdiction over the matter and its failure to create a procedural framework for impeachment was declared invalid.
2. The National Assembly must comply with its constitutional obligations promptly to avoid future violations ([222]).
3. It was directed to formulate rules for conducting the impeachment process in accordance with section 89 without delay ([222]).

This case highlighted critical implications for parliamentary procedures, accountability, and judicial oversight within the context of maintaining the rule of law in South Africa. The judgment reinforced the notion that parliamentary processes must be clearly defined to uphold constitutional obligations, specifically in terms of holding high office bearers accountable. The Court emphasized the necessity for efficiency and clarity within legislative mechanisms governing executive accountability, thereby shaping future procedural conduct within the National Assembly.
