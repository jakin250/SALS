Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Member of the Executive Council for Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs, KwaZulu-Natal v Nkandla Local Municipality and Others [2021] ZACC 46

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])

The case at hand involves disputes concerning the appointments of Mr. Langelihle Siphiwokuhle Jili as Municipal Manager of the Nkandla Local Municipality and Mr. Philani Philemon Sibiya as Municipal Manager of the Mthonjaneni Local Municipality. Both appointments were challenged by the Member of the Executive Council for the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs, KwaZulu-Natal (MEC), who argued that the appointments contravened section 54A(2) of the Local Government Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 (the Systems Act), rendering them null and void under section 54A(3) of the Act.

On 24 January 2017, the Nkandla Municipal Council appointed Mr. Jili, and on 19 December 2016, the Mthonjaneni Council appointed Mr. Sibiya. Following the appointments, the MEC expressed concerns regarding the qualifications and experience levels of both appointees, particularly noting Mr. Jili's lack of requisite senior management experience and Mr. Sibiya’s irrelevant qualifications ([paras 2–4]).

The MEC’s attempts to remedy the situation through correspondence with the Municipalities were met with delays and non-responses, ultimately leading to high court review applications which sought to assert the invalidity of both appointments. The High Court ruled in favor of the MEC but did not apply the ruling retroactively ([paras 5–6]). The respondents appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which also ruled in their favor, focusing on the alleged delays by the MEC in seeking remedial action ([para 6]).

---

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 8–12])

1. **Jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court**: Whether the Constitutional Court has the jurisdiction to hear the matter, given its nature involving the exercise of public power by the MEC and the interpretation of provisions within the Systems Act.

2. **Interests of Justice**: Whether the application for leave to appeal should be granted, considering the practicality and the principles of mootness, particularly the consequences of the Supreme Court of Appeal's decision.

3. **Mootness of the Application**: The respondents contended that the application was moot due to subsequent developments in the systems statute, raising questions about whether a ruling would have enforceable implications ([paras 9–16]).

4. **Effectiveness of Section 54A**: An assessment of whether the MEC could rely on a provision that has been declared unconstitutional following its suspension period ([paras 16–24]).

---

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 10–22])

1. **Local Government Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000**: Specifically sections 54A(2) and (3), detailing qualifications required for appointment as a municipal manager and consequences of unlawful appointments.

2. **Constitution of South Africa**: Section 167, which outlines the competency and functioning of the Constitutional Court in addressing constitutional matters, as well as Section 172, which allows the Court to declare legislation invalid.

3. **Notyawa v Makana Municipality** and **Khumalo v MEC for Education: KwaZulu-Natal**: These cases emphasized the importance of adhering to timelines prescribed within legislation and the due diligence required of public officials when executing their duties ([paras 25–29]).

4. **South African Municipal Workers' Union v Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs** (SAMWU): The decision declared section 54A unconstitutional, which suspended its application for a period to allow legislative correction, impacting the present judgments.

---

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 24–32])

The Constitutional Court grappled primarily with the issue of mootness, determining that while the MEC’s powers had been fundamentally altered following the declaration of invalidity concerning section 54A, it must also consider the broader implications of any ruling on municipal governance and service delivery ([paras 16–30]).

- **Jurisdiction**: The court cited its jurisdiction based on constitutional issues arising from the interpretation of public powers exercised by the MEC ([paras 9–11]).

- **Mootness and Consequences**: Despite arguments regarding the mootness of the case, the court acknowledged the long-term implications of its ruling on the functioning of local governance and the critical role municipal managers play in service delivery. It considered the practical impact of terminating the contracts of qualified appointees due to the delays alleged against the MEC ([paras 24–31]).

- **Delays by the MEC**: The court underscored the MEC's significant delays in seeking remediation and acting within the stipulated timelines of the Systems Act, thereby impacting the case's legal viability. The court noted that the lack of timely action led to a situation where the challenges to appointments did not account for the prolonged service periods of the involved managers ([paras 25–27]).

---

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 33–34])

The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled to refuse leave to appeal, affirming the Supreme Court of Appeal's decision. The court emphasized that while the MEC had raised valid constitutional concerns regarding the appointments, the prolonged delays by the MEC to act meant that any retrospective invalidation of the appointments would not serve the interests of justice. The rationale was clear: the public interest required stability in governance structures, and the absence of any performance-related grievances against the managers further supported the decision ([paras 33–34]).

The implications of this ruling are profound, establishing a precedent that highlights the importance of timely action by public officials and reinforcing the court's commitment to balancing legal accountability with practical governance needs in municipalities. This establishes a framework for how delays in public administration can influence judicial outcomes and the exercise of constitutional powers in South Africa.
