Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2005

# Case Note: The Local Transitional Council of Delmas v Wilhelm Abraham Boshoff [2005] ZASCA 10

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The dispute arises from the establishment of an informal township, Botleng Extension 3 (Botleng 3), which was created under the Less Formal Township Establishment Act 13 of 1991 ([para 2]). The local Botleng town committee, with the approval of the Transvaal Provincial Administration, facilitated this establishment on a property adjacent to that of the respondent, Wilhelm Abraham Boshoff, who owned a farm in Delmas ([paras 1–3]).

Boshoff had purchased his farm in 1988 and engaged in agricultural activities. However, from June 1994, he alleged that due to the actions of residents of Botleng 3—resulting in pollution, theft, property damage, and threats—he and his family were compelled to abandon their farm ([paras 6–8]). Boshoff subsequently brought action against the Local Transitional Council of Delmas and the Member of the Executive Council for Local Government: Mpumalanga Province for damages in the Pretoria High Court, claiming that the prior authorities failed to protect him from the adverse impacts of the township ([para 4]).

During the trial, the issues of liability were separated from those concerning the quantification of damages ([paras 5, 30]). The trial court ultimately found that the defendants were liable for any damages proven by Boshoff, particularly hinging on the failure to comply with the Act’s provisions regarding an impact study and the improper occupation of land ([paras 16–17]).

### I – Issues
The key legal issues in this case involve:
1. Whether the defendants (the Local Transitional Council of Delmas and the Member of the Executive Council) had a legal duty to take reasonable steps to prevent foreseeable harm to Boshoff resulting from the establishment of Botleng 3 ([para 14]).
2. The distinction between the legal concepts of wrongfulness and negligence, and how they are applicable to the alleged omissions of the defendants ([paras 18–19]).
3. The correctness of the lower court’s conclusions regarding the elements of negligence and causation in relation to the plaintiff's claims ([paras 30, 31]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on established legal principles concerning the liability for negligent omissions, recognizing key authorities, including:
- **Ewels v Minister van Polisie** ([1975] 3 SA 590 (A)), which discusses the necessity of a legal duty to avoid harm arising from negligent acts or omissions ([para 20]).
- The criteria from **Kruger v Coetzee** ([1966] 2 SA 428 (A)) for establishing negligence, which requires a reasonable foreseeing of harm and taking appropriate measures to mitigate that harm ([para 21]).
- **Diepsloot Residents’ and Landowners’ Association v Administrator, Transvaal** ([1994] 3 SA 336 (A)), reinforcing that public authorities cannot rely on statutory authority when acting contrary to statutory provisions ([para 16]).

### A – Application
The court assessed the defendants' actions under the framework of the above principles, particularly focusing on the necessity for a legal duty when establishing a township and the subsequent responsibilities to mitigate harm ([paras 14, 17, 24]). 

It concluded that the defendants had indeed acted in contravention of their duty under the Act by allowing occupation of Botleng 3 without completing necessary impact studies or complying with statutory requirements ([paras 9, 12, 16]). The court highlighted that even though the township's establishment complied with statutory provisions, the actions taken post-approval were negligent ([para 22]). 

The confusion between wrongfulness and negligence, as noted by the appellants, was critiqued since the trial court failed to properly isolate the negligence aspect from wrongfulness. Thus, the inquiry about whether omissions were wrongful in terms of community legal convictions was not fully explored ([para 24]). 

Furthermore, the assessment of preventative measures that could have been undertaken by the defendants (e.g., erecting fences or mitigating measures) was inadequately addressed by the trial court, which failed to explore their feasibility and efficacy ([paras 11, 28]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the trial court’s finding of liability was founded on confusion regarding the concepts of wrongfulness and negligence. It concluded that while negligent omissions could be deemed wrongful, a thorough examination of negligence and causation—including whether suggested preventative measures were practical and whether they could have effectively reduced the damages—had not been sufficiently conducted ([para 30]). 

Consequently, the appeal was upheld, and the matter was remitted for reconsideration regarding the specifics of negligence and causation as they relate to damages. The court declared that the defendants could be held liable for negligent omissions but did not establish immediate liability for damages without a full assessment of these elements ([paras 33, 32]). 

### Implications
This case underscores the necessity for public authorities to adhere to statutory frameworks and their responsibilities to minimize harm through reasonable preventative measures. It also highlights the importance of clear delineation between liability concepts in tort law, particularly in negligence claims, ensuring that courts properly analyze and differentiate between wrongfulness and negligence to avoid subsequent legal confusion in future analogous cases.
