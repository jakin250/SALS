Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: Republic of South Africa (Case No: 579/96)

### F – Facts
The case concerns an appeal by **National Media Limited**, **Khulu Sibiya**, **Allied Publishers Limited**, and **Perskor Limited** (collectively referred to as the "Appellants") against the judgment of the Western Cape High Court in **Bogoshi v National Media Ltd and Others** (1996 (3) SA 78 (W)). The original suit arose from allegedly defamatory articles published in the *City Press* newspaper from **17 November 1991 to 29 May 1994** ([para 2]). 

The defendants initially asserted that the statements made in the articles were substantially true and published for public benefit. Subsequently, they sought to amend their plea to include three additional defences: (1) Third defendant's ignorance of the defamatory nature of the articles, (2) Fourth defendant's similar ignorance, and (3) the defence of constitutional protection under the freedom of expression clause as per Section 15 of the **Interim Constitution of the Republic of South Africa** (Act 200 of 1993) ([para 3]). The High Court, presided over by Eloff JP, dismissed the application's amendment due to concerns that the third defence was "bad in law," thereby not considering the potential validity of the first two defences ([para 4]–[5]).

### I – Issues
The principal issue for determination by the appellate court was whether the amended plea of the appellants would be excipiable, specifically, the lawfulness of their publication in the context of the defamation action. This included examining the viability of the third proposed defence related to constitutional protections of freedom of speech and the implications of strict liability for defamation as traditionally applied within South African law ([para 8]). 

Additional issues reflected on whether a lack of knowledge of the defamation could serve as a valid defence under common law versus constitutional standards ([para 14]–[15]).

### R – Rules
1. **Common Law Defamation**: The principles dictating liability in defamation claims hinge on an objective element of unlawfulness and a subjective element of fault, particularly the requirement of *animus injuriandi* (the intention to injure). Previous cases dictated that liability extended to owners, editors, publishers, and printers unless they could demonstrate a lack of negligence or knowledge of the defamatory material ([para 9]–[12]).

2. **Pakendorf v De Flamingh** (1982 (3) SA 146 (A)): This key precedent established that strict liability exists in defamation cases involving newspapers, meaning liability could endure regardless of the defendants' lack of knowledge or intention to defame ([para 11]). 

3. **Constitutional Principles**: The **Interim Constitution** particularly Section 15 provides for freedom of expression, including freedom of the press, and calls for a balancing of this right against the right to reputation under Section 10 concerning dignity ([para 41]).

### A – Application
The court found that the third proposed defence, which sought to assert the lawfulness of the publication based on the defendants' ignorance and good faith, was an under-explored area of law. The concept that lawful publication could exist even with untruthful information was nuanced and required fresh interpretation in light of constitutional values ([para 29]–[30]).

The decision in this case crucially recognizes an evolving jurisprudence around defamation that balances the traditional concerns of reputational harm against the need for robust media freedom. It reflected on the fact that common law principles had to adapt to contemporary values, influenced by the **Interim Constitution**, that champion free speech as essential to democracy, questioning the rigid application of strict liability for media defendants ([paras 14]–[15], [41]–[46]).

Ultimately, the reasoning articulated by the judges suggests that the lawfulness of the publication should hinge on a consideration of reasonableness, taking into account the context, nature of the allegations, and public interest ([para 29], [30]). 

### C – Conclusion
The Court ruled in favour of the appellants, allowing the amendment of the plea and concluding that the amended defence was not excipiable, effectively recognizing the necessity to reevaluate the strict liability framework within the context of media publishing under South African constitutional law ([para 47]). The implication is a significant shift toward acknowledging the role of *freedom of expression* as enshrined in the Constitution and reconsidering traditional defamation standards, reflecting a growing commitment to balancing competing rights in a democratic society.

This decision serves as a pivotal juncture in defamation law, indicating that publishers may have recourse to constitutional protection, provided they can demonstrate due diligence and reasonable conduct in their publishing practices.
