Maxwele Royal Family & Another v The Premier of the Eastern Cape Province and Others (2023) ZASCA 73

The appellants in this case are the Maxwele Royal Family (MRF), a customary institution within Khwenxurha Location, Zimbane, and Asiphe Solanga Maxwele, the presumptive heir to the headman position following the death of his father, Mzimtsha Maxwele, in 2008.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–10])

The appellants in this case are the Maxwele Royal Family (MRF), a customary institution within Khwenxurha Location, Zimbane, and Asiphe Solanga Maxwele, the presumptive heir to the headman position following the death of his father, Mzimtsha Maxwele, in 2008. The first respondent is the Premier of the Eastern Cape Province, and the second is the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for the Department of Co-operative and Traditional Affairs. The third respondent, Baxolele Maxwele, was appointed acting headman of Zimbane by the MEC on 20 August 2020, which the appellants contested as unlawful.

The background of the case reveals that until 2008, Mzimtsha served as headman. Following his death, Mrs. Nomthandazo Maxwele (Mrs. Maxwele) was appointed regent for her son, Asiphe, due to his minority status. On 26 January 2017, while Mrs. Maxwele was still in office, the MEC instructed the Sangoni Royal Family (SRF) to identify an acting headman, leading to the appointment of Baxolele as acting headman, effectively ending Mrs. Maxwele’s regency. The Maxwele family contested the validity of this decision, asserting that they had correctly identified Asiphe as the successor under the Eastern Cape Traditional Leadership and Governance Act 4 of 2005 (EC Act of 2005).

The MRF initially sought to have the MEC's decision reviewed and set aside in the high court. The application faced several challenges, including questions of authority and the validity of the MRF as a legal entity. The high court dismissed the application, largely resting on the findings that the MRF was not a recognized royal family and that the SRF possessed the authority to identify headmen. The appellants then appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, challenging the legality of appointments made without due process.

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 2, 3, 10, 11])

The primary issue in this appeal is whether the MEC’s decision to appoint Baxolele Maxwele as acting headman of Zimbane was lawful, particularly in the context of the MRF's assertion that Asiphe was the rightful successor as headman following the established customary laws embodied in the EC Act of 2005. Specific legal questions include:

  1. Did the MEC have the authority to appoint Baxolele in light of the prior identified successor, Asiphe?
  2. Was there a lawful basis for the termination of Mrs. Maxwele's regency?
  3. Did the MRF possess the legal standing and authority to challenge the MEC’s decision?

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 5, 10, 12, 14, 16])

The court based its judgment on several legal frameworks, primarily the EC Act of 2005 and its related sections, specifically:

  • Section 18 establishes the procedure for identification and recognition of a successor to the traditional leadership role.
  • Section 21 governs the recognition of regents and their terms.
  • Section 22 outlines the policy for appointing acting headmen under specific circumstances.
  • The Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 informed the court’s understanding of the community status required for recognition as a traditional leadership entity.

The court also referenced previous decisions regarding the recognition process for traditional leaders, emphasizing the need for adherence to established procedures.

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 11–16])

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the MEC and Premier had acted unlawfully by not recognizing Asiphe Maxwele following his identification as the rightful successor. The identification had been formally made in accordance with the EC Act of 2005 prior to Baxolele's appointment, and the prior legislative framework necessitated that the MEC step aside from appointing another acting headman without first addressing the identification of Asiphe.

The court highlighted that the recognition of a headman must follow legal procedures as stipulated in the respective legislation, which the respondents had disregarded. Significantly, the court noted that the MEC's and Premier's decisions contravened prior administrative actions that had recognized Asiphe's status as the identified successor due to the supremacy of the identified successor in traditional governance protocols.

Furthermore, the court asserted that the procedural irregularities raised questions about the legitimacy of the MRF’s qualifications, which had been unnecessarily complicated by the lower court's determinations regarding the MRF's status as a valid legal entity. The ruling clarified that prior decisions issued by the same judge undermined the integrity of judicial process, rendering the judgment liable to be overturned.

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 16–17])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of the appellants, declaring that the MEC’s decision to appoint Baxolele Maxwele was unlawful and consequently reviewed and set aside the high court's decision. The court determined that the identification of Asiphe as headman had not been lawfully overridden. The ruling emphasized that the processes prescribed by statutory law must be diligently followed in the appointment and recognition of traditional leaders.

The implications of this ruling extend into the realm of administrative law, particularly within the contexts of traditional governance in South Africa. It delineates the requirements of lawful succession and the proper adherence to established legal frameworks in recognizing traditional authority in communities. Additionally, the case reaffirms the necessary link between customary law and the statutory framework governing traditional leadership.

The final order mandated that costs of the appeal, including those of two counsels where applicable, be borne by the respondents, thereby confirming the appellants' successful challenge against the earlier decisions and underscoring the importance of due legal recognition within traditional governance systems.
