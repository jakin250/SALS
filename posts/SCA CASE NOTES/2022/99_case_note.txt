Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: The Memorable Order of Tin Hats v Kenneth Paul Els (488/2021) [2022] ZASCA 99

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–5])
The appellant, the Memorable Order of Tin Hats (the M.O.T.H), is a voluntary association comprised of former South African soldiers, operating from its premises—M.O.T.H Hall—located in Parkhurst, Johannesburg. The Hall includes areas used by both the M.O.T.H and a school ([1]). Mr. Kenneth Paul Els, the respondent, frequently visited the M.O.T.H Hall pub. On 18 January 2014, while assisting a wheelchair-bound patron, Mr. Pat Levengs, up a two-step flight of stairs outside the pub, Mr. Els sustained injuries ([2]).

Mr. Els instituted legal proceedings for delictual damages, claiming negligence by the M.O.T.H based on several alleged omissions, including a failure to provide appropriate handrails and ramps, as well as failing to ensure that the staircase met safety regulations ([3]–[4]). Initial rulings favored Mr. Els, with the trial court finding 90% liability against the M.O.T.H, which subsequently prompted an appeal to the full court ([5]). The appeal was dismissed, leading the M.O.T.H to seek special leave for a further appeal, which was granted.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 14–21])
The central legal issues before the court were whether the M.O.T.H had been negligent in failing to provide safe access to its premises and whether that negligence was causally connected to the injuries sustained by Mr. Els. The key questions included:
1. Did the M.O.T.H owe a duty of care to ensure the safety of individuals accessing its premises? 
2. Was the omission of not installing additional safety measures, such as a second handrail, wrongful and negligent?
3. Was there a direct causal link between the M.O.T.H's omissions and the injuries sustained by Mr. Els? ([13], [14], [22]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 17–24])
The court relied on established principles of delictual liability, emphasizing that:
- Negligence arises from a failure to act where a duty exists, requiring a balance of public policy considerations ([17]).
- An omission does not attract liability unless it violates a legal duty ([18]).
- The plaintiff must prove that there was a culpable causative relationship between the omission and the harm suffered ([24]).

Key cases cited include:
- *The South African Hang and Paragliding Association v Bewick* [2015] ZASCA 34 for the necessity of proving factual circumstances supporting negligence claims ([19]).
- *Kruger v Coetzee* 1966 (2) SA 428 (A), establishing the plaintiff's burden of proof regarding negligence and causation ([20]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 22–25])
The court recognized that the M.O.T.H had a legal duty to take reasonable measures to ensure patron safety, thus acknowledging that their omission to install a second handrail constituted negligence ([21]). Specifically, the court assessed the causation factor, emphasizing that Mr. Els had not sufficiently proven that the absence of the second handrail directly contributed to his fall ([22]).

Contrary to the findings made at earlier hearings, the court concluded that while the M.O.T.H’s omission was negligent, it did not establish a sufficient causal link to Mr. Els’s injuries. Testimonies suggested that the fall occurred not solely due to the lack of safety features but as a result of Mr. Els's actions while attempting to assist Mr. Levengs. Thus, it was deemed improbable that the installation of an additional handrail would have prevented the accident, as the significant weight of the wheelchair and its occupant contributed to Mr. Els losing his balance ([24]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 25–26])
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the M.O.T.H’s appeal, concluding that while the omission to provide a second handrail was wrongful, Mr. Els had failed to demonstrate a culpable causative relationship between this negligence and his injuries. The court underscored the vital principle that the burden of proof lies upon the plaintiff to establish that the alleged negligence was a direct cause of the harm inflicted ([25]).

As a result, the court set aside the order of the full court and dismissed Mr. Els's claim with costs, emphasizing the importance of evidential substantiation in negligence claims. This case reinforces that not all negligent omissions lead to liability without a demonstrable link to the damages suffered ([26]).
