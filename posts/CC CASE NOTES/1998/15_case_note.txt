Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: The National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality v. Minister of Justice and Others [1998] CCT 11/98

## F – Facts ([1]–[6])

This case arose from a High Court judgment, presided over by Heher J, which declared various legal provisions regarding sodomy unconstitutional. The first applicant, the National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality, alongside the South African Human Rights Commission, challenged the constitutionality of **section 20A of the Sexual Offences Act, 1957**, and the inclusion of sodomy in **Schedule 1 of the Criminal Procedure Act, 1977** and **the Security Officers Act, 1987**.

The applicants sought confirmation from the Constitutional Court under **section 172(2)(a)** of the 1996 Constitution. They contended that the common-law offence of sodomy was inconsistent with sections regarding equality and human dignity enshrined in the Constitution ([1], [4]).

The first respondent, the Minister of Justice, initially opposed the application but withdrew this opposition after the applicants narrowed their claims. The other parties did not contest the application ([5], [6]).

## I – Issues ([2]–[9])

The central issue was whether the challenged legal provisions discriminated against individuals based on their sexual orientation and violated the Constitution’s guarantees of equality and dignity. Particular attention was given to:

1. The validity of the common law offence of sodomy.
2. The constitutionality of section 20A of the Sexual Offences Act, which specifically criminalized male homosexual acts.
3. The implications of including sodomy in the relevant schedules of existing statutes, which carried legal consequences for individuals based on their sexual orientation.

The court also had to address procedural questions regarding the confirmation of the High Court's declaration of invalidity, including the status of the common law and the potential retroactive application of its decision ([2], [3], [9]).

## R – Rules ([10]–[12])

The Court's decision was primarily guided by the **1996 Constitution**, particularly:

- **Section 9** (Equality): Mandating equal protection and benefit of the law and prohibiting unfair discrimination based on sexual orientation.
- **Section 10** (Dignity): Establishing that everyone has inherent dignity and the right to have it respected and protected.

In analyzing the situation, the Court referenced previous judgments that dealt with the constitutional implications of discrimination based on sexual orientation, such as **Harksen v Lane NO** and **S v K**, highlighting the presumption of unfairness in cases of discrimination based on listed grounds, including sexual orientation ([10], [11]).

## A – Application ([11]–[40])

The Court identified that the common law offence of sodomy operated to discriminate against individuals based on their sexual orientation, thus violating section 9 of the Constitution. The past jurisprudence recognized that such laws enforced an unjust differentiation between homosexual and heterosexual conduct without a legitimate governmental purpose.

1. **Common Law Offence of Sodomy**: The Court affirmed that the offence was inconsistently applied, as it criminalized specific sexual acts between men while not treating heterosexual or lesbian sexual acts similarly. The Court held that no reasonable justification for this discrimination existed ([11]–[15]).

2. **Section 20A of the Sexual Offences Act**: This section criminalized sexual acts between men at a party, ensuring unequal treatment under the law and contributing to the stigmatization and marginalization of the gay community. The court found it constituted direct discrimination on the basis of sexual orientation, and similarly could not withstand constitutional scrutiny ([16]–[20]).

3. **Inclusion of Sodomy in Criminal Provisions**: The implications of sodomy being included in the broader statutory provisions were deemed unconstitutional as they contributed to systemic disadvantage; thus, the inclusion in both the Criminal Procedure Act and the Security Officers Act was equally void ([21]–[30]).

The Court held that these discriminatory laws fostered a hostile environment that contributed to the devaluation of the dignity of gay men in society. Therefore, reliance on historical societal norms to uphold such laws was deemed insufficient and unjustifiable ([31]–[40]).

## C – Conclusion ([83]–[106])

The Constitutional Court, with Ackermann J delivering the majority judgment, confirmed the High Court's order declaring:

1. The common law offence of sodomy unconstitutional and invalid.
2. Section 20A of the Sexual Offences Act unconstitutional and invalid.
3. The inclusion of sodomy in Schedule 1 of the Criminal Procedure Act and in the Security Officers Act unconstitutional and invalid.

Specific limitations were placed on the retroactive application of these findings, ensuring that only those involved in consensual sodomy were eligible for appeals or redress regarding past convictions, while allowing the law to retain its protective measures against non-consensual acts ([83]–[106]).

This landmark judgment not only reinforced the constitutional protections against discrimination but also marked a significant step toward greater societal acceptance and legal acknowledgment of the rights of LGBTQ+ individuals in South Africa. The implications extend beyond the legal landscape, promising a shift in public attitudes and governmental policies towards inclusivity and respect for diversity.
