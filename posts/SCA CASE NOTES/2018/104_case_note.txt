# Case Note: Ludidi v Ludidi & Others (658/2017)

### FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts
The case revolves around the succession to the chieftaincy of the amaHlubi tribe in Qumbu, Eastern Cape, between the appellant, Mr. Ludidi Ludidi, and the first respondent, Ms. Nolitha Ludidi. Both parties claim to be successors to Chief Dyubhele Joubert Ludidi, who passed away after a long tenure. Mr. Ludidi Ludidi is the son of the previously installed chief's "right hand" wife and Ms. Nolitha Ludidi is the daughter of his "great wife," and the eldest surviving child of the late Chief Manzodidi, who succeeded after Chief Dyubhele.

Upon Chief Dyubhele's death, the Hlubi Royal Family identified Ms. Nolitha Ludidi for recognition as chief, while the Mancaphayi Royal Family backed Mr. Ludidi. After a prolonged dispute, the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs did not recognize either candidate but referred the matter to the Eastern Cape House of Traditional Leaders for recommendations. Ultimately, the MEC recognized Ms. Nolitha Ludidi as chief in 2015, but this recognition was contested by Mr. Ludidi, who argued that the process ignored established customs and statutes regarding traditional leadership ([paras 1-10]).

#### I – Issues
The primary legal issues before the court derived from Mr. Ludidi's appeal against the MEC’s recognition of Ms. Nolitha Ludidi as chief, which included:
1. Whether the appellant had a legitimate expectation of succeeding to the chieftaincy based on the purported identification by his father.
2. Whether the MEC acted arbitrarily by recognizing only one faction of the royal family without due consideration for the Mancaphayi Royal Family's claims.
3. Whether the MEC was obliged to afford Mr. Ludidi a hearing prior to the recognition of Ms. Nolitha.
4. Whether the failure of the MEC to inform the House of Traditional Leaders prior to recognition rendered the process null and void ([paras 17-18]).

#### R – Rules
The legal framework governing the recognition of traditional leaders is primarily established by the *Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003* and the *Traditional Leadership and Governance Act, 2005 (Eastern Cape)* (Act 4 of 2005). Specifically, Section 18 of the Provincial Act mandates that a royal family identifies a chief and informs the Premier, who must then recognize that individual in accordance with applicable customary laws ([paras 21-22]).

The concept of legitimate expectation is addressed in various legal precedents which stipulate that for a claim to be valid, it must be unambiguous, reasonable, and based on clear representations made by the decision-makers ([para 24]).

#### A – Application
The court analyzed the appellant’s claims by first acknowledging that the expectations tied to custom and prior appointments under repealed laws do not hold under current legislation. The court leaned heavily into the statutory provisions which state that the recognition of a chief must align with the rights vested in the royal family as core custodians of their traditions ([paras 20-21]).

Concerning the appellant's claim for a hearing, the court pointed out that he had ample opportunity to air his views through the royal family's deliberations. Thus, it found that the MEC's actions complied with procedural fairness ([para 27]).

On the matter of the MEC’s oversight in communicating with the House of Traditional Leaders before publishing the recognition, the court determined that the failure did not invalidate the recognition process. The court emphasized that the identification of leadership is a matter for the royal family and that the House's notification serves a more administrative function and is not contingent upon an additional consultation that could delay or nullify the process ([paras 28-32]).

#### C – Conclusion
The court dismissed Mr. Ludidi’s appeal, agreeing with the lower court's determination that there was no basis for his legitimate expectation of succession based on the invalid claim his father had to the chieftainship. It upheld the MEC’s recognition of Ms. Nolitha Ludidi, affirming the decision made by the Hlubi Royal Family.

The ruling emphasizes the importance of adhering strictly to both customary practices and statutory requirements in the recognition of traditional leadership, reinforcing the authority of royal families as primary decision-makers in succession matters ([paras 32-33]). There was no order for costs as the nature of the right asserted was sensitive and deeply tied to community structures ([para 32]).
