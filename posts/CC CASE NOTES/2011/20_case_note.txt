Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: Arnold Michael Stainbank v South African Apartheid Museum at Freedom Park and Another [2011] ZACC 20

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The applicant, Arnold Michael Stainbank, filed a case at the Constitutional Court of South Africa to challenge a ruling made by the North Gauteng High Court relating to his urgent application to stay the taxation of a bill of costs. The High Court had previously dismissed his application, which claimed bias and apprehension of bias from Acting Justice Ebersohn ([1]). The dispute stems from a lengthy litigation history over the trademark "The Apartheid Museum." The applicant first registered the trademark in 1990 and later faced counteractions from the first respondent, who also claimed rights to the museum concept, eventually leading to a punitive costs order against Stainbank ([2]–[3]).

Following a series of actions, the High Court ordered Stainbank’s attorney to pay costs from his own pocket due to procedural misconduct, including the improper setting down of a motion for hearing at an unconventional hour. This culminated in an order barring Stainbank from initiating further litigation without first settling the costs ordered against him ([15]–[16]). The applicant sought amendment in the appeal’s relief to assert that the proceedings were vitiated by bias ([21]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal questions raised in this case are:
1. Whether the conduct of Acting Justice Ebersohn during the proceedings constituted actual bias or gave rise to a reasonable apprehension of bias against the applicant ([19], [31]).
2. Whether the costs order made by the High Court was proper and competent, given the circumstances surrounding the case ([30]).

### R – Rules
The adjudication of bias in judicial proceedings relies on established legal principles. The test is whether a reasonable person, apprised of all relevant circumstances, would perceive that a judge may not be impartial. This standard has been articulated in several judgments, setting precedents that underscore the necessity for judicial officers to maintain decorum and impartiality ([35]–[36]). The decision in *SACCAWU* emphasizes that such bias allegations should not be frivolously raised but must demonstrate concrete evidence ([36]).

### A – Application
In analyzing the case, the court noted that while the judge displayed frustration towards the applicant’s attorney, the overall context was critical. The remarks regarding the attorney's conduct, although unfortunately worded, did not solely manifest bias. The applicant’s attorney had committed procedural infractions that placed the court under considerable pressure, particularly in an urgent motion environment where compliance with court norms is mandatory ([44]–[45]).

Ultimately, the court found that the applicant had not successfully established a case for bias strong enough to overturn the initial rulings. The mere irritation exhibited by the judge, while inappropriate, did not amount to disqualifying prejudice against the applicant. Therefore, the appeal based on bias was not successful ([46]).

In relation to the costs order, the court found two fundamental misdirections in the High Court's reasoning: equating the attorney’s actions with a personal failure of the applicant and unjustly labeling the recusal application as an act of bullying. These missteps indicated improper conduct in granting punitive costs, justifying interference from the higher court ([48]–[60]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court concluded that while the appeal concerning bias was dismissed, the costs order imposed by the High Court was set aside. The court replaced it with an order that recognized the attorney’s procedural errors without extending punitive measures against the applicant himself. The final ruling outlined that the attorney would bear costs on a party-and-party scale rather than on an attorney-and-own-client scale, marking a significant contribution towards clarifying the extents of punitive costs in litigation ([63]).

This case affirms the delicate balance courts must strike in upholding judicial propriety while managing the challenges of procedural compliance in urgent matters, with broader implications for future litigation concerning claims of bias and procedural misconduct in South African law.
