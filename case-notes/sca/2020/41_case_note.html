Liberty Group Limited t/a Liberty Life v K & D Telemarketing CC and Others

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 41 (20 April 2020) Coram: Navsa and Van der

Neutral Citation: Liberty Group Ltd v K & D Telemarketing CC (Case no 1290/18) [2020] ZASCA 41 (20 April 2020)

Coram: Navsa and Van der Merwe JJA, Ledwaba AJA

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case revolves around an appeal by Liberty Group Limited (the appellant), which sought to reopen its case after being granted an order of absolution from the instance by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria (Tuchten J). This order arose in a trial that began in 2010, where Liberty had sued K & D Telemarketing (first respondent) for R 515,964.95—commission that Liberty claimed it was entitled to refund due to lapsed insurance policies procured through the appellant. The second and third respondents, Karen Shafer and Eric Butowsky, were joined as sureties.

During the trial, after presenting two witnesses, Liberty closed its case. The respondents then applied for and received an absolution from the instance, with the trial court concluding that Liberty had failed to adequately prove the quantum of its claim. Notably, this judgment became final as Liberty did not appeal, resulting in the prescription of its claim. In subsequent proceedings, Liberty sought to amend its pleadings and re-open its case following the judgment, both of which were dismissed by the trial court as procedurally irregular ([1–9]).

I – Issues

The key legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether Liberty could reopen its case under the same case number and on the same pleadings after an order for absolution from the instance had been granted. This inquiry explored the implications of the prescription laws, procedural norms regarding reopening a case, and potential exceptions to established legal principles such as res judicata and the finality of judgments ([1–10]).

R – Rules

The legal framework involved in this case includes principles around absolution from the instance, the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, and relevant case law. Notably, the following legal precedents were cited:

  1. Colman v Dunbar 1933 AD 141, where the court held that further evidence could be heard in cases where an absolution from the instance had occurred.
  2. Steytler v Fitzgerald 1911 AD 295, which provided that an order of absolution from the instance effectively ends the suit, requiring fresh proceedings for a plaintiff wishing to pursue claims anew ([8–14]).
  3. Provisions of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, concerning limitations on pursuing claims after a prescribed period ([11]).

A – Application

The SCA applied established legal principles regarding the finality of judgments. It indicated that once an order of absolution is made, a court is functus officio; it cannot entertain further evidence in relation to the concluded case. The court emphasized that Liberty had not sought to appeal the decision or challenge the order of absolution, and thus, it could not thereafter reopen the case based on the same pleadings, as doing so would violate principles of legal certainty and finality.

The court also examined Liberty's argument that it should be allowed to re-open the case based on the constitutional right to access courts (per Section 34 of the Constitution). However, this argument was found unpersuasive as Liberty had its opportunity to present their case but failed to provide sufficient evidence ([15–16]). The court reiterated that the procedural aspects Liberty pursued did not entitle them to bypass the ordinary requirements of initiating new legal proceedings.

C – Conclusion

The SCA dismissed Liberty’s appeal, affirming the lower court’s ruling that Liberty could not reopen its case after having been granted an order of absolution from the instance. The ruling highlighted the sanctity of procedural norms within litigation, asserting that the court's function post-absolution is limited, thereby reinforcing the importance of finality in judicial proceedings. Consequently, any further claim by Liberty would have to be initiated afresh, and its failure to do so within the prescription period left it without recourse. The court’s decision has significant implications for future cases involving absolution, reinforcing the clarity of procedural boundaries in litigation ([17]).
