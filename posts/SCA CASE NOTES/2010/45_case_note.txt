Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: The Commissioner for SARS v Foskor (375/09) [2010] ZASCA 45

## Facts
The matter at hand involves a dispute between the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Services (the Commissioner) and Foskor (Pty) Ltd (Foskor) concerning whether extracted mineral-bearing ore constitutes "trading stock" under the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962. Foskor operates as a mining company and primarily engages in phosphate mining and the production of fertilizer ([1]–[6]).

In 1952, Foskor acquired mining rights for various base minerals, including phosphates. From 1979 onwards, it entered into an agreement with Phalaborwa Mining Company Limited (PMC) allowing PMC to mine ores, which included phosphate-bearing material called foskorite, as a by-product of copper mining operations. Over roughly two decades, significant quantities of foskorite were extracted and dumped, awaiting further processing by Foskor ([5]–[9]).

The core of the dispute arose when the Commissioner included a sum of R203,205,437 in Foskor's taxable income for the 1999 assessment year, categorizing it as trading stock. Foskor contested this assertion, arguing not only that the mined ore did not constitute trading stock but also that the Commissioner should remit interest of R51,170,908 charged due to underpayment of tax ([2], [12], [13]). Foskor's appeal to the Johannesburg Income Tax Special Court was successful, prompting the present appeal by the Commissioner ([15]).

## Issues
The legal issues presented in this appeal involve: 
1. Whether the stockpiled foskorite constitutes "trading stock" as defined in the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 ([1]–[2], [16]).
2. Whether the Commissioner should have exercised discretion to remit the interest charged on unpaid tax ([2], [48]).

## Rules
Key legal provisions considered in the judgment include:
1. **Definition of Trading Stock**: As per Section 1 of the Income Tax Act, "trading stock" is defined to include materials produced or acquired for the purpose of manufacture, sale, or exchange ([17]).
2. **Provisions for Tax Assessment**: Section 22 governs the inclusion of trading stock in the taxable income, stipulating the valuation methods for stock retained by the taxpayer at the end of the assessment year ([16]).
3. **Interest on Underpayment**: Section 89quat provides the framework for interest charges on provisional tax underpayment and allows the Commissioner to remit interest under certain circumstances ([49]).

Additionally, the court referenced precedents such as *Richards Bay Iron & Titanium (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner for Inland Revenue* and principles concerning the distinct nature of mining versus manufacturing ([18]–[23]).

## Application
The court analyzed Foskor's appeal against the backdrop of the definitions and principles outlined in the Income Tax Act. It examined the nature of the processes involved in transitioning foskorite into a saleable product. The court noted that while the raw foskorite is not saleable in its unprocessed form, it undergoes a series of complex processes culminating in various mineral concentrates, significantly distinct from the mined ore ([9], [45]).

The court emphasized that the legal interpretation of "trading stock" encompasses materials intended for manufacture, irrespective of their immediate saleability. It found that the processes applied by Foskor did indeed constitute a transformative action, resulting in distinct products that were marketed globally ([40], [48]). The earlier judicial interpretation in *Richards Bay* was similarly applicable, indicating that stockpiled materials do not need to be inherently saleable to be classified as trading stock ([30]).

On the matter of interest remittance, the court considered Foskor’s reliance on prior legal opinions that led it to believe the Commissioner would not classify the stockpiles as trading stock for over two decades. Given the Commissioner’s passive inaction during that time, the court agreed to remit the interest associated with the tax assessment ([48], [52]).

## Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by the Commissioner, concluding that the stockpiled foskorite constituted trading stock under the Act, thereby affirming its inclusion in Foskor’s taxable income. However, the court exercised its discretion to remit the interest on unpaid taxes, acknowledging Foskor's reliance on previous interpretations and the Commissioner’s lack of action over an extended period ([53]). 

This case underscores the flexible nature of legal interpretations in tax law and the importance of understanding the underlying rationale behind statutory definitions concerning trading stock while also addressing equity and procedural considerations in the administration of tax laws.
