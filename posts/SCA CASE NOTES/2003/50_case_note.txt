Case Note: DIE HOOGSTE HOF VAN APPÈL
Source Folder: 2003

# FIRAC Case Note: DIE HOOGSTE HOF VAN APPÈL VAN SUID-AFRIKA

### F – Facts
[1] This appeal concerns the authority of a body corporate as defined by section 36(1) of the Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986 ("the Act") to claim damages for injuries sustained to individual properties that form part of a sectional title scheme, particularly where such properties are not communal property. 

[2] The respondent, Body Corporate Faeriedale, successfully obtained a judgment in the magistrate's court against the appellant, the Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (formerly the Pretoria City Council), for R53,542.24 plus interest and costs. This amount was awarded as damages for harm caused to units 1 and 2, including their roofs, and the boundary wall of the complex, following the negligent actions of the appellant's employees who cut down a tree.

[3] During the proceedings, the sole point of contention was the respondent's right to claim damages regarding the individual units. The appellant appealed to the Transvaal Provincial Division, disputing the magistrate's finding that the respondent had the standing to bring the lawsuit concerning damages to the individual property. The respondent was acknowledged to have proper standing regarding communal property.

[4] In its particulars of claim, the respondent argued that it had suffered damage to property not owned by it but rather by individuals registered as owners. However, the respondent's legal representative clarified at the outset of the magistrate’s court proceedings that the claim was not based on ownership of the damaged property.

[5] The magistrate found that the respondent had been mandated to insure the entire complex and, accordingly, considered itself authorized to act under the powers granted by section 36(6) of the Act.

### I – Issues
[6] The principal legal question is whether a body corporate can act as a plaintiff to recover damages for property belonging to individual members when such property is part of a sectional title scheme.

[7] Furthermore, it must be determined whether the magistrate properly interpreted the respondent's locus standi in light of the statutory provisions governing bodies corporate.

### R – Rules
[8] Section 36(6) of the Act specifies the locus standi of a body corporate concerning its powers to act in legal proceedings as plaintiff or defendant regarding various matters, including:

(a) Contracts entered into by the body corporate,  
(b) Damage to communal property,  
(c) Issues relating to the land or building for which the body corporate or owners are collectively responsible,  
(d) Matters arising from the exercise of its powers or duties under the Act and relevant rules.

[9] Further jurisprudence, including *Shill v Milner* 1937 AD 101, adds clarity to the interpretation of a party's obligation to its pleadings and the potential implications of not adhering to these obligations.

### A – Application
[10] The appellant argued that the body corporate was limited to claims concerning common property, and there was insufficient evidence to establish whether any portion of the damage related to communal property or individual units.

[11] The court a quo found that the body corporate had established a right to sue based on its obligations to insure the premises and carry out repairs as mandated by the Act. It concluded that damages from the negligent actions causing harm even to individual properties still fell under the purview of responsibilities associated with the body corporate.

[12] However, this interpretation is contested on the basis that the real issue should be the body corporate's right to claim damages for the specific injury (the tree falling) rather than general insurance and repair obligations.

[13] The majority concluded that the damage itself—a direct result of the appellant's negligence—does not align with the body corporate's obligations under subsection (c) or (d). Specifically, the court noted that these subsections concern the body corporate's responsibilities in its capacity to address grievances, not as a plaintiff acting upon indiscriminate damage claims.

### C – Conclusion
[14] Ultimately, the court held that the body corporate did not have the authority under the Act to sue for damages concerning individual properties owned by its members. Consequently, the appeal was upheld, and the a quo court’s judgment was reversed, leading to an order dismissing the body corporate's claim with costs.

[15] This case serves as a critical precedent regarding the locus standi of bodies corporate in claims against third parties for damages affecting individual properties within sectional title schemes, reiterating the need for precise statutory grounding for such claims.
