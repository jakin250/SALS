# Case Note: Richard Gordon Volks NO v Ethel Robinson and Others 
**CCT 12/04**

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The appeal concerns the constitutionality of section 2(1) and the definition of "survivor" under the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act 27 of 1990 (the Act), following a ruling from the Cape Provincial Division [1], [2]. Ethel Robinson, the first respondent, maintained a permanent life partnership with the late Mr. Shandling, which lasted from 1985 until his death in 2001. They cohabited, shared finances, and made life decisions together, but were never legally married [3], [5]. Upon Mr. Shandling's death, his will provisioned Robinson limited assets, leading her to seek maintenance from his estate under the Act, asserting her status as a surviving spouse, despite not being married [8], [10].

The executor of the estate, Mr. Volks, rejected her claim, arguing she did not meet the definition of “spouse” within the legal framework of the Act [9], [13]. Consequently, Robinson and the Women’s Legal Centre Trust challenged the constitutionality of the law, claiming it discriminated against cohabitants in permanent life partnerships by denying them similar protections [12], [12]. The High Court ruled in favor of Robinson, declaring the exclusion unconstitutional, reasoning that unmarried cohabitants should have rights similar to those of married partners [20]-[25].

### I – Issues
The key legal questions revolve around:
1. Whether the exclusion of surviving partners of permanent life partnerships from the Act is unconstitutional, violating the right to equality and dignity as enshrined in sections 9 and 10 of the Constitution [12], [47].
2. Whether the discrimination based on marital status is unfair and, if so, whether it can be justified under section 36 of the Constitution [46]-[230].

### R – Rules
The court's analysis primarily referenced the following legal principles:
1. **Constitutional Framework**: The Constitution prohibits unfair discrimination on several grounds, including marital status (sections 9(3) and 9(5)) [47]-[48].
2. **Legal Definitions**: According to the Act, the term "survivor" specifically referred to a surviving spouse in a marriage dissolved by death, which excludes long-term partners not formally married [40], [41].
3. **Judicial Precedents**: The court cited several precedents such as *Harksen v Lane NO* regarding the rational connection between legislative differentiation and legitimate governmental purposes, emphasizing the need for fairness in assessing discrimination [48]-[49].

### A – Application
The High Court's application of the equality test ultimately established two forms of unfair discrimination:
1. The legal distinction made between married individuals and cohabitants was deemed to violate the dignity of cohabitants, who often fulfilled similar roles and obligations as spouses [22]-[23], [230].
2. The court held that the Act failed to address the reality of many South African families, which include stable domestic partnerships, thus neglecting the needs of economic dependents like Mrs. Robinson. This failure highlighted a lack of acknowledgment of domestic partnerships as valid family forms deserving protection under law [24]-[25].

In the Supreme Court, the appeal led to considerations of whether the rights of survivors of cohabitation could realistically be equated to those of married spouses concerning maintenance and inheritance rights. The court was particularly concerned with the implications of societal prejudices against unmarried partnerships, framed by the broader socio-economic context in which many South Africans live [162]-[166].

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the High Court’s ruling, acknowledging that excluding partners from the Act based on marital status reflected an unfair discrimination that could not be justified [242]-[143]. The decision mandated that legislation should accommodate the rights of surviving partners of permanent life partnerships, recognizing the evolving definitions of family within South African society.

The court provided a 2-year suspension period for the legislature to consider amendments, encouraging a legislative response that reflects the realities of contemporary family forms and addresses their specific needs [238]-[240]. The ruling serves as a pivotal point for future legislative recognition and protection of non-marital partnerships in South Africa, advancing the principles of dignity, equality, and fairness embedded in the Constitution.
