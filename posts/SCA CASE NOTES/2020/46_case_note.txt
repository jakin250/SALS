Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

### FIRAC Case Note: Tsambo v Sengadi (244/19) [2020] ZASCA 46

#### F – Facts
The case centers on the existence of a customary marriage between the deceased, Jabulani Tsambo, and the respondent, Lerato Rubeta Sengadi. The marriage proposal took place on 6 November 2015, and upon acceptance, the families engaged in discussions to formalize the marriage in accordance with customary law ([1–2]). Lobola negotiations were undertaken on 28 February 2016, with a fee of R45,000 agreed upon, of which R30,000 was paid as a deposit ([3]). After the negotiations, the families celebrated, during which time Sengadi was dressed in wedding attire and referred to as the "wife" of the deceased, culminating in her acceptance into the Tsambo family ([5–6]). Despite this, the marriage was never registered with Home Affairs ([6]).

Following a series of relationship difficulties, including infidelity and substance abuse by the deceased, the relationship deteriorated, ultimately leading to the deceased's suicide on 23 October 2018. Shortly thereafter, Tsambo's father, Robert Tsambo, contested the recognition of this alleged marriage, refusing Sengadi access to the matrimonial home ([7–9]).

Sengadi filed an application with the Gauteng Division of the High Court, seeking a declaration to recognize her as the lawful wife of the deceased, asserting that a valid customary marriage occurred on 28 February 2016. The appellant opposed this claim, arguing that the necessary customs and rituals were not fulfilled, specifically the "handing over" of the bride, which he contended was crucial for a valid marriage according to customary law ([10–11]).

#### I – Issues
The primary issue is whether a valid customary marriage between the deceased and Sengadi existed, particularly whether the handing over of the bride was necessary for its validity under the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 ([1]). Ancillary to this is the determination of the role of customary practices in the context of evolving interpretations of such law ([12–13]).

#### R – Rules
The relevant legal framework includes the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act, specifically Section 3, which states that, for a customary marriage to be valid:
- Both parties must be 18 or older and consent to the marriage.
- The marriage must be negotiated and celebrated according to customary law ([14]).

The court also referenced various cases pertinent to customary law, including *Mbungela v Mkabi* and *Ngwenyama v Mayelane*, which highlight that the adherence to specific rituals is evolving and that flexibility in the performance of such customs is recognized within the legal framework ([15–19]).

#### A – Application
In adjudicating the appeal, the court carefully examined both parties' assertions. The appellant's argument hinged on the notion that without a physical handing over of the bride, no valid marriage could exist. However, the court emphasized that historical adherence to customs has evolved, noting that a symbolic acceptance and the behavior of the parties and families involved could satisfy the requirements of customary law without strict compliance to ritual ([13–18]).

Evidence showed that after lobola negotiations, the families celebrated, and Sengadi was ceremoniously dressed and introduced to guests as the wife, which the court interpreted as a form of symbolic handing over acceptable under the circumstances. The presence of both families and the actions taken during the celebration were considered indicators of the formation of a marital bond, thus affirming a waiver of formalities that would not invalidate the marriage ([21–28]).

In addressing the appellant's claims that there existed a factual dispute requiring oral evidence, the court found the appellant's denials to be insufficient and unsupported by evidence. No substantial evidence contradicted Sengadi's account, particularly given the corroborating testimonials provided by her relatives present during the ceremony ([19–23]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court's finding that a valid customary marriage was established on 28 February 2016 compliant with the conditions laid out in the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act. The court underscored that strict adherence to the handing over custom was not determinative of the marriage's validity, reinforcing that the spirit of living customary law should guide interpretations in such cases. 

Furthermore, the court noted its unease with the High Court's unilateral declaration of the "handing over" custom as unconstitutional, highlighting that this issue was not properly raised within the context of the original case and should not have impacted the decision ([31–34]). The case underscores evolving standards in customary law and affirms the recognition of marriages based on mutual consent and cultural practices rather than rigid adherence to tradition. 

The appeal was dismissed without costs, signaling an acknowledgment of the complexities inherent in the case and the pro bono nature of the appellant's legal representation ([34–35]).
