Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Minister of Police & 2 Others v Umbhaba Estates (Pty) Ltd & 53 Others

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa involves Umbhaba Estates (Pty) Ltd (Umbhaba) and the South African Police Service (SAPS), whereby Umbhaba sought damages from the Minister of Police, the National Commissioner of the SAPS, and the Provincial Commissioner of Police Service due to alleged police negligence during a strike at its Kiepersol farm ([paras 1–2]). The strike, commencing on July 5, 2007, involved significant violence, including assaults, vandalism, and intimidation of non-striking employees, which escalated over the following weeks ([paras 3–11]).

Umbhaba had taken over the Kiepersol farm only six months earlier, and during the strike, multiple requests for police intervention were made by Umbhaba's management to control the dangerous situation and uphold the law ([para 5]). However, police response was deemed inadequate, often departing shortly after arriving and failing to take meaningful action against the strikers, even after multiple court orders were issued directing the striking workers to maintain a safe distance from farm operations ([paras 12–15]).

Testimonies, supported by video evidence, indicated ongoing violence, including an incident where an employee, Ms. Alida Mkhabela, was injured while the police were present but failed to intervene ([paras 20–40]). The high court found the police negligent for not adequately fulfilling their duty to maintain public order ([para 42]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues at stake were:
1. **Was the conduct of the SAPS in failing to prevent damage during the strike wrongful and negligent?** This addressed whether their actions constituted a breach of the duties assigned to them under the Constitution and the South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995, specifically regarding maintaining public order ([para 26]).
   
2. **Was there a causal link between the police negligence and the damages incurred by Umbhaba?** Though the issue of causation was deferred for future consideration, the existence of wrongful conduct and negligence was to be established ([para 43]).

### R – Rules

The court relied on several key legal statutes and judicial precedents:

1. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa (1996)**, specifically Section 205(3), which mandates that the police must prevent crime, maintain public order, and ensure the safety of the community ([para 26]).

2. **South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995**, which outlines the duties of police personnel with respect to public safety and crime prevention ([para 26]).

3. **Delict principles**, particularly the tests of wrongful conduct and negligence as laid out in *Kruger v Coetzee* and *Mashongwa v PRASA*, established the requirement of reasonable foreseeability and preventability of harm when assessing negligence ([paras 30–34]).

4. **Country Cloud Trading CC v MEC**, which clarified the legal implications of conduct that causes harm, underscoring the duty not to harm others ([para 27]).

### A – Application

The court applied these rules by scrutinizing the evidence presented, particularly the pattern of police inaction in the context of escalating violence during the strike. The judges were convinced that the evidence demonstrated a systemic failure on the police's part to respond appropriately to multiple calls for help. 

1. **Wrongfulness and Negligence**: The court found that the police's conduct was not merely inadequate but constituted a failure to act, given their acknowledged duties under the Constitution. They had a clear legal obligation to respond to crimes in progress, which they manifestly failed to fulfill ([paras 26–41]).

2. **Foreseeability of Harm**: The testimony of farm management, particularly Mr. Plath, coupled with video evidence, highlighted how the standoff with armed strikers indicated a predictable return to violence. The police's retreat from the scene on multiple occasions, thus leaving employees vulnerable, demonstrated a lack of reasonable intervention ([paras 31–39]).

3. **Causation Considerations**: Although causation was not directly ruled upon, the court acknowledged that the damages suffered by Umbhaba were a direct consequence of the police's failure to act as would be expected of reasonable officers under similar circumstances. This indicated a clear pathway towards establishing liability, despite the deferral of the specific causation issue ([para 43]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the high court's finding that the police were indeed negligent and acted wrongfully during the strike at Umbhaba Estates. The appeal was dismissed, and it was declared that from July 5 to July 24, 2007, the police failed to prevent damage to Umbhaba's property and injury to non-striking employees ([paras 1, 2, 45]). 

This case underscores the obligations of police in maintaining public order, particularly during civil unrest, and sets a precedent concerning state liability for failure to adequately protect citizens in a private setting. It highlights the judicial recognition of the duty owed by the police to ensure public safety and the consequences of neglecting such a duty in a volatile environment.
