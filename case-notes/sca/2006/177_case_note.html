Supreme Court of Appeal

Transnet Limited v PNN Chirwa [2006] SCA 131 (RSA)

This case revolves around the dismissal of Petronella Nellie Nelisiwe Chirwa by Transnet Ltd (the First Appellant) from her position as Human Resource executive manager within the Transnet Pension Fund Business Unit.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2006 1995 (LRA). 2

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1]–[2])

This case revolves around the dismissal of Petronella Nellie Nelisiwe Chirwa by Transnet Ltd (the First Appellant) from her position as Human Resource executive manager within the Transnet Pension Fund Business Unit. The dismissal occurred on 22 November 2002, following an inquiry led by her direct supervisor, Patrick Ian Smith (the Third Appellant), regarding her performance, which included allegations of incompetence and poor employee relations. Chirwa contested the inquiry’s fairness, specifically objecting to Smith’s dual roles as both supervisor and presiding officer, arguing it compromised impartiality, especially since she had filed a grievance against him just days prior.

The Johannesburg High Court, presided by Brassey AJ, ruled in favour of Chirwa, deeming her dismissal procedurally unfair due to a breach of natural justice principles. Brassey AJ ordered her reinstatement retrospectively for nine months. This ruling was appealed, presenting two critical legal questions regarding the jurisdiction of the Labour Court and the classification of the dismissal as "administrative action."

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([5]–[5])

  1. Whether the Labour Court has exclusive jurisdiction to hear dismissal disputes under Section 157(1) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA).
  2. Whether Chirwa’s dismissal constituted "administrative action" as defined by the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), and whether it violated her constitutional right to a lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair administrative action under Section 33 of the South African Constitution.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([6]–[12])

The court considered several statutory provisions and case law:

  • Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA):
  • Section 157(1), which grants the Labour Court exclusive jurisdiction over matters within its purview.
  • Section 157(2), which allows the Labour Court concurrent jurisdiction with the High Court in cases involving alleged constitutional violations arising from employment.
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA):
  • Defines "administrative action" and outlines the requirements for fair administrative processes.
  • Precedent cases, including:
  • Administrator, Transvaal v Zenzile and Administrator, Natal v Sibiya, which established that dismissals by public sector employers are subject to principles of natural justice.
  • Fredericks v MEC for Education and Training, establishing the concurrent jurisdiction of the High Court in constitutional matters.
  • Fedlife Assurance Ltd v Wolfaardt and United National Public Servants Association v Digomo, which elucidate that remedies under the LRA do not exhaust other available actions.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([10]–[14])

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the High Court had jurisdiction to hear Chirwa's claim due to the constitutional nature of her challenges, which invoked Section 33 rights. It determined that the existence of a constitutional issue permits access to both the Labour Court and the High Court.

On assessing whether the dismissal itself constituted administrative action under PAJA, the court analyzed the nature of the power exercised in the dismissal process. It concluded that the action of dismissal by Transnet, despite its status as a public entity, did not amount to administrative action as per PAJA; rather, it stemmed more from contractual, employer–employee relations than from the exercise of public authority or power. The court distinguished the facts from precedent cases, asserting that the context had evolved, and the mere status of Transnet as an organ of state did not confer public law characteristics on the employment contract.

Additionally, the court noted that Brassey AJ erred by applying common law principles instead of PAJA provisions, emphasizing that PAJA was intended to codify the right to administrative action and that litigants cannot bypass these provisions by invoking the Constitution directly.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([17]–[19])

The appeal was upheld, overturning the High Court's reinstatement order, which had granted Chirwa substantive relief. The court ruled that the dismissal was not administrative action within PAJA’s definition, thus affirming that her challenge should have been directed through the LRA's mechanisms and remedies.

The ruling confirmed that:

  • Employment disputes involving constitutional rights are indeed triable in the High Court; however, it restricted their characterization as administrative action.
  • The decision effectively delineates the boundaries between administrative law and employment law concerning public entities, emphasizing a need for disputes around employee dismissals to be resolved within the labour framework as established by the LRA.

This case clarifies the interaction between employment law and administrative justice, indicating that while constitutional principles confer rights, the specific remedies afforded by legislation such as the LRA must be pursued within their prescribed frameworks, thus preserving the legislature's intention to regulate labour relations in a distinct manner.
