FIRAC Case Note: Primedia Broadcasting v Speaker of the National Assembly [2016] ZASCA 142

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 142 #### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Proc

FIRAC Case Note: Primedia Broadcasting v Speaker of the National Assembly [2016] ZASCA 142

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–9])

The case arose against the backdrop of a parliamentary session on 12 February 2015 during the State of the Nation Address (SONA) by President Jacob Zuma. The appellants, comprising Primedia Broadcasting, the South African National Editors' Forum (SANEF), the Right2Know Campaign, and the Open Democracy Advice Centre, argued that their constitutional right to view and broadcast parliamentary proceedings was violated due to the actions of the State Security Agency and Chairman of the National Assembly. Two critical incidents prompted the case: the jamming of telecommunication signals by the State Security Agency and the restrictive broadcasting policies that limited media coverage during parliamentary discord involving the Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF) during the SONA, where security personnel forcibly removed EFF members from the chamber. The Western Cape Division of the High Court (Dlodlo and Henney JJ) denied relief for the appellants, with one judge dissenting, subsequent leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was granted ([paras 4–9]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 10–14])

The two primary legal issues involved:

  1. Whether the broadcasting policy and rules adopted by Parliament that govern how broadcastable disorder in the Parliamentary chamber is handled contravened the public's right to an open Parliament, as afforded under sections 59 and 72 of the South African Constitution, thereby unconstitutional ([paras 10–14]).
  2. The legality of the jamming of cellular signals in Parliament by the State Security Agency according to Section 4(1) of the Powers, Privileges and Immunities of Parliament and Provincial Legislatures Act 4 of 2004 ([paras 54–64]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 15–20])

The legal foundations upon which the court relied included:

  • Sections 59 and 72 of the South African Constitution, which guarantee public access to parliamentary proceedings and public participation.
  • The Powers, Privileges and Immunities of Parliament and Provincial Legislatures Act 4 of 2004, particularly Section 21, which regulates broadcasting.
  • Precedents regarding open and transparent government, notably in cases such as Independent Newspapers (Pty) Ltd v Minister for Intelligence Services and Doctors for Life International v Speaker of the National Assembly, addressing the significance of public access and involvement in democratic processes ([paras 21–25]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 30–52])

The court analyzed the regulations concerning parliamentary broadcasts, considering whether the limitations they imposed on public access to live proceedings were reasonable. It concluded that the broadcasting policies served no purpose in preventing disorderly conduct while preventing meaningful public oversight and participation in legislative processes.

The policies governing disorder were found to be excessively restrictive; the court noted that prohibiting the broadcast of such disorder not only fails to serve the parliamentary dignity or function but severely hampers public understanding of parliamentary operations. The court highlighted that incidents of disorder should be visible to foster accountability — the public has a right to comprehend the behavior of their representatives ([paras 30–40]).

The court also addressed the jamming incident, remarking that the State Security Agency's actions violated section 4(1) of the Powers Act because prior authorization from the Speaker was required. This act disrupted the parliamentary process, constituting a breach of the right to an open Parliament and perceived as an overreach of security measures without due process ([paras 54–64]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 65–66])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, declaring that clause 8.3.3.2 of Parliament’s broadcasting policy and Rule 2 of the TV Broadcasting Rules regarding handling disorder were unconstitutional and unlawful for violating the public's right to a transparent Parliament. Furthermore, it ruled that the manner in which the SONA proceedings were broadcast was similarly unconstitutional and noted that the use of telecommunication jamming devices was unlawful.

The judgment emphasized that the public's right to access information on parliamentary proceedings is paramount and that any limitations must be justified and proportionate. This landmark ruling signifies a strong affirmation of the principle of open governance in South Africa and reinforces the importance of accountability in democratic institutions ([paras 65–66]).
