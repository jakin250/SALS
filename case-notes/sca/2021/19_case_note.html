Case Title: City of Cape Town Metropolitan Municipality v Nu-Way Housing Developments (Pty) Ltd (1139/2019) [2021] ZASCA 19 (12 March 2021)

Case Title:

City of Cape Town Metropolitan Municipality v Nu-Way Housing Developments (Pty) Ltd (1139/2019) [2021] ZASCA 19 (12 March 2021)

I. Facts

In this appeal, the respondent, Nu-Way Housing Developments (Pty) Ltd, sought recovery from the appellant, the City of Cape Town Metropolitan Municipality, for a payment of R2 109 009. This amount was claimed as an electricity development contribution that Nu-Way allegedly paid "under protest" ([1]). The basis for Nu-Way’s claim rested upon two grounds: the condictio indebiti and an alternative claim based in contract, following the principles established in Commissioner for Inland Revenue v First National Industrial Bank Ltd 1990 (3) SA 641 (A) ([1]).

Nu-Way applied for the designation of land in Langa under the Less Formal Township Establishment Act 113 of 1991 (LFTEA) for less formal settlement ([2]). The City, upon notification of the application, recommended approval with conditions, one of which required Nu-Way to provide for internal electrical reticulation and the payment of a connection fee and other related charges determined by the City's electricity department ([2–4]). While the residential component was successfully completed and connected, the business erf on which Nu-Way intended to erect a shopping centre faced delays until October 2014, when Nu-Way requested an electricity supply of '1440A three phase at 400V' ([5]).

On application, the City informed Nu-Way of a connection fee of R31 073 and a DC charge amounting to R2 109 009, which Nu-Way did not anticipate and had not factored into its tenants’ leases ([5–6]). To avoid breaching its lease obligations, Nu-Way made the payment subject to the condition that it would be refunded if it was later determined not to be due ([6]). The City contended that the DC charge was legally due and payable based on the relevant tariffs in effect during the application ([7–9]).

II. Issues

The main legal issue presented before the court was whether the DC charge was due and payable at the time Nu-Way applied for the electricity supply in October 2014 ([7]). Additionally, the court needed to evaluate whether the conditions of approval for Nu-Way’s application under the LFTEA prohibited the City from imposing the DC charge, particularly given that the charge was not specified as a prior condition of approval ([18–23]).

III. Rules

The court examined several legal instruments, including the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 (Systems Act), specifically:

  • Section 74(1), which mandates municipal councils to adopt a tariff policy for municipal services.
  • Section 75(1), which requires the adoption of by-laws for the implementation of said tariff policies ([9]).
  • The relevant Electricity Supply By-Law ([11–12]).

The court also referenced the Less Formal Township Establishment Act 113 of 1991, determining whether the LFTEA's provisions created a basis for exemptions from the imposition of municipal charges, particularly when related to non-residential developments ([22–23]).

IV. Application

Judicial analysis focused on interpreting the conditions set forth in the approval granted to Nu-Way and evaluating whether they included the right of the City to impose a DC charge at the time of application ([22–27]). The court rejected Nu-Way's argument that the 2014 policies of the City implicitly prohibited the levying of DC charges, instead holding that the term "conditions" in the approval encompassed requirements for payment, including DC charges, as they relate to the connection to existing municipal infrastructure ([24–26]).

Moreover, the court found the concept of legal retrospective actions inapplicable, clarifying that the terms governing the connection fee and DC charge at the time of application should be considered relevant ([28–30]). This conclusion aligned with the evidence presented that neither the nature of requests for electricity nor the financial demands imposed on developers remained static and were subject to change based on municipal regulations ([28–30]).

V. Conclusion

The court concluded that Nu-Way's contention that the Montarii Development Contribution (DC) was not due and payable was unfounded. The judgment of the court a quo was overturned, thereby establishing that such charges could be applied at the time the electricity supply was requested, which reflected the policies in effect ([31]). Consequently, the appeal was upheld with costs, including those for two counsel, signifying the complexities in municipal law and its connection to development planning and charges ([32]).

Implications:
This ruling underscored the importance of clarity in municipal regulations regarding development charges, illustrating the balancing act local authorities must play between facilitating development and ensuring fair recovery of infrastructure costs. It also reaffirmed that conditions for electricity supply are legally valid when interpreted according to the prevailing laws and regulations at the time of application, thus eliminating any notions of indefinite exemptions based on prior approvals.
