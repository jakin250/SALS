Supreme Court of Appeal

SARS v Terraplas South Africa (Pty) Ltd (375/2013) [2014] ZASCA 69

This case centers around the South African Revenue Service (SARS) and its challenge against a prior determination made at the North Gauteng High Court regarding the tariff classification of certain imported plastic tiles.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 69

F - Facts

This case centers around the South African Revenue Service (SARS) and its challenge against a prior determination made at the North Gauteng High Court regarding the tariff classification of certain imported plastic tiles. The tiles, imported by Terraplas South Africa (Pty) Ltd, are described as "terratile (terraflor) pitch protection tiles," intended for protecting turf surfaces during various events in stadiums ([1]–[4]).

In November 2010, Terraplas submitted a bill of entry for the tiles under tariff heading 3918.90.20, which would subject them to a lower import duty of 1.3% ([2]). However, the Customs Controller later classified the tiles under tariff heading 3926.90.90, attracting an import duty of 10% ([4]). Terraplas contested the classification through an internal administrative appeal, which was dismissed by the National Appeal Committee in August 2011 ([5]).

Terraplas subsequently served a notice of intended litigation as required by section 96(1)(a) of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 (the Act) and proceeded to appeal to the High Court ([6]). The High Court found in favor of Terraplas, arguing that the tiles should rightfully be classified under heading 3918.90.40, as they fulfilled the criteria for "floor coverings" as outlined in tariff heading 39.18 ([8]–[10]).

I - Issues

The central issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the tiles were correctly classified under tariff heading 3926.90.90 by the Commissioner or if they should instead be classified under tariff heading 3918.90.40 as contended by Terraplas ([1]). Specifically, the issues raised included:

  1. Whether the stadium turf could be classified as a "floor" given the context of the tariff headings.
  2. The implications of the tiles' intended use in stadiums for their classification under the tariff provisions.

R - Rules

The legal framework governing this case includes the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964, particularly sections 47(9)(a)(i)(aa) and 47(9)(e), addressing tariff classification and appeals against such determinations ([1], [11]).

The court relied on previous decisions that highlighted the importance of harmonized classifications in international trade, particularly regarding the International Harmonized System ([12]). Key rules in the interpretation of the tariff schedule include:

  1. General Rule 1 of the Harmonized System, which states that classification shall be determined according to the terms of the headings and any relevant section or chapter notes, establishing a prima facie foundation for classification decisions ([15]).
  2. The definitions of terms such as "floor," drawn from the Shorter Oxford English Dictionary, supported a contextual interpretation of "floor covering" ([17]).
  3. Previous case law indicators asserted that the novelty of an article does not warrant a different interpretive process in tariff classifications ([22]).

A - Application

In applying the aforementioned rules, the SCA analyzed the definitions and purposes of the tariff headings in question. The court concurred that tariff heading 39.18, which pertains to "floor coverings of plastics," was not intended to capture protective coverings for natural grass surfaces in stadiums. Instead, the court observed that the tiles did not merely "cover" a floor but aimed to preserve the grass underneath ([19], [20]).

The SCA agreed with the Commissioner’s assertion that the term "floor" should be understood in a conventional sense, and the crucial point was whether the tiles effectively constituted an enhancement of an existing floor. The tiles were noted to only provide a temporary surface for events, failing to qualify under the heading's definition of a "floor covering” that typically implies a more permanent application to existing floors ([20], [21]).

Moreover, the Court emphasized that the interpretations adhered to international treaty obligations, ensuring the uniformity necessary for international trade classifications ([13], [14]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately overturned the High Court's ruling, concluding that the Commissioner’s tariff classification under heading 3926.90.90 was indeed appropriate and upheld the appeal with costs ([24]). The ruling reinforces the understanding that classifications in customs and excise law hinge not only on statutory language but also on the substantive characteristics and intended use of goods, aligning with international standards and trade regulations.

The implications of the decision extend beyond the specific case, underscoring the necessity for clear categorizations in tariff headings and the importance of preserving established legal interpretations in taxation law and international trade contexts.
