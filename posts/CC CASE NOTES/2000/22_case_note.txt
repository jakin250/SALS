Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2000

## FIRAC Case Note: South African Association of Personal Injury Lawyers v Heath and Others

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–9])

The case arises from the appeal of the South African Association of Personal Injury Lawyers (the appellant) against the decision of the Transvaal High Court, which dismissed its application challenging the constitutionality of the Special Investigating Units and Special Tribunals Act 74 of 1996 (the Act) and Proclamations issued thereunder ([paras 1–8]). The Act was intended to address serious malpractices in state institutions and to facilitate investigations into corruption and maladministration ([para 1]).

In March 1997, the President established a Special Investigating Unit (SIU) with wide-ranging powers to investigate allegations of misconduct by state officials ([para 5]). Controversies arose when allegations were referred to the SIU regarding attorneys' management of claims from the Road Accident Fund (RAF), which the appellant argued was beyond the scope of the Act ([paras 6–7]). Ultimately, the appellant sought orders declaring specific provisions of the Act unconstitutional due to infringements on judicial independence, the right to privacy, and an alleged lack of authority to investigate the alleged misconduct of attorneys ([paras 8–9]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 10–11])

The principal issues raised by the appellant in this appeal include:

1. Whether the appointment of the head of the SIU (the first respondent) under section 3(1) of the Act is inconsistent with the Constitution as it undermines judicial independence and the separation of powers ([para 11a]).
   
2. Whether the Proclamation referring allegations concerning attorneys’ conduct in managing RAF claims is invalid as it exceeds the Act’s intended scope ([para 11b]).
   
3. Whether the powers of search granted to the SIU contravene section 14 of the Constitution, which protects the right to privacy ([para 11c]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 12–14])

The Court deliberated using constitutional provisions, particularly those governing the separation of powers and judicial independence. The authoritative points of law include:

- Section 165 of the Constitution, which emphasizes the independence of the judiciary ([para 27]).
- The principle of legality from the Constitution that necessitates legislation to comply with constitutional mandates ([para 20]).
- Relevant precedents from both local and international jurisdictions pertaining to the separation of powers and judicial independence, including [Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council and Others 1999 (1) SA 374 (CC)] and comparative case law from the United States and Australia ([paras 20–29]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 19–62])

The Court examined the nature of the powers conferred upon the SIU and the implications of the first respondent being a judge. It held that:

1. The functions assigned to the head of the SIU are predominantly executive rather than judicial, which raises concerns regarding the integrity of judicial independence. The Court noted that an indefinite appointment to a role that demands executive tasks could blur the lines separating the judiciary from the executive ([paras 35–39]).

2. The investigation topics referenced in the Proclamation concerning attorneys do not fall under the statutory definitions of misconduct relevant to the Act, implying a lack of authority for the referral. The allegations were overly broad and failed to specify conduct that could cause serious harm to public interests, failing the statutory criteria established in section 2(2) of the Act ([paras 55–61]).

3. The Court further noted that the powers of search assigned to the SIU are seldom used and therefore did not present an immediate threat to the appellant or its members. Consequently, the challenge to the constitutionality of section 6 of the Act was rendered unnecessary ([paras 66–67]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 68–70])

The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the appeal, declaring section 3(1) of the Act and Proclamation R24 of 1997 unconstitutional and invalid due to violations of the separation of powers ([para 70]). The Court emphasized that while the intent of the Act was valid in combating corruption, the execution of its provisions must not infringe constitutional tenets. The declarations of invalidity were suspended for one year to allow for legislative amendments and orderly transitions to an appropriate head of the SIU who is not a judicial officer.

This judgment underscores the intricate balance needed between effective governance and adherence to constitutional principles, reinforcing judicial independence as essential for maintaining checks and balances in South Africa’s democratic framework.
