Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: **Sali v National Commissioner of the South African Police Service and Others** [2014] ZACC 19

## F – Facts

[1] This case concerns an application for leave to appeal brought by Daluxolo Nicholas Sali (the applicant) against the decision of the Labour Court, which dismissed his claim of unfair discrimination based on age in employment. The applicant was previously a police reservist stationed at the Humewood Police Station in Port Elizabeth, and he alleged that the South African Police Service (SAPS) unlawfully prevented his appointment due to his age. The respondents included the National Commissioner of the SAPS, the Provincial Commissioner, and the Minister of Safety and Security.

[2] The applicant joined the SAPS as a reservist in 2006 and sought full employment with the SAPS after years of service. A significant aspect of the case revolves around Regulation 11(1) of the SAPS Regulations, which stipulated that applicants for permanent positions must be between the ages of 18 and 30. Following protests by reservists regarding their employment status, a Task Team recommended amendments to increase the age limit for reservists to 40 years. The National Commissioner approved this amendment, but with conditions: reservists must have three years of experience and be under 40 years old.

[3] The applicant applied for vacancies in 2009 and was informed that his application was unsuccessful due to his age, as he was over the newly established age limit of 40 years. Following unsuccessful attempts to mediate through the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA), he lodged a claim in the Labour Court, arguing that the refusal to appoint him constituted unfair discrimination in terms of the Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998.

[4] The Labour Court dismissed his application, stating that the discrimination was not based on an employment policy or practice, as it stemmed from legislation. The Court denied him leave to appeal, leading the applicant to seek relief from the Constitutional Court.

## I – Issues

[5] The primary legal issues presented in this case were:

1. Whether the Constitutional Court should grant leave to appeal from the Labour Court's decision.
2. Whether Regulation 11(1) of the SAPS regulations should be declared invalid on constitutional grounds.
3. Whether the decision not to appoint the applicant was based on Regulation 11(1) and, if so, whether that Regulation fell outside the scope of Section 6 of the Employment Equity Act.
4. If it was not based on legislation, whether the respondents demonstrated that the alleged discrimination was fair.

## R – Rules

[6] The legal framework for this case is rooted in the South African Constitution, particularly Section 9 which prohibits unfair discrimination, and the Employment Equity Act which gives effect to these constitutional rights. Relevant provisions include:

- Section 9(1)–(5) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, which prohibits discrimination on various grounds, including age.
- Section 6(1) of the Employment Equity Act, which restricts unfair discrimination in employment policy or practice.
- Regulation 11 of the SAPS Regulations, which outlines age and experience requirements for appointment and provides for the National Commissioner to waive these requirements under specific circumstances.

[7] The test for unfair discrimination established in **Harksen v Lane NO and Others** [1998] ZACC 12 is also pertinent here, outlining a two-stage process for assessing whether differentiation amounts to unfair discrimination.

## A – Application

[8] In examining the case, the Constitutional Court considered whether leave to appeal was warranted. The Court noted that the issue involved constitutional rights and had implications for employment practices in the public sector. They emphasized that the applicant’s financial situation and the potential for success on the merits justified granting leave.

[9] The Court also addressed the challenge to Regulation 11(1). The applicant did not directly contest this regulation in the Labour Court; however, the pre-trial minute suggested a constitutional challenge. The Court found that due process was lacking as the challenge was not sufficiently clear, and thus, the claim for constitutional invalidity was dismissed.

[10] Despite this, the Court recognized that the applicant claimed discrimination under Section 6(1) of the Employment Equity Act. Hence, it is critical to assess whether the non-appointment of the applicant, allegedly due to age, could be justified as fair discrimination. The evidence presented indicated that individuals over 40 could perform alongside younger recruits, undermining the SAPS's position regarding age-related discrimination.

[11] Brigadier Govender’s assertion that older recruits underperformed in training was dismissed as inadequate due to it being largely based on hearsay, lacking any empirical backing or relevance to the applicant's capabilities.

[12] Ultimately, the Court found that the SAPS failed to demonstrate that the age-based discrimination against the applicant was fair, as it did not effectively justify the limits imposed by Regulation 11(1) and the practice adopted in the waiver.

## C – Conclusion

[13] The Constitutional Court upheld the applicant's appeal against the Labour Court's dismissal of his claim. The Court set aside the Labour Court's order and remitted the matter for a determination of appropriate relief. The Court emphasized the importance of fair employment practices, especially regarding age discrimination, marking this case as significant for future public-sector employment issues.

[14] The ruling signifies a strong affirmation of the constitutional rights enshrined in Section 9, promoting equality and recognizing the impacts of discriminatory practices within state employment, particularly in assessing the relevance of statutory age limits against practical abilities of applicants. The failure of the SAPS to provide adequate justification for age discrimination reflects ongoing challenges regarding equality and representation within law enforcement agencies in South Africa. 

By addressing both substantive and procedural aspects, the Court's decision not only clarifies the application of equitable employment policy but also underscores the procedural rigor required when constitutional challenges are brought forth in labor law contexts.
