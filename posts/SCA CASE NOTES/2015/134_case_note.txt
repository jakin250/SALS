Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Du Preez v Tornel Props (Pty) Ltd (20714/14) [2015] ZASCA 134

## F – Facts

The dispute in **Du Preez v Tornel Props (Pty) Ltd** arises from a building contract established on 10 November 2006 between Lorraine Du Preez (the defendant) and Jonker Projekte CC (the original contractor). The contract involved the construction of a residential house on Erf 4358, The Heads, Lydenberg, Mpumalanga Province, for a total price of R1 million (including VAT) payable in scheduled progress payments following completion of predetermined phases of work ([para 2]).

The building works began, and Du Preez made an initial payment of R80,000. However, Jonker CC was liquidated shortly thereafter. Tornel Props, represented by Mr. Cassim, acquired the rights to complete the construction from Jonker CC and negotiated a new agreement with Du Preez ([para 3]).

In December 2006, the parties orally and partially in writing agreed that the new contract price would be R920,000, excluding VAT. Disputes arose regarding whether the contract price included VAT and whether Tornel Props was obligated to complete certain aspects of the project, such as the swimming pool ([para 4]).

By March 2007, Tornel Props issued an invoice for R240,000, but payment was delayed. On 10 May 2007, a new invoice for R684,000 (including VAT) was issued, leading to further disputes. Tornel Props later stated work would cease unless payment was made, prompting a series of exchanges between legal representatives ([paras 5-9]).

On 15 June 2007, Tornel Props ceased work, asserting that Du Preez's failure to make progress payments constituted a repudiation of the contract. Du Preez's legal team responded by accepting this as repudiation and alleged cancellation of the contract, causing Tornel Props to seek damages ([paras 11-15]).

The North Gauteng High Court initially ruled that Tornel Props did not repudiate but rather that Du Preez's refusal to pay a progress invoice constituted a breach, allowing Tornel Props to cancel ([para 12]). The trial court awarded Tornel Props damages based on an unverified contractor's quotation, which was contested in the appeal ([para 20]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case revolved around:
1. Whether Tornel Props was justified in withholding performance and subsequently cancelling the contract due to Du Preez’s failure to make progress payments ([para 1]).
2. Whether Du Preez's refusal to pay constituted a material breach, thereby allowing Tornel Props to cancel the contract ([paras 2-10]).
3. The determination of damages awarded by the court a quo, particularly whether the reliance on unverified quotations constituted adequate proof of costs ([paras 12-20]).

## R – Rules

The applicable legal principles to the current case include:
1. **Contract Law**: The obligation of parties to perform in accordance with contract terms, including the reciprocal obligations of payment and performance ([para 4]).
2. **Repudiation**: Defined in the *Street v Dublin* (1961) case, where a refusal to perform lawful obligations can amount to repudiation if it shows a clear intention to no longer be bound by a contract ([para 17]).
3. **Assessment of Damages**: The necessity for concrete evidence to substantiate claims for damages, following guidelines observed in *Datacolor International v Intamarket (Pty) Ltd* ([para 18]).

## A – Application

Analyzing the questions of repudiation and cancellation, the court emphasized that the refusal by Du Preez to make progress payments significantly disallowed Tornel Props from continuing its contractual obligations. The standard for determining repudiation is objective—how a reasonable person would interpret the events. Given that Tornel Props had not received any payment and thus could not sustain work, the refusal was deemed justifiable ([para 18]). 

Furthermore, the external disputes regarding the scope of construction (e.g., inclusion of the pool or VAT) did not nullify the fundamental obligation of Du Preez to pay as per the contract ([para 14]). The court also pointed to a lack of evidence supporting the valuation of damages since the quotation provided by Neobuild was unverified and classified as “no evidence at all” ([para 20]).

Ultimately, it concluded that Du Preez’s unjustifiable refusal to pay constituted a repudiation of the agreement, leading Tornel Props to accept the repudiation and validly cancel the contract ([paras 19-20]).

## C – Conclusion

The **Supreme Court of Appeal** partly upheld the appeal, overturning the initial ruling of the High Court. The court concluded that Du Preez’s behavior constituted a breach of contract permitting Tornel Props to justifiably cease performance and claim damages ([para 24]).

The damages were ultimately quantified at R104,817, a figure agreed upon by parties during proceedings. The order was modified to reflect the appropriate compensation owed to Tornel Props and affirmed that Du Preez's counterclaims were dismissed with costs, thereby illustrating the implications of contractual obligations in construction law ([paras 21-24]).

This case underscores the paramount importance of adhering to contract terms concerning reciprocal obligations and the potential legal repercussions involved in non-compliance. Further, it serves as a critical reminder that evidence presented in court must be substantively validated, reiterating the court’s responsibility to ensure that conclusions regarding damages are based on rigorous evidentiary standards.
