Supreme Court of Appeal

Council for Medical Schemes v Selfmed Medical Scheme

The respondents, Selfmed Medical Scheme and Leon Bester, contested the statements as defamatory, claiming damages amounting to R200,000 each against the Council and the Registrar of Medical Schemes.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011

F – Facts:

In August 2005, the Council for Medical Schemes published its Annual Report for the 2004/2005 period, fulfilling its obligations under section 14 of the Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998. Within this report, particularly in the section titled 'Registrar’s Review,' two statements were made regarding the governance of Selfmed Medical Scheme. The statements indicated concerns about the scheme’s chairperson, Leon Bester, who allegedly appointed himself as the principal officer and CEO, as well as the questionable appointments of family members to executive positions within the scheme ([paras 1–2]).

The respondents, Selfmed Medical Scheme and Leon Bester, contested the statements as defamatory, claiming damages amounting to R200,000 each against the Council and the Registrar of Medical Schemes. The North Gauteng High Court ruled in favor of the respondents, awarding them the claimed amounts. The appellants, the Council and the Registrar, appealed the decision, contesting both the merits and the outcome of the damages award, as well as a cross-appeal concerning costs ([paras 2–4]).

I – Issues:

The appeal raised two primary issues:

  1. Defamation Reference: Whether the statements in the Annual Report referred to the first respondent, Selfmed Medical Scheme, and if so, whether they were defamatory in nature concerning both respondents ([paras 3, 50, 56]).
  2. Nature of Defamation: Whether the allegedly defamatory statements were sufficiently damaging in their implications to warrant damages and whether there existed a defence of qualified privilege ([paras 49, 65]).

R – Rules:

The judgment considered established principles of defamation law in South Africa, which require:

  • The plaintiff must prove that the statements complained of refer to them and that such a reference could be understood as defamatory by an ordinary reasonable person ([para 52]).
  • The objective test of defamation evaluates not only the literal meaning of the words but also any implications they may have, requiring a contextual analysis of the publication as a whole ([para 58]).
  • The concept of qualified privilege may protect publications in the performance of a statutory duty, provided it was made without malice ([paras 46, 65]).

The court also referenced FDG Brand and Tsedu v Lekota for guidance on determining whether statements are deemed defamatory and how they should be interpreted within their specific contexts ([paras 56, 58]).

A – Application:

The court focused on whether the statements in question referred to Selfmed and Bester individually, as the appellants contended that the extract, while referencing governance issues, did not conclusively imply that Selfmed was being mismanaged. The term "ostensibly" indicated a question rather than a definitive accusation, and the final remark about the unresolved status of the matter implied ongoing inquiry rather than established misconduct ([paras 61–62]).

In analyzing the statements, the court concluded that the average reasonable reader—likely a member of the medical scheme community or a healthcare professional—would interpret the statements as reflecting ongoing governance questions rather than definitive claims of corruption or impropriety. Thus, the ordinary understanding would not likely pose detriment to the reputations of Bester or Selfmed ([paras 57–65]).

Regarding the issue of qualified privilege, the court expressed that since the Council’s report was a statutory function aimed at transparency in governance, there existed a reasonable belief amongst the appellants that the publication was necessary and not made with malice. Therefore, the statements, in context, could be defended under this principle ([paras 49, 65]).

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal from the North Gauteng High Court, determining that the statements in the Annual Report did not adequately defame either Selfmed or Bester. The statements were seen as part of an ongoing discussion rather than conclusive accusations, hence not lowering the respondents in the estimation of reasonable members of society. Accordingly, the court dismissed the claims for damages and also dismissed the cross-appeal regarding costs, resulting in a final ruling that both the claims and counterclaims be dismissed with costs ([paras 1, 3, 67]).

The judgment reinforces the importance of context in defamation cases and establishes that statements concerning governance issues may not always yield liability under defamation law when they are part of an ongoing investigative process. The ruling illustrates the balance between public interest in governance and the protection of reputational rights.
