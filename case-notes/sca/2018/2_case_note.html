Mandela v The Executors, Estate Late Nelson Rolihlahla Mandela & others (131/17)

In October 2014, the Appellant filed a review application, seeking to annul the Minister's decision to donate the property and the bequest in Mr.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])

The case revolves around Nomzamo Winifred Zanyiwe Madikizela Mandela (the Appellant) and the late Nelson Rolihlahla Mandela's estate (the Respondents). In 1997, the Minister of Land Affairs donated a significant property located in Qunu to Mr. Mandela, following the dissolution of his civil marriage to the Appellant. Upon Mr. Mandela's death in 2013, he bequeathed the property to the Nelson Rolihlahla Mandela Family Trust in a will that also expressed a desire for the property to benefit his family, including his third wife, Graca Machel.

In October 2014, the Appellant filed a review application, seeking to annul the Minister's decision to donate the property and the bequest in Mr. Mandela's will. She contended that she was unaware of the donation until August 2014 and argued that her customary marriage to Mr. Mandela was not dissolved, thus claiming rights to the property.

The Eastern Cape Local Division dismissed her review application primarily due to an alleged unreasonable delay in bringing forth her claim, citing a nearly 17-year period without assertion of her rights. Following this dismissal, the Appellant sought special leave to appeal, which the Supreme Court of Appeal granted.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1, 4, 5, 30])

The central issues before the court included:

  1. Whether the delay in filing the review application was unreasonable and if it should be condoned.
  2. Whether the lower court properly assessed the Appellant’s prospects of success on the merits of her claim.
  3. The applicability of the Biowatch principles regarding costs, particularly against public bodies, in the context of this case ([paras 1, 30, 33]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 8, 9, 17, 19, 31])

The court relied on several legal principles, including:

  • The common law rule concerning unreasonable delay in filing review applications, established in prior cases such as Van Zyl and Gqwetha v Transkei Development Corporation Ltd ([paras 9, 17]).
  • The Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources & others precedent, which provides that costs should not be awarded against unsuccessful litigants seeking to assert constitutional rights in good faith ([para 33]).
  • The Upgrading of Land Tenure Rights Act 112 of 1991 and the Interim Protection of Informal Land Rights Act 31 of 1996 concerning land donation processes ([para 19]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 6–30])

The court found that the Appellant's almost two-decade delay in initiating her review application constituted unreasonable delay. The court noted that while the Appellant claimed ignorance of the Minister's decision until 2014, the circumstances suggested a failure to exercise reasonable diligence in asserting her rights during the divorce proceedings.

The court evaluated the merits of the Appellant’s claims related to her customary marriage status and assertions of her rights to the property. They noted inconsistencies in her narrative, particularly reliance on customary law claims that the court viewed as tenuous, considering the dissolution of the civil marriage and lack of evidence demonstrating her status under Abathembu customs.

The potential for severe prejudice to the heirs of the Mandela estate was emphasized. The court highlighted that Mr. Mandela's death significantly diminished the ability of the estate to challenge the Appellant's claims due to absence of direct evidence from him. The inherent prejudice arising from the delay was viewed as a critical factor influencing the court's discretion against condoning the delay, despite the Appellant's assumed prospects of success on the review's merits ([paras 17–30]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 31–36])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal set aside the costs order against the Appellant concerning the Minister and ordered that each party bear their own costs. However, the appeal against the dismissal of the review application was dismissed, affirming the lower court's findings regarding the Appellant's unreasonable delay and the associated potential for prejudice.

The court concluded that while customary law rights were acknowledged, the unreasonable delay and the resulting potential for significant prejudice to Mr. Mandela's estate justified the refusal to condone the Appellant’s late claims. This ruling reinforces the principles of finality in administrative decisions and emphasizes the importance of timely assertions of rights, particularly in land law disputes involving complex familial and customary issues.

The holding demonstrates the court's balancing act in dealing with the intersection of constitutional rights, customary legal principles, and procedural integrity, illuminating the sensitive nature of claims involving prominent public figures and estates ([paras 34–36]).
