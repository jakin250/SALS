Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note: Director-General: Home Affairs v Dekoba (224/2013) [2014] ZASCA 71

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts:
The case concerns Musena Nicole Dekoba, a Congolese national who came to South Africa seeking asylum, likely in 2004. She was granted an asylum seeker permit in accordance with section 22 of the Refugees Act 130 of 1998 ([1]). Following her asylum application’s rejection by the Refugee Status Determination Officer, Dekoba appealed this decision to the Refugee Appeal Board ([3]). Despite attending the Board on the scheduled date, her case was not heard, and her permit was subsequently extended multiple times over the next two and a half years ([4]–[5]).

On 14 October 2011, during an attempt to renew her permit, Dekoba was arrested as an illegal immigrant upon being informed her appeal had been dismissed due to her non-appearance at a hearing on 4 May 2009 ([5]). After an urgent application to the Western Cape High Court, she was temporarily released but faced deportation risks. She then initiated proceedings to restore her asylum seeker permit that had been confiscated ([6]–[8]). The apppellants, namely the Director-General of the Department of Home Affairs and the Minister of Home Affairs, maintained she had lost her lawful status and was subject to deportation ([4]).

### I – Issues:
The central legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether Dekoba was entitled to the restoration of her asylum seeker permit following the purported rejection of her asylum application. Specifically, it questioned the validity of the dismissal of her appeal by the Refugee Appeal Board ([1], [13]).

### R – Rules:
The relevant legal framework encompasses the Refugees Act 130 of 1998, particularly sections 22 and 24, which govern the issuance and status of asylum seeker permits ([1], [14]). Key judicial precedents include *Arse v Minister of Home Affairs* and *Bula and Others v Minister of Home Affairs*, both underscoring the entitlements of asylum seekers under similar procedural circumstances ([16]). Additionally, the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 provides general principles for fair administrative action ([19]).

### A – Application:
The SCA examined whether Dekoba’s absence from the alleged appeal hearing amounted to the legitimate dismissal of her appeal. The court emphasized the lack of evidence to support the claim that the Refugee Appeal Board had indeed conducted a legitimate hearing or followed due process ([8]–[13]). Notably, the appellants failed to provide testimony from any officials with direct knowledge of the hearing date or process, which led the court to regard their contention as unsubstantiated ([9]).

The evidence presented by Dekoba indicated a chronicled history of renewing her permit with officials without objection, thereby supporting her claim that she had not lost her status as an asylum seeker. This continual renewal suggested the Department had acknowledged her claim whilst her appeal remained pending ([11], [12]). The court found that if indeed, as she alleged, the appeal was never heard, her status as an asylum seeker remained intact, thus invalidating her arrest and the confiscation of her permit ([14], [15]).

### C – Conclusion:
The SCA held that Dekoba was wrongfully deprived of her asylum seeker permit and entitled to its restoration ([19]). The court amended an earlier order to provide for her permit’s restoration directly rather than its re-issue, affirming that it should remain valid pending the outcome of her appeal ([19]). The court expressed disapproval of the appellants' actions, pointing out the lack of due diligence and investigation into Dekoba’s circumstances, thus spotlighting the procedural failures within the administrative processes of the Department of Home Affairs ([17], [18]). 

This case illustrates significant implications for the rights of asylum seekers in South Africa, emphasizing the need for rigorous administrative practices and protection of refugees' legal entitlements within the framework of the Refugees Act and administrative justice principles. The SCA's decision underlines the judiciary's role in ensuring fairness in administrative actions affecting vulnerable populations, affirming the necessity for transparency and accountability within governmental functions.
