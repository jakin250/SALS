Edcon Pension Fund v The Financial Services Board of Appeal and Another (349/07) [2008] ZASCA 65 Case Note

[1] The appellant, the Edcon Pension Fund, commenced an appeal regarding a decision made by Preller J in the Pretoria High Court, which had dismissed a review application against a ruling by the Financial Services Board of Appeal (first respondent).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 65 Case Note

Facts

[1] The appellant, the Edcon Pension Fund, commenced an appeal regarding a decision made by Preller J in the Pretoria High Court, which had dismissed a review application against a ruling by the Financial Services Board of Appeal (first respondent). This ruling pertained to the rejection of transfer applications made by the appellant under Section 14 of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956, as amended (hereafter “the Act”). The rejection was premised on the determination that the applications were inconsistent with the amendments introduced by the Pension Funds Second Amendment Act 39 of 2001, commonly referred to as the surplus legislation ([paras 1-2]).

[2] The Edcon Pension Fund had closed its membership to new entrants on 7 July 1997, subsequently offering existing members the opportunity to transfer to newly established provident funds. During this transition, the fund initiated a restructuring plan aimed at distributing surplus assets. This restructuring occurred prior to the enactment of the surplus legislation, and involved considerable amendments to the fund’s operational rules ([paras 4-5]).

[3] On 7 December 2000, the fund submitted rule amendments for approval, but the Registrar expressed concerns, highlighting the absence of a negotiated settlement concerning the surplus distribution. After a series of negotiations, a modified set of amendments was submitted in June 2001; however, no approval was secured prior to the enactment of the surplus legislation on 7 December 2001. Following the enactment, the Registrar’s office advised that all rule amendments submitted prior to this date would be evaluated under the legal framework as it existed at that time ([paras 6-9]).

[4] Subsequently, on 4 June 2003, the appellant submitted transfer applications under Section 14 of the Act for approval. The Registrar’s office later indicated that it could only approve the transfer of members’ accrued liabilities, and any surplus would need to be addressed per the new legislation ([paras 13-15]).

[5] The appellant sought to appeal this decision, but the Financial Services Board of Appeal ultimately upheld the Registrar's determinations, indicating that the transfer applications did not align with the surplus legislation and failed to provide equitable recognition of stakeholders' interests ([paras 17-18]).

Issues

[6] The core legal question is whether the right to bring the transfer applications under Section 14 of the Act had accrued to the Edcon Pension Fund before the implementation of the surplus legislation on 7 December 2001 ([paras 2, 24]).

[7] A secondary issue arises concerning whether vested rights to benefit enhancements accrued to the fund’s members before the surplus legislation took effect, particularly in light of the registered amendments that were initially deemed effective from 1 September 2001 ([paras 30-32]).

Rules

[8] The relevant legal framework for this case includes sections 12 and 14 of the Pension Funds Act of 1956. Section 12 outlines the requirements for amending pension fund rules, while Section 14 stipulates the stipulations for transfer transactions involving pension funds. Specifically, Section 14 requires compliance with the reasonable and equitable treatment of members regarding the transfer of assets and liabilities upon the dissolution or transfer of fund business ([paras 21, 22]).

[9] Precedential authority from the Supreme Court of Appeal, particularly the Volkswagen decision, establishes the principle that a legal right must accrue when all conditions for its existence are met. Thus, if steps were underway prior to legislative changes, those steps might support claims made under the older law ([paras 20, 24, 29]).

Application

[10] The Supreme Court of Appeal applied the criteria from the Volkswagen case to assess whether the Edcon Pension Fund had accrued a right to have its transfer applications evaluated under the law prior to the surplus legislation. The court reasoned that for a right to accrue, all conditions—including member elections—had to be satisfied before the new legislation came into force. As the elections relevant to transfer options occurred after the surplus legislation was enacted, the court concluded that the right to apply for transfers did not exist prior to 7 December 2001 ([paras 28-29]).

[11] Furthermore, the appellant’s argument that the elections made by members should be deemed to have occurred before 7 December 2001 was dismissed. The court reasoned that, even if one assumed the elections could be legally fictitious, the actual timelines indicated that not only were the member elections delayed, but the applications themselves were submitted after the new legislation had taken effect ([paras 30-32]).

Conclusion

[12] The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling. It characterized the attempts to assert a right under the previous legal framework as unavailing due to the timing of member elections and the lack of requisite applications made prior to the enactment of the surplus legislation. The ruling underscored the principle that rights must be grounded in statutory compliance and cannot be retroactively asserted merely through legal fictions ([paras 33]).

[13] The judgment has critical implications for pension funds and their stakeholders, especially regarding the necessity of aligning operational rules with statutory amendments and the importance of taking prompt action to secure rights prior to legislative changes. This case emphasizes the constraints imposed on pension funds by the surplus legislation, guiding future transfer applications and the expectations of fund members.
