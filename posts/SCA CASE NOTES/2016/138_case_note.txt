Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of *CSARS v Van der Merwe NO (598/2015)*

## F – Facts

The case pertains to the winding-up of Pela Plant Proprietary Limited (in liquidation) (the "Company"), which was unable to pay its debts, leading to a final winding-up order granted on 16 September 2014 following a provisional order on 20 July 2014 ([1]). The Company had purchased heavy-duty earthmoving equipment valued at approximately R25 million, which it sent to the Democratic Republic of the Congo for operations. Upon the return of this equipment to South Africa, customs duties and VAT were due in accordance with relevant legislation but had not been paid. 

Prior to the liquidation, the Company’s equipment was stored in a warehouse under the control of UTI South Africa Proprietary Limited (the "Clearing Agent") and was subject to additional storage fees that escalated to almost R6 million ([4]). The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (the "Commissioner") contended that payment of customs duty and VAT must be made before the equipment could be released to the liquidators ([5]). The liquidators sought a court order to obtain the equipment without paying these amounts, invoking their responsibilities under the Companies Act and the Insolvency Act ([6]).

The court a quo ruled in favor of the liquidators, allowing them to take possession of the equipment despite outstanding duties ([6]). The Commissioner appealed this ruling ([8]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was: Whether sections of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 and the Value Added Tax Act 89 of 1991 created an embargo preventing liquidators from taking possession of property in custody of the Commissioner until the due customs duty and VAT were paid ([7]). This necessitated an examination of whether statutory provisions regarding insolvency allowed the liquidator to act without settling these debts first.

## R – Rules

The court's analysis heavily referenced various sections of the **Customs and Excise Act** (notably ss 20(4), 38, and 39), which govern the payment obligations for imported goods, as well as sections of the **Insolvency Act** that detail the powers and obligations of liquidators ([12], [20]). The **Value Added Tax Act** was also pertinent, especially regarding the treatment of outstanding VAT ([12]).

A core principle outlined in the insolvency law is the equal treatment of creditors (the principle of *concursus creditorum*), stating that all creditors should be treated equally and that no single creditor should receive preferential treatment unless explicitly provided for by law ([9]).

## A – Application

The court examined the statutes to determine whether they created a prohibition for the liquidators to access the equipment without prior payment of customs duties and VAT. It emphasized that the provisions of the Customs and Excise Act are primarily aimed at the regulation of customs duties in normal commercial transactions and are not inherently applicable in the context of insolvency ([20]). This reflects the broader legal understanding that insolvency laws take precedence in scenarios of liquidation.

The court concluded that no provision in the Customs Act expressly indicated that goods subject to a statutory lien must not be released to liquidators under the auspices of the law governing insolvency. The statutory lien provided by section 114 was found to serve only as additional security and not as a complete bar against the liquidators' rights ([24], [27]).

The court dismissed the Commissioner's assertions that the non-payment of duty and VAT constituted an insurmountable barrier to the release of the goods. It reasoned that interpreting the legislation as creating such an embargo would result in practical injustices, preventing the liquidation process from fulfilling its purpose of maximizing asset realization for all creditors ([25]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Commissioner's appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling. The judgment reinforced the notion that the provisions of the Customs and Excise Act do not prevent liquidators from taking possession of an insolvent company’s assets without first paying customs duties and VAT. This ruling has significant ramifications for the treatment of outstanding tax obligations in insolvency proceedings, establishing a precedent that prioritizes the equitable treatment of creditors and the efficient management of insolvent estates.

As a result, the decision underlines the principle that while tax obligations are important, they cannot overshadow the legislative intent and principles underpinning insolvency law, which emphasize proper asset realization for the collective benefit of all creditors ([28]).

The case ultimately contributes to the evolving landscape of insolvency and tax law in South Africa, clarifying the interaction between these legal frameworks and asserting the primacy of insolvency provisions over customs claims.
