# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of *The Minister of Water Affairs and Forestry and Others v Durr and Others* [2006] SCA 101 (RSA)

## F – Facts

[1] The dispute centers around a wildfire that occurred on 13 February 1999 in the Wemmershoek River Valley, Western Cape, originating on land owned by the City of Cape Town and spreading to nearby plantations belonging to the South African Forestry Company (Safcol), the Western Cape Nature Conservation Board, and various private farms including those of Hendrik Jacobus Storm Durr and the Penny Taylor Children's Trust. The exact cause of the fire could not be conclusively determined, although it was established that it began on an "island" created by altered river courses due to the construction of the Wemmershoek Dam in 1959.

[2] Employees of the Working for Water (WFW) project, acting under the direction of the Minister and the Nature Conservation Board, were responsible for felling trees and stacking the resultant vegetation into heaps, thereby creating a significant fire hazard. The stacked wattle trees, known to be highly flammable, ignited during high-risk fire conditions, and the flames spread rapidly, devastating neighboring properties.

[3] Following the fire, three consolidated actions were initiated in the Cape High Court to recover damages, wherein Durr and the Penny Taylor Trust sought damages from Safcol, the Minister of Water Affairs, and Nature Conservation. Subsequently, Safcol filed a claim against the same parties, with Cape Town being added as a third party. The trial judge separated the issues of liability and quantum, with liability addressed in the initial proceedings.

[5] Ultimately, the trial court found the Minister and Nature Conservation jointly and severally liable for a total of 75% of damages, while Cape Town was assigned 25% liability for their role in permitting the fire hazard to exist.

## I – Issues

[10] The pivotal legal question in this case regards the liability in delict for pure economic loss due to the fire's ignition and spread, with a particular focus on the nature of omissions by the parties involved. Specifically, the court needed to determine:
1. Whether the omissions constituting negligence by the Minister and Nature Conservation, as well as Cape Town, were legally wrongful.
2. The applicability of the presumption of negligence under Section 84 of the Forest Act 122 of 1984 and whether it could be rebutted by the appellants. 
3. The question of whether any contributory negligence on the part of Safcol should influence the liability distribution among the parties.

## R – Rules

[11] The case revolved around the interpretation of Section 84 of the Forest Act, which provides that in cases of veld, forest, or mountain fires occurring outside of fire control areas, negligence is presumed unless the contrary is proven. The legal standard for determining whether a negligent omission is unlawful is based on public policy considerations and community legal convictions, as stated in *Minister van Polisie v Ewels* and further elaborated in subsequent cases, including *Minister of Safety and Security v Van Duivenboden*.

[19] The court also applied principles regarding landowner responsibilities concerning fire safety, establishing that a landowner has an obligation to control or extinguish a fire if it begins on their land and has the potential to escape to neighboring properties.

## A – Application

[20] The court determined that as Cape Town owned the land where the fire originated, it bore the primary responsibility to manage that land and prevent the fire hazard from spreading. The court rejected arguments that Safcol or its employees had control over the island and thus should bear responsibility for the fire. The factual backdrop established that the failure of Cape Town to properly manage the condition of the island—specifically stacking flammable vegetation without a viable disposal plan—was negligent and constituted a breach of its legal duty.

[22] In evaluating the actions of the Minister and Nature Conservation, the court found that their employees had created a hazardous situation, failing to act upon known risks related to the stacked wattle. The negligence was compounded by their inaction despite being aware of the fire hazard since at least September 1997. Although the court noted a lack of direct causation between their actions and the ignition of the fire, it nonetheless upheld their liability due to their contributions to an extreme risk environment.

[25] The court also considered arguments related to the impracticality of taking preventative measures. However, it concluded that acceptable fire management practices were not only feasible but required, emphasizing the need for a coherent plan to address generated hazards during the WFW project's implementation.

[27] The court's decision also addressed the apportionment of liability, stating that while the contribution of Cape Town was significant, the joint and several liability of the Minister and Nature Conservation warranted an equal share of the responsibility for costs incurred by the plaintiffs.

## C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeals of the Minister of Water Affairs and Nature Conservation, affirming that they were collectively liable for 75% of damages due to their negligence in managing fire hazards. The appeal by Cape Town was also dismissed regarding liability for 25% of the damages, while the issue of costs was modified to reflect that Cape Town would not be responsible for costs incurred in the Durr and Penny Taylor matters, as it had not been a defendant in those claims.

The implications of this decision reinforce critical legal standards regarding fire safety responsibilities for landowners and bolster the presumption of negligence articulated in the relevant statutes, ensuring a heightened standard of vigilance against fire risks in vulnerable areas. This case establishes significant precedential value for future disputes involving environmental hazards and negligent omissions that lead to economic loss.
