### Case Note: In the matter between ASSOCIATED PAINT & CHEMICAL INDUSTRIES (PTY) LTD t/a ALBESTRA PAINT AND LACQUERS (Appellant) and ADRIAAN SMIT (Respondent) ([paras 1–21])

#### FIRAC Case Note

**F – Facts:**
In April 1996, the plaintiff, A P & C I (WYNBERG) (PTY) LTD t/a ALBESTRA PAINTS, initiated an action against the defendant, Adriaan Smit, in the Orange Free State Provincial Division. The plaintiff issued a summons claiming R190,462.43 for goods sold and delivered to the defendant between May and December 1993 ([para 1]). The defendant was initially granted leave to defend, leading the plaintiff to file a declaration. Crucially, the declaration identified the “plaintiff” as ASSOCIATED PAINT AND CHEMICAL INDUSTRIES (PTY) LTD t/a ALBESTRA PAINTS, without seeking formal amendment of the original summons ([paras 2–4]). 

The declaration also significantly altered the identity of the purchaser from the single defendant to a partnership, Danre, involving Smit and one van Rensburg ([para 3]). Despite this inconsistency, the defendant filed a plea acknowledging the new plaintiff but maintained a blanket denial of liability while also asserting a counterclaim related to the goods sold ([paras 4–5]). 

In July 1997, the plaintiff sought to amend the summons and pleadings to reflect the name change, which the defendant opposed on the grounds that the amendment would deprive him of asserting a defense based on prescription, as set out in the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([paras 7–9]). The court a quo denied the amendment and leave to appeal, leading to the application for leave to appeal to the Chief Justice, which was granted ([paras 10–11]).

**I – Issues:**
The central legal questions pertained to whether the amendment of the claim constituted an introduction of a new plaintiff and whether the original summons had interrupted prescription of the debt under section 15(1) of the Prescription Act ([paras 11–12]). Specifically, the Court needed to consider if the plaintiff, identified in the summons and the declaration, could be deemed the correct creditor and whether prescription had been interrupted ([paras 16–18]).

**R – Rules:**
The relevant statutory framework for this case is contained in the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly sections 15(1) and 14(1). Section 15(1) states that the prescription period can be interrupted by serving any process whereby the creditor claims payment of a debt ([para 17]). Section 14(1) concerns acknowledgment of liability by the debtor indicative of interruption of prescription ([para 19]). Key case precedents include *Sentrachem Ltd v Prinsloo* 1997 (2) SA 1 (A), *Standard General Insurance Co Ltd v Eli Lilly (SA) (Pty) Ltd* 1996 (1) SA 382 (W), and *Park Finance Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Van Niekerk* 1956 (1) SA 669 (T), which provided guidance on substantive vs. procedural amendments regarding the validity of claims and the impact on prescription ([paras 12–19]).

**A – Application:**
The Court analyzed whether the service of the summons constituted a process initiated by the correct creditor. It concluded that since the plaintiff cited in the summons was not the same entity that sought the claim in the declaration, the debt had not been effectively claimed, and thus, prescription was not interrupted ([paras 18–20]). The Court placed significant weight on the fact that a proper amendment was not made to the summons before the service, meaning there was no valid process against the debtor for interruption purposes.

Additionally, the Court considered that while the defendant did make admissions in his plea, these were conditional and did not constitute an unconditional acknowledgment of the debt, further bolstering the stance that prescription had not been interrupted ([para 19]).

**C – Conclusion:**
The appeal was dismissed with costs. The Court held that the amendment did not merely reflect a misnomer but introduced a new plaintiff, which materially altered the debt being claimed ([paras 20–21]). Ultimately, the failure to effectively interrupt prescription led to the conclusion that the debt had been extinguished prior to the amendment, rendering the plaintiff’s claim invalid as the underlying debt had lapsed due to prescription. This ruling underscores the necessity for companies to ensure proper procedures are followed when seeking to amend particulars in legal proceedings, particularly concerning creditor identification and claim amendments.

The case establishes important precedents regarding the adherence to legal entities' formalities in procedural law, specifically surrounding principles of prescription and creditor-debtor dynamics within contractual claims. 

This case note serves as a detailed legal digest of critical aspects within the judgment of ASSOCIATED PAINT & CHEMICAL INDUSTRIES (PTY) LTD t/a ALBESTRA PAINT AND LACQUERS v ADRIAAN SMIT, encapsulating its essential legal implications and adherence to procedural rectitude.
