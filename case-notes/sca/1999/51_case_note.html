Supreme Court of Appeal

Boy Titi Ndweni and Others v The State

The applicants were sentenced to death on the murder counts, subsequently reduced to life imprisonment, while the first applicant received a 17-year sentence ([para 2]). Their appeal was initially denied due to the lack of a right to appeal on certain counts ([para 3]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 1999

F – Facts

The case involves three applicants, Boy Titi Ndweni, Sipiwe James Bholo, and Sipho Samuel Gavin, who were convicted on 20 June 1994 in the Transvaal Provincial Division for three counts of murder and other charges related to the Eikenhof attack that occurred on 19 March 1993 ([para 2-3]). The incident involved the hijacking of a BMW vehicle, resulting in the attack on a stationary vehicle that killed three people, including two schoolchildren ([para 3]).

The applicants were sentenced to death on the murder counts, subsequently reduced to life imprisonment, while the first applicant received a 17-year sentence ([para 2]). Their appeal was initially denied due to the lack of a right to appeal on certain counts ([para 3]).

After years of conviction, the Azanian People's Liberation Army (APLA) claimed responsibility for the attack in a press statement dated 22 July 1997, which coincided with the amnesty application of a member, Phila Martin Dolo, who indicated his involvement in the attack ([para 4-5]). This prompted the current application for leave to adduce fresh evidence, which the applicants argued could affect the outcome of their case ([para 12]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues discussed in this case fall under two main concerns:

  1. Evidentiary Grounds: Whether the applicants can adduce further evidence that may exonerate them or reveal the true perpetrators of the Eikenhof attack. This includes the legitimacy of Dolo's claims and the identification of witnesses who could substantiate this new information ([para 4-14]).
  2. Procedural Fairness: Whether the refusal to consider new evidence after a conviction violates the principles of justice, especially with the substantial evidence arising post-trial that could potentially lead to different conclusions ([para 5-12]).

R – Rules

The court relied on established jurisprudence regarding the reopening of cases, particularly the criteria established in S v De Jager 1965 (2) SA 612 (A), which stipulates that to reopen a judicial investigation, applicants must satisfy the following requirements:

a. A sufficiently reasonable explanation for the absence of the new evidence during the original trial,
b. Prima facie likelihood of the truth of the evidence, and
c. Material relevance of the evidence to the trial's outcome ([para 6]).

The court also acknowledged the legal standard from S v Steyn 1981 (4) SA 385 (C), which clarified that “a prima facie likelihood of the truth of the evidence” should be interpreted as evidence that is reasonably possibly true ([para 14]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal thoroughly evaluated the application against the De Jager requirements. It noted that significant evidence had emerged that was not available during the initial trial. This included testimonies from potential witnesses and the delayed claims of responsibility by APLA ([para 6-8]).

The court recognized that the original trial’s decision relied heavily on witness identification and confessions from the first two applicants, which might be challenged by the new evidence ([para 12-16]). Notably, the comments and retractions from key witness Nelson Mpunge raised questions about the reliability of his original statements, particularly his claims of police influence during identification parades ([para 11-12]).

The court analyzed whether the newly presented evidence about Dolo’s claims indicated a substantial shift in the narrative that could warrant revisiting the case. They determined that the evidence met both the sufficiency criteria and prima facie likelihood conditions in relation to the evolution of witness testimonies ([para 12-15]).

The court expressed concern regarding the fairness of the original trial, emphasizing that the considered evidence might demonstrate the applicants' innocence or highlight inadequate investigation efforts by law enforcement ([para 16]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal resolved to set aside the convictions and sentences of the applicants, remitting the matter back to the original trial court for examination of the new evidence ([para 18]). The court mandated that the applicants be allowed to present their witnesses, and also provided discretion for the trial court to permit additional witnesses whose testimonies could aid in a fair determination of the issues ([para 18-19]).

This landmark decision reinforces the principle that post-conviction claims of innocence must be diligently explored to uphold justice, particularly where substantial new evidence may lead to a different outcome. The court’s emphasis on fair trial rights and the continual search for truth signifies a commitment to just legal processes, ensuring that the administration of justice acts not only for the state but also importantly for the individuals affected by judicial decisions.
