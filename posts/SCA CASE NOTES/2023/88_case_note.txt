Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: PIC SOC Ltd and Another v Trencon Construction (Pty) Ltd and Another (365/2022) [2023] ZASCA 88

## F – Facts

The case involves an appeal by the Public Investment Corporation SOC Ltd (the PIC) and the Government Employees Pension Fund (the GEPF) against Trencon Construction (Pty) Ltd (Trencon) and GVK-Siya Zama Building Contractors (Pty) Ltd (GVK) following a series of decisions made in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria ([paras 1–3]). 

Trencon, a building and civil engineering contractor, submitted a bid for a tender issued by the PIC on behalf of the GEPF for a shopping center in Pretoria in November 2019. The tender was awarded to GVK, prompting Trencon to seek a review and set aside the PIC's decision. Its application also included a request for a declaratory order stating that the GEPF is an organ of state as defined in section 239(b)(ii) of the Constitution. The high court dismissed Trencon’s review application on 2 November 2021 ([paras 3–4]).

Subsequent to the dismissal, Trencon sought an amendment to the court's order under rule 42(1)(b) to declare the GEPF an organ of state. The appellants did not oppose this application, leading to an amended high court order on 22 November 2021, which declared the GEPF an organ of state “for purposes of the present application” ([paras 5–7]). The appellants then sought leave to appeal against this order and the denial of a costs order in their favor. Trencon cross-applied for leave to appeal the dismissal of its application and the court’s costs decision ([paras 8–9]).

On 4 April 2022, the high court granted the appellants leave to appeal, while denying Trencon’s cross-appeal due to the lack of condonation for late filing ([para 9]). However, prior to the appeal hearing, the Registrar of the Supreme Court of Appeal issued a note raising concerns about the existence of a live dispute conducive to appellate jurisdiction, primarily because Trencon had not obtained leave to appeal from the high court regarding the initial dismissal of its review application ([paras 10–11]).

## I – Issues

The key legal issues addressed by the Supreme Court of Appeal revolve around:

1. Whether a live dispute or “lis” remained between the parties that would allow the court to exercise its appellate jurisdiction ([paras 1, 16]).
2. Whether the amended high court order constituted a nullity, given it was made in the absence of an existing dispute ([paras 15–16]).
3. The implications of the limitations placed on the high court's declaration regarding the status of the GEPF as an organ of state for the purposes of the case ([paras 18–19]).

## R – Rules

The court relied upon several foundational legal principles and statutory provisions:

1. The principle of “functus officio,” which dictates that a court lacks authority to amend or supplement an order once it has been duly pronounced as a final judgment ([para 12]).
2. The exceptions to this principle, under which a court may clarify an ambiguous order or correct clerical errors, were deemed inapplicable in this case ([para 13]).
3. Section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which establishes that an appeal may be dismissed if it is determined that a decision sought will have no practical effect or result ([para 10]).
4. Precedents including *Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Genticuro AG* and others established that once a court has pronounced a final judgment, it cannot revisit its own decision unless valid grounds exist ([para 12]).

## A – Application

In its analysis, the court emphasized that after the high court issued its final ruling dismissing Trencon's review application, it had exercised its jurisdiction completely and lost any authority to amend the order. Despite Trencon's subsequent claims to amend based on alleged omissions by the high court, these arguments did not align with the established legal framework that guards against the alteration of definitive orders once finality has been reached ([paras 12–15]).

The court concluded that the amended order issued by the high court was effectively a nullity, as it had been made without jurisdiction, considering Trencon had not sought leave to appeal the dismissal of its application and had thus rendered any existing dispute moot ([paras 15, 16]). The conversation prompted by the Registrar regarding the existence of a live dispute was crucial; without a live dispute, the court asserted that its appellate jurisdiction could not be exercised ([paras 1, 10, 16]).

Moreover, the court noted that the amended declaration concerning the GEPF's status confined itself to the specific circumstances of Trencon’s review application and lacked broader implications or applicability beyond that context, meaning any potential ruling from the appellate court would not have a consequential effect or practical outcome for the parties ([paras 18–19]).

In terms of costs, the court highlighted that both parties had continued with the appeal despite recognizing the lack of a valid basis for it, suggesting shared responsibility for the circumstances leading to the strike from the roll. Consequently, it ruled that each party should bear its own costs ([para 20]).

## C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal struck the matter from the roll, concluding that no live dispute remained for adjudication. The high court's amended order was deemed a nullity, and despite the potential importance of the status of the GEPF as an organ of state, the court found that this question did not yield sufficient basis for appellate consideration ([paras 20–21]). The ruling reinforces the principle of finality in judicial decision-making, the limitations on appellate jurisdiction, and the implications of functus officio, highlighting the necessity for a live dispute in order to invoke the court's appellate powers effectively. As a result, this judgment contributes to the broader understanding of jurisdictional constraints within the South African legal landscape, especially in cases regarding administrative reviews and declarations.
