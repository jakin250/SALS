Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note: Hattingh v Juta (440/2011) [2012] ZASCA 84

## F – Facts
The case arises from an eviction dispute concerning the appellants, Michael, Edwina, and Pieter Hattingh, who were residing in a worker's house on the respondent Laurence Edward Juta’s Fijnbosch farm in Stellenbosch ([1]). The first and third appellants (brothers) lived with their mother, Mrs. Magrieta Hattingh, in the house since December 2002, along with the second appellant, Edwina, who is the first appellant's wife. The family was initially able to reside in the worker’s house by virtue of Mrs. Hattingh’s employment with the respondent, commencing in approximately 1994, where she worked as a domestic servant ([3]).

The eviction proceedings were initiated by the respondent in 2010 after the Stellenbosch Magistrate's Court dismissed his application to evict the appellants. This decision was reversed by the Land Claims Court, which mandated their eviction by 20 May 2011 ([1], [2]). The appellants claimed their right to remain on the property was grounded in Section 6(2)(d) of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA), which ostensibly provides that “an occupier shall have the right to family life in accordance with the culture of that family” ([9]).

The appellants contended that they qualified for continued residence based on their cultural configuration as a family unit that offers mutual support ([13], [21]). However, the respondent disputed this, asserting that their right to reside on the property should terminate due to the conditions under which they moved onto the farm with his consent expiring ([6], [8]). The Land Claims Court ruled against the appellants, leading to this appeal ([1]).

## I – Issues
The focal question in this appeal is whether the appellants were correctly evicted from the worker’s house on the respondent’s farm and whether they satisfactorily established their entitlement to remain under the provisions of ESTA, particularly concerning the interpretation of “family life in accordance with the culture of that family” as envisaged in Section 6(2)(d) ([10], [21]).

## R – Rules
The primary legal issue invokes Section 6(2)(d) of ESTA, which articulates rights concerning family life. The precise interpretation of “family life” and “culture” forms a substantial foundation for adjudication ([9], [10]). The court must also consider wider statutory frameworks concerning family rights and human dignity as enshrined in the South African Constitution, particularly Sections 30 and 31, which secure cultural rights ([21], [22]).

Relevant case law includes the Constitutional Court’s decision in *MEC for Education, KwaZulu-Natal & others v Pillay*, which provided important insights on cultural rights as associative practices rather than individual assertions and emphasized the requirement of demonstrating cultural communal practices ([19]). Further precedents, such as *Dawood & another v Minister of Home Affairs & others*, underpin the constitutional protection of family and cultural rights ([16]).

## A – Application
The appellants’ argument focuses on their interpretation of “culture” as encompassing a familial ethos collectively shared, constituting their argumentative basis for “family life” as defined by Section 6(2)(d) of ESTA. They portrayed their living arrangement as indicative of a supportive familial culture ([13], [10]). However, the court referred to the need for a broader interpretation framed by associative cultural practices, as underscored in *Pillay*, asserting that cultural practices must be evidenced as collective rather than personal ([20], [19]).

The Land Claims Court emphasized the necessity for appellants to provide evidence that their residence on the farm was a cultural extension of family life according to their shared practices. Since they failed to demonstrate this associative quality, the court ruled that they were not protected from eviction under ESTA ([10], [21]).

In parsing the nuances of cultural and family life, the court reiterated that cultural identity and the associated rights are inherently linked to a community's traditions and values. This indicated a judicial reluctance to define “family” in overly broad terms that could undermine the foundational aspects of community in relation to the term “culture” ([15], [19]). The appellants' appeal hinged significantly on perceptions of their familial constructs, which, while sympathetic, did not conform to the broader associative requirements set forth in the statutory context and judicial precedents.

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Land Claims Court's judgment, dismissing the appeal. It reiterated that the appellants did not convincingly prove that their living arrangement was in adherence to cultural guidelines protecting their family life under Section 6(2)(d) of ESTA. The court observed that family life must be substantiated through cultural practices shared among community members, rather than individual or nuclear family claims ([21], [22]). The court modified the eviction dates to allow the appellants time to arrange alternative accommodation, emphasizing fair procedural treatment despite the outcome of the case ([22], [24]). This case underscores the critical interplay between cultural rights, legal definitions of family, and eviction statutes within the South African legal landscape, reflecting on broader societal values regarding human dignity and family cohesion.
