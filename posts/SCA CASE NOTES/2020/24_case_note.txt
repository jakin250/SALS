Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Auckland Park Theological Seminary v University of Johannesburg (1160/2018) [2019] ZASCA 24

## F – Facts
The case arose from a dispute over a long-term lease agreement involving a property owned by the University of Johannesburg (UJ). The property in question is situated at 51 Richmond Avenue, Auckland Park, Johannesburg, and the lease was originally established between the predecessor of UJ, the Rand Afrikaans University (RAU), and the Auckland Park Theological Seminary (ATS) on 6 December 1996 ([1]). This lease was subsequently ceded by ATS to Wamjay Holdings Investments (Pty) Ltd (Wamjay) on 28 March 2011, which was registered in the title deed of the property ([2]).

In June 2012, Wamjay submitted architectural plans to construct an educational facility with an Islamic ethos. On 5 October 2012, UJ attempted to cancel the lease, asserting that the rights therein were delectus personae—personal to ATS and therefore non-assignable without UJ's consent, which ATS had repudiated by ceding the lease ([3]).

UJ filed a summons against both ATS and Wamjay for eviction and cancellation of the lease ([4]). The trial court previously ruled in favor of UJ, affirming the delectus personae argument and ordering eviction ([4]). This decision was appealed to the full court and upheld, prompting a further appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa ([5]).

## I – Issues
The central legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the rights under the lease agreement were indeed delectus personae, thus personal to ATS and incapable of cession. This raises significant questions about contract interpretation, particularly in relation to the parol evidence rule and the integration principle ([6]–[7]). The case also touched upon whether the evidence presented by UJ to support its claim could be considered valid under the established legal doctrines ([6]).

## R – Rules
The court relied on established principles regarding contracts and the parol evidence rule, which asserts that, when an agreement is written to be a complete memorial of the parties' intentions, extrinsic evidence cannot be introduced to modify or contradict its terms ([6]). Key legal precedents included **KPMG Chartered Accountants (SA) v Securefin Ltd** and **National Board (Pretoria) (Pty) Ltd and Another v Estate Swanepoel**, which reinforced that extrinsic evidence is inadmissible when the intention is clearly stated in a written agreement ([7]–[9]).

According to the doctrine of delectus personae, rights arising from contracts can generally be ceded unless the contract explicitly indicates that the rights are personal to a party, which would restrict cession ([8]). It was also noted that leases of longer duration (in longum tempus) typically do not involve delectus personae unless explicitly stated in the agreement ([9]).

## A – Application
In assessing the case, the court meticulously applied the principles of contractual interpretation. It noted that the lease agreement made no explicit provision indicating that ATS's rights were personal and incapable of cession ([10]). The court rejected UJ’s attempt to rely on oral evidence aimed at altering the contract’s written terms, holding that such evidence was inadmissible due to the integration clause in the lease that specified that no warranties or representations external to the written text could be relied upon ([11]).

The court thus concluded that the arguments presented by UJ were fundamentally flawed since they tried to introduce external context to change the meaning of unambiguous contract language ([10]–[11]). The court emphasized that the lease’s provisions should be treated objectively, taking the language at face value without assuming hidden meanings or intent not reflected in the text.

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by ATS and Wamjay, concluding that the rights in the lease were not delectus personae and thus were capable of being ceded. It found that the lower courts erred in their application of the law to the facts presented ([12]). The court's decision has implications for contract law in South Africa, particularly regarding the interpretation of lease agreements and the enforceability of cession clauses. The ruling reinforces the weight of written agreements and the limitations on the admissibility of extrinsic evidence, affirming the importance of clarity and explicitness in drafting contracts.

### Order
The court ordered that the appeal be upheld with costs, setting aside the previous orders of the lower courts and dismissing UJ’s claim with costs ([12]). This ruling underscores the necessity for thorough and precise drafting in contractual agreements and the limitations on reliance on parol evidence to support contractual interpretations, aligning with broader principles of contract law.
