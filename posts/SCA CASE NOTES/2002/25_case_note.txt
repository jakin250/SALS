# FIRAC Case Note: Africa Solar (Pty) Ltd v Divwatt (Pty) Ltd

**Case Citation:** Africa Solar (Pty) Ltd v Divwatt (Pty) Ltd (365/2000) [2002] ZASCA 12

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Date of Judgment:** 28 March 2002

---

### F – Facts ([paras 1-6])

The appellant, Africa Solar (Pty) Ltd, a manufacturer of photovoltaic panels, entered into a transaction with the respondent, Divwatt (Pty) Ltd, who purchased a significant number of these panels for use in various applications, including water pumps in remote areas where traditional electricity sources were unavailable. The respondent withheld payment of R89,653.51, alleging that the panels did not meet the performance specifications outlined by the appellant, leading to customer complaints regarding underperformance. Despite this, the respondent did not cancel the existing orders but attempted to enhance performance by adding additional panels and withholding part of the payment as leverage during ongoing disputes.

The appellant initiated legal proceedings in the Transvaal Provincial Division to recover the outstanding balance, leading to a multifaceted litigation process. Central to the dispute was whether the appellant’s standard terms, which included provisions limiting liability for defective goods, were incorporated into the agreement. 

Both parties attempted to resolve the issue amicably, engaging Professor Leitch as an expert to test the panels. Following testing that reportedly favored the respondent's claims, the defendant issued a special plea asserting that the matter had been compromised through this agreement to test, which was dismissed by the trial court.

### I – Issues ([paras 7-8])

The legal issues raised in the appeal included:

1. Whether the appellant’s standard conditions of trade were incorporated into the sale agreement.
2. Whether the respondent was precluded from asserting a defense of non-payment due to alleged defects in the solar panels.
3. Whether the respondent could pursue a counterclaim for damages related to these alleged defects.

The resolution depended on the applicability of the standard terms and the surrounding circumstances of the parties' negotiations.

### R – Rules ([paras 19-34])

The trial court ruled that the appellant's standard terms were not incorporated due to the specifics of their initial dealings. The court considered various established principles including:

- **Exceptio Non Adimpleti Contractus:** A contract defense which allows a party to withhold performance when the other party has not fulfilled its contractual duties ([para 19]).
- The conditions under which standard terms can be incorporated into agreements, particularly focusing on the clear communication between parties and the requirement of mutual consent ([paras 29-30]).

Key findings emphasized that the defendant's director, Mr. Pichulik, had not been adequately informed of the standard terms at the time of the agreement due to the specific circumstances under which the credit application was executed.

### A – Application ([paras 35-46])

The court applied the established rules regarding contract formation and incorporation of terms to the facts of the case. The determination hinged on whether both parties exhibited the requisite *animus contrahendi* (intention to be bound). Pichulik's argument that he was unaware of the standard terms was deemed credible as the necessary documentation was not provided in full during the critical stages of negotiation.

The SCA ruled that the incorporation of the appellant's standard terms was ineffective because there was a lack of mutual understanding and agreement on those terms, particularly as related documents were not adequately shared. The rejection of certain liability clauses was pivotal as it allowed the respondent to claim damages and withhold payment based on the quality and performance of the panels.

Further procedural misconduct regarding the protracted cross-examination and the failure to adhere to the court’s rules for preparing appeal records were also addressed ([paras 36-46]), which influenced the court’s sanction.

### C – Conclusion ([paras 46])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the trial court's ruling that the appellant's standard terms did not apply to the sale. Consequently, the respondent was entitled to withhold payment based on the alleged defects in the solar panels and could pursue a counterclaim for damages. The court’s judgment reinforced key contract law principles regarding the necessity of clear communication and mutual agreement on terms before binding obligations are created. The procedural inefficiencies noted throughout the trial served as a reminder of the importance of adhering to court rules and maintaining clarity in legal proceedings.
