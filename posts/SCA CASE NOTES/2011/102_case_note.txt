Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

### FIRAC Case Note: Saidex v The Minister of Minerals and Energy (49/10) [2011] ZASCA 102

#### F – Facts
The case involves an appeal by the appellants—Saidex (Pty) Ltd and five other parties—against the refusal of an interim interdict by the North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria (Sapire AJ) ([1]). The appellants operate in the diamond industry, previously under the Diamonds Act 56 of 1986 (the Act), which was amended in 2005 and implemented on 1 July 2008 ([2]). They allege that these amendments have removed their ability to conduct their business while seeking to protect their rights until a main application, led by the South African Diamond Producers Organisation (SADPO), challenges the constitutionality of certain provisions of the amended Act ([2]).

The respondents include the Minister of Minerals and Energy and other regulatory bodies, who opposed both the interim application and the appeal ([3]). The central contention is whether the appellants can demonstrate a prima facie right to the relief sought, the apprehension of irreparable harm, and whether the balance of convenience favours their application ([6]). The appellants primarily refer to the legitimacy of their "tender business," which involves local dealers and unlicensed foreign buyers, alleging that this practice has been rendered illegal by the amendments ([10]).

#### I – Issues
The legal questions at stake include:
1. Whether the appellants established a prima facie right to the interim interdict ([8], [18]).
2. Whether the refusal of the interim interdict constitutes a final determination of their business rights ([4]).
3. Whether they have demonstrated a well-grounded apprehension of irreparable harm, and if the balance of convenience favours granting the interdict ([6]).
4. The implications and legality of their "tender business" under the amended Act ([11], [17]).

#### R – Rules
The legal principles governing the case include:
1. The requirements for an interim interdict, which necessitate proof of a prima facie right, a reasonable apprehension of irreparable harm, and that the balance of convenience favours the applicant ([6]).
2. The statutory framework under the Diamonds Act 56 of 1986, particularly its amendments, which explicitly regulate the dealings in diamonds and prohibit unlicensed transactions ([12], [18]).
3. Precedential authority, including prior judgments that establish the applicability of these principles, such as *Webster v Mitchell* and *Gool v Minister of Justice* ([6], [7]).

#### A – Application
The court analyzed whether the appellants met the criteria for an interim interdict. They needed to show not only that their allegations, if accepted, could lead to final relief but also that a serious doubt cast by the respondents would prevent them from obtaining that relief ([6]). The appellants argued that their operational model (the tender business) was legitimate based on a historical letter from the South African Diamond Board (SADB) which they claimed legitimized their practices ([12], [13]).

However, the court found that, rather than establishing a prima facie right, the appellants failed to link their "de facto" business practices to any legitimate statutory provisions under the amended Act ([11], [18]). The court noted that the Act is framed to prevent all dealings in unpolished diamonds by unlicensed persons, directly contradicting the appellants' claims about the legality of their operations ([12], [17]). No evidence was presented that their alleged exemption had been validly endorsed, which further undermined their case ([14], [15]).

Furthermore, the assessment of irreparable harm did not reveal any immediate threat as the appellants had applied for licenses under the amended Act, which allowed them to continue operations until applications were decided ([23]). The court ultimately found that the appellants did not substantiate claims of significant harm nor demonstrate that the balance of convenience favoured granting the interdict ([22], [24]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, upholding the lower court's refusal of the interim interdict. The court emphasized the appellants' failure to establish a prima facie right, the absence of any pressing threat to their operations, and the legality of their claimed practices being contrary to the statutory framework designed to regulate the diamond industry ([26]). The judgment reinforces the strict adherence to legislative provisions within regulatory frameworks while elucidating the requirements for interim relief in urgent applications ([26]).

The implications of this case extend beyond the individual parties involved, highlighting the critical nature of compliance within highly regulated industries, as well as the judiciary's role in upholding statutory schemes against claims of long-standing practices that may contravene legislative objectives.
