# Case Note: Van der Bank v The State (245/15) [2015] ZASCA 10 

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts 
The appellant, Daniël Johannes Stephanus van der Bank, was charged with rape and indecent assault concerning a then-16-year-old girl, who had a significantly impaired mental capacity ([1]). The incidents allegedly occurred in 1999 in Pretoria, wherein the appellant admitted to having sexual intercourse and inserting his finger into the complainant's vagina, asserting that he believed this was consensual ([1]).

Throughout the trial, the complainant's mental capacity was called into question, prompting the appointment of an intermediary to assist her during testimony ([2]). The Regional Court of Pretoria convicted the appellant on both counts in 2007, sentencing him to 12 years in prison, four of which were conditionally suspended ([2]). The appellant's subsequent appeals to the High Court and a full court were unsuccessful ([3]).

In granting special leave to appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal focused on whether the complainant's evidence was inadmissible since it was given through an intermediary, contrary to § 170A of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([4]). The appellant argued that once a witness reaches the age of eighteen, the power to invoke § 170A ceases ([5]). The provision was designed to facilitate testimony from vulnerable witnesses, specifically those under eighteen and those deemed mentally incapable ([6]).

### I – Issues 
The central issue before the court was whether the evidence of the complainant was admissible despite being provided through an intermediary, in conflict with § 170A of the Criminal Procedure Act at the time ([4]). The appeal also considered whether the complainant was mentally capable of giving consent, a necessary element for the validation of the appellant's defense ([9]).

### R – Rules 
The legal principles applicable to this case hinge on the definition of consent in sexual offences, notably that consent must be real, free, and given by someone capable of consenting ([10]). The court relied upon established precedents, including:

1. **K v The Regional Court Magistrate NO & others** 1996 (1) SACR 434 (E) - Examining the admissibility of evidence from vulnerable witnesses.
2. **S v Dayimani** 2006 (2) SACR 594 (E) - Addressing the interpretation of § 170A and the requirements for intermediary evidences.
3. **Director of Public Prosecutions v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development & others** [2009] ZACC 8 - Affirming the constitutional soundness of the provisions protecting vulnerable witnesses ([6]).

### A – Application 
In its application of the law, the Court critically assessed the evidence provided by both the complainant and expert witnesses regarding her mental capacity. The appellant's admissions in his plea explanation were noted, acknowledging the sexual encounters while asserting consent, which was the crux of his defense ([1], [11]). 

Testimonies from the complainant's mother detailed her intellectual limitations due to brain damage from birth complications, corroborated by a psychologist and a psychiatrist, both of whom established her functional mental capacity as being below six. The expert evidence made clear that the complainant could not understand court proceedings and could not provide informed consent ([18]). 

The court also addressed the appellant's contention regarding § 170A, emphasizing the necessity of interpreting its provisions, which had been amended to encompass not only biological age but also mental capacity ([6], [7]). The claimed reliance on precedents such as Dayimani was found to lack merit; the Court focused more on the current case's specific evidence rather than the historical context alone ([8]).

The court ultimately concluded that the appellant was aware of the complainant’s considerable mental impairment and could not have genuinely believed that she was capable of consenting to sexual intercourse. The evidence overwhelmingly supported the conclusion that she lacked the capacity to consent ([23]).

### C – Conclusion 
The appeal was dismissed, and the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the convictions on both counts. The ratio decidendi emphasized that a comprehensive evaluation of the complainant's mental capacity was crucial, thus determining the invalidity of the appellant's consent defense. The case underscored the judicial commitment to safeguarding vulnerable individuals within the justice system, highlighting the balance between the accused's rights and the protection of incapable individuals as a matter of public interest ([24]). 

This ruling reinforces the principle that consent in sexual offenses is contingent not only on age but also on mental capacity, shaping future interpretations of criminal law regarding vulnerable witnesses. The affirmation of the legal provisions aimed at aiding vulnerable witnesses also reaffirms their relevance in ensuring just trials.
