Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Black Sash Trust v Minister of Social Development

## F – Facts
The case in question, **Black Sash Trust v Minister of Social Development** [2017] ZACC 20, arose from concerns surrounding the administration of social grants in South Africa, notably the performance of the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA). The applicant, Black Sash Trust, sought to join the Minister of Social Development, Bathabile Dlamini, in her personal capacity, alleging failures in her oversight related to the payment of social grants, which were to be performed by SASSA from April 2017 onwards ([1]–[3]). This case highlighted a convoluted chain of events leading up to and beyond the deadline for the transition of grant payments to SASSA, with the Minister citing that issues were largely the fault of other officials, specifically Mr. Magwaza, the new SASSA CEO, and Mr. Dangor, the former Director-General of the Department ([1]–[2]).

Key procedural developments included the Minister's affidavit seeking to counter claims while alleging she was misinformed, which led to respective affidavits from Magwaza and Dangor, asserting that she had bypassed standard protocols by setting up parallel structures that undermined the regular governance of SASSA ([10]–[19]). These affidavits painted a picture of the Minister's potential liability concerning allegations of maladministration and breach of constitutional duties, prompting the Court to consider personal costs orders against her ([5]–[7]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues before the Constitutional Court were:
1. Whether the Minister should be joined as a party to the proceedings in her personal capacity and subject to personal liability for costs ([3], [24]).
2. Whether the evidence presented indicated that the Minister acted in bad faith or gross negligence that could justify such costs orders against her ([21]).
3. The larger constitutional implications of accountability and the interpretation of conduct that would warrant a personal costs order against state officials ([7]–[9]).

## R – Rules
The rules applicable to the case primarily stem from the common law regarding personal costs orders alongside the Constitutional principles of accountability. The common-law principles dictate that a personal costs order may be imposed when a public official's conduct is found to be:
- Motivated by malice,
- Characterized by improper conduct or bad faith ([5]–[6]).

Additionally, Sections 1(d), 41(1)(c), 195(1), 92(2), and 237 of the Constitution underpin the need for accountability and effective governance within public administration ([7]–[8]). The tests for determining bad faith and gross negligence applied in previous cases were referenced, stressing that such assessments must be conducted on a case-by-case basis ([9], [21]).

## A – Application
The Constitutional Court elaborated on the need for accountability from public officials, particularly where significant failures, such as those alleged against Minister Dlamini, are reported. The Court noted that actions taken under her oversight and the establishment of work streams indicated potential lapses in governance, thereby placing the Minister in a position of liability. Importantly, it refrained from concluding on the allegations outright but acknowledged the need for a proper process to evaluate them ([21]–[23]).

The Court debated the methodologies by which evidence should be distilled to determine whether the Minister’s alleged conduct constituted a breach of her constitutional duties, emphasizing the necessity of a structured inquiry to address disputed facts ([24]). The broad implications of accountability for state officials were considered, particularly in terms of setting a precedent that would uphold the principles enshrined in the Constitution regarding professional ethics and effective service delivery.

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ordered that:
1. The Minister be joined to the proceedings in her personal capacity.
2. The parties report whether they can agree on a process under Section 38 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 to address the Minister's role in the alleged mismanagement of social grant payments ([24]).

The Court underscored the fundamental principle of accountability in public administration and indicated that a failure to abide by constitutional standards could lead to personal liability for officials ([7]–[9]). This decision sets a significant precedential standard regarding personal accountability of government officials in South Africa, reinforcing the expectation of responsible exercise of duty within the public sector.
