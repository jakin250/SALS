THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA - AM MOOLLA GROUP LIMITED v THE GAP INC

Supreme Court of Appeal 2005

F – Facts

The case originated from a dispute involving several registered South African trade marks prominently featuring the name "GAP," with the first appellant being AM Moolla Group Limited and the primary respondents being The Gap Inc and its subsidiaries ([1]–[2]). The appellants claimed use of trade marks, including various forms of "GAP," while the respondents asserted that "GAP" is a well-known trade mark protected under South African law and international conventions, despite not being registered in South Africa.

The legal proceedings began when the respondents sought an interdict against the appellants to stop them from using the GAP marks, arguing that they were well-known and deserved protection under Section 35(3) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, incorporating Article 6bis of the Paris Convention ([2]–[3]). The first instance court upheld the respondents' claims and ordered an interdict against the appellants, leading to this appeal ([3]).

The background of the case traces the origins of the GAP brand back to 1969, detailing its international expansion and eventual trademark filings in multiple jurisdictions, including the United States, United Kingdom, and Canada ([5]–[6]). In South Africa, the proprietor of the trade marks in question had earlier registered "GAP"—prior to the respondents' marks being recognized—through Hirsch in 1973, which predated any use by the respondent in South Africa ([7]–[8]).

I – Issues

The primary issues before the court included:

  1. Whether the respondents' trade marks were well known in South Africa, providing them legitimate grounds for protection under South African and international law ([4]–[13]).
  2. Whether the appellants had engaged in non-use of their registered marks, warranting their expungement ([26]–[29]).
  3. The implications of the principle of territoriality in trademark law as it pertained to the marks claimed by both parties ([9]–[12]).
  4. Whether the counter-application for interdict against the respondents could be upheld given the circumstances surrounding the expungement ([3]).

R – Rules

The pertinent legal rules and standards addressing these issues included:

  • The Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, particularly Sections 35(3) and 27(1)(b), which govern the protection of well-known marks and the criteria for removing marks from the register due to non-use ([2], [27]).
  • Article 6bis of the Paris Convention, outlining the protection of well-known trademarks ([12]–[14]).
  • The principle of territoriality, as clarified in judgments such as Victoria’s Secrets Inc v Edgars Stores Ltd and Barcelona.com v Excelentisimo Ayuntamiento De Barcelona, which dictate that trademark rights are determined by the laws of individual countries, thus affecting the enforceability of foreign marks in South Africa ([9]–[10]).
  • Precedents, including McDonald’s Corporation v Joburgers Drive-In Restaurant (Pty) Ltd and remarks on the protection of well-known marks within their jurisdictional boundaries ([12]).

A – Application

The court assessed whether the respondents proved the well-known status of the GAP marks in South Africa. The evidentiary burdens placed on the respondents involved demonstrating that the marks were recognized by the relevant public and had a commercial reputation ([18]–[21]). A market survey suggested a significant recognition of the marks among a target demographic, although doubts remained regarding the sufficiency of the evidence ([20]–[21]).

Regarding non-use, evidence indicated that the appellants had not effectively utilized their registered marks during the relevant time frame. The appellants conceded that certain marks were dormant or not in true use. The court thus found this ground valid for the expungement of the registered trade marks under Section 27 of the Act ([26]–[32]).

Despite the complexities of corporate structures and potential inter-company licenses cited by the appellants, the court determined insufficient evidence to establish a genuine claim of bona fide use or permitted use under Section 38 ([29]–[32]). The failure to demonstrate that any use met the standard for protecting against expungement was critical in the court's reasoning.

In applying these principles, the court observed the significant differentiation between registrations that remained valid and those invalid due to non-use, leading to a nuanced analysis of the trade marks in question ([43]–[45]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the interdict against the appellants regarding the use of the respondents' well-known marks due to their established reputation in South Africa ([51]). However, the appeal concerning the expungement of trade mark registrations based on non-use was affirmed, meaning that the ownership claims by the appellants were significantly weakened ([52]).

Furthermore, the cross-appeal by the respondents regarding the grounds for expungement under Section 10(3) was permitted, reinforcing the notion that mere ownership without active usage does not suffice for trademark protection. The implications of this judgment highlight the importance of maintaining active use of trade marks and the ramifications of territoriality in trademark law as it applies to international brands operating in local jurisdictions ([51]).

This ruling underscores the importance of establishing robust rights to well-known marks while navigating local legal frameworks, shaping the discourse on trademarks' territoriality and their international enforcement.
