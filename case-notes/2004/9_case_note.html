Constitutional Court

- Mkontwana v Nelson Mandela Metropolitan Municipality & Others (CCT 57/03)

Nokuthula Phyllis Mkontwana and Peter William Bissett were among the applicants challenging the constitutionality of these provisions, arguing that they unduly burdened property owners for consumption charges incurred by non-owners ([paras 2–5]).

Constitutional Court 2004 CCT 57/03

F - Facts

This case involved a challenge to the constitutionality of section 118(1) of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 (the Act) and section 50(1)(a) of the Gauteng Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939 ([paras 1–5]). The provisions impose certain obligations on property owners regarding the payment of municipal service charges for water and electricity supplied to premises occupied by others. The High Court had previously ruled that the legislation arbitrarily deprived property owners of their rights, specifically contradicting section 25(1) of the South African Constitution, which prohibits arbitrary deprivation of property.

Nokuthula Phyllis Mkontwana and Peter William Bissett were among the applicants challenging the constitutionality of these provisions, arguing that they unduly burdened property owners for consumption charges incurred by non-owners ([paras 2–5]). The High Court declared the sections unconstitutional, prompting an appeal by the municipalities, leading to a referral to the Constitutional Court for confirmation ([paras 3–4]).

I - Issues

  1. Does section 118(1) and section 50(1)(a) of the Act impose arbitrary deprivation of property as prohibited by section 25(1) of the Constitution? This issue was central to the appellants' arguments against the earlier High Court decision ([paras 25–26]).
  2. Is there sufficient connection between the deprivation of transfer rights and the purposes intended by the legislature in enacting the provisions? The nuances of this issue arise from concerns regarding the financial sustainability of municipalities and the constitutional obligation to provide services to communities ([paras 31–32]).

R - Rules

The relevant legal standard here is established in section 25(1) of the South African Constitution, which prohibits arbitrary deprivation of property. The interpretation of arbitrariness has been discussed in precedents such as First National Bank of SA Ltd t/a Wesbank v Commissioner, South African Revenue Service and Another ([paras 34–35]). The courts have determined that:

  • A deprivation is arbitrary if it lacks sufficient reason or is procedurally unfair ([para 36]).
  • The court assesses the relationship between the means (the deprivation) and the ends (the purpose of the legislation) ([paras 36–37]).
  • The importance of the public interest in maintaining sustainability in municipal finances forms part of the consideration when addressing the scope of property rights ([paras 72–74]).

A - Application

The Court analyzed the constitutional validity of sections 118(1) and 50(1)(a) by examining:

  1. Constitutional Compliance: The Court found that both provisions effectively impose limits on the transfer of property until all outstanding debts owing in respect of that property by any occupant are paid, thus constituting deprivation of property rights. However, such deprivation was not considered arbitrary because it served a legitimate legislative purpose, namely the collection of outstanding municipal debts and ensuring the provision of essential services ([paras 41–46]).
  2. Public and Private Interest Balance: The Court recognized the significant public interest involved in ensuring municipalities maintain their ability to provide essential services. The potential for accumulated municipal debt to jeopardize this function was deemed a "sufficient reason" under section 25(1). The reasoning reported evidence of substantial municipal debts and the need for a structured means of ensuring payment by property occupants ([paras 105–107]).
  3. Impact on Property Owners: Additionally, the Court acknowledged that property owners do have mechanisms at their disposal to mitigate the financial risks associated with non-payment by occupiers. They can enforce payment obligations through lease agreements and take action to avoid unlawful occupancy ([paras 110–112]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately held that sections 118(1) and 50(1)(a) of the Act do not constitute arbitrary deprivation of property. The deprivation that occurred as a result of these provisions was deemed necessary to ensure the sustainability of municipal services and thus was constitutional ([para 123]). Consequently, the appeals by the municipalities were accepted, and the High Court's earlier ruling of unconstitutionality was not confirmed ([paras 124–125]).

This decision clarifies the limits of property rights in relation to municipal obligations and reinforces the imperative of ensuring the viability of local government services while recognizing the need for responsible ownership and tenant management.
