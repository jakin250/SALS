# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of *Snyman v De Kooker N O and Others* (400/2023)

**Citation**: Snyman v De Kooker N O and Others (400/2023) [2024] ZASCA 119 (2 August 2024)

## F – Facts: Background and Procedural History

[1] The appellant, Mrs. Ruanda Snyman, appealed against a judgment from the full court of the Gauteng Division of the High Court. The full court had overturned an initial order by a single judge, Mali J, which had directed the trustees of the Stapelberg Investment Trust to account for their management of the trust and for the trust to be terminated and replaced with a new one. The Trustees were also ordered to pay costs de bonis propriis.

[2] The appellant suffered bodily injuries from a motor vehicle accident, which led to her instructing Ms. Tonya Nadine Ehlers of Ehlers Attorneys to file a damages action against the Road Accident Fund (RAF) under the RAF Act. A curator ad litem was appointed for her interests, leading to a court order that R4,973,922.00 be paid to a trust for her benefit. The creation of the trust was formalized through a ‘Deed of Donation in Trust,’ and the respondents were appointed as trustees.

[3] Subsequent to the trust's establishment, the appellant raised concerns regarding the trustees’ management, particularly regarding their duty to provide adequate accounting. After attempts to obtain further financial details from the trustees fell short, the appellant filed for a court order compelling the trustees to furnish a detailed account and for the trust to be terminated and replaced due to alleged irregularities in the administration.

[4] The court of first instance granted the relief sought, emphasizing the trustees' failure to properly account for the trust's financial state and ordering them to account in detail within a specified timeframe. The trustees were also held liable for costs due to their negligence.

[5] However, upon appeal, the full court found that the trustees had accounted sufficiently and that terminating the trust could financially prejudice the appellant. Consequently, the order from the court of first instance was set aside, and the application was dismissed with costs.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

[6] The primary legal questions presented in the appeal included:

1. Whether the trustees had sufficiently accounted for their administration of the trust to the appellant, given her specific requests for financial information.
2. The correct legal interpretation of the provisions under the Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988 concerning the termination of a trust as legally distinct from the removal of trustees.

[7] Furthermore, there was an underlying conflict between the actions of the trustees and the legal obligations imposed by the trust deed and statutory requirements to adequately manage the assets for the appellant, contrasting her interests against a potential financial fallout from termination.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents

[8] The case draws primarily on the **Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988**. Particularly, Section 13 provides the legal framework for the termination of trusts when their original conditions can no longer be fulfilled as intended by the founder, while Section 20 governs the removal of trustees.

[9] Key legal precedents relevant to this case include:

- *Doyle v Board of Executors* and *Doyle v Fleet Motors*, emphasizing the obligations of trustees to account in good faith and maintain accurate financial records.
- *Gowar v Gowar* and *Volkwyn v Clarke*, which address the conduct required of trustees and the grounds for their removal.

[10] The court of first instance explored the duties and expectations of trustees under fiduciary obligations while addressing the sufficiency of the accounting measures taken by the respondents, contrasting these with applicable legal standards.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

[11] The Supreme Court of Appeal clarified misconceptions between the termination of a trust and the removal of its trustees. The Court asserted that the two provisions under the Trust Property Control Act serve separate legal functions: termination (s 13) addresses the overarching purpose and survival of the trust, while removal (s 20) concerns the management and performance of individual trustees.

[12] Upon assessing the sufficiency of the trustees’ account, the court criticized the full court's acceptance of the trustees' unilateral reports as adequate. The appellant's concerns regarding financial statements lacking transparency and accountability were deemed legitimate, reinforcing the duty of trustees to furnish clear, supported records, including dealing with any discrepancies in reported fees and expenses.

[13] The Court emphasized ensuring that an accounting rendered was sufficiently detailed and understandable, particularly for beneficiaries lacking legal or financial expertise. This comprised a reiteration of the principles that require supporting documentation and explanations for financial transactions as outlined in *Doyle v Board of Executors*.

[14] With respect to the termination of the trust, the court evaluated the deviations from what the judicially sanctioned trust encompassed. It noted that certain provisions within the trust deed directly contradicted the purpose of safeguarding the appellant’s assets, indicating that the trust established was inadequately aligned with the founder’s intentions.

[15] The Court found it necessary to terminate the existing trust due to the various problematic provisions established under the deed, which included clauses allowing excessive discretion by trustees and conflicts of interest relating to financial management. The remedy dictated that a new trust be created with clearer terms to protect the interests of the appellant.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

[16] The Supreme Court upheld the appeal, reversing the full court's decision. It ordered the trustees to produce a comprehensive account of the trust’s financial status, acknowledged the need for reformulation of the trust into a new entity to ensure better management aligned with the appellant's interest, and imposed costs liabilities on the trustees personally.

[17] The court underscored the importance of clear and accurate financial accountability from trustees, reinforcing the notion that fiduciary duties must be executed with utmost care and diligence to safeguard beneficiaries’ interests. 

[18] This judgment delivers significant implications for trust law in South Africa, particularly concerning the rigid separation between the roles of trustees and the management of trusts. It emphasizes that legal frameworks should not only delineate fiduciary duties but also ensure that the administrative conduct of trusts aligns with their intended altruistic purposes, thereby offering a structured approach should similar disputes arise in the future.

[19] Consequently, this case reiterates the principle that trustees who materially fail in their accounting duties may be held personally liable for related legal costs, thus enhancing the enforcement of fiduciary responsibilities in trust administration.

Ultimately, the decision in *Snyman v De Kooker N O and Others* sets a notable precedent regarding trust fidelity and legal accountability, ensuring that the vital interests of beneficiaries remain steadfastly at the forefront of trust law applications.
