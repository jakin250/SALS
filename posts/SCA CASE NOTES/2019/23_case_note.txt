Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: The Director of Public Prosecutions, KwaZulu-Natal v Ramdass (1236/2017) [2019] ZASCA 23

## F – Facts
In this case, the respondent, Mr. Donovan Ramdass, was acquitted of the murder of Ashika Singh and robbery with aggravation by the KwaZulu-Natal Local Division of the High Court (Ploos van Amstel J). The acquittal was predicated on reasonable doubt concerning his criminal capacity to appreciate the wrongfulness of his conduct and to act accordingly due to intoxication resulting from alcohol and drug use prior to the incident. The respondent claimed to have no memory of the events leading to the deceased's death ([1]–[2]).

Following the acquittal, the State sought to have specific questions of law reserved for consideration by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) under Section 319(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA). However, the trial court refused this application. Consequently, the State incorrectly attempted to appeal this refusal directly, invoking Section 16(1)(b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which led to procedural irregularities that complicated the appeal process ([3]–[11]).

The appeal process involved multiple layers of statutory provisions, and the court ultimately analyzed the legal foundation guiding the State's request and whether the issues raised constituted questions of law or, instead, constituted factual matters ([19]–[22]).

## I – Issues
The primary issues for determination were:
1. Whether the trial court erred in declining to reserve specific questions of law for the Supreme Court of Appeal.
2. Whether the questions submitted by the State were indeed of a legal nature or merely factual determinations improperly framed as legal questions ([20]–[22]).

## R – Rules
The authoritative legal framework is grounded primarily in the CPA and the Superior Courts Act. Key sections include:
- **Section 319(1) of the CPA**: Allows a trial court to reserve questions of law for the Appellate Division. 
- **Section 317(5) of the CPA**: Outlines the protocol for appealing against a refusal to reserve questions of law, requiring applications to follow a detailed procedural route.
- **Sections 16(1) and 17(2)(f) of the SC Act**: Govern appeals from decisions of lower divisions, including provisions for special leave and for reconsiderations in exceptional circumstances ([4]–[12]).

Additional case law provided context for interpreting the applicability of statutory provisions:
- **S v Eadie (2002)**: Clarified the burden on the State regarding establishing criminal capacity and the nuances of intoxication as a defense ([27]–[37]).
  
## A – Application
Upon analyzing the merits of the State's requests for questions of law, the SCA noted that the trial court properly focused on whether the issues raised genuinely constituted legal questions. The court emphasized that many of the questions posed were rooted in the factual determinations of the case that had been adequately resolved by the trial court ([24]–[52]).

For example, the interpretation of criminal capacity, as grounded in established precedents, required the State to prove beyond reasonable doubt whether the respondent was capable of distinguishing right from wrong. The SCA determined that questions surrounding the respondent's intent, capacity for self-control, and circumstantial evidence were ultimately factual in nature and therefore not suitable for reservation as legal queries ([25]–[43]). 

Additionally, the SCA scrutinized procedural missteps made by the State in handling its applications and highlighted that the grounds for appealing the refusal to reserve questions of law did not fulfill the necessary legal requirements and therefore warranted dismissal of the application for leave to appeal ([14]–[19]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled to adjust the prior order regarding the nature of the appeal. The initial order refusing special leave to appeal was varied; it was replaced with a dismissal of ordinary leave to appeal based on the absence of reasonable prospects of success. The implications of this ruling reaffirm the importance of adhering to established procedural norms when contesting decisions in lower courts and clarify the distinction between fact and law in criminal proceedings ([56]–[57]).

The overturning of the trial court’s dismissal of the State's application for special leave, redefined the standard under which further appeals could proceed within the statute’s framework. The SCA emphasized that issues of law must be precisely delineated from factual findings to prevent the procedural irregularities that had plagued this case, thus reinforcing legal standards in criminal appellate practice in South Africa.
