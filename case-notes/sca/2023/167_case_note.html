Supreme Court of Appeal

Legal Practice Council v Bulelani Rubushe (Case No. 1004/2022) [2023] ZASCA 167

In this matter, Rubushe had entered into a contingency fee agreement with Mfengwana that violated the Contingency Fees Act 66 of 1997.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 167

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The appellant, the Legal Practice Council (LPC), appealed against a decision of the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, which had suspended the respondent, Mr. Bulelani Rubushe, for two years rather than striking him off the roll of legal practitioners ([1]). Mr. Rubushe was an attorney practicing under the name B.R. Rubushe Attorneys in East London. His misconduct stemmed from a case, Mfengwana v Road Accident Fund, where he had represented Mr. Zama Mfengwana ([2]).

In this matter, Rubushe had entered into a contingency fee agreement with Mfengwana that violated the Contingency Fees Act 66 of 1997. The High Court noted that the agreement allowed Rubushe a fee of 25% of the damages claimed, which was disproportionate given the nature of the legal services rendered ([4]–[9]). Judge Plasket found Rubushe's conduct in trying to circumvent the law “disingenuous” and “rapacious” ([10]).

Moreover, after the judgment was delivered, Rubushe attempted to withhold payment of a significant portion of the awarded damages to Mfengwana, despite lacking authority to do so after formally withdrawing as his attorney ([15]–[16]). This led to further proceedings initiated by Mfengwana against Rubushe, where he was ordered to pay the withheld amount ([17]). The LPC subsequently moved to have Rubushe's name struck from the roll of legal practitioners due to this dishonest conduct ([18]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question addressed by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether Mr. Rubushe was a “fit and proper person” to remain on the roll of legal practitioners, focusing specifically on the implications of his dishonesty and the appropriate judicial response to such findings ([21]). The court evaluated the High Court's reasoning concerning the appropriateness of a suspension versus striking off the roll ([19]).

R – Rules

The court considered various established legal precedents and statutory frameworks in its ruling. The Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014 and various decisions regarding legal practitioners' conduct, including Jasat v Natal Law Society and Malan and Another v Law Society of the Northern Provinces, which stress that dishonesty usually leads to disqualification from practice unless exceptional circumstances warrant otherwise, were cited ([21]–[23]).

The court highlighted the necessity of framing conditions for suspension if such a penalty were to be considered, following principles stated in A v Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope ([23]). The judgments articulated that a mere suspension is insufficient to rehabilitate an attorney found unfit due to dishonest conduct, as it does not address the root cause of unfitness ([24]–[28]).

A – Application

The SCA found that the High Court misapplied these legal standards, leading to a materially incorrect decision. It emphasized that Mr. Rubushe’s dishonesty was severe and not mitigated by the fact that he did not ultimately succeed in robbing his client ([20]). The court critiqued the High Court for concluding suspension was appropriate based on the alleged lack of success in Rubushe’s dishonorable attempts rather than fully assessing the implications of his conduct ([25]).

The SCA underscored that the lack of accountability displayed by Rubushe in his aftermath of misconduct—where he failed to show insight, blamed others, and engaged in further deceptive practices—was sufficient to invalidate his claim to remain on the roll ([27]). Moreover, the High Court’s determinative stance to suspend rather than strike reflected a misunderstanding of the principles that govern legal practitioner conduct, which necessitates protection of the public from dishonest attorneys ([26]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA concluded that the appeal should be upheld, striking Rubushe from the roll of legal practitioners. It found that his actions constituted a significant breach of the standards expected from attorneys and that the High Court's reasoning failed to apply the controlling legal principles accurately. This decision not only rectified the previous order but further reinforced the importance of honesty and integrity within the legal profession, underscoring that such qualities are foundational for practitioners ([29]).

The SCA’s decision holds significant implications for legal practice in South Africa, reinforcing the message that misconduct, especially involving dishonesty, cannot be condoned, establishing a strong precedent for future cases involving ethical conduct in the legal profession.
