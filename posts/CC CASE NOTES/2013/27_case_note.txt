Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: **Magidiwana v President of the Republic of South Africa** [2013] ZACC 27

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([1–3])

This case revolves around the tragic events that unfolded during a violent confrontation between members of the South African Police Service and miners at Lonmin's Marikana mine in August 2012. The applicants, a group consisting of individuals arrested or injured during these events, sought legal assistance to participate in the Marikana Commission of Inquiry established by the President of South Africa to investigate these incidents ([1]). The Commission commenced on 1 October 2012 and was initially set to conclude its work by December 2012, later extended to 30 October 2013 ([1]).

The applicants were experiencing difficulties in securing funding for their legal representation at the Commission after initial support from the Raith Foundation ran out ([2]). Attempts to obtain funding from the Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Legal Aid South Africa were unsuccessful, as both entities cited either lack of a legal framework or budgetary constraints as reasons for denial ([3]–[4]). Consequently, the applicants filed an urgent application with the North Gauteng High Court, seeking both immediate and final relief, demanding that the state covers their legal costs to participate in the Commission effectively ([5]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([6–10])

The central legal issues before the court were whether the applicants were entitled to state-funded legal representation for participation in the Commission, and if the High Court had appropriately dismissed their urgent application for temporary relief ([6]). Specifically, the court had to consider the implications of the state’s obligations under the Constitution in light of the applicants' assertions that their rights under sections 34 (access to courts) and 9 (equality before the law) were infringed by the refusal to provide legal aid ([12]–[13]).

Moreover, the case raises broader questions about the separation of powers, particularly regarding whether the judiciary could or should interfere in executive decisions relating to the allocation of public resources and legal aid provisions ([16]).

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([8–16])

The court relied on several constitutional and statutory provisions while adjudicating the matter. Pertinently, sections 28(1)(h), 35(2)(c), and 35(3)(g) of the Constitution establish the rights to legal representation at state expense under certain conditions, which were deemed not applicable to the applicants as they do not fall under the categories of children or detained individuals ([12]). 

Key legal principles cited from **National Treasury and Others v Opposition to Urban Tolling Alliance and Others** ([8]) emphasized the reluctance of courts to entertain appeals against interim orders lacking finality and the importance of respecting the executive's budgetary discretion ([9]). The court underscored that courts should refrain from interfering with executive functions unless clear unlawful conduct is demonstrated. 

The court also referenced the **Legal Aid Act 22 of 1969**, which outlines the framework for providing legal assistance to indigent persons and was central to rejecting the applicants' claims ([16]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([9–17])

In applying the relevant legal rules, the court noted the prudence of the High Court's judgment, which emphasized respect for the executive's jurisdiction over public resource allocation ([9]). The absence of proof of any unlawful or corrupt conduct in the refusal of legal aid led the High Court to conclude that it could not issue an order encroaching on the executive's prerogative ([10]). 

The applicants' reliance on sections 34 and 9 of the Constitution was ultimately seen as insufficient. Access to courts in terms of section 34 applies distinctly to disputes resolved within a formal judicial framework, rather than the investigatory context of a commission ([14]). The court also assessed the implications of the Commission's operations being hindered by its inability to provide legal aid to participants, but reaffirmed that such concerns did not bestow upon the judiciary a mandate to dictate public resource allocations ([15]–[16]). 

The court's stance that fairness does not equate to a guaranteed right to representation at public expense was critical, indicating the limitations of judicial authority in rectifying perceived inequalities before the Commission ([15]). 

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([19])

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal, affirming that there were no reasonable prospects for success regarding both the interim relief sought and the broader issues implicated in the main application. The court ruled that, in the absence of a constitutional mandate compelling state-funded representation under these circumstances, the issue fell under executive discretion ([17]). 

The implications of this decision reinforce the judiciary's cautious approach toward matters intersecting with executive powers and budgetary considerations, establishing a clear precedent regarding the limitations of access to legal aid in non-criminal contexts ([17]). Furthermore, it highlights the enduring challenges faced by vulnerable groups in accessing equitable legal representation in administrative settings, raising essential discussions about the provision of legal aid and the state's responsibilities in promoting fair representation beyond traditional court settings. 

This decision serves as an important reflection on the intersection of law, public interest, and state policies regarding funding for legal representation, underscoring the complexities involved in cases where economic resources and judicial oversight collide.
