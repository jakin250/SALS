Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: President of the Republic of South Africa v South African Rugby Football Union (CCT 16/98)

## F – Facts

On **22 September 1997**, the President of the Republic of South Africa appointed a commission to investigate financial and administrative matters related to the South African Rugby Football Union (“SARFU”), as permitted under **section 84(2)(f)** of the South African Constitution. The appointment proclamation was published on **26 September 1997** ([1]). Various parties, including SARFU and Dr. Louis Luyt, objected to this commission and subsequently filed an application for review in the **Transvaal High Court** on **20 October 1997** ([2]).

The High Court, presided over by Judge De Villiers, treated the application as urgent. After a lengthy process, which included a request for oral evidence from the President, judgment was ultimately rendered on **17 April 1998**. This judgment set aside the commission's establishment and the corresponding proclamation ([4]). Following this, the President and other respondents sought leave to appeal to the **Supreme Court of Appeal** on **15 May 1998**, but they had not yet received a response ([5]). They then proceeded to appeal to the **Constitutional Court** on **11 September 1998**, requesting condonation for the late filing ([6]).

## I – Issues

The core issues for determination were:

1. Whether the order by the High Court declaring the President’s proclamation invalid constituted an order of constitutional invalidity as defined in **section 172(2)(a)** of the Constitution ([22]).

2. The implications of **section 167(4)(e)** regarding the conduct of the President and whether it involves a failure to fulfill a constitutional obligation ([22]).

3. Whether the appeal and the matters raised qualified as “constitutional matters” or “issues connected with decisions on constitutional matters” under **section 167(3)(b)** of the Constitution ([22]).

4. The question of whether the applicants had waived their right to appeal by seeking to appeal first to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([44]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions included:

- **Section 84(2)(f)** of the Constitution, detailing the President's powers to appoint commissions of inquiry ([1]).

- **Section 172(2)** which outlines the procedure for matters concerning constitutional invalidity. Specifically, it states that orders of constitutional invalidity must be confirmed by the Constitutional Court to have any force ([10]).

- **Section 167** which establishes the Constitutional Court as the highest court in constitutional matters, allowing only it to decide disputes involving the constitutional status of organs of state ([10]).

- The **Constitutional Court Complementary Act** and its procedural rules regarding appeals, particularly **Rule 15** relating to appeals against orders of constitutional invalidity ([13]).

## A – Application

The Constitutional Court analyzed the appeal based on the legal framework established by the Constitution and assessed the nature of the issues raised. It found that orders of constitutional invalidity are of significant importance and must be addressed by the Court.

1. The Court confirmed that the order made by the High Court was indeed an order concerning the constitutional validity of the President's actions, and thus within the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court under **section 172(2)(d)**, allowing for a direct appeal ([28]).

2. It ruled that the determination of whether a failure to fulfill a constitutional obligation occurred fell under the ambit of **section 167(4)(e)**, thus denoting this as a constitutional issue ([25]).

3. The question of whether the appeal was limited to constitutional matters of substance or not was addressed, clarifying that the applicants did not waive the right to appeal, and their actions were divisible between appealing to both courts ([45], [46]).

4. The Court also tackled the issues surrounding the alleged bias in the High Court proceedings, asserting that even allegations of bias were integrally linked to constitutional matters, solidifying the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court over these aspects ([40]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted condonation for the late filing of the appeal, recognizing the significant constitutional issues involved. The decision underscored the necessity of the Court's engagement with questions pertaining to the constitutional validity of executive actions, specifying that any orders addressing constitutional invalidity must be confirmed by it as it uniquely holds such jurisdiction ([55]).

The Court established clear procedural guidelines for future cases involving appeal matters concerning constitutional invalidity and provided avenues for redress while balancing the need for procedural integrity. The matter highlights the Court’s role in constitutional governance by reinforcing that the Constitution upholds the principle of legality over administrative actions ([55]).
