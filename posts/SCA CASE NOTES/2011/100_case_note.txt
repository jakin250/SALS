Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Maphango v Aengus Lifestyle Properties (Pty) Ltd (611/2010) [2011] ZASCA 100

## F – Facts

The case of **Maphango v Aengus Lifestyle Properties (Pty) Ltd** involved 18 appellants who were tenants in a building named Lowliebenhof located in Braamfontein, Johannesburg. The respondent, Aengus Lifestyle Properties, sought the eviction of the appellants on the basis that their lease agreements had been lawfully terminated ([1]). The appellants argued that the termination was invalid, and if valid, that eviction would not be just and equitable as per the **Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998** (commonly referred to as PIE) ([1]).

While proceedings were initially heard in the South Gauteng High Court, the court found in favor of the respondent with respect to 16 of the leases, ruling that they were validly terminated. It evicted nine of the appellants, while postponing eviction for a further seven appellants to allow them to seek alternative accommodation through the City of Johannesburg ([2]).

The respondent had acquired the property in May 2009, and only became the landlord at that point. The leases were negotiated with the preceding owner ([4]). The eviction notices indicated that current rental amounts would increase significantly for continued occupancy ([8]).

## I – Issues

The key issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

1. Whether the leases held by the appellants were validly terminated.
2. Whether the respondent could be restrained from exercising the termination clause on public policy grounds, taking into consideration the appellants' rights under Section 26(1) of the South African Constitution, which guarantees the right of access to adequate housing ([3], [26]).

## R – Rules

The rules pertinent to the case included:

- Common law principles related to lease agreements, particularly the doctrine of **huur gaat voor koop**, which stipulates that a property owner's rights and obligations transfer upon purchase ([4]).
- **Section 4(6)** of PIE, which emphasizes just and equitable considerations before ordering eviction ([2]).
- Interpretation of tacit terms of contracts as defined in case law, including tests set forth in **Alfred McAlpine & Son (Pty) Ltd v Transvaal Provincial Administration** 1974 (3) SA 506 (A) ([12]).
- The principles laid down in **Barkhuizen v Napier** 2007 (5) SA 323 (CC), concerning the enforcement of contractual provisions against public policy ([22]).
- Relevant statutory provisions of the **Rental Housing Act 50 of 1999** and **Gauteng Unfair Practice Regulations 2001** ([32]).

## A – Application

In its analysis, the Supreme Court addressed the appellants' argument regarding the existence of a tacit term that would prevent the landlord from utilizing the termination clause solely to enforce rental increases beyond the agreed terms. The court determined that such a tacit term was unnecessary for the lease's business efficacy, given the clear terms regarding rental increases during its duration ([16], [21]).

Further, the court addressed whether the termination of the leases violated public policy principles, including the rights enshrined in Section 26(1) of the Constitution. It ultimately concluded that reasonable notice for termination was an inherent part of lease agreements, negating the appellants' assertions regarding their ongoing security of tenure ([28]). 

The termination was deemed neither unreasonable nor unfair. The court recognized the respondent’s right to terminate the leases in a manner that serviced its economic interests while complying with legal procedures. This was considered essential for the sustainability of their rental model, which involved upgrading and maintaining properties in a financially viable manner ([25]).

The court also examined the premise of unfair practices under the **Rental Housing Act** and found that the termination did not constitute oppressive conduct. The exigencies of business did not render the fulfilled contractual terms unenforceable ([34]).

## C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the findings of the South Gauteng High Court, ruling that the leases had been validly terminated and dismissing the appeal. The court emphasized that the nature of lease agreements did not provide tenure security beyond the limits stipulated in the agreements themselves ([38]). The decision underscored the importance of upholding contractual integrity even in the face of socio-economic challenges, while providing guidance on the interpretation of public policy in the realm of contract law and eviction cases.

The implications of this judgment reaffirm the authority of landlords to manage rental agreements consistent with business sustainability while balancing tenants' rights within constitutional boundaries. The court also provided further clarification on tacit terms, emphasizing that enforcement of existing provisions must align with the contractual terms agreed upon by the parties ([32], [34]). The appeal was dismissed, underscoring that contractual obligations must be honored unless explicitly contravened by law ([38]).
