Supreme Court of Appeal

Tertiary Education National Union and Another v Durban University of Technology (796/2018) [2019] ZASCA 151

The unions claimed that clause 3.7 of the document indicated that DUT agreed to extend a PRMA subsidy to former employees of ML Sultan, with specifics on the implementation left to future negotiations.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 151

F – Facts

The case arose from the merger of the Natal Technikon and ML Sultan Technikon on April 1, 2002, into the Durban Institute of Technology (now the Durban University of Technology, DUT). The appellants, the Tertiary Education National Union of South Africa (TENUSA) and the National Education Health and Allied Workers Union (NEHAWU), contended that during the harmonization process of employment terms following the merger, an agreement was made regarding a post-retirement medical aid (PRMA) subsidy for certain former employees of ML Sultan. This claim was based primarily on a document referred to as Version 7, signed on November 4, 2005, by Professor Bonganjalo Goba, the then Principal and Vice-Chancellor of DIT, and representatives of the three unions involved in negotiations ([1]-[2]).

The unions claimed that clause 3.7 of the document indicated that DUT agreed to extend a PRMA subsidy to former employees of ML Sultan, with specifics on the implementation left to future negotiations. The DUT countered that while discussions about medical aid and the subsidy occurred, no binding agreement was reached, emphasizing that any adjustments to employees' conditions, including the PRMA subsidy, required Council approval under section 34(3) of the Higher Education Act 101 of 1997 ([3]-[4]).

The dispute mainly revolved around whether the Council of DUT had approved the subsidy for former ML Sultan employees as part of the terms of service specified in Version 7. The High Court dismissed the unions’ claims, leading to this appeal ([4]).

I – Issues

The central legal questions in this case included:

  1. Was there a binding agreement to provide the PRMA subsidy to certain former employees of ML Sultan, as specified in the negotiated conditions of service?
  2. Did the Council of DUT approve the terms in Version 7 as they related to the PRMA subsidy for the specific group of employees?
  3. If the conditions as pleaded were found to be binding, what scope did these terms have regarding the PRMA subsidy? ([19]-[20]).

R – Rules

The court primarily relied on statutory provisions, especially section 34(3) of the Higher Education Act 101 of 1997, which mandates that the Council of a public higher education institution determine conditions of service subject to applicable labour law ([3]). Relevant legal precedents were invoked regarding the principles of contract interpretation and the burden of proof, establishing that the unions bore the onus to demonstrate that a contract had been formed, including proving the Council’s approval of the disputed terms ([21]). As cited, precedents such as Kriegler v Minitzer 1949 (4) SA 821 (AD) and others were instrumental in delineating the contractual obligations arising from the parties’ negotiations ([21]).

A – Application

The court undertook a thorough interpretation of clause 3.7 of Version 7 to ascertain whether it indeed constituted an agreement for the PRMA subsidy as claimed by the unions. The judge found that the language in clause 3.7, particularly sub-clauses 3.7.2 and 3.7.3, was ambiguous and did not indicate a definitive agreement to provide the PRMA subsidy without further negotiation ([29]-[40]).

The phrase "as part of the harmonisation process" indicated that any entitlement to a subsidy depended on further negotiations, thereby undermining the unions' argument that a binding obligation had been conclusively formed ([36]). The court highlighted the minutes from various meetings showing that discussions about medical aid and subsidy remained unresolved, reinforcing that no agreement had been reached at the time the Council approved Version 7 ([45]-[46]). The resolution from the Council on November 23, 2005, validating the Conditions of Service but explicitly noting unresolved issues, further signaled that the subsidy was not approved ([48]).

Moreover, subsequent events, including meetings after the Council's resolution where the status of the PRMA subsidy remained contentious, showcased the absence of consensus on this matter. The unions’ representatives themselves did not assert that an agreement had been reached, which was pivotal in evaluating their claims ([49]-[51]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal lodged by TENUSA and NEHAWU, affirming the High Court's finding that the unions failed to prove the existence of a binding agreement for the PRMA subsidy for former ML Sultan employees. The resolution from the DUT Council neither acknowledged the existence of such an agreement nor approved terms including the PRMA subsidy as part of the overall Conditions of Service.

This decision underscores the significance of formal approval in contractual agreements, especially in the context of higher education institutions where statutory frameworks govern operational procedures. The ruling reinforces the necessity for clear and unambiguous contractual terms, particularly in situations involving potential financial implications like the PRMA subsidy. As such, it emphasizes that parties must ensure that negotiations and agreements are clearly documented and ratified by governing bodies to avoid ambiguities and disputes in employment-related matters ([53]).
