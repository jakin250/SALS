Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Dlamini & Another v The State (553/12) [2012] ZASCA 207

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-7])

In **Dlamini & Another v The State**, Joshua Malefane Dlamini and Joseph Khosa (the appellants) were initially convicted in the regional court for robbery with aggravating circumstances, unlawful possession of firearms, and escaping from lawful custody. The robbery occurred on February 6, 1998, when Mrs Van der Watt was home alone. She was assaulted and restrained by armed intruders, who demanded the safe key and subsequently stole firearms and other valuables totaling approximately R60,000. The appellants, along with an accomplice, later attempted to flee but were apprehended after a pursuit involving the victims and community members, during which one accomplice was shot.

The regional court sentenced each appellant to a cumulative effective prison term of 36 years. Dlamini, at the time of the offence, was 32 years old, while Khosa was 36. They were both first offenders and sought mercy during sentencing by highlighting their family responsibilities and prior time spent in custody. However, the magistrate emphasized the seriousness of the offences and the need for public deterrence in concluding a harsh sentence. Upon appeal, the High Court dismissed their appeal, affirming the convictions and the harsh sentences. Subsequently, leave was granted to appeal specifically against the sentence imposed on the grounds that it was disproportionate and shocking.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 10-12])

The primary legal issue was whether the cumulative effective sentence of 36 years' imprisonment was appropriate given the nature of the crimes and the surrounding circumstances. The appellants contended that the trial magistrate misdirected herself by failing to properly consider the interconnectedness of the crimes when imposing consecutive sentences. Furthermore, there was a concern regarding whether the strict principles of proportionality in sentencing were upheld and whether the lengthy imprisonment served the objectives of punishment, such as deterrence, rehabilitation, and the protection of society.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 12-18])

The court referred to several precedents related to sentencing, notably *S v Mhlakaza & another*, where Harms JA articulated that excessively lengthy imprisonment fails to serve the purposes of punishment and risks merely appeasing public opinion (paras 12). The general principle posited was that sentences should not achieve a punitive level disproportionate to the rehabilitative aims of punishment, and excessive sentences could damage individuals beyond reasonable repair (para 13). 

The court also underscored general principles in *S v Skenjana*, asserting that extended imprisonment could lead to the physical and mental deterioration of prisoners (para 13), along with referencing *R v Cain*, about differentiating sentencing based on the nature of associated acts contributing to a conviction (para 15).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 14-18])

The Supreme Court of Appeal engaged in a thorough analysis of the magistrate's sentence, initially confirming the maximum sentence for count one—robbery with aggravating circumstances—was justified given the brutal conditions of the crime. However, the court identified misapplications regarding counts related to the illegal possession of firearms, where two of the firearms were taken during the robbery. It acknowledged that imposing maximum penalties on counts that closely related to the primary crime (robbery) undermined principles of justice.

The court determined that the possession of firearms, particularly those taken from the victims, should not independently warrant maximum sentences if they had already been considered in assessing the aggravating factors of the robbery (para 15-16). The court concluded that the sentences for unlawful possession were excessive, adjusted those to run concurrently, and decreased the prison term for escaping from lawful custody as it was deemed less severe compared to the robbery.

By calculating new concurrent sentences for specific counts, the effective imprisonment term was thereby reduced from 36 to 20 years total, allowing for a reconsideration of justice and public abhorrence towards the offences without imposing excessive cumulative harm on the appellants.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 18-18])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal against sentence, recognizing that the effective sentence of 36 years was grossly inappropriate under the circumstances. The court concluded:

1. The appeal against sentence was upheld.
2. The previous court’s sentencing order was replaced with lesser sentences that ran concurrently to reduce the total effective term to 20 years.

The court emphasized a balanced approach in sentencing that adequately reflects the seriousness of the crime while also caring for the rehabilitation aspect. The outcome suggests a judicial willingness to intervene in disproportionately harsh sentences and highlights the necessity for consideration of cumulative sentencing principles in cases involving interrelated offences.

This judgment underscores a pivotal judicial stance on sentencing in South Africa, advocating for proportionality and rehabilitative potential in criminal sentences while serving public interests. The implications are far-reaching, as it sets a precedent for future sentences involving cumulative offences, establishing guidelines that condemn excessive punitive measures and promote fairness in sentencing practices.
