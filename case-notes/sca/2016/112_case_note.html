Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 112 #### F – Facts: Relevant background and proc

FIRAC Case Note: Notshokovu v S (157/15) [2016] ZASCA 112

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history

The appeal in Notshokovu v S stems from a conviction in the Regional Court, East London, where the appellant, Thobani Notshokovu, was found guilty of rape and sentenced to six years' imprisonment. Notshokovu sought an application for leave to appeal, which was declined. Following this, a petition for special leave to appeal was also rejected by two judges of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) on 9 February 2015 ([1]). On 30 July 2015, the SCA’s President ordered a reconsideration of the earlier decision under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, leading to the present judgment ([1]).

The incident occurred on 27 January 2007, during a farewell party attended by the complainant and others, including the appellant. Following the consumption of alcohol, the complainant retired to her bedroom at approximately 3 AM, with her boyfriend recommending that the door not be locked to allow bathroom access later. During the night, she was awakened by the appellant, whom she believed to be her boyfriend. Upon realization that the person was Notshokovu, she screamed and fought back as he assaulted her. Subsequent to the incident, she reported the matter to police and underwent a medical examination ([4]).

The appellant admitted to sexual intercourse but claimed it was consensual. He described interactions with the complainant which were denied by her. The testimonies of witnesses, including the boyfriend, contradicted the appellant’s narrative, affirming the complainant's account that she had not consented ([5]–[8]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions

The primary legal issue revolves around whether the appellant demonstrated "special circumstances" justifying a special leave to appeal based on a higher threshold established by the Superior Courts Act, as opposed to the previous legislation. The court focused on whether reasonable prospects of success existed in granting such leave, taking into account both the conviction’s evidentiary context and the credibility of the testimonies presented at prior hearings ([1], [2]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon

The SCA referenced several precedents and statutory frameworks in evaluating the appellant's claims:

  1. Section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013: Establishes the framework under which special leave applications are assessed.
  2. S v Khoasasa and S v Matshona: These cases provide criteria regarding the assessment of reasonable prospects of success ([2]).
  3. S v Mkohle: Clarifies that contradictions in witness testimonies do not necessarily undermine a witness’s credibility but should be assessed in context ([8]).
  4. Relevant procedural standards concerning medical evidence under section 212(4) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 were also invoked, along with discussions on the role of assessors in rape cases as per the amendments to the Magistrates’ Court Act ([10], [11]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law

In applying the law, the court emphasized the stringent requirements imposed by the amendments under the Superior Courts Act, which necessitated the appellant to demonstrate not only reasonable prospects of success but also special circumstances warranting further appeal. The judges expressed that the trial court found the complainant's account credible and corroborated, rejecting the appellant’s version as implausible and contradictory ([3], [12]).

The trial court assessed the entirety of the evidence, reinforcing that the appellant's admission of sexual intercourse was forthright; however, the core dispute lay in the issue of consent. The judgment underscored the complainant’s immediate resistance and report to others post-assault as crucial evidence undermining the claim of consensual intercourse ([13], [14]).

The judges considered the appellant's attacks on the admissibility of the medical report but ultimately found them inconsequential, as the core of the prosecution’s case rested upon the testimony substantiating the absence of consent. The court noted that the complainant's explicit denial of consent throughout the narrative aligned with her behavior and actions, which were presented during the trial ([10], [15]).

As the court concluded the review of the lower courts’ decisions, it determined that there was insufficient demonstration of special circumstances by the appellant that would merit a re-examination of the case ([15]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications

The SCA ultimately refused the application for special leave to appeal, reinforcing its findings from prior judgments that the appellant had not illustrated the necessary thresholds of special circumstances or reasonable prospects of success. The court maintained that the issues presented were not of great public importance and that no substantial injustice would arise from the refusal ([16]).

The implication of this judgment highlights the rigorous standards imposed by the Superior Courts Act regarding appeals, particularly in serious criminal cases such as rape, reaffirming the necessity for clear and compelling evidence to challenge initial convictions. The ruling serves as a precedent for future applications, elucidating the evidentiary burdens placed upon appellants in criminal matters seeking to overturn convictions or sentences.

In sum, the court’s adherence to both statutory thresholds and the factual integrity of the witness testimonies underscores the judicial system's commitment to ensuring that convictions are soundly based on credible evidence and legal propriety.
