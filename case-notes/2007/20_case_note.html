Billy Lesedi Masetlha v The President of the Republic of South Africa (CCT 01/07)

On 20 October 2005, the Minister suspended Masetlha during a meeting with the President and the Inspector-General of Intelligence, although it was asserted that the decision to suspend was not formally made by the President at that time [13–14].

Constitutional Court 2007 CCT 01/07

F – Facts

This case arose from a series of decisions made by the President regarding the employment of Billy Lesedi Masetlha, who served as the Director-General of the National Intelligence Agency (NIA). Masetlha was appointed to a fixed three-year term starting on 14 December 2004 under the Intelligence Services Act 65 of 2002 (ISA) and the Public Service Act 103 of 1994 (PSA) [7–8]. A significant incident, known as the "Macozoma affair," involved unauthorized surveillance of businessman Sakumzi Macozoma, which led to internal investigations and dissatisfaction from the Minister of Intelligence Services regarding Masetlha’s explanations [9–10].

On 20 October 2005, the Minister suspended Masetlha during a meeting with the President and the Inspector-General of Intelligence, although it was asserted that the decision to suspend was not formally made by the President at that time [13–14]. Subsequently, on 15 November 2005, the President issued a Presidential Minute to retroactively formalize the suspension, although the legality of this procedure was contested by Masetlha [15–16].

In March 2006, the President unilaterally amended Masetlha’s term of office to expire on 22 March 2006, significantly earlier than the original expiry date [18]. Masetlha contested both the suspension and the amendment of his term, seeking reinstatement as Director-General while rejecting any financial pay-out for the unexpired term [20–21].

The Pretoria High Court dismissed Masetlha's applications, ruling that the President had acted within his lawful executive powers [22]. This led to Masetlha's appeal to the Constitutional Court, where he sought a declaratory order regarding the President's powers and the legality of the decisions affecting his employment [4].

I – Issues

  1. Power to Suspend: Did the President have the constitutional authority to suspend Masetlha from his position as Director-General of the NIA, either directly or through the Minister?
  2. Unilateral Amendment of Term: Was the President's unilateral amendment of Masetlha’s term of office lawful, and did it constitute a dismissal?
  3. Procedural Fairness: Did the President's actions comply with the principles of procedural fairness and the rule of law?
  4. Remedies: What appropriate remedies should be granted to Masetlha in light of the findings on the suspension and dismissal?
  5. Referral to Oral Evidence: Should any unresolved factual disputes be referred for oral evidence, particularly regarding the President's motive for suspension and dismissal?

R – Rules

The determination of these issues brings into play several legal principles:

  • Constitutional Framework: Section 209(2) of the Constitution provides the President with the power to appoint the head of intelligence services, while the ISA and PSA govern appointment procedures and conditions of employment ([22]).
  • Doctrine of Legality: The exercise of public power by the President must comply with the rule of law and be rationally related to a lawful purpose (Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of South Africa and Another: In re Ex Parte President of the Republic of South Africa and Others 2000 (2) SA 674 [76]).
  • Right to Fair Administrative Action: Section 33 of the Constitution guarantees a right to administrative action that is lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair ([76]).
  • Common Law Principles: Principles governing dismissal and alteration of terms in employment contracts apply where specific legislative provisions do not provide for the action taken ([12]).

A – Application

The court addressed the appellant’s claims by examining the President’s purported powers to suspend and dismiss Masetlha.

  1. Power to Suspend and Dismiss: The majority opinion, led by Moseneke DCJ, found that while the President has the constitutional authority to appoint under section 209(2), the power to revoke that appointment is not explicitly stated. However, it can be inferred as inherently necessary for effective governance. The court determined that the authority to dismiss is indeed embedded in the constitutional framework as part of the President's executive powers, aligning with the High Court's conclusion that a breakdown of trust justifies the dismissal ([66]-[70]).
  2. Unilateral Amendment of Term: On the issue of procedural fairness, it was determined that the President failed to consult Masetlha before amending his term. This lack of prior consultation was deemed a violation of the duty to act fairly, as outlined under the principles of legality that require any exercise of public power to adhere to due process ([203]-[207]).
  3. Procedural Fairness: The court noted that the failure to provide Masetlha with an opportunity to respond prior to his dismissal constituted a breach of the principles of fairness and legality. Despite urgent needs arising from the circumstances, no evidence was presented to justify bypassing the requirement of procedural fairness ([203]-[206]).
  4. Remedy: While Moseneke DCJ expressed that reinstatement was not suitable given the irretrievable breakdown of trust, the court recognized Masetlha's right to financial compensation for the term he would have served ([97]-[101]). Ngcobo J emphasized that the breakdown in trust justified the dismissal but concurred that Masetlha was owed remuneration and benefits until 31 December 2007 ([216]-[218]).
  5. Referral to Oral Evidence: Given the findings surrounding the procedural aspects, the court ruled that there was no necessity to refer matters to oral evidence, as the central legal questions could be determined based on the papers before it ([92]-[95]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court held that the President possessed the implied power to dismiss the Director-General of the NIA as necessary to enforce trust and ensure national security. However, by failing to act with procedural fairness, the President's actions were declared inconsistent with the Constitution and therefore invalid relative to the unilateral amendment of Masetlha’s employment term.

The court ordered the state to compensate Masetlha for the issue's unfair handling by paying his full salary and benefits up until the original end date of his contract, thereby placing him in the financial position he would have occupied had the breach not occurred ([104]). The matter illustrates the delicate balance between executive power and the procedural rights of public servants, reinforcing the principle that such authority must always operate within the bounds of constitutional fairness and legality.
