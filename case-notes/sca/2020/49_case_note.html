FIRAC Case Note: Hewetson v The Law Society of the Free State [2020] ZASCA 49

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–22])

The appellant, Mrs Yolandi Hewetson, and her husband, Mr Alexander Fowly Hewetson, had been practising as attorneys under the firm name Hewetson Incorporated in Welkom, Free State. They were the firm’s sole directors ([para 1]). Following concerns regarding the firm’s financial affairs, the Law Society of the Free State (the Law Society) obtained interim relief against the Hewetsons ([para 1]). On 15 December 2017, both were struck off the roll of practising attorneys due to serious allegations of financial misconduct, notably misappropriation of trust funds ([paras 2–3]).

Substantively, the appellant admitted that her firm had failed to maintain proper accounting practices, resulting in a significant trust shortfall. However, she contended that her husband was solely responsible for the firm's finances and that her awareness of the financial discrepancies only surfaced late in 2015 ([paras 10–11]). An informal investigation led by the appellant in December 2015 indicated a trust shortfall of R1,789,766.56, prompting her to report the matter to the Law Society ([para 12]).

During the legal proceedings, the high court acknowledged that the appellant's failure to ensure compliance with accounting regulations had direct implications for the public's trust in the legal profession. Nonetheless, the appellant contested the severity of the sanctions.

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1–5])

Key issues arose as to whether the high court's decision to strike the appellant from the roll was justified, particularly regarding her personal culpability in the misconduct. The legal queries that emerged included:

  1. Did the appellant possess actual knowledge of her husband’s misappropriation of trust funds?
  2. Should the appropriate remedy for the appellant's misconduct have been a suspension rather than removal from the roll of attorneys?
  3. What were the implications of the appellant’s alleged dishonesty or lack of full disclosure during the proceedings?

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 2–5, 9])

The proceedings were conducted under the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979, particularly section 22(1)(d), which allows for the removal or suspension of attorneys deemed unfit to practice. Precedential cases were invoked to outline the standards and criteria for determining an attorney's fitness and propriety, including:

  • Solomon v Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope ([para 2]), establishing the Law Society’s role in protecting public interests.
  • Malan v Law Society of the Northern Provinces ([paras 4–7]), which clarified the burden of proof in fitness determinations and the stringent nature of the evidentiary standards in disciplinary matters.

The court emphasized that the risk and implications of striking an attorney from the roll, particularly when dishonesty is involved, necessitate the highest respect for established legal protocols ([para 29]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 6–40])

The court scrutinized the appellant’s assertions regarding her lack of knowledge about financial irregularities. Evidence was presented, including affidavits from staff members indicating that the appellant was aware of her husband's misconduct well before her report to the Law Society ([paras 10–11, 21–22]). The WhatsApp exchanges between the appellant and her husband further evidenced her prior knowledge of the misuse of trust funds, contradictory to her later claims ([paras 25–27]).

Key points of potential dishonesty were evaluated, especially surrounding the dates of her awareness of misconduct. The court noted a substantial trust deficit caused by her failure to ensure adherence to professional integrity standards ([para 58]). Consequently, the court determined that while her initial reporting of her husband’s misconduct was a mitigating factor, it was overshadowed by her delayed reporting and contradictory statements under oath ([paras 35–39]).

Moreover, the majority opinion of the judges drew a line between the severity of the misconduct and the appropriateness of suspension as opposed to striking from the roll, ultimately concluding that her actions placed public trust at significant risk ([paras 49–75]).

C – Conclusion: Court's holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 41–78])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal to some extent, reinstating the high court's prior order regarding the appellant’s immediate suspension pending a further inquiry into her knowledge and participation in the financial misconduct ([paras 1–4]). However, it mandated a fresh hearing to investigate the nature of her knowledge and the circumstances surrounding her delayed reporting ([para 3]).

The findings indicated that the high court had misdirected itself regarding critical factual conclusions but affirmed that the appellant's conduct warranted close examination. The court suggested that dishonesty and lack of fiduciary integrity were fatal to her application for relief from the roll ([paras 64–70]). The court refrained from an outright dismissal of the appeal, emphasizing the necessity for further inquiries underlines the complex interplay between disciplinary measures and public protection.

In summary, the legal implications of this judgment reaffirm the stringent accountability standards imposed on legal practitioners and the critical public interest aspects underpinning disciplinary proceedings against attorney misconduct. The outcome underscores the need for a comprehensive examination of an attorney's fitness when serious allegations arise, allowing for due process while safeguarding the legal profession's integrity.
