Opposition to Urban Tolling Alliance v The South African National Roads Agency Limited (90/2013) [2013] ZASCA 148

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 148

F – Facts

The case arises from a public outcry against the implementation of tolling on seven national roads in Gauteng, which form part of the larger Gauteng Freeway Improvement Project (GFIP), initiated by the South African National Roads Agency Limited (SANRAL) with the approval of the Minister of Transport ([1], [6]). The appellants—comprising the Opposition to Urban Tolling Alliance (OUTA), the South African Vehicle Renting and Leasing Association (SAVRALA), the Quadpara Association of South Africa, and the South African National Consumer Union—sought to challenge the decision of SANRAL to declare these roads as toll roads under Section 27 of the South African National Roads Agency Act 7 of 1998 ([2], [4]).

The declarations were made through the publication in the Government Gazette in early 2008, following a series of public consultations that the appellants allege were inadequately conducted ([1], [12]). After SANRAL and the Minister granted approval for tolling, the outcry culminated in a legal challenge in March 2012 in the North Gauteng High Court, which both sought an urgent interdict against toll collection and a review application to set aside the decision to toll ([2], [3]).

The trial court initially granted an interim interdict but this was later set aside by the Constitutional Court on appeal ([3]). Subsequently, the High Court dismissed the review application, leading to the present appeal ([3], [4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue revolves around the procedural fairness of SANRAL's actions in declaring the roads as toll roads, particularly the adequacy of public consultation under Section 27 of the Act and the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([21]). Additionally, the appeal raised questions about the interpretation of the delay rule found in PAJA, specifically regarding the 180-day timeline for initiating judicial review and its extension provisions under Section 9 ([23], [24]).

Secondary issues included whether the method of funding through tolling rather than fuel levies constituted unreasonable administrative action, and the implications of legal and financial commitments arising from the decision to implement tolling ([17], [21], [41]).

R – Rules

The relevant legislative framework is primarily constituted by:

  1. Section 27 of the South African National Roads Agency Act 7 of 1998– This section outlines the procedures for declaring national roads as toll roads, including requirements for public notice and consultation ([4]).
  2. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)– Particularly, Sections 6(2)(h) prohibiting unreasonable administrative action and Section 7 regarding the time limits for review applications ([23], [24]).
  3. Case Law– Notably, precedents concerning the delay rule apply, indicating that any review application must be brought without unreasonable delay, with a period of 180 days post-notification of the administrative action to be a statutory benchmark ([24]).
  4. Constitutional Principles– Relevant to the case is Section 25 of the Constitution, which concerns the deprivation of property, underpinning the fairness of cost impositions through tolling ([42]).

A – Application

The court's judgment applied the outlined statutory rules and legislative provisions to assess whether there had been compliance with procedural requirements regarding public consultation and whether the appellants had adequately justified the delay in filing their application for judicial review.

Procedural Fairness

In determining procedural fairness, the court examined whether SANRAL had satisfied the notice and comment requirements of Section 27(4) of the Act. While the appellants argued that the consultations were insufficient, the court found that SANRAL had indeed met the statutory requirements by publishing notices and adequately soliciting public input, thus dismissing the argument of procedural unfairness ([10], [21]).

Delay Rule

Regarding the delay, the court found that significant time had elapsed—from the early communications in 2007 about the toll roads to the eventual application in March 2012—exceeding the 180-day timeline established by PAJA. The appellants sought to extend this limit based on their belief that they were attempting to resolve the dispute politically prior to pursuing legal avenues ([26], [29]). However, the court emphasized that this was insufficient to validate the delay under the interests of justice standard set forth in Section 9 of PAJA ([41]).

The court also highlighted that the realities of infrastructure development and political consequences made it imprudent to unwind actions taken based on the tolling declaration after such a significant lapse of time ([32]). Therefore, the procedural timeline established by PAJA precluded the court from considering the merits of the review application.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the appellants' application for judicial review was rendered untenable due to failure to adhere to the statutory time limit imposed by PAJA. The court concluded that the notices published by SANRAL sufficiently met the legal requirements for public consultation, thus upholding the declarations of toll roads as lawful administrative actions ([45]).

As a result, the appeal was dismissed with no order as to costs, reflecting the application of the Biowatch principle, acknowledging that while the appellants were not entirely successful, public interest litigation should not be discouraged by cost implications ([44]).

The implication of this ruling reinforces the importance of adhering to prescribed timelines in administrative law and affirms the legal foundations of SANRAL's tolling decisions, which were justified in the context of significant infrastructure needs and government policy.
