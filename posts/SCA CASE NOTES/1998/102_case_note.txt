# Case Note: Republic of South Africa - Santam Limited v Ethwar

## F – Facts

The appeal in **Santam Limited v Ethwar** arises from a dispute over the prescription of a claim for payment of costs stemming from a settlement agreement pursuant to the Motor Vehicle Accidents Act 84 of 1986. The respondent, Sonraj Ethwar, initiated legal proceedings against the appellant, Santam Limited, in the Durban and Coast Local Division in 1990 ([para 2]). Santam defended the action but subsequently made an offer to settle the matter on 14 September 1992, which included payment of R292,000 to the plaintiff and provision of a certificate for future treatment ([para 3]).

Ethwar accepted this offer on 17 September 1992. The appellant fulfilled its obligations regarding payment on the same day, and a certificate was issued on 6 October 1992. Following these interactions, there was no further communication until 29 September 1995, when Ethwar’s attorneys served a bill of costs and a notice of taxation ([para 4]). Santam argued that Ethwar's right to tax the bill had become prescribed, as more than three years had elapsed since the settlement ([para 5]).

The taxing master proceeded to tax the bill despite Santam's objections, leading Ethwar to obtain a default judgment and issue a writ of execution. Santam applied to have this judgment set aside, claiming a failure to give notice as required under Rule 34(7) before judgment was sought ([paras 6–7]). After negotiations, the court eventually set aside the default judgment while rejecting Santam's claim that Ethwar's right to costs had become prescribed.

## I – Issues

The principal legal question before the Court was whether the respondent's claim for costs, as outlined in the settlement agreement, had become prescribed ([para 5]). Specifically, the Court needed to determine the date on which the debt became "due" under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, which would affect the initiation of the prescription period ([para 6]).

Furthermore, the court examined whether the respondent's right to have costs taxed constituted a "debt" for the purposes of prescription and the implications of the requirement for mutual agreement or taxation prior to such costs being payable ([paras 10–11]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions are contained in the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly section 12(1), which states that "prescription shall commence running as soon as the debt is due" ([para 7]). This aligns with past case law, including **The Master v IL Back & Co Ltd** and **Benson and Another v Walters and Others**, which interpreted "debt due" as requiring a liquidated money obligation that is immediately claimable ([para 6]).

Judicial interpretations have further clarified that a debt does not necessarily become due simply upon the conclusion of an agreement, but rather when the creditor has a complete cause of action to recover that debt. This aspect is crucial as it establishes when the prescription period commences ([para 8]).

## A – Application

In applying these legal principles to the facts of the case, the Court explored whether the costs could be deemed "due" at the moment the parties entered into the settlement agreement on 17 September 1992. The Court noted that the settlement included a provision that required an agreement or taxation of costs before they could be claimed ([para 10]). Thus, the initiation of the prescription period was contingent upon the completion of these procedural steps.

The argument placed by Santam that the "claim for costs" had become due on the date of the settlement was a crucial point of contention. The Court found that unless agreement or taxation had occurred, the costs remained unliquidated and, therefore, not immediately recoverable, meaning prescription had not commenced ([para 20]).

The learned judge observed that the requirement for taxation served to protect both parties from indefinite liability and also to ensure that claims could not remain in abeyance indefinitely, which could unfairly prejudice the debtor ([para 21]). This was consistent with the overarching principles of the Prescription Act aimed at encouraging timely resolution of debts.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, upholding the lower court's decision that the respondent's claim did not become prescribed on the date of the settlement agreement due to the necessity of taxation or mutual agreement prior to recovery. Therefore, the costs could not be claimed until a bill had been taxed, which had not happened by the prescription date suggested by Santam ([paras 22-23]).

In conclusion, the decision in **Santam Limited v Ethwar** underscores the importance of clarity in settlement agreements regarding the recoverability of costs and the implications of the Prescription Act in preserving the rights of creditors to seek timely satisfaction of claims. The case illustrates how procedural requirements can impact both the accrual of claims and the running of prescription periods, reaffirming the need for parties to consider these aspects carefully during settlement discussions.
