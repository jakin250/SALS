DVB Behuising (Pty) Limited v North West Provincial Government

DVB Behuising (Pty) Limited (the applicant) contested this repeal, claiming it barred the registration of deeds for properties sold under the Proclamation, which severely impacted their business, particularly in terms of mortgage approvals from banks ([3]–[4]).

Constitutional Court 2000

F – Facts

This case revolves around the constitutionality of the North West Local Government Laws Amendment Act 7 of 1998, specifically Section 6 which aimed to repeal the Proclamation R293 of 1962 (the Proclamation). The Proclamation was a remnant of apartheid legislation, establishing townships and governing land tenure for African citizens within these areas ([1]–[2]).

DVB Behuising (Pty) Limited (the applicant) contested this repeal, claiming it barred the registration of deeds for properties sold under the Proclamation, which severely impacted their business, particularly in terms of mortgage approvals from banks ([3]–[4]). The North West Provincial Government (the first respondent) did not contest the application, and the Registrar of Deeds chose to abide by the court’s decision ([5]).

The High Court ruled in favor of DVB, declaring the repeal unconstitutional, and referred the matter to the Constitutional Court for confirmation ([6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question before the court was whether the North West Provincial Legislature had the authority to repeal parts of the Proclamation, specifically concerning land tenure, a subject intertwined with national legislative competencies. Further inquiries focused on whether the sections of the Proclamation in question were properly assigned to the North West for administrative purposes under the interim Constitution ([16]–[20]).

R – Rules

Key legal provisions relevant to this case included:

  1. Constitution of South Africa, 1996: Sections 43(b), 104, and 239 defined the legislative competencies assigned to provincial and national governments. Particularly crucial was Section 235(8) which allowed the President to assign administrative authority over certain laws to provinces, provided they fell within functional areas outlined in Schedule 6 of the Constitution ([21]–[23]).
  2. Interim Constitution: This initially governed the legal context of the Proclamation's repeal and included crucial sections determining the assignment of legislative powers related to historical land laws ([20]–[26]).
  3. Upgrading of Land Tenure Rights Act 112 of 1991: This aimed to convert insecure tenure rights into full ownership, implicitly phasing out politically motivated forms of tenure established under the Proclamation ([8]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court's analysis began with the function and historical context of the Proclamation ([41]–[47]). The court first recognized that the legislative intent behind the Proclamation was fundamentally tied to apartheid policies, regulating racial segregation and land usage ([2]).

The majority opinion, led by Ngcobo J, argued that the Proclamation, while administratively assigned to the North West, still retained matters that fell outside provincial competencies, particularly in relation to land tenure and registration which were national matters as per the provisions of Section 126(3)(a)-(e) of the interim Constitution ([108]).

The Court ultimately held that the North West lacked the competence to repeal the provisions regarding the tenure rights and registration laid down in the Proclamation, framing these as concerns requiring national, uniform regulation ([106]–[108]). The repeal of Sections 1 and 3 of Chapter 1 and Chapter 9 was found to be unconstitutional while confirming the rest of the High Court’s ruling that proclaimed a constitutional invalidity of the North West Act’s provisions regarding land tenure ([66]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the North West Provincial Legislature could validly repeal the Proclamation, except for the provisions concerning tenure and deeds registration. The judgment emphasized the imperative of removing vestiges of apartheid legislation while affirming national control over substantive land reform ([72]–[73]).

The significance of this decision reinforces the constitutional mandate to ensure secure land tenure and the transformation of historically inequitable land law. The ruling illustrated the delicate balance between provincial legislative power and the overarching need for national uniformity in addressing historical injustices within South Africa's land reform landscape ([70], [98]).

This case sets a precedent that strengthens the constitutional framework aimed at rectifying past inequalities and reaffirms the duties of national government in land reform contexts, particularly in the transition from apartheid towards a more equitable democratic society.
