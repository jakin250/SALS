Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Mohuba v University of Limpopo (730/2022) [2023] ZASCA 139

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The appeal arises from the Limpopo Division of the High Court (Ledwaba AJ), which upheld the University of Limpopo's special plea against the claim for specific performance by Mr. Dinkwanyane Kgalema Mohuba ([1]). The appellant, employed by the university as the Executive Director of Marketing and Communication, applied for and was accepted as a doctoral student in August 2016. Mohuba had his thesis proposal approved in June 2017, and by March 2018, the relevant committee recommended the award of his degree ([2]). 

However, on 14 March 2018, complaints arose regarding Mohuba's eligibility due to his relatively short enrollment period, leading the university to investigate these allegations. Subsequently, the university refused to confer the degree and terminated his enrollment on 5 October 2018 ([3]–[4]). Mohuba initiated legal proceedings against the university in July 2019, seeking an order for specific performance based on an alleged tacit contract that he believed entitled him to the graduation despite the termination of his enrollment ([4]–[6]). 

The university countered with a special plea, asserting that the matter constituted administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), which required Mohuba to pursue a review rather than a contract claim after his registration was terminated ([7]–[8]). The high court ultimately upheld the special plea, leading to this appeal ([10]–[11]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues raised by this case include:
1. The nature of the relationship between a university and its student: Is it contractual or administrative? ([12], [18]).
2. Whether the university's refusal to confer a degree constituted a breach of contract or was merely administrative action requiring judicial review under PAJA?
3. The validity of the high court’s decision to uphold the special plea and dismiss Mohuba's claim for specific performance ([1], [11]).

### R – Rules
The ruling in this case is guided by several statutes and legal precedents:
1. **Higher Education Act 101 of 1997, Section 65B**: This stipulates that no degree may be conferred upon anyone who has not been registered as a student for the required period and met the corresponding academic standards ([13]).
2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: This act defines administrative actions and outlines the processes relevant to judicial reviews ([7]).
3. **Case Law**: Precedent set in **Haynes v King Williamstown Municipality** provides that specific performance is an appropriate remedy in contract law ([15]1). Further cases like **Sibanyoni v University of Fort Hare**, **Mkhize v Rector of University of Zululand**, and **South African National Parks v MTO Forestry** elaborate on the relationship between universities and students ([18]).

### A – Application
The court's analysis began with the nature of the relationship between the university and Mohuba. It noted that while the high court recognized the relationship as contractual, it nonetheless treated the university's actions as administrative. The Supreme Court of Appeal found this rationale to be contradictory. If the court held the relationship to be contractual, the university’s special plea asserting that the matter was solely administrative should have been dismissed, allowing the claim for specific performance to proceed ([15]–[19]).

The court further noted that Mohuba had indeed established the terms of the alleged tacit contract, including compliance with the requisite conditions set by the university and the Senate's expectations for student registration ([11], [15]). The court emphasized the need for a determination on whether his termination was a breach of that contract or just a legitimate administrative decision, thus making it imprudent to dispose of the matter at the special plea stage ([18]).

Moreover, the court highlighted the complexities of the issues surrounding Mohuba's case, indicating that these were better suited for thorough evaluation during trial, where comprehensive evidence could be presented ([19]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the high court had erred in its judgment by upholding the special plea and dismissing Mohuba's claim. The matter was remitted back to the high court for trial, allowing for a detailed examination of both the contractual and administrative aspects of the case ([20]–[21]). Consequently, each party would bear its own costs, acknowledging the inappropriate separation of issues handled in the initial court proceedings. This decision underscores the interplay between administrative and contract law in the context of higher education, emphasizing that issues concerning the conferral of degrees and student rights necessitate careful judicial scrutiny and should not be hastily resolved through procedural devices like special pleas.
