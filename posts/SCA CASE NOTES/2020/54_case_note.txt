# Case Note: Monyepao v Ledwaba and Others (Case no 1368/18) [2020] ZASCA 54

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–12])

This appeal arises from the estate of the late Tlou Coliphta Phago, who passed away intestate on 22 December 2012 ([1]). The appellant, Matsatsi Dinah Tiny Monyepao, and the first respondent, Mokgaetji Maria Ledwaba, both claim to have been married to Mr Phago under customary law at the time of his death. Ms Monyepao asserts that Ms Ledwaba’s marriage to Mr Phago ended in February 2008, which Ms Ledwaba contests, claiming their marriage remained valid until Mr Phago’s death ([1], [16]).

Both women were appointed as executors of Mr Phago’s deceased estate, leading to a dispute where Ms Monyepao sought legal relief to declare Ms Ledwaba’s marriage invalid and to have her benefits forfeited to the estate. The initial court granted Ms Monyepao partial relief, ordering that Ms Ledwaba’s interest in the estate was forfeited. However, the full court later overturned this decision ([2]). This appeal is treated on special leave ([2]).

A notable procedural aspect included applications for condonation for the late filing of necessary documents, which will be addressed before the merits of the appeal are considered ([3]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 16–18])

The core legal issues raised by this appeal are as follows:

1. **Validity of Marriages**:
   - Was the customary marriage between Ms Ledwaba and Mr Phago dissolved in February 2008 ([16](a))?
   - Did Ms Ledwaba’s subsequent civil marriage to Mr Kwele invalidate her customary marriage with Mr Phago ([16](b))?

2. **Forfeiture of Benefits**:
   - In the alternative, can Ms Ledwaba’s matrimonial benefits from her marriage to Mr Phago be forfeited in favor of his estate, given that Ms Monyepao is not a party to the original marriage ([16](c))?

3. **Administrative Relief**:
   - Should the court order the allocation of immovable property and review the co-executor appointment ([17])?

These issues necessitate an examination of customary marriage validity and the capacity for forfeiture of matrimonial benefits.

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 19–22])

The court’s ruling is grounded in statutory provisions and prior judicial authority. Firstly, the **Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998** stipulates that a customary marriage may only be dissolved by a decree of divorce issued by a court, which Ms Monyepao failed to establish ([18]). 

The court also relied on precedents that affirm the principle that a civil marriage contracted while a party remains married under customary law is a nullity ([19]). The judgments in *Netshituka v Netshituka and Others* ([19]) and (*Thembisile and Another v Thembisile and Another* 2002 ([19])) illustrate that customary marriages remain valid despite the subsequent civil marriage.

Regarding forfeiture of benefits, Section 9(1) of the **Divorce Act 70 of 1979** elucidates that forfeiture claims arise only in the context of divorce proceedings, highlighting Ms Monyepao's ineligibility to claim this relief as an outsider ([20]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 23–24])

In determining whether Ms Ledwaba’s marriage was dissolved, the court firmly relied on the absence of a divorce decree, as Ms Monyepao failed to present any admissible evidence supporting her claim of dissolution ([18]). Consequently, the claim was dismissed.

With regard to Ms Ledwaba’s civil marriage to Mr Kwele, the court reiterated previous rulings establishing that civil marriages do not contravene existing customary marriages ([19]). Therefore, the appellants' argument was invalidated, confirming the persistence of Ms Ledwaba’s marriage to Mr Phago.

On the matter of forfeiture, the court underscored that this remedy is exclusively applicable in divorce proceedings. Since the circumstances did not present such a framework, the appeal against this aspect was also dismissed ([21]). The relief claimed to award property and alter executor appointments was equally noteworthy, as the court emphasized the absence of legal foundation and necessity for substantiating evidence in the application ([22]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([24])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the ruling of the full court that the initial forfeiture order was beyond jurisdictional reach, as Ms Monyepao lacked standing to petition for benefits forfeiture. Furthermore, the court emphasized that customary marriages cannot simply be nullified through subsequent civil marriage and stressed the necessity of judicial orders to dissolve such unions ([24]).

The decision carries significant implications for similar proceedings involving customary marriages and reinforces the legal framework governing dissolution and the conditions under which matrimonial benefits may be forfeited. It underscores the critical importance of adhering to established legal procedures, ensures the protective measures afforded by statutory law continue to be upheld, and clarifies that beneficiaries in estate matters must navigate their claims within a clearly defined legal context.
