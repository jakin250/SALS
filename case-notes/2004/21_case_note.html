Case Title: The City of Cape Town & Others v Anita Marie Robertson & Guy Trevor Robertson Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa Case No: CCT 19/04 Judgment Date: 29 November 2004 Judge: Moseneke J

Constitutional Court 2004 CCT 19/04

Case Title: The City of Cape Town & Others v Anita Marie Robertson & Guy Trevor Robertson

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case No: CCT 19/04
Judgment Date: 29 November 2004
Judge: Moseneke J

F - Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–8])

The case concerns an elderly couple, the Robertsons, who successfully obtained declaratory and interdictory relief from the Cape High Court, prohibiting the City of Cape Town (the City) from levying property rates based on a provisional valuation roll formulated for the 2002/2003 financial year. The provisional roll indicated that their property in Camps Bay, initially acquired in 1969 for R7,250, was now valued at R1.7 million. This substantial increase resulted in their annual property tax liability rising from R8,203.44 to R16,321.80, which they deemed exorbitant and unaffordable ([paras 5–7]).

The High Court also ruled that section 21 of the Local Government Laws Amendment Act 2002 (the Amendment Act) was inconsistent with the Constitution, and thus invalid. The ruling was suspended for one year to allow for legislative rectification. The matter was subsequently referred to the Constitutional Court for confirmation ([para 2]).

The City and the Minister of Provincial and Local Government appealed this decision. The appeal addressed the validity of the provisional valuation roll, the powers of the City concerning property rates, and the necessity to decide the constitutional challenge against the Amendment Act ([paras 3–4]).

I - Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 33–34])

  1. Was the Property Valuation Ordinance (PVO) a law "in force" at the commencement of the interim Constitution, and if not, is subsection 93(7) of the Structures Act necessary to remedy this perceived lacuna?
  2. Is the City a "local authority" within the definition of the PVO, and if not, does it have the power to conduct general valuations and levy property rates based on the PVO?
  3. Can the City utilize a provisional valuation roll under the powers vested in it by subsection 10G of the Transition Act, and does it need to rely on subsection 93(9) of the Structures Act?
  4. Is it necessary for the Court to address the constitutional challenge to section 21 of the Amendment Act?
  5. If so, does the impugned legislation contravene the Constitution?

R - Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 27–28, 60–62])

The court relied on the following legal frameworks and rulings:

  1. Property Valuation Ordinance, 1993 (PVO) - This ordinance outlines the legislative framework for property valuation and taxation.
  2. Structures Act, 1998 - It establishes the legal framework for municipalities and outlines their powers and obligations.
  3. Subsections 154(2) and 229(5) of the Constitution - These provisions dictate procedural requirements for legislation affecting local government, necessitating public consultation with organized local government and the Financial and Fiscal Commission (FFC).
  4. Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council - This precedent articulated the nature of local government's powers and reinforced that local government entities derive powers directly from the Constitution, not merely as delegated powers from the provinces ([paras 49–56]).
  5. Mashavha v President of the RSA - Clarified the interpretation of laws “in force” at the inception of the interim Constitution ([paras 35–36]).

A - Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 30–27, 62–78])

The Constitutional Court upheld the High Court's reasoning that the PVO remained a valid law during the transitional period and continued to exist as a law "in force" under the interim Constitution. The court noted that the PVO, having been enacted prior to the Constitution's inception but only coming into effect later, did not lapse simply due to the transitional provisions ([para 34]).

Regarding the binary question of whether the City qualifies as a "local authority," the Court concluded that although the City was established as a category A municipality under the Structures Act and was not a "local authority" in the traditional sense embodied in the PVO, it nevertheless possessed the necessary powers to conduct property valuation and levy taxes by virtue of the transitional regulations. Subsections 93(4) and (5) were interpreted as preserving the City's powers ([paras 38–62]).

The court critically analyzed whether the City could levy rates based on a provisional roll, concluding that there is no statutory requirement in the PVO precluding the use of a provisional valuation roll for tax purposes. It asserted that such a provisional valuation facilitates timely municipal revenue generation, which aligns with the objectives of the municipal fiscal structure ([paras 63–69]).

Finally, regarding the constitutional challenge against section 21 of the Amendment Act, although the High Court found it invalid due to procedural irregularities relating to public consultation and the absence of preliminary assessments involving the FFC, the Constitutional Court determined that due to its findings on the property ratings, it need not confirm the High Court's invalidation of the Amendment Act. The challenge against section 21 was deemed unnecessary for their determinations ([paras 71–78]).

C - Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 78–80])

The Constitutional Court held that the appeal by the City and Minister was upheld, consequently abolishing the High Court's orders. The judgements reaffirmed that the City has the power to levy property rates based on valuations enacted via the PVO, notwithstanding the controversy surrounding its status as a local authority. The provisions within the PVO and relevant legislative framework empower the City without additional legislative alteration due to an expository function within existing laws ([para 80]).

The implications of the ruling ensure local municipalities retain the fiscal and sovereignty necessary for governance while remaining compliant with the overarching legal frameworks and constitutional mandates. The Court’s judgment further clarifies the powers and responsibilities of municipal governance within constitutional confines, promoting focused and effective local administration.

This case signifies a critical intersection of property law and constitutional requirements within the South African legal system, reflecting ongoing complexities in the transition towards effective local governance.
