Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

### Case Note: Clear Enterprises (Pty) Ltd v SARS  [2011] ZASCA 164

---

#### FIRAC Method

**F - Facts**  
The appellant, Clear Enterprises (Pty) Ltd, is a Botswana-based company that had three of its trucks detained by the South African Revenue Service (SARS) under the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964. The first truck was detained on 22 February 2007, with additional trucks detained later in April and May 2007 ([paras 3-4]). Clear Enterprises contested the lawfulness of these detentions in separate applications before the North Gauteng High Court, seeking, inter alia, the return of the trucks, which they claimed had been lawfully imported into Botswana and were allowed to temporarily enter South Africa under certain interstate customs provisions ([paras 5-9]).

The High Court dismissed the applications, yet permitted Clear Enterprises to appeal. However, subsequent developments revealed that the vehicles had been seized by the International Trade Administration Commission (ITAC) prior to the High Court's decision, raising doubts as to the practical implications of the appeal ([paras 10-11]). The Central questions turned on whether the judgment sought would yield any practical effect ([paras 12-20]).

---

**I - Issues**  
1. **Practical Effect**: The primary legal issue was whether the appeal had practical significance, given that the trucks in question had already been seized by ITAC, potentially rendering the original appeal moot ([paras 11-12]).
2. **Declaration of Rights**: A secondary issue arose from the request of Clear Enterprises to explore declaratory relief concerning interpretations of the SACU agreements and the Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) on road transportation ([para 14]).

---

**R - Rules**  
Section 21A(1) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 states that an appeal may be dismissed if the judgment sought will have no practical effect ([paras 2-4]). The Court emphasized that it traditionally refrains from deciding cases that are merely of academic interest, referencing established precedents such as *Radio Pretoria v Chairman, Independent Communications Authority of South Africa* 2005 (1) SA 47 (SCA) ([paras 12-13]). Moreover, the principle of "ripeness" in legal disputes was examined, affirming that courts should not adjudicate cases that have not matured into concrete controversies ([paras 17-19]).

---

**A - Application**  
The court assessed whether the appeal had practical merit, noting that the initial relief sought—return of the trucks—had become academic following their seizure by ITAC. Despite arguments from both parties to continue with the appeal due to potential implications in related matters, the court concluded that each case must be decided on its own facts, and the current dispute did not present a live issue ([paras 14-19]). 

The court reiterated that, without the original live controversy, the appeal could not yield practical results, thereby satisfying the conditions for dismissal under s. 21A ([para 19]).

---

**C - Conclusion**  
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal struck the appeal off the roll, mandating each party to bear its own costs. The ruling reflects a strong adherence to judicial economy and the principle that courts should not provide advisory opinions on moot issues. The judgment reinforces the importance of "practical effect" in appellate jurisdiction and underlines the necessity for concrete controversies to be presented before courts ([paras 20]).

This case serves as a key reminder of the courts’ role in adjudicating only those disputes that possess clear, practical significance, thus preserving judicial resources and ensuring that the merits of cases are properly adjudicated in context ([paras 18-19]).
