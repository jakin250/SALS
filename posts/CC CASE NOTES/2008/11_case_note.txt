# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of *Walele v City of Cape Town* [2008] ZACC 11

### F – Facts
The applicant, Azeem Hassan Walele, owns property adjacent to the erf of the second to fifth respondents in Walmer Estate, Cape Town, where they applied to the City of Cape Town for approval to construct a four-storey block of flats. The plans were submitted to the City on 2 March 2006 and were subsequently approved following a series of internal reviews by various departments, with the Building Control Officer endorsing the plans as compliant with the zoning regulations ([para 6]). 

The approval was contested by Walele, who claimed it would cause a devaluation of his property, asserting that the City failed to provide him with a hearing prior to the approval, thus breaching the **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act** (PAJA) ([para 9]). After unsuccessful attempts to overturn this decision in the Cape High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal, Walele sought leave to appeal in the Constitutional Court. 

### I – Issues
The principal legal issues presented in this case include:

1. **Delegation of Power**: Did the decision-maker, Mr. Griffiths, possess the necessary authority to approve the building plans? 
2. **Procedural Fairness**: Was the City obliged to provide Walele an opportunity to be heard prior to approving the plans in light of PAJA?
3. **Existence of a Recommendation**: Did the Building Control Officer's endorsement constitute a valid recommendation as per sections 6 and 7 of the Building Standards Act?
4. **Assessment of Disqualifying Factors**: Did the City properly consider whether the proposed building would trigger disqualifying factors under section 7(1)(b)(ii) of the Building Standards Act?

### R – Rules
1. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Section 3 mandates procedural fairness for administrative actions that materially and adversely affect rights or legitimate expectations.
2. **Building Standards Act 103 of 1977**: Sections 6 and 7 outline the approval process for building plans, stipulating the need for a recommendation from the Building Control Officer and the criteria for approval that must not trigger certain disqualifying factors.
3. **Case Law Precedents**: 
   - *Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Environmental Affairs* ([2004] ZACC 15) confirms that judicial review arises from PAJA rather than common law.
   - *Erf 167 Orchards CC v Greater Johannesburg Metropolitan Council* (1999) recognized a neighbour's legitimate expectation to be heard prior to approval of plans.
   - *Odendaal v Eastern Metropolitan Local Council* (1999) affirmed that a neighbour does not have an inherent right to a hearing for minor approvals compliant with zoning.

### A – Application
In determining whether the City acted within its authority, the court analyzed the delegation of power to Mr. Griffiths, ultimately finding that he was duly delegated and acted within his capacity as per the City's documentation ([paras 99–100]).

Concerning procedural fairness, the court found that the applicant failed to establish a "right" as defined under section 3 of PAJA, as the regulations permitted the proposed development within zoning laws, meaning any adverse effects on Walele's property were subject to the lawful use by neighbouring owners ([para 132]). The argument that Walele had a legitimate expectation to be heard was also addressed by examining past practices, revealing that merely one case where notice was given did not establish a reasonable expectation of continuance ([para 135]).

Regarding the recommendation from the Building Control Officer, the court distinguished between administrative recommendations requiring detailed reporting and those that could suffice with a simple endorsement. It rejected Walele's argument that a lack of written reasoning invalidated the approval, highlighting the efficiency required in urban planning and indicating that the Building Control Officer’s endorsement sufficiently indicated compliance ([paras 104–109]).

Lastly, the court noted that Mr. Griffiths' approval, based on the Building Control Officer's assurance and the absence of objections from relevant departments, met the rationality test required by administrative law. It concluded that the decision was not influenced by unauthorised dictates and that there was no evidence Griffiths failed to exercise his discretion inappropriately ([paras 116–121]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court upheld Walele's appeal in part, ultimately setting aside the previous approval by the City due to inadequate assessment of the disqualifying factors related to potential property value reductions. The matter was remitted for reconsideration, emphasizing that the decision-maker must ensure the absence of any disqualifying factors before approving plans. The court awarded costs to Walele, mandating the City to cover these expenses due to its failure in proper compliance with the legislative requirements ([paras 75–76]). 

This case significantly impacts urban planning and administrative law, reaffirming the duty of local authorities to ensure that decisions regarding land use are made transparently and with due consideration to the impacts on neighboring property rights. The judgment helps clarify the contours of legitimate expectations and procedural fairness in the context of municipal approvals, establishing a balance between development rights and community interests.
