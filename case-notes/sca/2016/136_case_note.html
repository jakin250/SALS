Supreme Court of Appeal

Gees v The Provincial Minister of Cultural Affairs and Sport

Following this, Gees appealed to the first respondent, the Provincial Minister of Cultural Affairs and Sport (the MEC).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016

F – Facts

The appellant, Peter Gees, is the registered owner of a small block of flats located on Erf 1444, Vredehoek, Cape Town, which is over 60 years old ([para 2]). As such, the demolition of this structure requires a permit under Section 34 of the National Heritage Resources Act 25 of 1999 (the Act) ([para 2]). Initially, Gees’ application for a demolition permit was refused by the Built Environmental and Landscape Permit Committee of Heritage Western Cape (HWC) on July 24, 2013 ([para 3]). This refusal led to an appeal to HWC's appeals committee, which was denied on September 18, 2013 ([para 3]).

Following this, Gees appealed to the first respondent, the Provincial Minister of Cultural Affairs and Sport (the MEC). On January 21, 2015, the appointed appeal tribunal upheld Gees’ appeal and granted the demolition permit with certain conditions intended to control future development on the property ([para 4]). These conditions included maintaining the town-planning envelope of the existing building and ensuring that new building materials would be consistent with the existing structure ([para 4]). Discontented with these conditions, Gees sought judicial review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 and applied to the Western Cape High Court to set aside the conditions attached to the permit ([para 5]). The court dismissed his application, prompting this appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 6–7]).

I – Issues

The central legal question is whether a provincial heritage resources authority, when granting a permit for the demolition of a structure older than 60 years without formal heritage status, can lawfully impose conditions regulating future development on that property.[[para 1]]. Gees contended that such imposition of conditions was ultra vires the authority's powers as outlined in Section 48(2) of the Act ([para 7]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework is primarily derived from the National Heritage Resources Act 25 of 1999. Section 34 prohibits the alteration or demolition of structures older than 60 years without a permit ([para 12]). Section 48(2) allows the heritage resources authority to issue permits with conditions that might include various stipulations regarding future work ([para 13]). The key principles drawn from the Act suggest its overarching purpose is the conservation, protection, and management of heritage resources ([para 8]).

Case law cited includes Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council, Qualidental Laboratories (Pty) Ltd v Heritage Western Cape, and Vorster v Department of Economic Development, Environment and Tourism which assert that entities exercising public power must operate within the lawful parameters established by statute, adhering to the principle of legality ([para 7]).

A – Application

The court asserted that the discretion conferred upon the heritage authority under Section 48(2) is indeed broad and does permit the imposition of conditions related not only to the demolition but extending to future developments pertinent to the heritage significance of the area ([para 18]). The court drew on various factors indicating the importance of Vredehoek as a cultural area, emphasizing the significance of surrounding structures and the preservation of the overall character of the area ([paras 19–24]).

The ruling rejected the appellant’s interpretation of Section 48(2) as unnecessarily limiting, asserting that additional regulations ensure that heritage resources management is adequately robust and not confined to narrowly defined protected areas ([paras 24–25]). The imposition of conditions was subsequently framed as essential to upholding the heritage conservation mandate under the Act, rather than merely a means to control development ([para 29]).

In response to constitutional concerns raised by the appellant regarding property rights under Section 25(1) of the South African Constitution, the court found that the conditions imposed do not equate to arbitrary deprivation. It emphasized that while there is a curtailment of certain property rights, such limitations align with community interests and broader conservation goals, therefore maintaining their validity under constitutional scrutiny ([paras 30–34]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the legality of the conditions imposed by the appeal tribunal on the demolition permit, aligning with the objectives of the National Heritage Resources Act ([para 36]). The decision reinforces the critical role that heritage authorities play in protecting cultural significance while demonstrating a balance between individual ownership rights and communal heritage preservation ([props 34]). The judgement underscores the necessity for ongoing considerations within the heritage framework, supporting proactive measures for areas under potential conservation but not yet officially designated as heritage sites.

Concisely, the court's ruling elucidates that the heritage resources authority possesses the mandate to enforce conservation measures, ensuring that development aligns with preserving cultural heritage while furthering the broader aims of the National Heritage Resources Act. The costs of the appeal were awarded to the MEC, including the costs of two counsels ([para 36]).
