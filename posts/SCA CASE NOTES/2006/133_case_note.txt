Source Folder: 2006

# Case Note: Meter NO v Geo Parkes & Son (Pty) Ltd

**In the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa**

**Neutral Citation:** [2006] SCA 161 (RSA)

---

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts

The dispute arose from a veld fire that occurred on 23 September 2000, affecting properties owned by the A E Berman Children’s Trust (“the Trust”), represented by the appellants, and Geo Parkes & Son (Pty) Ltd (“the respondent”). The Trust, the registered owner of a portion of Farm Hoogekraal 182, had been in a contractual arrangement with André de Jager, a master's student in apiculture, who was conducting bee-keeping operations on the property. As detailed in the affidavits, De Jager engaged Heinrich Kemp to assist him on the day of the fire ([1]-[5]).

During the bee-smoking operation, Kemp ignited pine needles on a paved area, which subsequently caught alight and spread uncontrollably. Despite efforts by De Jager and Kemp to extinguish the fire, it rapidly progressed into the adjoining properties of the respondent, causing extensive damage estimated at R393,448.08 ([6]-[11]).

The respondent initiated legal action against the appellants and De Jager, alleging negligence regarding the starting and control of the fire. The appellants contended that they were not liable, asserting contributory negligence on the part of the respondent ([12]-[13]).

In a trial presided over by Zondi AJ, the court ruled in favor of the respondent. The trial judge found De Jager negligent in his conduct and the appellants liable for damages, attributing 30% fault to the Trust and 70% to De Jager ([14]-[15]).

### I – Issues

The central legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

1. **Negligence**: Whether De Jager was negligent in his activities on the day of the fire and if the Trust could be held vicariously liable for his actions ([14]-[17]).
2. **Presumption of Negligence**: Whether the Trust managed to rebut the legal presumption of negligence under section 34(1) of the National Veld and Forest Fire Act 101 of 1998 ([20]-[21]).
3. **Contributory Negligence**: Whether the respondent failed to maintain adequate fire breaks which could have mitigated the damage caused by the fire ([29]-[30]).
4. **Apportionment of Liability**: Issues concerning the apportionment of damages and whether the trial court’s decision regarding joint liability was appropriate ([33]-[34]).

### R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions and precedents considered in the judgment include:

1. **National Veld and Forest Fire Act 101 of 1998**: Specifically, sections 2 and 12, which impose duties on landowners to prevent fires from starting or spreading ([19]-[20]).
2. **Presumptions of Negligence**: Section 34(1) provides that a defendant is presumed negligent if a fire causing loss spread from their property unless they are a member of a fire protection association ([20]).
3. **Vicarious Liability**: The principles governing vicarious liability arise when an employee or agent causes harm within the scope of their employment or duty ([14]-[18]).
4. **Contributory Negligence**: Explores the degree of fault attributable to the respondent concerning their fire prevention measures ([29]-[32]).

### A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the court found multiple failures by De Jager, including conducting bee-smoking activities on a hot and dry day, failing to take necessary fire safety precautions, and neglecting to ensure that firefighting equipment was available. Consequently, De Jager's actions were deemed negligent, directly contributing to the fire ([17]).

Turning to the Trust's liability, the court determined that the absence of firebreaks on the Trust's property constituted a breach of duty under the Act, which includes obligations to manage the risk of fire spreading to adjacent properties. The court noted that the Trust could not establish a proper employment or agency relationship with De Jager that would warrant vicarious liability, given that their arrangement resembled a tenant relationship without specific safety mandates ([18]).

The Trust attempted to rebut the presumption of negligence by introducing expert testimony that indicated the extreme weather conditions, including wind speed and fuel loads, were significant factors in the fire’s spread. However, the court critically assessed this evidence and found that the experts' testimonies did not adequately counter the presumption of negligence arising from the lack of proper firebreaks ([21]-[27]).

Concerning the respondent’s contributory negligence claims, the appellants argued inadequate fire-fighting resources at their facility. However, the court noted that any delays in response did not amount to negligence that contributed to the fire spreading, especially given the scale of the fire by the time assistance arrived ([29]-[32]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the trial court's findings that:

1. De Jager’s negligence in the bee-smoking operation directly led to the fire, affirming the liability attributed to him.
2. The Trust's failure to maintain adequate firebreaks established a breach of duty under the National Veld and Forest Fire Act, cementing its liability for the damages caused.
3. The evidence did not support a finding of contributory negligence on the part of the respondent as the measures taken were deemed reasonable under the circumstances.

The judgment underscores the critical importance of fire prevention measures in agricultural and forestry activities, setting a precedent on the responsibilities of landowners under the National Veld and Forest Fire Act and the legal ramifications of negligence in such contexts ([28]-[35]).
