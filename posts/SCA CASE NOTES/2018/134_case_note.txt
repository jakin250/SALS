# FIRAC Case Note: Rossouw v Hanekom (741/2017)

**F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–4])**

On 12 February 2011, Ms. Estelle Rossouw (the appellant) entered into a deed of sale with Mr. Jacobus Petrus Hanekom (the respondent) for the property known as erf [...], [...] Street, Strand. Shortly after moving into the property, Ms. Rossouw discovered significant issues, including a leaky roof and blocked drains, leading to the determination that alterations made to the property lacked statutory approval.

Consequently, Ms. Rossouw initiated legal proceedings in the regional court of the Western Cape, claiming damages amounting to R232,017 based on fraudulent misrepresentation regarding the roof and non-disclosure concerning the lack of statutory approval and the concealed sewage system. The regional court, after hearing testimonies from Ms. Rossouw, her husband, and expert witnesses, found in her favor, establishing Mr. Hanekom’s liability for damages and ordering him to pay costs.

Mr. Hanekom subsequently appealed the ruling to the Western Cape Division of the High Court, which overturned the regional court’s judgment, resulting in an absolution from the instance being granted in favor of Mr. Hanekom. Following this decision, the Supreme Court of Appeal granted Ms. Rossouw special leave to appeal.

**I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1–4, 17–18])**

The principal legal issues arising from this case include:

1. Whether fraudulent misrepresentation and non-disclosure were adequately established, warranting a claim for damages despite the inclusion of a voetstoots clause in the deed of sale.
2. The extent to which Mr. Hanekom was obliged, personally or through his agent, to disclose pertinent information related to the condition of the property, specifically regarding the roof and sewage system, which were critical to Ms. Rossouw's purchasing decision.
3. The appropriate standard for the assessment of evidence and credibility by the appellate court, particularly in respect of the factual findings made by the trial judge.

**R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 2–4, 16–18])**

The court's analysis referenced several key principles from South African law, particularly those surrounding fraudulent misrepresentation and the obligations of sellers in a property transaction. Notably:

- The principle that a seller's voetstoots clause does not shield them from liability for fraudulent misrepresentations (as cited from GB Bradfield Christie’s Law of Contract in South Africa).
- The obligation under the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1997, which mandates obtaining prior written approval from local authorities for any alterations made to a property.
- The procedural framework following *Banda & Another v Van der Spuy and Another* ([2013] ZASCA 23) which emphasizes the burden of proof on the person alleging misrepresentation in contractual agreements, and the necessity of demonstrating intent and knowledge when alleging fraud.
- The appellate court's obligation to respect factual findings on credibility made by lower courts unless clearly flawed.

**A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 5–19])**

Upon reviewing the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the regional court had appropriately concluded that Mr. Hanekom was liable for fraudulent misrepresentations. The evidence presented showed that Mr. Hanekom had made assurances regarding the status of the roof's leakages, despite being aware of ongoing issues. He had also failed to disclose the lack of statutory approval for alterations and concealed the sewage system under a concrete floor—essential information that would have impacted Ms. Rossouw’s decision to purchase the property.

The court methodically analyzed evidence, finding Ms. Rossouw's account credible while dismissing the conflicting testimonies of Mr. Hanekom and the estate agent as unconvincing. It underscored that the high court's decision to grant an absolution from the instance was unwarranted, given the sufficient evidence linking Mr. Hanekom's conduct to fraudulent activity that induced the sale.

The Supreme Court highlighted the legal expectation that sellers must reveal crucial property defects and any non-compliance with building regulations. Mr. Hanekom’s acknowledgment of his failure to secure the necessary approvals along with the evidence of serious property defects underlined the fraudulent nature of his actions, thereby fulfilling the criteria for establishing fraudulent misrepresentation.

**C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 19])**

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Ms. Rossouw's appeal, reversing the high court's decision and reinstating the regional court's ruling. The court held that the trial court's finding that Mr. Hanekom had engaged in fraudulent misrepresentation and non-disclosure was adequately supported by the evidence and warranted damages.

The ratio decidendi articulated in this judgment clarifies that the presence of a voetstoots clause does not absolve a seller from liability when fraudulent misrepresentations are involved. The case thus reinforces the legal principle that transparency and disclosure are imperative in property transactions, particularly with respect to structural problems and compliance with regulatory standards.

This decision has substantial implications for property law in South Africa, establishing a heightened standard for seller disclosure and public accountability in contractual agreements. It underscores the judiciary's intolerance for fraudulent practices in real estate transactions, asserting the necessity for due diligence and honesty on the part of sellers.
