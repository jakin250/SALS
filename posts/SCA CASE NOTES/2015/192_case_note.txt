Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Probest Projects (Pty) Ltd v The Attorneys, Notaries and Conveyancers Fidelity Guarantee Fund

### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts:** Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–3])

The case revolves around a claim for payment by Probest Projects (Pty) Ltd ("Probest") against The Attorneys, Notaries and Conveyancers Fidelity Guarantee Fund ("the Fund") for R9,100,000, representing the theft of trust funds by an attorney, Mr. Izak Minnie. The funds, entrusted to Mr. Minnie, were intended for property transactions but were instead misappropriated. The trial court initially ruled in favor of the Fund based on a special plea which argued that Probest had failed to institute action within three months of becoming aware of the theft, as stipulated by § 48(1)(a) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 ("the Act"). The claim was originally dismissed by the Western Cape Division of the High Court, prompting Probest to appeal this decision, which was ultimately upheld by the Supreme Court of Appeal.

**I – Issues:** Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 2, 3, 15]

The primary legal issue concerns the interpretation of § 48(1)(a) of the Attorneys Act, specifically when Probest became aware of the theft of funds, a determination crucial for compliance with the statutory period allowed for bringing a claim against the Fund. Another issue includes the validity of inferences drawn by the lower courts regarding Probest's knowledge of the theft. 

**R – Rules:** Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 16–21])

The court examined applicable laws and precedents, chiefly focusing on the interpretation of the Attorneys Act and the evidentiary burden of proof concerning the knowledge of misappropriation. The court referenced *S v Naik* and *Caswell v Powell Duffryn Associated Collieries Ltd* to clarify distinctions between inferences and assumptions, emphasizing that valid inferences must be based on the objective facts established in court. The court noted the statutory requirement under § 48(1)(a) that a claimant must institute action within three months of becoming aware of the theft, a stipulation inherently bound to the facts surrounding the claimant's awareness.

**A – Application:** Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 17–22])

Upon analysis of testimonies from both Mr. Annandale of Probest and Mr. Minnie, the court found inconsistencies in the inferences drawn by the trial court. The trial and full court had concluded that Probest should have known about the theft by July 23, 2009, based on Mr. Minnie’s acknowledgment of debt and assertions about his accounting difficulties. However, the Supreme Court of Appeal determined that these conclusions misapprehended the agreed-upon facts. Both Mr. Annandale and Mr. Minnie unwaveringly indicated that Probest only became aware of the theft at the end of November or December 2009. The court asserted that the earlier conclusions were based on conjecture rather than substantiated inferences drawn from verifiable facts. The court emphasized that inferences must be consistent with all established facts, and the burden of proof rested on the Fund to substantiate claims of Probest’s earlier knowledge.

**C – Conclusion:** Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 21–23])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the trial court and full court had erred in accepting the Fund’s special plea. The key determination was that Probest had only acquired knowledge of the theft in late November 2009, thereby complying with the requisite three-month period for action as stipulated in the Attorneys Act. The original order was set aside, and the appeal was upheld with costs. This ruling reaffirms the legal principle that the burden of proof lies with the party alleging a claim and emphasizes the need for courts to rely on substantiated evidence rather than speculative conclusions. The decision underscores the importance of rigorous evidentiary standards and reinforces the protection mechanisms available to claimants under the Attorneys Act.
