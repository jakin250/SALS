Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: *Seebed CC t/a Siyabonga Convenience Centre v Engen Petroleum Limited* [2022] ZACC 28

## Facts
The applicant, Seebed CC, trading as Siyabonga Convenience Centre (“Seebed”), is a licensed retailer operating an Engen filling station in Robertville, Johannesburg. The respondent, Engen Petroleum Limited (“Engen”), is a licensed wholesaler of petroleum products under the Petroleum Products Act 120 of 1977. In 2009, Seebed acquired a fuel and service station business on premises owned by Engen, leading to a lease agreement that initially ran from April 1, 2008, to May 31, 2010, but was later extended until July 31, 2015.

Seebed claimed that an oral agreement was reached to extend the lease until July 31, 2020. However, the lease included a “whole contract” clause, likely precluding reliance on any oral agreements. In 2010, Engen introduced a Corner Bakery franchise, leading to disputes with Seebed over aspects of the franchise agreement. After Seebed refused to vacate the premises following Engen's notice, eviction proceedings were initiated.

The litigation history included a first set of eviction proceedings launched in September 2012, which were referred to trial due to significant factual disputes. After a series of procedural maneuvers, including a second eviction proceeding initiated by Engen in 2016 based on concessions made by Seebed during the first proceedings, the High Court ruled in favor of Engen. The High Court's orders culminated in an eviction order with retrospective effect from July 31, 2017 ([paras 1–20]).

## Issues
The constitutional issues raised in this case include whether the High Court had jurisdiction to grant the eviction order despite the pending trial on related matters, and whether the applicant's right to fair access to legal recourse was infringed, invoking Section 34 of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to access courts. Key legal questions also centered on whether the High Court misapprehended the contractual relations and representations that led to the eviction ([paras 24–25]).

## Rules
The legal framework referred to included the Petroleum Products Act 120 of 1977, particularly Section 12B, which concerns arbitration referrals in cases involving alleged unfair or unreasonable contractual practices between retailers and wholesalers. The court also referenced *Crompton Street Motors CC v Bright Idea Projects 66 (Pty) Ltd* [2021] ZACC 24, which addressed the jurisdiction of the High Court regarding matters already referred to arbitration under Section 12B. Additionally, general contract law principles regarding good faith, contractual representation, and the consequences of factual concessions in legal proceedings were invoked ([paras 12B; 27–28]).

## Application
In addressing the merits, the court assessed whether the High Court appropriately dealt with the eviction proceedings in light of conflicting claims regarding the lease agreement's terms. It evaluated whether Seebed had a legitimate expectation of an extended lease and whether Engen's actions constituted an infringement of Seebed's contractual rights. The court found that factual concessions made by Seebed during the earlier proceedings supported Engen's claim that the surviving arrangement was a month-to-month lease, which Engen could terminate with one month's notice ([paras 48–51]). 

Moreover, the Court reviewed procedural aspects, confirming that the High Court had discretion to reject Seebed's supplementary affidavit and maintain the integrity of the trial process. The conclusion of the High Court to grant an eviction order based on the established month-to-month arrangement was upheld, considering the adequacy of notice given by Engen and the lack of compelling evidence to support Seebed's claims of misrepresentation or unfair practices, particularly as effective allegations under Section 12B were not initiated in a timely manner ([paras 52–55]).

Furthermore, the court clarified that the policy objectives behind the Petroleum Products Act were not intended to shield parties from the outcomes of contractual arrangements once equitable and reasonable terms had been duly addressed ([para 54]).

## Conclusion
The Constitutional Court, by refusing leave to appeal, concluded that the High Court properly exercised its discretion in granting the eviction order. It found that the High Court was justified in its application of law regarding evictions based on month-to-month lease agreements as well as its handling of processes surrounding supplementary affidavits and lis pendens claims. The court emphasized the importance of finality in legal disputes while maintaining procedural fairness, thereby affirming the lower court's conclusions and effectively rendering the previous disputes moot. Therefore, the order was made for Seebed to pay the costs of Engen ([paras 57–58]). 

## Implications
The judgment highlights key principles surrounding contractual relations in South Africa's petroleum industry and reinforces the legal necessity for parties to adhere to the established terms when invoking statutory arbitration. It underscores the weight of factual concessions within litigation and the court's reluctance to entertain appeals that introduce novel arguments not presented at lower instances. Ultimately, the ruling establishes important precedents concerning the enforcement of lease agreements while rendering further claims moot due to an inability to substantiate entitlement post-expiration or notice of cancellation.
