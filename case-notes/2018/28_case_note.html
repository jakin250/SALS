Wezizwe Feziwe Sigcau and Another v Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs and Others [2018] ZACC 28

The case revolves around a dispute over the kingship of the amaMpondo aseQaukeni community in the Eastern Cape of South Africa.

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 28

F – Facts

The case revolves around a dispute over the kingship of the amaMpondo aseQaukeni community in the Eastern Cape of South Africa. The conflict dates back to 2008 when the Commission on Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims (Commission) recognized the paramountcy of amaMpondo aseQaukeni, establishing its right to have a king. The Commission's decision indicated a claim by Zanozuko Sigcau to be recognized as the rightful king, contesting the title of Mpondombini Justice Sigcau, who had served as the paramount chief.

In 2006, Zanozuko brought forth a claim that he was entitled to be king, thereby challenging Mpondombini’s authority. The Commission held hearings where both parties presented evidence, and in January 2010, it ruled in favor of Zanozuko. The prime legal issue under review stems from the procedural steps the President must take to implement the Commission’s decision under the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act (unamended Act).

Following Mpondombini’s passing before the finalization of any court order arising from the prior disputes, Wezizwe Feziwe Sigcau, his daughter, sought to succeed him as the community's queen. The President of South Africa did not recognize Wezizwe, believing that he was required to adhere to the Commission's prior decision regarding Zanozuko. This prompted the first and second applicants (Wezizwe and her mother, Lombekiso) to challenge the President's interpretation of his obligations under the unamended Act.

The High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal sided with the President, determining he need merely publish a notice in the Government Gazette and issue a certificate of recognition for Zanozuko, rather than invoke the broader procedures delineated in sections 9 and 10 of the unamended Act. The applicants appealed to the Constitutional Court, which granted leave to appeal the decisions of the lower courts ([paras 1-66]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue is: what specific actions are mandated of the President upon receiving the Commission's decision regarding a traditional leadership dispute concerning the amaMpondo kingship? Specifically, the court needed to determine whether the President must follow the entire process outlined in sections 9 and 10 of the unamended Act for implementation of the Commission's decision, or whether he could simply publish a notice and issue a certificate of recognition as he argued ([paras 44-46, 56-57]).

R – Rules

The governing statutes in question are primarily the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 (unamended Act), particularly sections 9, 10, and 26. Section 26(2)(a) mandates that a decision from the Commission, affecting a king or queen, must be conveyed to the President for “immediate implementation in accordance with section 9 or 10.”

Section 9 outlines the recognition of kings and queens, stating that the royal family must identify a suitable candidate for kingship according to customary law. Section 10 relates to the process of removing a king or queen from their position. A critical part of the legislation is that, in this dispute, it is specified that the identity of a king or queen must be reconfirmed through due process — but what that process entails when the Commission has already made a determination is contentious ([paras 1-5, 55-62]).

The purpose of these provisions is to create a statutory framework that respects and incorporates customary law while providing mechanisms for resolving disputes within traditional communities. As underscored by the court, these provisions need to be interpreted purposively, with respect to the language of the statute and its context ([paras 1-2, 11]).

A – Application

Upon analysis, the Constitutional Court emphasized that the phrase “immediate implementation” cannot allow for delaying processes that lead to reassessment of the Commission's decision. If the President were to adhere to an extended procedural route through sections 9 and 10, he would fail to satisfy his duty under section 26(2)(a) to implement the Commission's decision. The judgment clarified that the President must fulfill his obligations without being encumbered by the procedural delays resulting from the royal family's internal dynamics or potential disputes ([paras 1, 53-57]).

The court reasoned that since the Commission had already resolved the dispute about kingship, including based on customary law, the outcome necessitated immediate action rather than further due process as suggested by one of the dissenting Justices. To construe the President's obligation in a manner that requires him to initiate an entirely new identification process following a Commission decision leads to unnecessary complications. The authority vested in the Commission was reaffirmed, and the decision it made was upheld unless reviewed directly in court ([paras 11, 56, 63-66]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal, reinforcing that the President must take immediate steps to implement the Commission's decision without reinstating the process outlined in sections 9 and 10 of the unamended Act. This judgment clarifies the imperative for administrative efficiency in context to traditional leadership disputes, asserting that as long as the Commission's decision stands, the President must act in accordance with it ([paras 1, 56-66]).

This decision illustrates the balancing of traditional law against statutory provisions intended to modernize and systematize traditional leadership in South Africa. It emphasizes the need for courts to respect the findings of the Commission, thus affirming the role of customary law and its permissible evolution under the watch of constitutional tenets ([paras 1-2, 11-12]).

The outcome of the case has significant implications, not just for customary law and its role within the framework of modern legislation, but also for community governance structures in South Africa, which continue to evolve in post-apartheid governance ([paras 1-3]).
