# FIRAC Case Note: Elmir Property Projects (Pty) Ltd t/a Elmir and Another v Bankenveld Homeowners Association (Pty) Ltd

**Neutral Citation**: Elmir Property Projects (Pty) Ltd t/a Elmir and Another v Bankenveld Homeowners Association (Pty) Ltd (522/2023 and 524/2023) [2024] ZASCA 141 (21 October 2024)

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram**: PONNAN, SCHIPPERS, NICHOLLS, SMITH JJA and MANTAME AJA

**Date**: Heard on 26 August 2024, delivered electronically on 21 October 2024

---

## F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–33])

The case centers around Bankenveld Golf Estate, a luxury housing development in Emalahleni, Mpumalanga, which suffers from malfunctioning sewage reclamation plants ([1]). The Bankenveld Homeowners Association (Pty) Ltd (the respondent) claimed that Elmir Property Projects (Pty) Ltd and the Emalahleni Local Municipal Council (the appellants) were jointly responsible for maintaining these facilities. In June 2020, the respondent filed an application with the Mpumalanga Division of the High Court for an order mandating the appellants to provide sanitation services compliant with the legal standards ([2]).

Elmir contested this by filing a counter-application, arguing that the conditions imposed by the municipality regarding the operation and maintenance of the sewage plants should be considered null and void since Bankenveld Extension 11 was not formally proclaimed as a township ([3]). The High Court ruled against Elmir, determining that both Elmir and the municipality were jointly responsible for sanitation services as their obligations were administrative actions defined by the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 ([4]–[6]).

Elmir appealed the High Court decision in its entirety, while the municipality appealed only those parts that confirmed its joint liability ([8]). The core question was who bore responsibility for the development, operation, and maintenance of the sewage reclamation plants. A significant point of contention was whether the pre-proclamation conditions applicable to Extension 11 “fell away” due to its lack of proclamation ([16]–[37]).

## I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 26–33])

Several key legal questions arose from the case:

1. **Responsibility for Maintenance**: Who is primarily responsible for the maintenance and operation of the sewage reclamation plants—the municipality or Elmir?
2. **Validity of the Township Establishment Conditions**: Did the conditions imposed by the municipality regarding sanitation services remain enforceable given that Extension 11 was never formally proclaimed as a township?
3. **Administrative Action**: Are the township establishment conditions imposed by the municipality classified as administrative actions that elude invalidation until overturned by a competent court?
4. **Constitutional Obligations**: Does the municipality bear a greater constitutional obligation to provide sanitation services compared to the obligations imposed on the developer?

## R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 26–38])

The court's analysis relied heavily on statutory provisions, administrative law principles, and case law:

1. **Town-Planning and Townships Ordinance 15 of 1986**: This ordinance outlines the conditions under which townships may be established but does not explicitly address the responsibilities for maintenance in this context.
2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000**: Section 1 defines “administrative action” and emphasizes that such decisions are valid until set aside, making municipal actions binding ([37]).
3. **Constitutional Provisions**: Sections 24 and 27 of the South African Constitution outline the rights to an environment that is not harmful to health and to have access to sufficient water, placing obligations on the municipality as a local authority.
4. **Case Precedents**: The court referenced *Newlands Surgical Clinic (Pty) Ltd v Peninsula Eye Clinic (Pty) Ltd* [2015] ZASCA 25, emphasizing that administrative decisions remain valid until set aside.

## A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 34–41])

The court reasoned that Elmir’s argument indicating that no responsibility exists due to the absence of a formal proclamation lacked merit. Elmir had voluntarily accepted the operational responsibility for maintaining the plants during the township establishment process ([34–35]). The court concluded that Elmir’s acceptance was evident through its conduct, including the operation and maintenance of the facilities for over 15 years ([36]).

Additionally, the municipality's imposition of conditions pursuant to its statutory authority represented administrative actions that remain effective until challenged by a competent body. The court dismissed Elmir's assertion that the conditions applied were nullified due to the lack of proclamation, underscoring that these obligations to operate and maintain the plants were integral to the township development's approval ([38–39]).

In terms of constitutional obligations, the court differentiated between the municipality's overarching responsibilities to provide water and sanitation services and the specific, private obligations contracted by Elmir. The municipality was deemed responsible for overall compliance; however, Elmir bore the primary duty for the physical maintenance of the sewage facilities ([40–41]).

## C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 42–43])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Elmir's appeal, affirming the High Court’s ruling that it must maintain the operation of the sewage plants. Conversely, the municipality's appeal was upheld, concluding that it bears no joint liability for the operations—a view supporting that Elmir alone is responsible for sanitation services to the Bankenveld Estate ([43]).

The case has broader implications for town planning and municipal responsibilities, underscoring that conditions imposed upon developers in township establishments remain enforceable unless formally nullified. This ruling also reinforces that local authorities must oversee compliance with environmental and public health regulations while delineating the responsibilities assumed by private developers.

This case note captures the essence and crucial dimensions of the judgment, reflecting a thorough understanding of the legal principles at play in the operation of municipal obligations and private developer responsibilities within the South African context.
