Constitutional Court

Nokotyana and Others v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality and Others

FIRAC Method F – Facts The case arises from the Harry Gwala Informal Settlement (the Settlement) located within the jurisdiction of the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality) ([5]-[10]).

Constitutional Court 2009

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case arises from the Harry Gwala Informal Settlement (the Settlement) located within the jurisdiction of the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality) ([5]-[10]). Applicants, including Johnson Matotoba Nokotyana, sought to enforce their right to adequate housing by requesting improved sanitation facilities and high-mast lighting, arguing that the existing provision of one chemical toilet per ten families was inadequate. This request followed a sustained lack of infrastructure development despite a proposal made by the Municipality to upgrade the Settlement in 2006 under the National Housing Code ([13]-[15]). After receiving only partial victory in the South Gauteng High Court, where the Municipality was mandated to provide clean water and refuse removal, the applicants sought appeal on the failure to secure adequate sanitation facilities and safety lighting ([16]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions before the Constitutional Court were whether the Municipality was constitutionally required to provide the requested services under the National Housing Code, specifically in Chapters 12 and 13, and whether the applicants could assert their rights directly under section 26 of the South African Constitution regarding access to adequate housing ([17]-[19]). Additional issues included the relevance of the new policy adopted by the Municipality and the appropriateness of the court’s intervention given the three-year delay in deciding on the upgrading of the Settlement ([20]).

R – Rules

This case primarily involved interpretations of sections 26 and 27 of the Constitution, which enshrine the rights to adequate housing, healthcare, and social security ([1]-[2]). Relevant statutory frameworks included the Housing Act 107 of 1997 and the National Housing Code, particularly Chapters 12 and 13, which provide mechanisms for emergency housing and the upgrading of informal settlements ([13]-[16]). The Constitutional Court's previous case law, particularly in cases such as Grootboom and Mazibuko, established precedents regarding the state's obligations and the interpretation of socio-economic rights ([4]-[5]).

A – Application

The court ruled that the Municipality was not mandated to provide the requested services until a decision on the upgrading of the Settlement was made by the MEC, in accordance with the National Housing Code ([25]-[27]). The High Court's rejection of the applicants' reliance on Chapter 12 (which applies to emergencies) was upheld given that no emergency had been proclaimed by the MEC. Thus, the court found that the Municipality’s actions complied with existing legislation ([27]-[30]). The court acknowledged the delay in upgrading decisions as unjustified but indicated that such systemic issues could not automatically compel the provision of additional resources or services without regulatory compliance ([31]-[33]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the applicants' appeal, reinforcing the principle that administrative processes must be adhered to before socio-economic rights can be enforced. However, the court ordered the MEC to make a decision on the upgrading of the Settlement within 14 months to ensure the applicants' rights under the Constitution were recognized and preserved ([34]-[36]). This judgment emphasizes the necessity of bureaucratic efficiency in advancing socio-economic rights, while also underlining the significant limitations imposed by regulatory frameworks and budgetary constraints affecting local municipalities in South Africa.
