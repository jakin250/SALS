Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Trustees for the Time Being of the Burmilla Trust v President of the Republic of South Africa

### F – Facts
The appellants, Trustees for the Time Being of the Burmilla Trust and Josias van Zyl, initiated a claim against the President and Government of the Republic of South Africa for damages totaling approximately R800 million, inclusive of interest and costs ([para 1]). The essence of their claim stems from a complex legal battle over five mining leases, originally granted by the Kingdom of Lesotho to Swissborough Diamond Mines (Pty) Ltd, which was controlled by Van Zyl. The key asset, the Rampai mining lease, was declared void by the Lesotho courts due to a failure to obtain necessary consents from local chiefs as required by Lesotho law ([paras 3–4]).

The claim progressed to the Southern African Development Community (SADC) Tribunal, alleging that Lesotho, in breach of its international obligations under the SADC Treaty, expropriated the mining leases without compensation ([paras 5–6]). Following a series of legal maneuvers, the SADC Tribunal was effectively shuttered by actions of the SADC Summit, and the appellants' case was thus stalled, prompting them to seek justice within South African courts ([para 8]).

The respondents raised exceptions against the appellants’ claim, leading the Gauteng Division of the High Court to uphold most of the exceptions raised, effectively terminating the appellants' claims, with the exception of the claim concerning the value of the Rampai lease and associated legal costs which would be assessed later ([para 13]).

### I – Issues
The essential legal questions arising from this case include:
1. Whether the appellants successfully disclosed a cause of action against the respondents for the constitutional damages incurred by the closure of the SADC Tribunal, which they argued obstructed their access to justice ([paras 13–14]);
2. Whether the SADC Tribunal could lawfully have determined the validity of the Rampai mining lease despite contrary decisions from the Lesotho courts ([paras 19–20]);
3. Whether res judicata principles from earlier judgments, notably **Van Zyl v Government of the Republic of South Africa**, prevent the appellants from asserting their claims in the current proceedings ([para 52]).

### R – Rules
In adjudicating the present exceptions, the court relies on the principles governing exceptions in South African law, particularly that factual allegations in the particulars of claim must be assumed correct if they are not patently untenable. The excipient (respondents) must show the particulars do not disclose a cause of action ([para 16]). 

Furthermore, the court considered international law principles, emphasizing that property rights arise from municipal law and that expropriation claims also require assessment under international legal standards ([paras 30–31]). In relation to res judicata, the court noted established requirements: same parties, same relief sought, and final decision in earlier proceedings ([para 43]).

### A – Application
The court examined whether the particulars of claim substantiated a legal basis for the claims made by the appellants. Primarily, it noted that the SADC Tribunal procedures allow claims by natural persons against member states, contingent on exhausting domestic remedies and compliance with international law ([paras 8, 115]). 

Claim A (value of the Rampai lease) was pivotal, where the appellants contended the SADC Tribunal would have upheld their rights under the SADC Treaty due to the alleged wrongful actions of the South African government participating in shuttering the Tribunal ([para 24]). The court responded that the previous decisions from Lesotho courts declaring the Rampai lease void could still be contested before the SADC Tribunal, potentially allowing for a ruling contrary to Lesotho's judicial determinations.

Conversely, claims for moral damages (Claim B) were dismissed on grounds that the SADC Tribunal would not have adjudicated on these damages given that the domestic remedies had not been exhausted by van Zyl. Claims for costs related to the SADC proceedings (Claim C) were ultimately upheld, implying that these costs could be recoverable under constitutional damages as the SADC Tribunal might have awarded them had it not been defunct ([para 55]).

### C – Conclusion
The judgment concluded with the following critical points:
1. The appeal was partially upheld regarding the claim for the value of the Rampai mining lease and associated prosecution costs, signaling that these claims could proceed ([paras 61–62]).
2. The appeal of the second appellant, Josias van Zyl, was dismissed in its entirety ([para 62]).
3. The court underscored the importance of the status of international obligations, suggesting that while res judicata did not preclude the claims, the earlier judgments in **Van Zyl SCA** significantly impacted the claims' viability. The implications extend beyond mere procedural considerations to the alignment of domestic law interpretations within the operating frameworks of international law ([para 52]).

This decision has substantial ramifications for future claims originating from domestic judicial actions and how they interface with international obligations under tributaries such as the SADC Treaty. The appellants have been given the opportunity to amend their particulars of claim, allowing for further litigation on the matter, reaffirming the balance between local judicial determinations and international law remedies.
