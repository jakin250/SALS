Supreme Court of Appeal

FIRAC Structure for De Aguiar v Real People Housing (426/09) [2010] ZASCA 67

Following a settlement agreement on 25 April 2006, the lease was cancelled, and the appellant was to vacate the premises by 30 June 2006 unless he secured the purchase of the property for R1.5 million.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 67

F – Facts

The appellant, Anselmo Inacio De Freitas De Aguiar, challenged an eviction order granted by the South Gauteng High Court to the respondent, Real People Housing (Pty) Limited, concerning the property located at 90 Main Road, Walkerville, Meyerton ([1],[2]). The property was originally acquired by the appellant's father in 1970, and the family has occupied it since then. After financial difficulties, the property was sold in execution to First National Bank (FNB) and later to the respondent in May 2001. A lease was signed between the appellant and the respondent on 14 May 2001, but the appellant fell into substantial rental arrears, exceeding R130,000 by April 2006 ([3],[5]).

Following a settlement agreement on 25 April 2006, the lease was cancelled, and the appellant was to vacate the premises by 30 June 2006 unless he secured the purchase of the property for R1.5 million. He failed to meet these obligations, leading to the eviction application, which the court granted ([4],[5]).

During the subsequent court proceedings, the appellant introduced a new legal strategy suggesting he had incurred costs for improvements to the property, which he sought to argue constituted an enrichment lien, hoping to avoid eviction ([7],[8]). This appeal focused on whether the court would allow further evidence on this claim, something previously unraised during the original proceedings ([1]).

I – Issues

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal is whether the appellant should be granted leave to adduce further evidence to support his claim for an enrichment lien, which could serve as a defence against the eviction order ([1]). A secondary issue pertains to the procedural requirements for introducing new evidence on appeal and whether the appellant has satisfactorily met these requirements ([9],[11]).

R – Rules

The court operates under the provisions of Section 22(a) of the Supreme Court Act of 1959, which allows for the introduction of further evidence on appeal only in exceptional circumstances. Several precedents have clarified the stringent criteria for such applications, emphasizing:

  1. A reasonable explanation for the failure to introduce the evidence at the trial ([11]).
  2. A prima facie likelihood of the truth of the evidence ([13]).
  3. The material relevance of the evidence to the trial’s outcome ([9],[14]).

Judicial precedents, including S v N and Business Aviation Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Rand Airport Holdings (Pty) Ltd, outline the necessity of adhering to these principles to prevent the reopening of cases unnecessarily and to uphold the finality of judicial decisions ([10]).

A – Application

Upon examination, the court found that the appellant's application for further evidence did not satisfy any of the stringent criteria outlined above. The court emphasized that the settlement agreement's terms eliminated any room for an enrichment lien defence, as the agreement strictly established his obligation to vacate the premises upon failing to secure financing ([14],[15]).

The appellant failed to provide any viable explanation for his previous legal representation's omission of the enrichment lien claim, citing only speculation on their ignorance of relevant case law. This lack of a substantive explanation weakened his application under the requirement that a reasonable excuse must be provided ([16],[17]). Furthermore, the proposed evidence regarding property improvements lacked specificity and credibility, failing to establish a prima facie case relevant enough to alter the trial's outcome ([18]).

The potential testimony from multiple witnesses and documentation outlined by the appellant suggested the need for a full retrial rather than a simple introduction of evidence, which does not align with the requirements set forth for new evidence on appeal ([20]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appellant's application to adduce further evidence, concluding that he did not meet the necessary legal standards for such requests. The appeal was dismissed with costs, and the court varied the high court's prior order concerning the vacation period from 120 to 60 days, reflecting a compromise while emphasizing the appellant's long-standing occupancy and the need for prompt resolution ([21],[22]).

This case illustrates the court's reluctance to allow further evidentiary submissions on appeal while reiterating the importance of finality in legal proceedings. The rejection of the appellant's claim reinforces the principle that a party must be thorough in presenting their case at the initial hearing, or risk losing the opportunity to assert it later.
