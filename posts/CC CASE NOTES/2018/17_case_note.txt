# FIRAC Case Note: My Vote Counts NPC v Minister of Justice and Correctional Services and Another [2018] ZACC 17

## F – Facts
In the case of *My Vote Counts NPC v Minister of Justice and Correctional Services* [2018] ZACC 17, the applicant, My Vote Counts NPC, contested the constitutional validity of the Promotion of Access to Information Act 2 of 2000 (PAIA) based on its failure to facilitate access to information regarding the private funding of political parties and independent candidates ([6]). The applicant argued that access to such information is essential for the exercise of the constitutional right to make informed political choices, as enshrined in Sections 32, 7(2), and 19 of the South African Constitution. 

The Western Cape Division of the High Court had previously ruled that PAIA was unconstitutional in this respect, stating that it did not adequately cover the private funding of political entities and consequently failed to promote transparency and accountability ([9]). The decision emphasized that the obligation to provide this information was not merely a function of PAIA but also a constitutional necessity ([8]).

The matter was then escalated to the Constitutional Court of South Africa for confirmation. Various parties, including the Minister of Justice and Correctional Services, contested the confirmation order on the grounds that PAIA was sufficient and compliant with the Constitution ([12]).

## I – Issues
The critical issues presented in this case were as follows:
1. Whether the right to vote includes the right to an informed vote, necessitating access to information on private funding ([5]).
2. Whether the existing regulatory framework, specifically PAIA, fulfills the constitutional obligation to provide this information ([9]).
3. Whether the definition and scope of a "private body" within PAIA encompass the private funding of political parties and independent candidates, thereby enabling access to that information ([61]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal rules and constitutional provisions include:
1. **Section 32 of the Constitution**: Guarantees the right of access to information held by the state and any other person for the exercise or protection of rights ([19]).
2. **Section 7(2) of the Constitution**: Mandates the state to respect, protect, promote, and fulfill the rights in the Bill of Rights ([8]).
3. **Section 19 of the Constitution**: Affirms the rights of citizens to make political choices, participate in political life, and have free and fair elections ([26]).
4. **PAIA (2000)**: Seen as the national legislation to give effect to the rights established in Section 32 of the Constitution, but was contested in terms of its failure to provide adequate provisions for private funding information ([60]).

### Case Precedents and International Norms
The decision also referenced prior judgments establishing the importance of access to information for exercising constitutional rights, such as **Buckley v. Valeo**, which emphasized how transparency aids in minimizing corruption ([4]). International conventions regarding corruption also emphasized the necessity for transparency in political funding ([49]).

## A – Application
The Constitutional Court applied the above legal precepts to ascertain that the lack of disclosure requirements concerning private funding was indeed inconsistent with the constitutional provisions designed to protect electoral integrity. 

### Informed Vote
The Court reasoned that the right to vote is fundamental in a democracy, positing that voters must be empowered with relevant information to make informed decisions. It held that information on financial contributions influences the conduct of political parties and independent candidates and therefore is vital for voters’ decision-making processes ([32]).

### PAIA Deficiency
The Court critically analyzed PAIA and found substantial deficiencies. Sections 18 and 53 of PAIA were held to create cumbersome processes that inhibit rather than encourage public access to information about political funding. Instead of establishing an obligation for political entities to record and disclose funding information, PAIA placed the onus on citizens to request such information, often subjected to fees and various requirements ([66]). The inability of PAIA to encompass political parties and independent candidates as "private bodies" further weakened its efficacy in regulating political transparency ([63]).

### Public Interest and State's Duty
A significant aspect of the ruling revolved around the Court’s assertion that the state holds a Constitutional obligation to facilitate and enable the access to information for its citizens, which includes making relevant funding information readily available and not subjecting it to bureaucratic hurdles ([74]). This is especially pertinent in electoral contexts where integrity and transparency must be upheld to ensure public confidence in democratic processes ([45]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court confirmed the Western Cape High Court's order declaring that:
1. Information on the private funding of political parties and independent candidates is crucial for exercising the right to make political choices and is therefore necessary for public access.
2. PAIA is constitutionally invalid insofar as it fails to provide for the recording, preservation, and reasonable accessibility of such information ([91]).
3. Parliament is mandated to amend PAIA within 18 months to ensure compliance with the Constitutional principles enshrined in Sections 32, 7(2), and 19 ([91]).

The ruling reinforces the foundational democratic principle of transparency and accountability in political financing, aiming to mitigate the risks associated with corruption and unduly influenced political actors. Moreover, it sets a precedent for future legislation and regulation regarding political funding, emphasizing the need for a proactive approach by the state to guarantee the constitutional rights of its citizens.
