Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Nativa (Pty) Limited v Austell Laboratories (Pty) Limited
Neutral Citation: Nativa (Pty) Limited v Austell Laboratories (Pty) Limited (1298/2018) [2020] ZASCA 11 (19 March 2020)

---

## F – Facts
The appellant, **Nativa (Pty) Ltd** ("Nativa"), is a pharmaceutical marketing and sales company operating in South Africa and internationally, holding a sizeable market share in the joint care supplement market. Its product, **OSTEOEZE GOLD**, contains glucosamine and chondroitin, marketed as beneficial for joint health ([para 2–3]). The respondent, **Austell Laboratories (Pty) Ltd** ("Austell"), broadcasts an advertisement for its product, **PIASCLEDINE**, which does not contain these ingredients but claims to be the "only clinically proven" treatment for osteoarthritis ([paras 4–5]).

In early March 2018, the initial advertisement aired on national television, suggesting that glucosamine and chondroitin posed health risks for individuals with certain medical conditions. Nativa demanded an immediate withdrawal of the advertisement, alleging it was disparaging and false, ultimately leading to it being taken down on March 12, 2018 ([paras 4–6]). Following modifications to the advertisement, which included blurring the visual presentation of Nativa's product, Nativa obtained an interim interdict from the High Court ([para 6]). However, the High Court dismissed this application, stating Nativa had not demonstrated a prima facie right ([para 7]).

Nativa appealed against the High Court's decision, contesting that the advertisement constituted unlawful competition through disparagement of its goods ([para 8]).

---

## I – Issues
The pivotal legal question presented to the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether Nativa established a case for an interim interdict based on unlawful competition, specifically relating to the claims of disparagement against its product OSTEOEZE ([paras 8–9]). This consideration included:

1. Whether Nativa possessed a prima facie right to the protection of its trade from unlawful competition.
2. Whether there was well-grounded apprehension of irreparable harm if the interdict was not granted.
3. Whether the balance of convenience favoured Nativa.
4. Whether Nativa had any other adequate remedy ([para 9]).

---

## R – Rules
The requirements for the granting of an interim interdict in South African law were reaffirmed through various precedents, stipulating that the applicant must demonstrate a prima facie right, irreparable harm, balance of convenience, and absence of an alternative remedy ([para 9]). The concept of unlawful competition encompasses the right to a trade free from false disparagement by competitors ([para 10]) as echoed in historical cases like *Matthews and Others v Young* and recent elaborations on the principle of injurious falsehood ([para 10]).

Specific mention is made of the requirements for a claim based on injurious falsehood, which necessitate proving that a false representation was made intentionally and that it caused economic loss to the aggrieved party ([para 11]).

---

## A – Application
In reviewing the facts of the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the content of both the initial and altered advertisements. Despite changes made to the initial advertisement, the court determined that both versions continued to convey disparaging messages regarding the OSTEOEZE product ([para 21]). The court highlighted how the warnings against glucosamine and chondroitin were presented in a manner that implied these ingredients were detrimental, misleading consumers about potential risks without adequate medical backing ([paras 25–26]).

Expert testimonies presented by Nativa countered the claims made by Austell, with conclusions reached by well-credentialed medical professionals asserting that there was no substantial evidence that linked the ingredients to health risks for the demographic in question ([paras 26–30]). The court found significant flaws in the counterarguments proffered by Austell, particularly noting the lack of a robust evidentiary basis for its claims ([paras 30–32]).

Ultimately, the court concluded that the balance of convenience favoured Nativa as it risked irreparable harm to its reputation and financial standing if the altered advertisement continued to be aired ([para 34]). Therefore, the prerequisites for granting an interim interdict were met ([para 34]).

---

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Nativa's appeal, reinforcing the notion that unfair competition through false disparagement is actionable under South African law. The court's rulings that both advertisements were substantially similar in their misleading implications concluded that Austell engaged in unlawful competition, warranting an interim interdict ([paras 34–36]).

The court ordered Austell to cease broadcasting the advertisements while allowing Nativa to seek a final interdict and damages stemming from the unlawful competition ([para 36]). The implications of this decision underscore the judicial commitment to maintaining fair competition standards and protecting consumer rights in the pharmaceutical market, ensuring that false narratives do not undermine legitimate business interests.
