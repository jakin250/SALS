# FIRAC Case Note: Vhembe District Municipality v Stewarts & Lloyds Trading (397/2013)

**F – Facts**  
This appeal concerns a default judgment granted against the Vhembe District Municipality (hereafter referred to as the "Municipality") by the Limpopo High Court in November 2010, in favor of Stewarts & Lloyds Trading (Booysens) (Pty) Ltd (hereafter referred to as "Stewarts"). The default judgment, amounting to R698,885 together with interest and costs, arose after the Municipality failed to respond to a combined summons effectively served at its business address, as indicated [1]–[2].

The summons was served to a Mrs. Ramukhotheli at the Municipality's office, and subsequent to the Municipality's non-compliance with the judgment, a writ of execution was issued and executed, leading to the attachment of moveable assets [2]. The Municipality, upon learning of the default judgment through the Sheriff’s actions in March 2012, filed an application for rescission of the default judgment on several grounds, including alleged improper service of the summons, non-compliance with the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 and claims of a bona fide defense to Stewarts' claim [3]–[5].

The first two grounds argued that the service did not comply with the Uniform Rules of Court, particularly relying on the assertion that Mrs. Ramukhotheli was not an employee of the Municipality, thereby questioning the validity of the service [6]. Additionally, the Municipality contended that notice as required under section 3 of the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act (Act 40 of 2002) was not provided by Stewarts. The court ultimately rejected the Municipality's arguments regarding service and statutory compliance, leading to the appeal [9]–[12].

**I – Issues**  
The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were:

1. **Whether there was proper service of the summons on the Municipality in accordance with the Uniform Rules of Court and the relevant section of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 ([9])?**
   
2. **Whether the Municipality was required to comply with the notice provisions under section 3 of the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002 ([10])?**

3. **Whether the Municipality demonstrated a bona fide defense against Stewarts' claim, sufficient to warrant the rescission of the default judgment ([17])?**

**R – Rules**  
The relevant rules and laws governing this case include:

1. **Uniform Rule 31(2)(b)**, which addresses the rescission of a default judgment and stipulates that a defendant must show good cause, including a reasonable explanation for the default and a bona fide defense [4].

2. **Section 115(3) of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000**, which allows legal processes to be effectively served when delivered to the municipal manager or an individual present at the municipal manager’s office [9].

3. **Section 3 of the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002**, which requires a creditor to give advance written notice before instituting legal proceedings for the recovery of a debt against an organ of state [10]–[12].

**A – Application**  
The SCA assessed whether the service of the summons was compliant with legal requirements. The court noted that the summons bore the Municipality's official receipt date, confirming its acknowledgment of receipt. Furthermore, the location of service was the same address where the official documents were later served, fulfilling the requirements for proper service under both the Uniform Rules of Court and the Municipal Systems Act. The Municipal Manager’s assertion regarding Mrs. Ramukhotheli not being an employee was undermined by a lack of supporting evidence and a failure to specify who was authorized to receive legal process for the Municipality [6]–[9].

On the matter of statutory compliance with section 3 of the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002, the SCA cited precedents where it was established that a 'debt' as defined under the Act encompasses claims for damages, and not contractual payment claims such as in this case involving the cession agreement. The SCA agreed with the lower court's interpretation that the claim was not merely a debt requiring notice under the Act [12]–[14].

Regarding the bona fide defense asserted by the Municipality, the SCA found the arguments presented vague. The Municipal Manager’s attempt to refute the claimed amount owed lacked requisite details and corroborative evidence, rendering the Municipality’s defense ineffective and insufficient to merit rescission under the "bona fide defense" clause [17]–[19].

**C – Conclusion**  
The SCA dismissed the appeal, reaffirming the Limpopo High Court's findings that the Municipality had failed to satisfactorily establish either improper service of the judgment or a valid and bona fide defense to Stewarts' claim. The SCA concluded that the Municipality’s failure to provide substantiated evidence led to the inescapable conclusion that the appeal must fail, noting that the procedural issues involved—primarily ineffective legal representation and a lack of candor from the Municipality—had profound implications for its liability [20]. As a result, the SCA upheld the lower court’s decision with costs, indicating that the Municipality's conduct did not warrant leniency or a waiver of standard costs procedures [20]. 

This case underscores the importance of diligent procedural compliance and adequate representation in legal disputes involving public entities, particularly in circumstances where failure to act has tangible financial consequences.
