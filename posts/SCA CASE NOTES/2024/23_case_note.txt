Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Director of Public Prosecutions, Kwazulu-Natal Pietermaritzburg v Ndlovu (888/2021) [2024] ZASCA 23

## F – Facts
The case arises from serious allegations of rape against the respondent, Xolani Ndlovu. On 29 November 2014, a 22-year-old woman, referred to as NM, was violently kidnapped from her home by three men. She was subsequently taken to a neighboring homestead where she endured repeated sexual assaults by her captors, involving both vaginal and anal rape ([1]–[2]).

Ndlovu was arrested on 19 December 2014 and charged with statutory contravention and common law offences relating to the rape of NM. The prosecution invoked sections 51(1) and 51(2) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (the 1997 Act), which impose a mandatory life sentence upon conviction for specified serious offenses, including rape. Ndlovu pleaded not guilty, and the trial court found him guilty on both counts, sentencing him to life imprisonment for the rape and three years for kidnapping ([2]–[6]).

Dissatisfied with the life sentence, Ndlovu appealed to the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court. The High Court upheld his conviction but set aside the life sentence, substituting it with a sentence of 15 years’ imprisonment based on a previous Supreme Court of Appeal decision, *S v Mahlase*, which held that section 51(1) does not apply if not all co-perpetrators were before the court. The High Court posited that since not all perpetrators were convicted, the mandatory sentence could not apply ([8]–[11]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue revolves around whether the High Court was correct in adhering to the principles established in *S v Mahlase* and subsequently finding that the trial court lacked the authority to impose a life sentence in this case. The questions can be summarized as follows:

1. Was the High Court justified in determining that it was bound by the reasoning in *Mahlase* despite differing factual circumstances?
2. Did the High Court overlook the applicability of the mandate in section 51(1) of the 1997 Act regarding the imposition of a life sentence?
3. Is the appellant, the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP), competent to appeal based on a question of law under section 311 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977? ([16]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework applicable in this case includes:

- Section 51(1) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, which mandates life imprisonment for specified serious offenses, including multiple rapes.
  
- Section 311 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 grants the DPP an automatic right to appeal against a decision of the High Court if that decision is in favor of a convicted person based on a question of law.

- The principle of *stare decisis*, as exemplified by judicial precedents, such as *S v Mahlase* and *S v Legoa*, which provide context for interpreting section 51(1) ([17]–[19]).

## A – Application
In determining this appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal considered the High Court's interpretation of *Mahlase*. The majority within the High Court had asserted that in light of *Mahlase*, life imprisonment could not be mandated if not all co-perpetrators were present in the trial. The majority argued that the prosecutor was bound by this ruling and thus could not exceed the lower court's authority ([10]–[13]).

However, the Supreme Court examined the decisions in greater depth, finding that the substantive requirements of section 51(1), which enables the imposition of a life sentence upon errors where there is a factual basis showing that multiple rapes occurred, were misapplied by the High Court. It reaffirmed that the existence of co-perpetrators does not negate the fact that Ndlovu had been correctly convicted of rape, and thus section 51(1) could appropriately apply ([67]–[68]).

The Court concluded that a defendant's connection to the crime must be assessed distinctly from the presence of co-perpetrators. The punitive aim of the legislation demanded that the life sentence be reinstated, reflecting societal condemnation of rape and the specific context of this case ([73]–[75]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the DPP's appeal, reinstating Ndlovu's life sentence. The Court determined that when the rape was committed, the law clearly mandated a life sentence regardless of the fact that not all perpetrators were charged or convicted. The judgment emphasized the heinous nature of rape and the legislative intent to ensure severe penalties in such cases to reflect the seriousness of the crime ([77]).

In doing so, the Supreme Court reaffirmed legal standards surrounding the application of mandatory sentencing, emphasizing judicial precedents that guide sentencing practices in South Africa. The decision serves to bolster the application of punitive measures against serious sexual offenses and ensures consistency in how the judicial system addresses such critical issues. Thus, the DPP was deemed competent to appeal, and the legal landscape surrounding sentencing in similar cases was clarified and reinforced.
