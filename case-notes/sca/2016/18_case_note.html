FIRAC Method – Distell Ltd v KZN Wines and Spirits CC ([2016] ZASCA 18)

The case involves Distell Limited (the appellant), a prominent producer of wines and spirits in South Africa, against KZN Wines and Spirits CC (the respondent), concerning an alleged infringement of trade marks under the Trade Marks Act 94 of 1993.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 18)

F – Facts

The case involves Distell Limited (the appellant), a prominent producer of wines and spirits in South Africa, against KZN Wines and Spirits CC (the respondent), concerning an alleged infringement of trade marks under the Trade Marks Act 94 of 1993. Distell claimed that KZN Wines' use of the trade mark ‘BLACK KNIGHT’ for a whisky product infringed upon its registered marks: ‘KNIGHT’S GOLD’ (registered on 30 October 1990) and ‘KNIGHTS’ (registered on 24 January 1994) ([paras 1–2]).

KZN Wines entered the South African market by importing whisky branded as ‘BLACK KNIGHT’ from Scotland, exclusively distributing this product through Midmar Liquors Limited, which operates approximately 40 outlets ([para 2]). Distell filed an application for an interdict in May 2011 after discovering the use of ‘BLACK KNIGHT’, arguing that it would likely cause confusion with its own marks under section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act. The application was dismissed by the KwaZulu-Natal High Court by Mnguni J, who found no indication that consumers would likely be confused or deceived ([paras 4–5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case involves whether KZN Wines' use of the mark ‘BLACK KNIGHT’ constitutes infringement of Distell's registered trade marks ‘KNIGHT’S GOLD’ and ‘KNIGHTS’ under section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act 94 of 1993. This raises several sub-issues:

  1. Is there a likelihood of deception or confusion among consumers regarding the source of the goods?
  2. Does the presence of the word “black” in KZN Wines' mark diminish the potential for confusion with Distell's marks?
  3. Was the High Court correct in its assessment of the likelihood of confusion in a commercial context?

R – Rules

The relevant legal authorities include section 34(1) of the Trade Marks Act 94 of 1993, which states:

> ‘The rights acquired by registration of a trade mark shall be infringed by—(a) the unauthorized use in the course of trade in relation to goods or services in respect of which the trade mark is registered, of an identical mark or of a mark so nearly resembling it as to be likely to deceive or cause confusion.’

The landmark case, Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd ([1984] (3) SA 623 (A)), established that the onus is on the plaintiff to demonstrate a probability of confusion or deception. This principle, along with global appreciation for the visual, aural, or conceptual similarity of trademarks, was reiterated in several cases, including Bata Ltd v Face Fashions CC & another ([2001] (1) SA 844 (SCA)) and Cowbell AG v ICS Holdings Ltd ([2001] (3) SA 941 (SCA)) ([paras 7–10]).

Furthermore, the judgment referenced comparative case law, highlighting that numerous factors must be taken into account, including the average consumer’s likely perception and the overall impression created by the marks ([paras 9–12]).

A – Application

The court analyzed whether consumers might confuse ‘BLACK KNIGHT’ with Distell's marks. Key postal decisions emphasized the importance of a “global” appreciation of the two marks, considering their distinctive and dominant components ([paras 8–9]). The inherent differences in sound, appearance, and meaning between the two marks were critically examined.

  1. Distinctiveness: The court agreed with the High Court’s assessment that the adjective “black” significantly differentiates ‘BLACK KNIGHT’ from Distell’s marks. This distinction was deemed crucial in mitigating potential confusion ([paras 16–17]).
  2. Consumer Context: The judgment noted the lack of evidence for actual confusion, citing that both brands had coexisted for several years without any recorded incidents of consumers misidentifying products. This absence of confusion leaned in favor of KZN Wines ([para 17]).
  3. Commercial Environment: The High Court's rationale that consumers were likely to exercise due care when purchasing whisky (as a relatively expensive product) was endorsed. The court highlighted that discerning consumers, even in busy environments, typically demonstrate discernment in purchasing alcoholic beverages ([para 15]).

Thus, the court concluded that while perception of marks is subjective, the existing differences were sufficient to prevent confusion regarding product origin.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the High Court's decision to dismiss the appeal. The court held that there was no likelihood of confusion or deception under section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act, thus affirming KZN Wines' right to use the mark ‘BLACK KNIGHT’ without infringing Distell's trademarks. The judgment underscored the importance of evaluating potential consumer confusion in a holistic manner, taking into account a variety of contextual factors and the average consumer's perspective ([paras 19]).

The implications of this judgment extend beyond this dispute, reinforcing the legal principles governing trade mark confusion in South Africa, highlighting the necessity for a comprehensive assessment of all relevant factors in determining trade mark infringements.

This case serves as a critical reference for future trade mark cases dealing with the complexities of consumer perception and the distinctions between competing marks.
