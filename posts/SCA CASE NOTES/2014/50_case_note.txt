Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Grancy Property Ltd v Seena Marena Investment (Pty) Ltd

**Case Citation**: Grancy Property v Seena Marena (244/13) [2014] ZASCA 50 (01 April 2014).

**Jurisdiction**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

### F – Facts
[1] The dispute arises from an investment agreement between appellants Grancy Property Limited and Montague Goldsmith AG (the appellants) and respondents Seena Marena Investments (Pty) Ltd, Mr. Dines Gihwala, Mr. Lancelot Manala, and the Gihwala Family Trust. The appellants, based overseas, sought to invest funds in two projects known as Spearhead and Scharrig ([1]).

[3] The appellants transferred money for these investments but alleged that not all funds were invested. Following a breakdown in relations with the respondents, the appellants demanded an account. When no satisfactory account was provided, they initiated proceedings in the Western Cape High Court regarding the adequacy of the accounts rendered by the respondents ([4]).

[5] The Spearhead proceedings began in November 2007, culminating in a court order on 9 March 2009 that required the respondents to provide an account of how the appellants' funds were utilized. After the respondents submitted an account deemed "woefully inadequate" by the presiding judge, the appellants requested a more detailed account, leading to further legal actions ([6–9]).

[10] The Scharrig project involved significant funds, which the appellants later discovered had not been invested, prompting further demands for a detailed accounting. This resulted in four consolidated applications in the high court that were ultimately heard by Judge McDougall ([10–12]).

### I – Issues
[2] The primary issues before the appellate court were:

1. Whether the order of the high court was appealable.
2. Whether the appellants had established a proper case for a two-stage judicially controlled process to investigate both the adequacy and accuracy of the account provided by the respondents ([2]).

### R – Rules
[12] Central to the appeal were established principles regarding appealability as articulated in **Zweni v Minister of Law and Order** [1993] 1 SA 523 (A), which stipulates three attributes for a judgment: it must be final, definitive of the rights of the parties, and dispose of a substantial portion of the relief sought. Also referenced was **Moch v Nedtravel (Pty) Ltd** [1996] 3 SA 1 (A), which highlights that the context of the order's impact is crucial, even if it doesn't conform strictly to all attributes ([12]–[14]).

[18] The court also considered precedent regarding the judicial procedures related to the accounting process established in **Doyle & another v Fleet Motors PE (Pty) Ltd** [1971] 3 SA 760, which allows for flexibility in judicial procedure when rendering and debating accounts ([24]).

### A – Application
[22] The court found the order of the high court to be appealable because it effectively precluded the appellants from contesting the adequacy of the accounts. It established that the ambiguity surrounding the adequacy of the accounts presented a significant legal issue, diverging from the other interlocutory applications that might not be final in their effects ([18–22]).

[29] On the merits, the Supreme Court criticized the high court's rejection of the two-stage judicially controlled process proposed by the appellants, which would initially address the adequacy of the accounts followed by the accuracy. The court highlighted a critical flaw in the high court’s position, as the absence of a clear, agreed procedure to determine adequacy needs significant judicial scrutiny before accuracy can be debated ([23][29]).

[31] The court ultimately ruled that a two-stage process was appropriate because it would ensure that the appellants received an adequate account before being subjected to a debate regarding accuracy. This was consistent with the principles of justice and proper legal accountability outlined in prior judgments ([31]).

### C – Conclusion
On 1 April 2014, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, emphasizing that the previous order negated the appellants' rights to challenge the adequacy of the accounts they received. The ratio decidendi revolved around the judicial need for clarity regarding account adequacy before entering into discussions about accuracy. The implications of this ruling mandated remediation of the process for the appellants to receive a fair assessment of the funds handled by the respondents. The appeal was granted with costs, including two counsels' fees, setting aside the high court's previous orders and revising the procedure for account debate ([32]).
