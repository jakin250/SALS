Supreme Court of Appeal

Zikhulise Cleaning Maintenance & Transport CC v The Chairman of the Investigating Committee of the Construction Industry Development Board (1112/2018)

The appellant, Zikhulise Cleaning Maintenance & Transport CC (hereinafter referred to as "Zikhulise"), is a close corporation registered as a contractor under the Construction Industry Development Board Act 38 of 2000 (the "Act").

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019

F - Facts

The appellant, Zikhulise Cleaning Maintenance & Transport CC (hereinafter referred to as "Zikhulise"), is a close corporation registered as a contractor under the Construction Industry Development Board Act 38 of 2000 (the "Act"). Zikhulise previously faced charges from the Investigating Committee of the Construction Industry Development Board (the "Board") relating to various alleged breaches of the Code of Conduct published under Section 5(4) of the Act. These allegations culminated in a formal inquiry conducted under Regulation 29 ([paras 1–10]).

Following the institution of the inquiry on 25 April 2013, Zikhulise was notified of 20 charges that included allegations of submitting false documents and misrepresentations to the Board ([paras 9–10]). The Committee, comprising the first to third respondents, was tasked with investigating these charges and rendered a ruling rejecting Zikhulise’s objections to the inquiry's validity ([para 12]). A review application was subsequently launched in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, where Zikhulise contested the Committee's decision on grounds including the alleged lack of jurisdiction and procedural irregularities ([paras 13–14]).

The High Court dismissed the review application, leading Zikhulise to appeal this decision ([para 14]). The appeal was heard on 11 November 2019, with judgment delivered on 2 December 2019 ([para 1]).

I - Issues

The legal issues at the core of the appeal were as follows:

  1. Whether the conduct complained of constituted a breach of the Code of Conduct published under Section 5(4) of the Act ([para 16]).
  2. Whether the Investigating Committee had jurisdiction to inquire into the alleged misconduct given that some actions occurred when Zikhulise was not registered as a contractor ([para 13]).
  3. Whether there was compliance with the investigatory provisions set out in Regulations 28 and 29 necessary for a valid inquiry to be held ([paras 28–34]).
  4. The implications of the procedural improprieties on the substantive findings against Zikhulise ([para 34]).

R - Rules

The legal framework applicable to the case was primarily derived from the Construction Industry Development Board Act 38 of 2000 and the associated Construction Industry Development Regulations promulgated under it. Key provisions included:

  • Section 5(4) obliges the Board to publish a Code of Conduct for all construction-related procurement participants ([para 3]).
  • Section 18(1) prohibits any contractor from executing public sector construction works unless registered with the Board ([para 6]).
  • Regulation 28 outlines the procedural framework for preliminary investigations into breaches of the Code, detailing the responsibilities of the investigating officer and the steps to follow ([para 27]).
  • Regulation 29(1) provides for inquiries into alleged breaches of the Code where grounds for such action exist ([para 16]).

The distinction between the procedural requirements for inquiry under these Regulations and the context within which the Code operates was fundamental to the case ([paras 20–22]).

A - Application

The court adopted a multi-layered approach in its analysis of whether the Committee's inquiry complied with statutory requirements. First, the court examined whether the charges related to conduct that could be considered breaches under the Code. It concluded that while some charges may potentially constitute infractions under the Act or Regulations, the primary 17 charges concerned non-compliance that fell outside the ambit of the Code, as they involved dealings between Zikhulise and the Board rather than procurement processes ([paras 22–24]).

The court then addressed the procedural shortcomings asserted by Zikhulise, noting the procedural steps outlined in Regulation 28 had not been followed. Key deficiencies included the lack of a preliminary investigation report by the investigating officer, which was critical before any inquiry could be convened ([paras 30–34]). The court emphasized that adequate adherence to procedural safeguards exists to uphold the legality and fairness of administrative decisions in public governance. Consequently, the absence of compliance with these requirements led to a finding of illegitimacy surrounding the Committee’s inquiry.

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal should succeed based on both the lack of jurisdiction over the majority of charges and the failure to comply with prescribed investigatory procedures. The previous orders of the court a quo were set aside, and the charges against Zikhulise were dismissed ([para 35]).

The ruling has significant implications for the governance of procurement in the construction industry, particularly enhancing the standards of procedural fairness required in inquiries by regulatory entities. It reinforces the necessity for regulatory bodies to rigorously comply with statutory protocols, ensuring that contractors are afforded the due process guaranteed under the law. The judgment serves as a pivotal precedent in affirming the importance of both substantive and procedural compliance in administrative review processes within the construction sector.
