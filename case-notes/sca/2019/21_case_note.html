21 Case Note

On August 20, 2012, Rockland Asset Management and Consulting (Pty) Ltd (RAM) and two related companies were placed under provisional curatorship pursuant to section 5 of the Financial Institutions (Protection of Funds) Act 28 of 2001.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019

FIRAC Case Note: Born Free Investments 247 (Pty) Ltd v Kriel NO (1183/17)

F – Facts [1–6]

On August 20, 2012, Rockland Asset Management and Consulting (Pty) Ltd (RAM) and two related companies were placed under provisional curatorship pursuant to section 5 of the Financial Institutions (Protection of Funds) Act 28 of 2001. Pierre Du Plessis Kriel was appointed as the curator of these entities. A rule nisi was issued as part of the provisional order, requiring RAM and any interested parties to show cause why legal proceedings against these entities should not be stayed without the permission of the court ([2]). On December 6, 2012, a final order was issued, confirming the rule nisi and making the curator's appointment final, thereby imposing a moratorium on legal actions against RAM without court leave ([3]).

In pursuing a claim related to lease agreements with RAM, Born Free Investments 247 (Pty) Ltd (the applicant) initiated legal action on May 29, 2014, without obtaining prior leave from the court as required by the moratorium ([4]). The respondent, Kriel, raised a special plea arguing that this failure rendered the action null and void. The applicant acknowledged its oversight but contended that it did not need court permission based on an interpretation of the orders ([5]).

The Western Cape Division of the High Court dismissed the special plea and allowed the applicant to appeal to a full court, which overturned the decision and upheld the special plea, leading to the dismissal of the applicant's action with costs ([6]).

I – Issues [6–8]

The central legal questions arising in this case include:

  1. Whether the final order confirmed the provisional order in its entirety, including the moratorium on legal actions without court leave.
  2. Whether the failure to obtain court leave prior to instituting the action rendered the action a nullity or merely required a stay pending such leave.

R – Rules [7–13]

The court relied on principles of statutory interpretation and the substantive legal framework surrounding curatorship under the Financial Institutions (Protection of Funds) Act. Key rules of construction indicated that the intention of the order should be ascertained primarily from the language of the order, considering its context, purpose, and known circumstances of its creation ([7]).

The court referenced established legal precedents, notably Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Gentiruco AG ([2] 1977 (4) SA 298 (A) at 304D-H), which emphasizes the need for a sensible meaning to legal documents to avoid absurdities. The interpretation of the rule nisi and its confirmation in the final order was anchored in common legal understanding, implying that the confirmation included the moratorium on actions against the entities under curatorship ([8]).

A – Application [14–16]

In applying the law to the facts, the court concluded that the language of the final order explicitly confirmed the provisional order's rule nisi, which included the moratorium on claims against RAM ([12]). The court disagreed with the applicant's interpretation, which sought to isolate sections of the order based on a strained reading; instead, it adopted a comprehensive approach to ascertain the entire scope of the confirmed rule.

The court acknowledged the detrimental consequences of a finding that the action was a nullity. Such a rigid interpretation would potentially lead to unjust outcomes, particularly infringing upon the right to fair legal recourse as protected under Section 34 of the Constitution of South Africa. The moratorium served a practical purpose – allowing the curator to stabilize the companies without the chaos of simultaneous litigation ([15]).

Thus, the court held that while leave was indeed required before instituting the action, the appropriate remedy for non-compliance was a stay of proceedings rather than outright dismissal, thereby aligning with principles of justice and fairness ([16]).

C – Conclusion [17–18]

The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the full court's dismissal of the applicant's action, substituting it with an order to stay the action pending either court permission to proceed or the termination of the curatorship. Special leave to appeal was granted, and the costs were awarded to the applicant, reflecting the court's recognition of the substantial success achieved in appealing the earlier judgment ([18]).

This decision reinforces the principle that procedural requirements should not be interpreted in a manner leading to unjust outcomes and maintains the broader constitutional protection of the right to legal remedy, emphasizing that procedural missteps can often be remedied by a stay rather than outright dismissal.
