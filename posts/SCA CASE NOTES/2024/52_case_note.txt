# Case Note: FIRAC Method

## Case Title: MM on behalf of GM v Member of the Executive Council for the Department of Health, North West Province (782/2022) [2024] ZASCA 52 (18 April 2024)

### F – Facts
This case revolves around a claim for medical negligence filed by the appellant (M M), acting on behalf of her minor child, G M, against the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for the Department of Health in North West Province. The incident leading to the legal action occurred on 16 October 2010 when GM was born and subsequently diagnosed with cerebral palsy. The appellant alleged that the condition resulted from the negligence of the hospital staff at Moses Kotane Hospital (MKH) and Job Shimankana Tabane Hospital (JSTH) ([2]–[3]).

The trial at the High Court found the hospital staff negligent in certain respects regarding the treatment given to the appellant prior to and during GM's birth. However, it concluded that there was no causal link between the negligence and GM's resultant cerebral palsy, attributing the brain damage instead to an abruptio placentae, a complication that deprives the fetus of oxygen ([3]).

The appellant's claims included damages for future medical expenses, loss of earnings, and general damages for pain and suffering, along with a personal claim for emotional trauma. The case underwent multiple legal assessments, concluding with an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal following a dismissal of the appellant’s claims by the Full Court of the North West High Court ([1]–[2]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues addressed in this case include:
1. Whether the negligence of the medical staff at MKH and JSTH was causally linked to GM’s cerebral palsy ([2]–[3]).
2. The implications of GM's death on the locus standi of the appellant to pursue claims for damages on behalf of GM ([7]–[22]).
3. The legal basis for the appellant’s claim for emotional shock, particularly in light of her failure to produce evidence substantiating this claim ([21]–[24]).

### R – Rules
The rules of law applied in this case comprise:

1. **Causation in Medical Negligence**: The burden lies on the appellant to prove a causal link between the alleged negligence and the damage sustained (Bester v Commercial Union; Road Accident Fund v Sauls) ([11]).
2. **Emotional Trauma Claims**: A plaintiff must demonstrate the existence of a detectable psychiatric injury to successfully claim damages for emotional shock (Komape v Minister of Basic Education) ([12]).
3. **Transmissibility of Claims**: As confirmed in Government of the Republic of South Africa v Ngubane and similar rulings, claims for personal damages are not transmissible unless litis contestatio has been reached, thereby necessitating the appointment of an executor for the deceased’s estate ([22]).
4. **Locus Standi**: A claimant's legal standing to sue depends on their interest in the matter and their ability to show they can rightfully pursue the claim ([22]–[23]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal began by emphasizing the necessity of establishing a clear causal relationship between negligence and the resulting harm. The High Court had found negligence but failed to connect it to GM's cerebral palsy. The Supreme Court upheld this conclusion, reiterating that the causal linkage must be sufficiently evidenced to warrant a claim for damages ([3], [19]).

The court further examined the impact of GM's death on the appeal. Given that GM had died prior to the appeal, an inquiry arose regarding whether M M retained locus standi to continue pursuing the claims for damages. Following precedent, the Court held that because litis contestatio had been reached before GM's death, the claims were transmissible and could only be pursued by GM's estate (operating via an appointed executor) ([21]).

Regarding the personal claim of emotional trauma, the court determined that no substantive evidence had been presented to support M M's assertion of psychological injury. The appellant's testimony did not fulfill the requisite burden of proof as outlined in earlier decisions. Consequently, the court ruled the emotional trauma claim needed remitting back to the trial court for a proper determination ([12], [20]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that while negligence was established, the lack of a causal relationship to GM's condition led to the dismissal of the appellant's claims for damages concerning general medical expenses and earnings ([21]). In light of GM's death, the appellant was deemed to lack the necessary standing to pursue the claims related to GM’s suffering without an executor representing the deceased’s estate ([23]). 

The appeal court ordered that the claims be remitted to the High Court specifically for the matter of emotional damage, indicating that the court should properly assess this claim in light of its previous omission to adjudicate it ([24]). The overall decision underscored the importance of legal representations in personal claims, especially after the death of a plaintiff, and highlighted the court's limited jurisdiction to address claims lacking proper procedural foundation ([25]).
