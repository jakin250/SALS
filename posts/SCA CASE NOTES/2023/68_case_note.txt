Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note

### Case Title: Oaker and Others v Kriel NO (118/2021); Rockland Group Holdings (Pty) Ltd v Kriel NO and Others (185/2022) [2023] ZASCA 68

### F – Facts

The case concerns an appeal and cross-appeal arising from the placement of two bewind trusts, Rockland Targeted Development Investment Fund (TDI) and Rockland Property Investment Fund (PIF), under curatorship, alongside their fund manager, Rockland Asset Management and Consulting (Pty) Ltd (RAM) ([1]–[2]). The curator, Pierre du Plessis Kriel NO, initiated claims against the appellants—individuals involved in the management and control of the trusts—based on breaches of fiduciary duties during the handling of funds that were meant to benefit pension and provident funds ([2]).

The key appellant, Wentzel Oaker, was the sole director and CEO of RAM and was deeply involved in various corporate transactions, including establishing TDI and PIF as bewind trusts, with Global Pact Trading 151 (Pty) Ltd serving as the corporate trustee ([3]–[4]). Over a series of transactions, properties were acquired through two companies, Rapicorp 122 and Rapicorp 123, at prices that benefitted the purchasers grossly at the expense of the trusts, leading to allegations of self-enrichment and mismanagement ([23]–[24]).

The high court, after an extensive trial involving almost 8,000 pages of evidence, ruled largely in favor of the curator and granted them leave to appeal their decision, which included claims for wrongful diversion of corporate opportunities, excessive fees, and irregular charges ([8]–[9]).

### I – Issues

The central issues presented to the court were:

1. Whether the appellants breached their fiduciary duties by diverting corporate opportunities for their personal profit at the expense of the bewind trusts ([23]–[24]).
2. Whether the high court incorrectly limited the curator's claims regarding the amount recoverable ([58]).
3. Whether the high court was right to dismiss aspects of the Curator's claims related to excessive management and performance fees, as well as irregular charges ([26]–[27]).
4. Whether the necessity to stay the appeal proceedings was justified pending resolution of related issues in the Constitutional Court ([45]).

### R – Rules

The court primarily relied upon various statutory provisions, including:

- Section 5 of the Financial Institutions (Protection of Funds) Act 28 of 2001, which provides grounds for appointing a curator to protect the integrity and management of fiduciary funds ([1]).
- The principles governing fiduciary duties as stipulated by common law, mandating utmost good faith, diligence, and loyalty from fiduciaries in managing trust property ([6]).
- Cases establishing standards regarding the evaluation of whether breaches of fiduciary duties have occurred, including the principles governing diversion of corporate opportunities ([46]).

### A – Application

In its judgment, the court meticulously analyzed the evidence and state of affairs surrounding the acquisitions and management of the properties in question. It concluded that the appellants did indeed divert corporate opportunities which rightfully belonged to the trusts ([56]–[57]). The court emphasized the reliance on investor funds for purchases conducted with self-serving motives rather than in pursuit of the trust beneficiaries’ best interests ([74]).

The high court's initial ruling to grant only a fraction of the total claimed amount was deemed erroneous. It claimed one-third of the diversion instead of the full amount sought, based on insufficient evidence against co-defendants, which the Supreme Court of Appeal found to be unfounded due to the collective nature of the breaches ([58]). 

Moreover, the excessive management fees charged were found to violate fiduciary duties and lacked justifiable bases, confirming an obligation on the part of the appellants to return the misappropriated funds to the trusts ([88]).

Lastly, the court dismissed the application for a stay of proceedings, citing the absence of merit in the conflict raised by the appellants, and emphasizing the greater need to address the mismanagement and loss of trust assets promptly for the vulnerable beneficiaries ([39], [45]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal must be dismissed, whereas the cross-appeal succeeded with necessary adjustments outlined by the judgment. The erroneous considerations by the high court regarding the quantum of recoverable amounts were corrected, returning liability for the full claims against the appellants. 

Furthermore, the court established that the actions taken by the appellants, characterized by self-enrichment through fiduciary breaches, warranted immediate action for recovery and protection of the trust beneficiaries' rights. The decision ultimately reinforced the critical importance of stringent adherence to fiduciary duties and the legal obligations imposed on those managing trust funds. The Registrar was directed to forward copies of the judgment to the relevant authorities for possible prosecution, underscoring the severity of the misconduct involved ([120]).
