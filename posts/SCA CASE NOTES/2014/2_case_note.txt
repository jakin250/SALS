Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Dean of the Law Faculty of the University of North West & Others v Masisi (297/2013) [2014] ZASCA 2

## F – Facts

The case centers around a dispute involving the North West University (NWU) and its refusal to grant full academic credit to Mr. Moramang Simon Masisi for courses completed at the University of the North, which he held before enrolling for an LLB degree at NWU. Masisi argued that he was discriminated against, as students graduating from NWU's own B.Proc program received credit for all their courses, whereas he was only given credit for 50% of his completed courses, violating his constitutional right to equality as outlined in the Promotion of the Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (the Equality Act) ([1]).

The university justified its stance by citing its General Academic Rules and the statutory foundation provided by the Joint Statute of the Universities in the Republic of South Africa, which mandated at least half of degree requirements be completed at the university conferring the degree ([2]). Masisi brought his complaint before the Equality Court, which ruled in his favor, declaring the university’s policies null and void, allowing him full exemption based on his previous studies ([3]). 

The implications of the Equality Court’s order extended beyond Masisi’s case, impacting the statutory framework governing all universities in South Africa, and as a consequence, the Minister of Education and the collected voice of other universities, represented by HESA, were not involved ([4]-[5]).

## I – Issues

The principal legal question revolved around whether the Equality Court had the authority to invalidate statutory provisions and university policies without allowing necessary stakeholders, such as the Minister of Education and other universities, an opportunity to participate in the proceedings ([10]). Additionally, the court evaluated whether the procedural limitations arising from the directions hearing in the Equality Court were adhered to and whether the court could address matters beyond the initially identified issues ([8]-[9]).

## R – Rules

The Equality Act mandates that disputes involving violations of equality must provide all relevant parties, particularly those with a vested interest in the outcome—such as the national and provincial authorities responsible for administering the laws—an opportunity to participate in the proceedings ([10]). The principle of audi alteram partem, or the right to be heard, underscores the necessity of including all parties who may be affected by court decisions ([11]). 

Section 20(3)(a) of the Equality Act may provide mechanisms for the referral of such cases to appropriate forums, protecting the integrity of legal processes affecting significant public interests ([12]). 

## A – Application

The court assessed whether the Equality Court appropriately evaluated the policies governing the recognition of academic credit and whether its authority allowed for the striking down of those policies without necessary stakeholder participation. The ruling from the Equality Court failed to consider implications for other universities, nor did it afford the Minister a chance to defend the constitutionality of the statutory provisions governing academic credit transfer policies. The judges emphasized the impropriety of issuing wide-reaching orders that could unsettle the broader educational framework without due process ([10]-[11]).

The appeal court held that the expansive nature of the Equality Court's order exceeded the matters originally convened in the directions hearing and resulted from a lack of thorough consideration regarding the implications on statutory provisions ([9]). Furthermore, by failing to engage the Minister and other relevant parties in the Equality Court proceedings, the fundamental principles of justice were potentially undermined ([10]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, setting aside the Equality Court’s decision and remitting the case for further consideration in the Equality Court with specific instructions. Notably, the court mandated that Masisi inform all stakeholders, including the Minister of Education and HESA, about the proceedings and invite their participation ([12]). This ruling reinforced the importance of inclusive legal processes in matters impacting public interests and upheld the principle of procedural fairness while recognizing the role of statutory frameworks in educating South Africa's future lawyers.

The judgment has essential implications for university governance and the role of the Equality Court, ensuring that issues relating to statutory provisions are addressed with due diligence and appropriate party participation, thereby stabilizing the legal landscape surrounding higher education and reinforcing equality before the law.
