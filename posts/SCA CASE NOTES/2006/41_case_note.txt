# Case Note: Minister of Transport v Du Toit [2006] SCA 40 (RSA)

## F – Facts

In *Minister of Transport v Du Toit*, the first respondent, referred to as the plaintiff, suffered serious injuries on 27 September 1995 when a vehicle, driven by her husband, veered off the road near the Grasmere toll gate on the N1 highway, south of Johannesburg ([1]). The accident occurred after the couple, unfamiliar with the route, mistakenly took an exit at the toll gate, believing they remained on the N1. After paying for the toll at the extreme left booth, they entered an area that led them onto a poorly marked off-ramp ([6]) where a ‘T’ intersection awaited.

Upon emerging from the toll booth, the driver accelerated, initially in an illuminated area but soon transitioned into darkness. Neither the plaintiff nor her husband could identify any effective warning signs prior to the accident that would indicate their transition off the freeway ([6] and [7]). The plaintiff subsequently sued the South African Roads Board for negligent failure in maintaining adequate road signs.

When the Minister of Transport was substituted as the responsible party and was joined by Toll Road Concessionaries (Pty) Ltd as a third party, the first appellant claimed that Tolcon had assumed some supervisory responsibilities regarding road signage. They also alleged that the husband's negligence contributed significantly to the accident ([2]).

The trial court separated the aspects of negligence, determining first whether the first appellant was liable and whether the plaintiff's husband’s driving contributed to the accident. Ultimately, the lower court found the first appellant negligent and apportioned 20 percent blame to the state and 80 percent to the husband ([3]).

## I – Issues

The central issues before the court included:

1. Whether the first appellant (the Minister of Transport) was negligent in failing to provide adequate signage for road users ([15]).
2. If found negligent, what degree of contributory negligence could be ascribed to the driver of the vehicle, specifically in relation to the signage available ([20]).

## R – Rules

The standard for negligence encompasses the establishment of a legal duty to act, the breach of that duty, and causation linking the breach to the harm suffered ([17]). The relevant rule indicates that the responsible authority must ensure road users are warned adequately of foreseeable dangers, particularly in unfamiliar and poorly lit areas. Precedents cited include:

- *Gouda Boerdery BK v Transnet* 2005 (5) SA 490 (SCA) which involves evaluating whether adequate warning and safety measures were in place ([3]).
- *Langley Fox Building Partnership (Pty) Ltd v De Valence* 1991 (1) SA 1 (A) concerning the obligations of a party when relying on an independent contractor ([3]).

The court further noted that subsequent changes in signage do not confirm previous negligence; rather, it emphasizes the need for the preliminary signage to have been adequate in the first instance ([16]).

## A – Application

In determining the negligence of the first appellant, the court evaluated the adequacy of the signage. It acknowledged the complexity of the layout near the toll gate and the confusing nature of the overhead signs—specifically criticizing the directionality of the signage that misled the driver into thinking they were still on the freeway ([18]). The junction's ‘T’ intersection posed an inherent risk, particularly at night, demonstrating that a well-informed motorist would still struggle without clear warnings.

Expert testimony from Mr. Konrad Lötter supported the claim of deficiencies in the road signage ([13]). He asserted that the misleading placement and condition of the signs significantly contributed to the accident scenario, thus tying the deficiencies directly to the resulting injury.

The court argued that although the driver bore some measure of attention-related negligence, the inadequacy of the road signs played a substantial role in the driver’s misjudgment ([19] and [21]). The positioning and visibility challenges led to his misinterpretation of the road layout, which did not inform him effectively of the impending 'T' intersection.

The trial court's apportionment of liability was upheld. The appellate court confirmed that both parties bore some responsibility, with the husband's negligence acknowledged but outweighed by the first appellant's failure to provide clear warnings ([22]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the finding of negligence against the first appellant, the Minister of Transport, concluding that the signage provided was inadequate and contributed to the accident. The court reaffirmed the trial court's apportionment of 80 percent liability to the husband and 20 percent to the state, emphasizing both parties' responsibilities in road safety but highlighting the primary accountability of the road authority for insufficient warning signs ([23]). 

This case illustrates the critical importance of clear and unambiguous road signage, particularly in contextually hazardous situations, and underscores the legal obligations of state authorities to ensure the safety of road users.
