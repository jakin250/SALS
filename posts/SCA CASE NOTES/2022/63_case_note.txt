Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note

## Title: IGS Consulting Engineers & Another v Transnet SOC Limited (Case No 198/2020) [2022] ZASCA 63

**Coram**: Dambuza, Schippers and Nicholls JJA and Tsoka and Molefe AJJA  
**Heard**: 18 February 2022  
**Delivered**: 29 April 2022  

---

### F – Facts 

This case deals with an appeal regarding contracts that were found to have been unlawfully awarded by the organ of state, Transnet SOC Limited (Transnet), to IGS Consulting Engineers CC (IGS) and its joint venture partner, Turnmill Proquip Engineering (Pty) Ltd (Turnmill). The contracts—five in total—were for significant projects related to the maintenance of a multi-product pipeline, valued at over R204 million ([para 3]). 

The Gauteng Division of the High Court ruled that the contracts were void due to a corrupt relationship between IGS's sole member, Mr Sipho Sithole, and Transnet's chief executive of capital projects, Mr Sipho Linyenga Herbert Msagala. This relationship was deemed to violate Section 217 of the South African Constitution, which mandates that government procurement must be fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([paras 1–8]).

IGS and Turnmill, while contesting their liability, argued that the joint venture agreements simply regularized relationships between them and Transnet and that Turnmill had no involvement in the alleged fraudulent conduct. IGS eventually withdrew its appeal and conceded costs to Transnet, complicating the status of Turnmill's liability in relation to the second contract ([paras 5, 12]).

While Turnmill asserted that it had performed work and incurred expenses regarding the second contract, it claimed to have been underpaid and not to have realized any profits from its work. The court recognized that Turnmill had maintained its innocence and argued that it should not be liable for any profits made by IGS due to the unlawful nature of their contract ([paras 14, 20]).

However, the court also emphasized that Turnmill's claims lack a solid factual basis given their prior agreements and their understanding of the joint venture's purpose. Ultimately, the appeals court had to resolve whether any live issues existed following IGS’s withdrawal, especially in relation to Turnmill's liability for profits resulting from the second contract ([[paras 20–25]).

### I – Issues

The legal questions arising from this case primarily involve:

1. Whether the high court's order to set aside the contracts was justified due to non-compliance with constitutional procurement requirements.
2. Whether any live issues remained for adjudication after IGS withdrew its appeal.
3. The appropriateness of Transnet’s arguments concerning the liability for costs in light of Turnmill’s claims of limited involvement and actual financial losses associated with the contracts ([paras 5, 20, 25]).

### R – Rules

The primary legal rules and precedents relevant to this case include:

- **Section 217 of the Constitution**: Requires that organs of state conduct procurement processes that are fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([para 1]).
- **Section 172(1)(a) and (b) of the Constitution**: Provides that courts must declare conduct inconsistent with the Constitution as invalid and must consider just and equitable remedies ([para 2]).
- **Section 16(2)(a) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**: States that appeals may be dismissed if the issues have no practical effect ([para 23]).
- **Common law principles regarding costs**: An appeal court generally will not interfere with a lower court's discretion in costs unless it acted improperly ([para 26]) and courts avoid adjudicating matters where the only issue is costs ([para 27]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal reaffirmed that, under Section 217 of the Constitution, any non-compliance in procurement undermines the legitimacy of the contracts and mandates a declaration of invalidity ([para 1]). The court highlighted the damaging effects of such violations on public trust and financial integrity concerning state-owned enterprises ([para 2]). 

In terms of the appeal's procedural status, the court upheld that, with IGS's withdrawal, the only potential dispute was about costs, which does not constitute a live issue that warrants judicial consideration ([paras 20, 29]). The court noted that Turnmill sought to argue that it had been substantially successful regarding costs, but this claim was undermined because it had not prevailed on significant legal issues concerning contract validity and was not entitled to claim costs against Transnet ([para 25]).

Despite Turnmill's assertions of vindication given the lack of evidence implicating it in any wrongful conduct concerning the second contract, the court found that the joint venture's unlawful nature justified the high court's initial findings ([paras 20, 22]). The submissions indicating that Turnmill was merely a subcontractor to IGS and not involved in direct contractual engagements were found unsupported as they knowingly entered the joint venture resulting in an unlawful agreement ([paras 16–19]).

### C – Conclusion 

The court concluded that the appeal should be dismissed as moot, given the lack of remaining live issues following IGS's withdrawal, with costs awarded against the appellants ([paras 30]). Additionally, the court modified the high court’s order regarding the contracts, reaffirming their unlawful status and detailing necessary financial disclosures from both IGS and Turnmill without introducing further joint liabilities ([paras 30–31]).

This decision highlights the importance of constitutional compliance in public procurement processes and underscores the duty of the courts to enforce legal clarity while minimizing judicial resources on moot matters. The ruling further affirms accountability in state contracts, especially when corruption or fraudulent conduct is suspected, establishing a precedent for future cases involving similar procurement disputes ([paras 1–8, 23]).
