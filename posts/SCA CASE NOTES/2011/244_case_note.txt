Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Rademan v Moqhaka Municipality & Others (173/11) [2011] ZASCA 244

## F – Facts
The appellant, Olga Rademan, a resident of No 1 Panorama Park, Kroonstad, fell within the jurisdiction of the Moqhaka Municipality (the first respondent). She was a member of the Moqhaka Ratepayers and Residents Association, which withheld their municipal rate payments due to complaints about service delivery from the municipality. On 17 August 2009, following her failure to pay municipal taxes and levies amounting to R2,986.96, the municipality disconnected her electricity supply without a court order. The appellant launched an urgent application which restored her electricity supply; however, an appeal by the municipality to the Free State High Court was successful. The current case is an appeal from this high court decision ([1–2]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue is whether the municipality acted lawfully in disconnecting the appellant’s electricity supply without a court order, and whether such action constituted self-help, contravening the law's requirements ([4]).

## R – Rules
1. **Municipal Powers**: Section 151 of the Constitution of South Africa asserts that municipalities have the authority to govern local affairs of their communities, adhering to national legislation ([7]).
2. **Credit Control and Debt Collection**: Per Section 96 of the Municipal Systems Act, municipalities must adopt a credit control and debt collection policy to ensure collection of money owed to them ([10]).
3. **By-laws on Service Disconnection**: Section 25 of the Credit Control and Debt Collection By-laws (2004) empowers municipalities to disconnect services for non-payment of any municipal service ([13]).
4. **Self-Help in Law**: The case of Joseph & Others v City of Johannesburg stipulates a requirement for a notice period before service disconnection, although that ruling did not equate to municipal service contracts ([4]).

## A – Application
The court examined whether the disconnection was lawful. The municipality's disconnection action was justified according to its statutory powers under the Municipal Systems Act, allowing it to consolidate accounts and take appropriate actions for unpaid dues ([18]). The practicalities of enforcing service charges without a court order were deemed reasonable, especially in contexts of public protests and the necessity for municipalities to manage resources effectively ([16–20]). The court rebutted the appellant's argument regarding self-help, finding that the lack of payment justified the municipality's action of disconnection of services for any unpaid municipal dues ([21]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the municipality's right to disconnect the appellant's electricity supply based on her non-payment of taxes, despite being current on other service payments. The judgment underscores the municipality's authority to manage non-compliant residents without necessitating court intervention, emphasizing the importance of fulfilling civic responsibilities ([23]).

### Implications
This ruling establishes a precedent affirming that municipalities possess the authority and discretion to enforce payment for municipal services through disconnection of services as a measure of last resort for non-payment. It highlights the legal framework supporting necessary municipal actions to ensure adherence to civic responsibilities and fiscal management, influencing future compliance and governance at local government levels. It clarifies that denying services for non-payment is valid even without judicial enforcement, provided there are procedures in place for fair notice and the opportunity for residents to contest unjustified charges.
