Big G Restaurants (Pty) Limited v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service [2020] ZACC 16

Constitutional Court 2020 [2020] ZACC 16

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–4])

Big G Restaurants (Pty) Ltd, a franchisee operating Spur and Panarottis restaurants, sought a deduction under section 24C(2) of the Income Tax Act for future expenditure related to refurbishing restaurant premises. This expenditure arose from franchise agreements mandating periodic upgrades. Specifically, from 2011 to 2014, Big G claimed that income generated through sales to customers was linked to these obligations and thus qualified for the tax deduction as "future expenditure". The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service disallowed this claim, asserting that the income associated with individual customer contracts did not accrue “in terms of” the franchise agreements, which imposed the obligations leading to future expenditure. Big G's unsuccessful objection led to subsequent appeals through the Tax Court, which ruled in Big G's favor, and then the Supreme Court of Appeal, which reversed the Tax Court's decision.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–4])

At the heart of the dispute were two fundamental questions:

  1. Does the income received by Big G from operating its restaurants constitute income “in terms of” the franchise agreements for the purposes of section 24C(2) of the Income Tax Act?
  2. Is the future expenditure for refurbishing the restaurants incurred "in the performance of [Big G’s] obligations under such contract"?

Both issues hinged on the interpretation of the phrase "in terms of" within section 24C(2), questions of law which implicate broader implications for franchisees across South Africa ([para 6]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 7–14])

The relevant statutory provision under scrutiny was section 24C of the Income Tax Act (58 of 1962), particularly subsection 2. Legal interpretation principles require that contracts be construed in a manner that reflects their commercial context and intent. The Supreme Court of Appeal's prior ruling in Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Big G Restaurants (Pty) Ltd emphasized that the income and expenditure-causing contracts must be the same for the allowance to apply ([paras 7–15]).

The court also considered public interest and fairness in interpreting statutory provisions, drawing from precedents such as SATAWU v Garvas and Liesching v S, where the courts prioritized interpretations that avoid absurdities or inequalities in tax policy applications ([paras 15–17]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 17–26])

The Constitutional Court assessed the interrelationship between the franchise agreements and the individual sales contracts to determine their effect on the income-earning status of Big G. It acknowledged Big G's obligations to operate restaurants profitably and maintain franchise standards, yet concluded that general operational obligations did not confer the specific contractual linkage required by section 24C for claiming tax allowances.

The court critically analyzed the premise that revenue generated from sales of food is directly attributable to obligations imposed by the franchise agreement, ultimately finding the franchise agreements and customer contracts to be separate and distinct. This separation negated the essential "sameness" of contracts needed to satisfy section 24C(2), reinforcing the initial determinations by the Commissioner and the Supreme Court of Appeal.

The majority opinion shed light on the necessary conditions under which operational income could be viewed as functioning under a singular “in terms of” contract. It ruled that Big G failed to sufficiently connect the income derived from customer sales with an obligation imposed by the same contract that warranted future expenditure ([paras 17–26]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 27–32])

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed Big G’s appeal, reaffirming the decisions of the Commissioner and the Supreme Court of Appeal. The court reasoned that, because the income-generating contracts with customers did not share a direct correlation with the obligations of the franchise agreements for future expenditure, the tax allowance claimed could not be sustained under section 24C(2).

The case sets a decisive precedent establishing the necessity for a direct contractual relationship when applying for tax allowances under the Income Tax Act. The ruling has broader implications for franchisees and other sectors, as it clarifies the interpretation of tax provisions and underscores the importance of explicit contractual terms in tax-related litigation. This decision serves as a guide for future disputes involving franchise operations and income generation, compelling businesses to establish clear contractual frameworks linking income with imposed obligations to qualify for tax benefits ([paras 27–32]).
