Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: South African Football Association v Fli-Afrika Travel (Pty) Limited [2020] ZASCA 4

## F – Facts

The appellant, the South African Football Association (SAFA), and the respondent, Fli-Afrika Travel (Pty) Ltd (Fli-Afrika), commenced their business relationship in the 1990s, with Fli-Afrika serving as SAFA's travel agent. In anticipation of the 2010 FIFA World Cup, the parties entered into a joint venture agreement (the SLA) on January 23, 2009, which was subsequently subject to a settlement agreement dated April 16, 2010.

Fli-Afrika claimed substantial damages from SAFA, alleging a breach of the SLA after SAFA failed to supply the tickets necessary for their travel packages. The initial trial concluded with Matojane J dismissing Fli-Afrika's claim. However, this outcome was overturned by Mabesele J and the full bench, which ordered SAFA to pay Fli-Afrika R13,989,452.78, along with interest and costs, leading to SAFA's appeal ([pars 1–2], [paras 10–11]).

Prior to the SLA, an MOU was established on July 10, 2007, which laid a framework for their cooperation. The SLA outlined the obligations of both parties, particularly stating that SAFA was to supply tickets to Fli-Afrika, allowing Fli-Afrika to create travel packages for the World Cup ([paras 3-8]).

Fli-Afrika incurred substantial costs by securing hotel bookings but received no tickets from SAFA. Efforts to resolve this issue included correspondence and meetings, which ultimately failed and led to the settlement agreement intended to formalize the terms of their relationship, particularly concerning the provision of tickets ([paras 12-50]).

## I – Issues

The primary issue in this case was whether the SLA imposed an obligation on SAFA to provide tickets to Fli-Afrika and whether Fli-Afrika was entitled to reimbursement for prior expenditures made in anticipation of receiving these tickets. Additionally, the issue of whether the settlement agreement constituted a mutual termination or waiver of claims under the SLA was also raised ([paras 32-35]). 

### Key Questions:
1. Did the SLA contain express or tacit terms obligating SAFA to reimburse Fli-Afrika for accommodation and travel arrangements prior to ticket delivery?
2. Did the settlement agreement extinguish any claims Fli-Afrika might have had under the SLA?

## R – Rules

The court cited general principles of contract interpretation established in *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality* ([2012] ZASCA 13; 2012 (4) SA 593), which emphasized a contextual understanding of contractual terms, considering their literal meaning within the entire document and surrounding circumstances ([para 36]).

The court also acknowledged the significance of mutual agreements in contractual obligations, particularly concerning the wording of the settlement agreement, which was described as a "full and final settlement" of any claims regarding the provision of tickets by SAFA ([paras 44–50]).

## A – Application

### Interpretation of the SLA
The court examined clauses 3.2 and 4 of the SLA, concluding that the obligations therein did not explicitly obligate Fli-Afrika to incur costs for accommodation without the prior knowledge of the ticket allocations from SAFA. The mere definition of "packages" did not create a condition precedent for Fli-Afrika to expend funds. Thus, the court ascertained that there was no allowance under the SLA for reimbursement ([paras 37–41]).

### Settlement Agreement
Further, considering the settlement agreement, the court highlighted that it effectively eliminated any obligations regarding ticket provision, thus releasing both parties from previous commitments as attested in the written document. The agreement was a direct response to SAFA’s inability to provide the previously agreed-upon tickets ([paras 44–51]). Hence, the settlement agreement conclusively settled all claims, including any potential for reimbursement claimed by Fli-Afrika.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that:
1. The SLA did not obligate Fli-Afrika to pay for accommodation or travel prior to receiving tickets from SAFA. Therefore, SAFA was not liable to reimburse Fli-Afrika.
2. The settlement agreement effectively terminated any claims arising from the SLA, including Fli-Afrika's expectations of ticket provision and any associated costs incurred.

As a result, the appeal was upheld, reversing the lower court's decision and affirming that both parties were released from their contractual obligations, concluding the dispute ([paras 52–53]). 

### Implications
The case underlines the essential nature of clear contractual terms and the significance of mutual settlements in business relationships, particularly in high-stakes environments such as preparations for international events. It reinforces the principle that parties must ensure mutual understanding and explicit documentation of their obligations to avoid disputes over reimbursement and liability ([paras 1–2], [52–53]).
