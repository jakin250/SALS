Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Maroveke v Talane N.O. and Others [2021] ZACC 20

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 2–10])
The applicant, Mr. Nesu Maroveke, was employed by Fermel (Pty) Limited as an artisan and mine technician from October 15, 2007, until his dismissal on July 16, 2009. His dismissal arose from an incident where a company vehicle he was driving sank in a waterlogged area while responding to a breakdown in a mine ([para 2]). Following the vehicle's damage, a disciplinary hearing found Maroveke guilty of misconduct based on a lack of foresight regarding potential vehicle damage, despite the absence of the expert damage report during the proceedings ([para 3]).

Following his dismissal, Maroveke pursued a dispute of unfair dismissal with the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA), which found in his favor and ordered his reinstatement with back pay. However, this decision was reversed by the Labour Court following a review, which held that the CCMA’s findings relied on a flawed reasoning and reinstated the applicant only for the two months he was unemployed ([paras 5–10]). The matter bounced through various legal avenues: the Labour Appeal Court ordered a retrial at the CCMA, which subsequently reinstated Maroveke but calculated the back pay based on flawed figures.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 21–23])
The key questions before the Constitutional Court were:
1. Does this matter engage the Court’s jurisdiction concerning constitutional rights?
2. Is it in the interests of justice to grant leave to appeal?
3. What is the appropriate back pay due to the applicant given that he found new employment shortly after dismissal?

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 24–28])
The judgment emphasized the principles of fairness in reinstatement under the Labour Relations Act and referenced notable cases including *Toyota South Africa Motors (Pty) Ltd v CCMA* ([para 24]) and *Davids v Boland Rugby (Pty) Ltd* ([para 27]). The Court reaffirmed that reinstatement should not impoverish or enrich the employee but should restore them to their previous financial position prior to dismissal. Furthermore, the Court highlighted the necessity of accurate and relevant information regarding the employee’s earnings for a just restitution ([paras 25–28]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 25–34])
The Constitutional Court upheld the Labour Court's decision regarding the reasonableness of the 12-month back pay initially determined by the CCMA. It concurred that the Labour Court correctly discerned that the Commissioner should have considered the employee's post-dismissal employment for reinstatement purposes, and thus justified the reduction of back pay to reflect the period of actual unemployment ([paras 25–26]).

However, the Court diverged on the quantification of back pay. It found that the Labour Court had relied on incorrect salary figures when determining the compensation owed, failing to account for the substantial difference between Maroveke's prior salary and his post-dismissal earnings at Gold Fields. The Court noted that while Maroveke earned a gross salary of R24,730.60 with Fermel, he earned only R15,000 at Gold Fields, leading to a significant loss which undermined the fair restitution principle ([paras 30–34]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 34–35])
The Constitutional Court concluded that the applicant was entitled to a payment of R146,767.20, reflecting both the back pay for two months and the earnings differential calculated over the ten months that he was employed at Gold Fields. The Order from the Labour Court was thus modified to mandate Fermel (Pty) Limited to pay this amount within 15 days, affirming the importance of accurate consideration of an employee’s salary history in reinstatement cases ([paras 34–35]).

This ruling not only underscored the necessity of ensuring fair labor practices but also clarified protocols regarding the quantification of back pay in reinstatement situations. It serves as a critical precedent in South African labor law, emphasizing detailed analysis and fairness in rectifying instances of unfair dismissal.
