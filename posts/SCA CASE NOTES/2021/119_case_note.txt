Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Kouwenhoven v Minister of Police and Others [2021] ZASCA 119

## F – Facts
The case arises from Mr. Augustinus Petrus Maria Kouwenhoven’s appeals regarding his extradition to the Netherlands to serve a 19-year prison sentence for complicity in war crimes related to the civil war in Liberia (2021) [1]. Kouwenhoven, a Dutch citizen residing in South Africa, was arrested on 8 December 2017 following a warrant issued under section 5(1)(b) of the Extradition Act 67 of 1962 (the Act). This warrant was issued by a magistrate in Pretoria after various interactions between Kouwenhoven’s legal representatives and South African police officials, which included alleged verbal undertakings not to arrest him without notice ([2]–[3]). Kouwenhoven sought declaratory relief in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, challenging the lawfulness of his arrest, the validity of the arrest warrant, and the conduct of the extradition enquiry ([3]). The High Court dismissed his review application on 19 September 2019, after which Kouwenhoven appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([4]).

## I – Issues
The central legal issues for determination were:
1. Whether the police officials had indeed given a binding undertaking not to arrest Kouwenhoven, and if so, whether this rendered the subsequent arrest unlawful.
2. The validity of the affidavit sworn by Warrant Officer van der Heever, properly attested under the Justices of the Peace and Commissioners of Oaths Act 16 of 1963 (the Oaths Act) ([7]).
3. Whether the magistrate who issued the warrant had properly applied her mind to the circumstances surrounding its issuance, or merely rubber-stamped the application ([8]).
4. The ramifications of the magistrate's failure to inform the Minister of Justice regarding the warrant, as mandated by section 8(1) of the Act ([9]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework undergirding the case includes:
1. **Extradition Act 67 of 1962**: Governing the process of extradition and the criteria for issuing arrest warrants ([5]).
2. **European Convention on Extradition**: Guides the procedural aspects of extradition requests, including provisional arrests ([5]).
3. **Justices of the Peace and Commissioners of Oaths Act**: Outlining the protocols for administering oaths and affirmations ([29]).
4. **Principles regarding the validity of administrative actions**: Such as the requirement for judicial oversight and factors that impact the legality of warrants ([29][48]).
5. **Case law precedence**: Such as *Ebrahim v S*, which discusses the implications of unlawful arrests on prosecutorial procedures ([56]).

## A – Application
### Undertaking Not to Arrest
The court analyzed the supposed undertakings made by South African officials not to arrest Kouwenhoven. The judges considered whether these undertakings, if found to have been made, could bind the state. Critical to this assessment was the consideration that such agreements, particularly those related to extradition procedures, must be formalized and understood at a governmental level rather than being concluded through informal channels ([12]–[20]).

### Validity of the Affidavit
The affidavit submitted by Warrant Officer van der Heever faced scrutiny regarding whether it was appropriately sworn. The court found that the affidavit was validly attested, referencing established precedents that allowed for such attestations under employment circumstances without disqualifying interest ([28]–[30]).

### Magistrate’s Decision-Making
Kouwenhoven claimed the magistrate acted without due diligence in assessing the warrant application, simply 'rubber-stamping' the document. The court found this argument unsupported as the magistrate provided no evidence indicating a lack of consideration. It affirmed that the lawfulness of the warrant stood independently from administrative oversights that followed its issuance ([42]–[46]).

### Failure to Notify the Minister
On the issue of the magistrate's failure to notify the Minister pursuant to section 8(1) of the Act, the court held that while the statute necessitated a prompt notification of particulars following the issuance of a warrant, failure to execute this duty did not retroactively invalidate the warrant once properly issued. Clarity was sought on the procedural integrity of the warrant itself, which the court confirmed remained intact above administrative errors post-issuance ([49]–[53]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Kouwenhoven's appeal, affirming the legality of his arrest and the warrant issued against him. The court upheld the findings of the High Court, reasoning that the absence of a legitimate, binding undertaking prohibiting his arrest, the validity of the sworn affidavit, the proper judicial review of the warrant application, and the non-retroactive application of administrative shortcomings led to the determination that the processes followed were lawful. The case serves as a significant reference point for the interplay between extradition procedures and constitutional protections in the South African legal context ([57]). The complexities of extradition law highlighted in this case reiterate the necessity for diplomatic clarity and adherence to legislative protocols in sovereignty-respecting legal frameworks.
