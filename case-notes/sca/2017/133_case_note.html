Supreme Court of Appeal

Jaron du Preez v Eugene Pretorius (949/2016) [2017] ZASCA 133

Du Preez sought IPT treatment from Pretorius from 10 November 2010 to 8 February 2011, during which he received multiple treatments.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 133

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-2])

The appellant, Jaron du Preez, was diagnosed with advanced testicular cancer and underwent the surgical removal of his left testis on 3 November 2010. Following the surgery, he was referred to a conventional oncologist, Dr. Slabber, but found himself dissatisfied with the oncologist's bedside manner. Consequently, he began exploring alternative treatments and encountered Eugene Pretorius, a general practitioner advocating for insulin potentiation therapy (IPT) as an alternative treatment methodology ([para 1]).

Du Preez sought IPT treatment from Pretorius from 10 November 2010 to 8 February 2011, during which he received multiple treatments. Following concerns regarding the effectiveness of IPT, particularly after abnormal tumour marker results, du Preez sought conventional treatment from Dr. Rens, who administered four cycles of chemotherapy, resulting in his cancer going into remission ([paras 1-6]).

Du Preez subsequently sued Pretorius in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, alleging medical negligence through misrepresentation and improper treatment. The trial court, presided over by Janse Van Nieuwenhuizen J, dismissed his claim with costs, leading to the present appeal ([paras 2-3]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2-9, 21])

The primary issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

  1. Misrepresentation: Whether Pretorius negligently misrepresented the efficacy of IPT, thereby inducing du Preez to undergo the treatment, and whether such misrepresentations were a cause of his subsequent health complications.
  2. Negligence: Whether Pretorius's conduct constituted a negligent breach of duty, giving rise to contractual and delictual claims.
  3. Causation: Whether du Preez could establish a causal link between the alleged negligent conduct by Pretorius and the need for further conventional chemotherapy ([paras 8-20]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 7-19, 21])

The court relied on established principles of delictual liability as outlined in the precedent Lillicrap, Wassenaar & Partners v Pilkington Brothers (SA) (Pty) Ltd, which allows for concurrent claims in contract and delict ([para 16]). Additionally, the fundamental elements of misrepresentation were discussed, which include:

  1. Making a False Representation: That a false or misleading statement was made.
  2. Negligent Conduct: The representation was made negligently.
  3. Inducement: The claimant was induced to act on the representation.
  4. Causation: Establishing that damages resulted as a direct consequence of reliance on the misrepresentation ([para 9]).

The ruling further emphasized the need for clear causal links within medical negligence cases, highlighting that medical evidence must substantiate claims of damages due to alleged negligence ([paras 19-20]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 8-21])

The court evaluated each of the alleged misrepresentations made by Pretorius. Regarding the claim that du Preez was a suitable candidate for IPT, the court noted that while Pretorius admitted to making this statement, du Preez failed to prove it was false. Evidence, including supporting literature, did not convincingly establish that du Preez was not a suitable candidate for the treatment ([paras 12-13]).

Additionally, both du Preez and Pretorius denied any explicit representations regarding the ability of IPT to cure his condition, with Pretorius asserting that he provided an estimate of treatment efficacy rather than a guaranteed cure. The court found this estimate was indeed communicated as a possibility rather than a factual assertion about guaranteed outcomes ([paras 10-11]).

The court further examined the causal aspects of du Preez’s second and third causes of action, focusing on whether the alleged negligence directly resulted in harm or additional treatment needs. Notably, it was concluded that du Preez was aware that IPT may not yield a cure, suggesting he could not solely attribute his need for chemotherapy to the IPT treatment ([para 18]). The testimony of Dr. Van Niekerk established that the standard treatment would remain consistent regardless of any initial treatment modality pursued ([para 19]).

Ultimately, the court found that there was insufficient evidence to connect Pretorius’s conduct with the damages claimed by du Preez. As such, the allegations of negligence were found to lack substantiation, allowing the court to forgo a detailed analysis of the respondent’s alleged negligence ([para 20]). Due to these findings, the appeal was thereby dismissed ([paras 21-23]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 22-23])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed du Preez's appeal, endorsing the decision of the High Court. The ruling elucidated that the appellant failed to establish the necessary elements of misrepresentation and negligence requisite for establishing liability. Consequently, the court advised that clarity in the formulation of issues was paramount to ensure proper judicial handling in the context of separated issues under Uniform Rule 33(4) ([para 22]).

The judgment serves as a crucial reminder of the rigour required in medical negligence claims to establish direct causation and the necessity of presenting concrete evidence to support allegations of negligent conduct. This case marks a significant point of reference in South African jurisprudence, particularly concerning alternative medical therapies and the obligations of medical practitioners in conveying treatment efficacy.

The court awarded costs to the respondent, including costs for two counsel, confirming the weighted consideration given to the litigation’s complexities ([para 23]).
