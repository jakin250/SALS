Supreme Court of Appeal

Media 24 (Pty) Ltd v Nhleko & Another (Case No. 109/22) [2023] ZASCA 77

Following Media 24's initial pleadings, the respondents filed a Rule 30A notice, objecting to Media 24's plea as a bare denial, arguing it lacked clarity and did not address substantive issues.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 77

FIRAC Method

F - Facts:
The appeal arises from a defamation suit initiated by Nkosinathi Nhleko, the Minister of Police, and Dr. Noncebo Mthembu against Media 24 (Pty) Ltd, following the publication of an article titled “Nhleko’s R30m blessing” in the City Press newspaper on November 27, 2016. The article alleged that Nhleko was implicated in corrupt practices, particularly regarding a payment exceeding R30 million made to Mthembu’s non-profit organization, Indoni. Media 24 admitted the publication but contested the defamatory nature of the statements made, positing defenses of truth in the public interest, protected comment, and reasonable publication.

Following Media 24's initial pleadings, the respondents filed a Rule 30A notice, objecting to Media 24's plea as a bare denial, arguing it lacked clarity and did not address substantive issues. In response, Media 24 sought to amend its plea, which was opposed by Nhleko and Mthembu on grounds of alleged deceit and lack of substance, leading to an application for leave to amend being made to the Western Cape Division of the High Court ([paras 1-4]).

The High Court, presided by Thulare AJ, ultimately dismissed the application for amendment, asserting that Media 24’s initial plea constituted an inadequate response and lacked clarity essential for adjudication. The High Court's findings were grounded on a view of the defendant's obligations in defamation cases and its broader role in society, including an obligation to combat misinformation ([paras 5-20]).

I - Issues:
The principal legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) involved whether:

  1. The High Court erred in its interpretation of Media 24's obligations as a defendant in a defamation suit.
  2. The application for leave to amend Media 24's plea should have been granted, considering the principles of amendment in pleadings, especially regarding the characterization of the plea as a bare denial.
  3. The High Court's order regarding the amendment and costs constituted an infringement of Media 24's rights to properly litigate on issues it deemed necessary ([paras 7-14]).

R - Rules:
The SCA referred to several legal principles regarding amendments in pleadings:

  1. Bare Denial: A litigant may plead a simple denial when responding to allegations unless a special meaning or sting is asserted ([para 9]).
  2. Nature of Defamatory Claims: A defendant in a defamation claim does not need to justify all aspects of the article but only the allegations deemed defamatory ([para 8]).
  3. Amendments: The power of a court to refuse an amendment is constrained to considerations of prejudice or injustice to the opposing party, with a benign view taken toward amendments that do not specifically contravene rules of court ([paras 16-17]).
  4. Judicial Scrutiny: Obligations imposed in defamation cases must promote responsible journalism without unduly impeding media freedom ([para 20]).

These principles clarify the limitations of judicial interpretation over the content of pleadings and emphasize that the role of pleadings is to define the issues for a trial, thus upholding parties’ rights to determine their disputes ([para 18]).

A - Application:
In applying the established rules, the SCA rebuffed the High Court's assertions regarding the nature of Media 24’s denial as insufficient. The court underscored that the requirements for a plea involve stating material facts regarding the defenses to the particulars of claim rather than ascribing responsibility for the overall article. It noted that the High Court's judgment was premised on personal overreach regarding the article and its implications, rather than the specified claims made in the particulars ([paras 13-20]).

Moreover, the SCA criticized the High Court for presuming prejudice to the plaintiffs where the proposed amendments merely sought to provide clarity rather than substantive changes to the defenses offered. It emphasized that amendments designed to clarify denials ought not to face unnecessary barriers as this detracts from the efficient adjudication of disputes ([paras 14-19]).

The SCA concluded that the High Court had misapplied legal principles concerning defamation and media obligations, and thus the refusal of the amendment constituted a significant error ([paras 20-21]).

C - Conclusion:
The SCA upheld Media 24's appeal, concluding that the High Court's order was founded on erroneous legal principles and denied Media 24 the opportunity to present a fully articulated defense. The ruling reversed the High Court's order, granting leave to Media 24 to amend its plea within ten days. It also directed the respondents to bear the costs of the amendment application on an attorney-client scale, reflecting the Court's stance against the opportunistic objection raised by Nhleko and Mthembu ([para 22]).

This case reinforces the critical balance between media freedom and accountability while affirming the principles of fair litigation, particularly the necessity for courts to remain neutral arbiters of disputes grounded in factual pleadings, rather than allowing subjective interpretations to dictate procedural outcomes.
