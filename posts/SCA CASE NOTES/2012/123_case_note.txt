Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

### FIRAC Case Note: **Foize Africa (Pty) Ltd v Foize Beheer BV and Others** 

**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case Number:** 752/2011  
**Neutral Citation:** Foize Africa v Foize Beheer (752/2011) [2012] ZASCA 123 (20 September 2012)  
**Coram:** Mthiyane DP, Cloete, Heher, Shongwe, and Leach JJA

---

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–12])

The appellant, **Foize Africa (Pty) Ltd**, is a South African company that entered into a licensing agreement with the third respondent, **Foize Sales BV**, in December 2009 in Johannesburg. This agreement granted Foize Africa exclusive rights to sell and distribute the **"Foize on Mobile platform"** in specific territories, including South Africa, for five years until 6 December 2014 ([para 1]). 

The relationship became contentious when the second respondent, **Algemeen Beheer Nederland BV**, claimed that it held the intellectual property rights to the product and intended to market it in South Africa through two of its companies, identified as the seventh and ninth respondents ([paras 4-5]). This led the appellant to seek **interim relief** in the North Gauteng High Court in April 2011, aimed at compelling the respondents to adhere to the licensing agreement and specifically to prevent them from marketing the product in South Africa ([para 5]).

The respondents failed to file answering affidavits within the stipulated time and subsequently raised objections in limine, claiming that the appellant was bound by a foreign arbitration clause within the licensing agreement ([paras 7-9]). In a ruling by the court a quo, the objections were upheld, dismissing the applicant's claims and offering the basis that the South African courts lacked jurisdiction due to the agreement's arbitration clause ([para 9]).

The appellant, with leave from the court a quo, appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 9-10]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8-12, 19-21])

1. **Jurisdiction of South African Courts:** The primary issue was whether the South African courts had jurisdiction given the licensing agreement’s jurisdiction and arbitration clauses that mandated proceedings in Holland ([para 20]).
  
2. **Effectiveness of International Arbitration Clauses:** The court needed to determine if the arbitration clause effectively ousted the High Court's jurisdiction and whether the appellant could seek interim interdictory relief despite such clauses ([paras 19-21]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 21-28])

The court referenced several key principles regarding jurisdiction and arbitration clauses. Notably, it established that:

- **Principle of Non-Ousting Jurisdiction:** A foreign jurisdiction or arbitration clause does not preclude a court’s jurisdiction; parties cannot unilaterally exclude a court's jurisdiction through their agreement. Instead, disputes of this nature should be managed through a special plea to stay proceedings ([para 21]).
  
- **Discretionary Authority:** Courts retain discretion to enforce or stay proceedings in light of foreign clauses. Numerous precedents were cited, including *Butler v Banimar Shipping Co SA* and *Yorigami Maritime Construction Co Ltd v Nissho-Iwai Co Ltd*, where courts determined whether to enforce such clauses depending on the circumstances ([para 21]).

- **Criteria for Enforceability:** Factors influencing whether to enforce an arbitration clause include the location of evidence, potential prejudice to parties, and the convenience of litigation ([paras 27-28]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 25-34])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the court a quo erred in dismissing the appellant's application for an interdict based solely on the foreign jurisdiction clause in the licensing agreement. The court clarified that the mere presence of such clauses does not automatically remove the jurisdiction of local courts ([para 20]).

It suggested that the proper approach would have been to:

1. Recognize the high court's jurisdiction over the matter while assessing the implications of the arbitration clause.
2. Consider the specific facts and surrounding circumstances, including potential damages to the appellant and the nature of the relationship and agreements between the parties ([paras 27-30]).

The court concluded that sufficient grounds existed for interim relief since the appellant sufficiently exhibited a claim based on wrongful actions by the respondents, asserting threats to breach contractual obligations ([para 32]). 

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 34-35])

The Supreme Court of Appeal granted the appeal, setting aside the earlier ruling of the High Court and issuing an interim interdict against the first to sixth and eighth respondents. The holding emphasized that the High Court could have exercised jurisdiction, reflecting on the nature of the interim relief sought, which aimed to protect the appellant's rights under the licensing agreement ([para 34]).

The **ratio decidendi** of the ruling underlined the essential principle that arbitration clauses do not inherently oust jurisdiction. This decision has broader implications for international commercial contracts involving conflicting jurisdictional claims and emphasizes the judiciary's discretion in managing such disputes effectively ([para 34]).

In conclusion, the court directed the appellant to institute final action by 31 October 2012 while allowing an extension upon demonstration of good cause, effectively balancing the need for justice with procedural requirements ([para 35]).

--- 

This case highlights the delicate interplay between domestic legal authority and international commercial agreements, reaffirming that local courts play a critical role in adjudicating contractual disputes even when international arbitrational agreements are present.
