Centre for Child Law v T S and Others [2023] ZACC 22

[2] The applicant asserted that never-married parents must engage in a more cumbersome legal process—approaching a court for a two-pronged application, rather than merely filling out a form, as required of divorced parents to access Family Advocate services ([2]).

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 22

F – Facts: Background and Procedural History
[1] This case involves an application to confirm a declaration of constitutional invalidity concerning section 4 of the Mediation in Certain Divorce Matters Act 24 of 1987 ("the Act"), made by the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Johannesburg. The applicant, the Centre for Child Law (CCL), argued that the Act unfairly discriminated against never-married parents and their children by denying them the same access to the Office of the Family Advocate as that enjoyed by married or divorced parents ([1]).

[2] The applicant asserted that never-married parents must engage in a more cumbersome legal process—approaching a court for a two-pronged application, rather than merely filling out a form, as required of divorced parents to access Family Advocate services ([2]). This distinction, the applicant claimed, infringed on multiple constitutional rights, including the right to equality (section 9), dignity (section 10), and the best interests of the child (section 28(2)) ([3]).

[3] The applicant was represented by legal counsel from the CCL, while the respondents included Ms T S (first respondent), Mr B N (second respondent), and the Minister of Justice and Correctional Services (third respondent) ([4]). The High Court concluded that section 4 was inconsistent with the Constitution and declared it invalid, prompting the matter to be referred to the Constitutional Court for confirmation ([15]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions
[4] The principal legal issue was whether section 4 of the Mediation in Certain Divorce Matters Act unjustifiably discriminated against unmarried parents in violation of the rights protected under the Constitution ([16]).

[5] Secondary legal issues included:

  • Whether the differentiation in treatment of married versus unmarried parents regarding access to the Office of the Family Advocate could be justified and whether it was based on rational government purpose ([28]).
  • Examination of whether the High Court's reading-in remedy and the proposed amendments to the legislation were justifiable within constitutional limits ([60]) and if the discrimination established was indirect or direct ([39]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon
[6] The Constitutional Court applied numerous constitutional principles, notably:

  • Section 9(1) and 9(3) of the Constitution, which guarantees equality before the law and prohibits unfair discrimination on various grounds, including marital status ([25]).
  • Section 28(2), which asserts the primacy of the best interests of the child in all matters affecting them ([49]).

[7] Important case law included:

  • Harksen v Lane NO, which established a framework for assessing discrimination based on whether it is unfair and on a listed ground ([26]).
  • Bwanya v Master of the High Court, emphasizing the need to avoid entrenching specific family forms at the expense of all family dynamics ([34]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law
[8] The Court recognized that the legislation created a clear differentiation between never-married and married parents, thereby violating section 9(1) and 9(3) ([28]). It found that the Minister conceded that the Act served no legitimate governmental purpose that warranted such differentiated treatment ([30]). The differentiation against unmarried parents and their children in accessing beneficial services was acknowledged as discriminatory ([28]).

[9] In terms of remedies, the Court evaluated the High Court's reading-in approach as a temporary, workable solution to cure the constitutional defect while affording Parliament 24 months to amend the Act. This included an explicit right for non-married parents and others interested in child custody to access Family Advocate services through a simpler process akin to that available to divorced parents ([60]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications
[10] The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's order, declaring section 4 of the Mediation in Certain Divorce Matters Act unconstitutional as it precluded never-married parents from accessing Family Advocate services on equal terms with married parents undergoing divorce ([69]).

[11] The ratio decidendi emphasized that the legislative framework must evolve to reflect contemporary understandings of family structures in South Africa, ensuring equitable access to child custody and welfare processes. The Court's ruling thus mandates legislative reform, ensuring that all configurations of parentage are treated with equal legal regard ([69]).

[12] Ultimately, the decision reinforces constitutional commitments to equality and the best interests of children, while the proposed legislative adjustments will likely have broader societal implications, promoting inclusiveness and fairness in family law. The matter underlines the ongoing need to ensure that pre-Constitutional statutes align with constitutional values and standards ([63][68]).
