Constitutional Court

Ngaka Modiri Molema District Municipality v Chairperson, North West Provincial Executive Committee and Others [2014] ZACC 31

The High Court denied the temporary interdict application, concluding that the Municipality did not demonstrate substantial harm, as it is the residents who would suffer due to the disruption of service delivery ([8], [10]).

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 31

F – Facts:
The Ngaka Modiri Molema District Municipality (the applicant) sought to challenge the dissolution of its council by the North West Provincial Executive Council (the Province), which occurred on 3 September 2014, under section 139(1)(c) of the Constitution. In response to the dissolution, the Municipality approached the North West High Court seeking two forms of relief. Initially, in Part A, it sought a temporary interdict to prevent the newly appointed administrator, Mr. Nair (the seventh respondent), from interfering in the Municipality's affairs. In Part B, the Municipality sought the review and setting aside of the dissolution decision ([1–3]).

The High Court denied the temporary interdict application, concluding that the Municipality did not demonstrate substantial harm, as it is the residents who would suffer due to the disruption of service delivery ([8], [10]). The Municipality then sought leave to appeal this decision and requested direct access to the Constitutional Court under section 167(6)(a) of the Constitution, citing urgency in ensuring the continued provision of essential services like water and sanitation ([3], [4]).

The applicant alleged that the intervention by the Province resulted in significant disruptions to service delivery, affecting the communities it served. The High Court found that these concerns did not establish sufficient grounds for the requested urgent relief ([6], [7]).

I – Issues:
The primary legal questions arose from the Municipality's claims regarding its dissolution and the alleged violation of constitutional rights related to service delivery. Specifically, the Court had to consider:

  1. Whether the Municipality demonstrated urgency justifying direct access to the Constitutional Court ([4], [10]).
  2. Whether the grounds for the temporary interdict positioned against the administrator were valid, particularly in light of the public interest and urgency related to basic service delivery as mandated by the Constitution ([9], [13]).
  3. The implications of the dissolution under section 139(1)(c) and its impact on the rights of residents to essential services, specifically the right of access to water under section 27(1) of the Constitution ([14], [16]).

R – Rules:
The case invoked several constitutional provisions and precedents, particularly:

  • Section 139(1)(c) of the Constitution: Grants provincial governments the authority to intervene when municipalities cannot fulfill their obligations.
  • Sections 152 and 153 of the Constitution: Outline local government's objectives, including the provision of basic services.
  • Section 27(1) of the Constitution: Affirms the right of access to sufficient water, establishing a baseline for municipal service delivery.
  • Previous case law, including Joseph and Others v City of Johannesburg [2009] ZACC 30 and OUTA [2012] ZACC 18, which emphasize the importance of safeguarding people's rights in the context of service delivery by local government ([13], [14], [8]).

A – Application:
In its deliberation, the Constitutional Court upheld the High Court's rationale that the urgency faced by the Municipality was not about the loss of its council but centered on the need for uninterrupted service delivery to residents. The Court reiterated that the core of the public interest lay in addressing the service disruptions impacting the community rather than reinstating the Municipality's council ([10], [12]).

The Court highlighted that the administrator, under the governance structure established by the Province, had the necessary authority to address the urgent service delivery needs, citing that harm to the Municipality or its councillors did not equate to irreparable harm to the communities affected ([10], [23]). This evaluation affirmed that while local government bodies have rights, the pressing constitutional obligations to provide basic services to residents supersede bureaucratic concerns ([18]).

C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court dismissed the Municipality's applications for both leave to appeal and direct access, concluding that the need for a judicial intervention by the Court was obviated by the urgent and ongoing efforts of the administrator to restore services. The ruling underscored that the interests of justice were better served by allowing the matter to be adjudicated in the lower court first, maintaining the legal framework established for municipal governance under exceptional circumstances outlined in section 139.

In summary, the decision recognized the paramount importance of ensuring continuous service provision as a constitutional obligation while balancing the judicial intervention's role concerning local governance disputes ([24]). This ruling establishes a notable precedent regarding the interplay between municipal autonomy and provincial intervention, affirming that constitutional imperatives prioritize public service over institutional legalistic disputes.
