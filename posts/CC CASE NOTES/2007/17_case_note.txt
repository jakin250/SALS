Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2007

## FIRAC Case Note: Michael Hermann Armbruster & Others v Minister of Finance & Others

### F – Facts

This case arose from the seizure of foreign currency in possession of Mr. Michael Hermann Armbruster at O R Tambo International Airport on 18 June 2004, amounting to a total rand value of R102,675.65 ([para 3]). The currency was seized by a customs official, Mr. Collen Khoza, who noted that Armbruster could not provide proof from an authorized dealer to justify the possession of such a large amount of foreign currency ([para 4]). Armbruster subsequently explained that his intention was to transfer the money to establish a business in the United Arab Emirates ([para 5]). The Treasury cited violations of the Exchange Control Regulations, specifically Regulations 3(1), 3(5), and 3(8), as grounds for potential forfeiture of the currency [6]. 

Following the seizure, Armbruster submitted written representations claiming the money was lawfully earned and expressing ignorance of the relevant regulations [8]. The Treasury declined to return the currency, asserting that there had been significant contraventions of the regulations ([para 9]). The applicants then sought a review of this decision in the Pretoria High Court, which ultimately upheld the Treasury's decision ([para 15]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues that emerged from the case included:

1. Whether the Treasury’s decision to forfeit the currency violated the constitutional rights of the applicants under sections 25(1), 34, and 165(1) of the Constitution of South Africa ([para 20]).
2. Whether Regulation 3(5) of the Exchange Control Regulations was constitutional, particularly in how it permitted the forfeiture of property without clear guidelines ([paras 24–27]).
3. The interpretation of the timing of forfeiture – whether it occurs immediately upon seizure or only after a decision by the Treasury ([para 39]).

### R – Rules

The case was set against the backdrop of the legislative framework established by the Currency and Exchanges Act of 1933, particularly sections 9 and the Exchange Control Regulations promulgated under its authority ([paras 10–12]). Regulation 3(1) prohibits the unlawful export of foreign currency without appropriate permission from the Treasury, while Regulation 3(5) states that foreign currency seized shall be forfeited to the National Revenue Fund, subject to the discretion of the Treasury to return any part of it ([paras 12–38]). 

Additionally, the court considered the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) regarding the review of administrative actions and the standards of fairness inherent in such reviews ([para 16]).

### A – Application

The Constitutional Court applied the aforementioned legal principles in evaluating whether the Treasury’s actions met the tests of legality and proportionality outlined in the Constitution. It concluded that the deprivation of property could not be deemed arbitrary given the contextual justifications provided by the Treasury ([para 72]). The court placed significant emphasis on the need for procedural fairness, concluding that affected individuals must be given an opportunity to make representations before forfeiture occurs ([para 45]). 

Critically, the court found that forfeiture does not occur immediately upon seizure but rather follows a decision made by the Treasury regarding the appropriateness of returning the seized currency ([para 46]). This interpretation allowed the Treasury to exercise discretion in mitigating undue hardship, aligning with the values enshrined in the Constitution ([para 49]). 

The court also addressed concerns regarding the lack of guidelines surrounding the Treasury’s discretion, asserting that the discretion must still be exercised within the broad framework of law, thereby safeguarding against arbitrary outcomes ([para 80]).

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court dismissed the appeal, affirming the constitutionality of Regulation 3(5) and the Treasury’s guidelines for the seizure and forfeiture of foreign currency. The court concluded that both sections 34 and 165 of the Constitution were not violated, and the Treasury retained lawful authority to enforce the regulations ([para 83]).

The ruling underscores the delicate balance between enforcing regulatory compliance regarding exchange controls and upholding individual constitutional rights, which remains a critical consideration within South Africa's legal landscape. Additionally, the decision highlights the necessity for administrative bodies to exercise discretion judiciously, reinforcing the importance of procedural fairness within the regulatory framework.
