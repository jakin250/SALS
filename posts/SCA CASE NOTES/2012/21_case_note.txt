Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Mokala Beleggings (Pty) Ltd & another v Minister of Rural Development and Land Reform and others [2012] ZASCA 21

### F – Facts
This case arises from a land claim in South Africa concerning property owned by the appellants, Mokala Beleggings (Pty) Ltd and Willem Hendrik Steyn Snyman ([para 1]). The appellants were the registered owners of fixed property that was subject to a land claim, which they conceded, leading to an agreement to sell the property to the State under two separate sale agreements signed on 29 January 2009. The agreements stipulated that the State, designated as the purchaser, was required to appoint conveyancers and that transfer of the properties was to be effected within two months from the signatures ([para 2]). 

The conveyancers, however, were instructed to delay the lodgment of transfer documents, attributing this to a lack of funding in the department’s budget ([para 3]). As a result of these delays, the appellants sent letters of demand, which prompted a partial payment of the purchase price. The Land Claims Court (LCC) subsequently denied the appellants' plea for mora interest, leading to this appeal ([para 4]).

### I – Issues
The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether a purchaser (the State) who deliberately delays payment and transfer can be held liable for mora interest ([para 1]). The court also needed to determine the nature of the appellants' claims based on the principles of mora ex re and mora ex persona ([para 5]).

### R – Rules
Mora, in legal terms, refers to delay or default in contractual obligations. Two types of mora were discussed in this case: mora ex re, which arises directly from the terms of a contract, and mora ex persona, where a debtor is placed in mora through a demand by the creditor ([para 6]). 

The established rule delineates that a debtor in default may be liable for compensation for loss resulting from delayed performance, generally represented through interest on owed amounts ([para 6]). Previous case law, including *Linton v Corser* and *Bellairs v Hodnett*, provided foundational legal principles regarding mora interest and the conditions under which it may be awarded ([paras 1 and 6]).

### A – Application
The court found that the LCC had misinterpreted clause 5.2 of the agreements regarding the timing for transfer, mischaracterizing it as “pro non scripto” ([para 7]). Instead, the clauses necessitated an interpretation that could support a claim for mora ex re based on the obligation to transfer in a timely manner.

The court then considered the written notices from the appellants to the Department, which placed the State in mora. The LCC's conclusion that the notices were ineffectively dispatched because they were addressed to the community rather than the department was rejected. It was determined that the department had effectively received the notices, which were clear in setting out its obligations regarding the transfer of property ([para 10-12]).

Due to the State's admitted financial constraints—indicating a deliberate delay in fulfilling its contractual obligations—the court established that the appellants were not required to demonstrate fault from the State; instead, the unequivocal delay warranted the claim for mora interest beginning from the date of dispatch of the letters ([paras 13-14]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appellants were entitled to mora interest. The court set aside the LCC's decision and awarded mora interest to the appellants at a rate of 15.5% per annum on specified amounts from planned dates, reflecting the financial losses incurred as a result of the delay ([paras 17, 3]). This ruling underscores the importance of enforcing timely contractual obligations, especially in the context of land reform in South Africa, reinforcing the notion that public institutions must adhere to their financial commitments to restore land rights as mandated by law ([para 16]).

In essence, the court's judgment not only rectified the lower court's misjudgment but also fortified the principles of accountability and timely performance expected from state entities in the land reform context. This has significant implications for future transactions involving governmental bodies and highlights the courts' role in ensuring that financial delays do not undermine the rights of private individuals and communities entitled to restitution.
