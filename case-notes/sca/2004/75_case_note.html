Supreme Court of Appeal

Swire Pacific Offshore Services (PTE) Ltd v MV ‘Roxana Bank’

The appellant claimed it was the "operator" of the Pacific Lance and that its master and crew acted within the scope of their employment or as seconded employees by Swire Pacific Ship Management Ltd.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2004

F – Facts

On the morning of 25 January 2002, the MV Roxana Bank suffered mechanical failures that caused it to drag its anchor while anchored off Mossel Bay, South Africa. As the vessel was drifting dangerously close to a submarine oil pipeline, a pilot onboard requested assistance from the nearby MV Pacific Lance. The Pacific Lance responded by taking the Roxana Bank under tow out to sea to prevent a potentially disastrous incident. Following this, the appellant, Swire Pacific Offshore Services (PTE) Ltd, initiated proceedings in rem against the MV Roxana Bank and its cargo, claiming a salvage reward of R1,000,000 along with interest and costs, which fell under the definition of a maritime claim as enumerated in paragraph (k) of the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983 ([1]).

The appellant claimed it was the "operator" of the Pacific Lance and that its master and crew acted within the scope of their employment or as seconded employees by Swire Pacific Ship Management Ltd. However, evidence indicated that while the appellant had effective control over the vessel, it was neither the legal owner nor charterer and did not employ the master and crew of the Pacific Lance ([2]). Consequently, the court a quo granted the respondents’ application for absolution from the instance, opining that the appellant failed to demonstrate the requisite locus standi for claiming a salvage reward. The appellant subsequently appealed with leave from the court a quo ([2]).

I – Issues

The primary issue on appeal was whether the evidence presented was sufficient to establish a legal relationship between the appellant and the Pacific Lance that would justify a salvage award. The particular question arose from the respondents' concession that, although the evidence suggested the salvage services were rendered, the appellant had not sufficiently established its entitlement to a salvage reward ([3]). The more generalized issue involves whether there exists a closed list of categories of persons entitled to a salvage reward under applicable law, as previously asserted by the court a quo ([9]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal framework in this case is primarily based on English law as it existed on 1 November 1983, as mandated by section 6 of the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983, subject to South African statutory provisions like the Wrecks and Salvage Act 94 of 1996 ([8]). Existing legal precedents establish that the owner of a salving vessel is entitled to pursue a salvage reward, even when the vessel is under a time charter. It is recognized that the demise charterer can claim such rewards as they effectively assume ownership-related rights and risks during the charter ([11], [12]). Further commentary in maritime law indicates that the categories eligible for salvage remuneration are not rigidly fixed, as demonstrated in Brice on Maritime Law of Salvage, asserting the principle that any recognized volunteer rendering salvage services may claim recompense ([10]).

A – Application

The court examined the relationship between the appellant and the Pacific Lance, particularly through the testimony of Mr. Brian Townsley, a director within the group. His evidence suggested that while no formal written agreement existed between the appellant and Swire Marine Corporation Ltd (the owner of the Pacific Lance), the appellant effectively controlled the vessel's operations, acting as though it was the owner ([5], [13]). Furthermore, the agreement with Soekor E and P (Pty) Ltd referred to the appellant as "owner", with provisions related to the vessel's operation under its guidance ([6]).

The court noted that although the master and crew of the Pacific Lance were technically employed by Swire Pacific Ship Management Ltd, directions from the appellant to the master reflected an operational control indicative of an agency relationship or possibly a tacit agreement between the parties ([7], [13]). The risks of loss, as outlined in the Soekor agreement, indicated that the appellant could incur financial damage if the vessel were lost or damaged during salvage efforts ([14]).

On these grounds, the court determined that the appellant had successfully established a reasonable basis for its claim to a salvage reward. The evidential claims that the appellant bore the risks associated with the vessel during the salvage operation led to the conclusion that the appellant was indeed in a position to exert control over the Pacific Lance, thus justifying its claim ([15]).

Moreover, the argument raised by the respondents—that the appellant's particulars of claim were inconsistent with the evidential basis for the salvage claim—was dismissed by the court. It reasoned that the core of the appellant's claims was always linked to the vessel's salvage operations and did not negate the operational control that was exercised ([16]).

C – Conclusion

The appeal was upheld, leading to the original ruling of absolution from the instance being set aside. The Supreme Court of Appeal reaffirmed that there is, in fact, no closed list of individuals or entities entitled to a salvage reward, allowing for flexibility in recognizing various parties who may effectively exert control over salvaging operations. The ruling thereby broadens the scope of who may seek a salvage reward, emphasizing the importance of practical control and economic responsibility over the salving vessel. This case signals a significant interpretation in maritime law that may impact future claims for salvage rewards within South African jurisdiction and potentially align it more with contemporary maritime practices ([17]).
