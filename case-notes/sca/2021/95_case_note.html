Supreme Court of Appeal

Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs v De Beer and Another [2021] ZASCA 95

[2] The respondents' application, filed in May 2020, demanded the invalidation of regulations stemming from the national state of disaster declared in response to COVID-19.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 95

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History

[1] This case arises from an appeal against a decision made by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria. The High Court declared nearly all regulations promulgated by the Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (the Minister) under section 27(2) of the Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002 (the Act), unconstitutional and invalid. The first respondent, Mr. Reyno Dawid de Beer, alongside the second respondent, Liberty Fighters Network (LFN), challenged these regulations, asserting they violated various constitutional rights. Additionally, the Hola Bon Renaissance Foundation and the Council for the Advancement of the South African Constitution participated as amici curiae.

[2] The respondents' application, filed in May 2020, demanded the invalidation of regulations stemming from the national state of disaster declared in response to COVID-19. Hyperbolic assertions of irrationality were made, alongside claims that the regulations exceeded legislative competencies, violated rights enshrined in the Bill of Rights, and neglected socio-economic contexts. Notably, the regulations instituted severe restrictions on movement and gatherings.

[4] The High Court ruled on these regulations on 2 June 2020, instituting a broad ban on the regulations while permitting the Minister 14 days to revise and reissue them. Following the Minister's appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal was tasked with addressing the validity of the High Court’s comprehensive dismissal of the regulations.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

[5] Central to this case are several pivotal legal issues:

  1. Whether the respondents sufficiently pleaded their case against the regulations, specifically with regard to constitutional violations.
  2. Whether the High Court adequately applied the rationality test when evaluating the regulations and whether it acted beyond the bounds of its jurisdiction by invalidating regulations that were not properly placed before it.
  3. Whether the regulations constituted a disproportionate limitation on the rights guaranteed under the South African Constitution.
  4. Whether the procedural requirements for a declaration of invalidity were satisfied.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon

[6] This case hinges primarily on the South African constitutional framework, particularly the sections of the Constitution concerning the Bill of Rights (sections 9–36) and the regulatory powers conferred upon the Minister under section 27 of the Disaster Management Act. The principles of rationality and legality in administrative action are governed by the Constitutional Court's precedents, notably Democratic Alliance v President of South Africa and Minister of Home Affairs and Others v Scalabrini Centre, Cape Town and Others.

[8] The court also referenced the procedural duties outlined in Uniform Rule 53 regarding the transparency and accountability of administrative decisions. The expectations of precision in constitutional pleadings and challenges were drawn from decisions such as Public Servants Association obo Ubogu v Head of Department of Health, Gauteng and Others.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

[10] The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the respondents' challenge, emphasizing their failure to plead the constitutional attack with specific clarity. The court noted that the broad and generalized nature of the claims resulted in a lack of underpinning evidence required to substantiate an infringement of rights.

[12] The court articulated the need for distinct articulation of the respondent's grievances against specific regulations, including which exact rights were impaired and how the regulations effectively caused those injuries. The broad assertions failed to meet the specificity required for constitutional challenges, rendering the heretofore discerned harm non-cognizable.

[13] In examining the High Court's ruling, the Supreme Court of Appeal recognized that the lower court had overstepped its division of powers by invalidating a regulatory framework without following due considerations. The lack of proper opportunity for the Minister to respond to the claims related to the undertaken alterations meant the alleged harm could not be adequately addressed or remedied.

[15] The Court highlighted errors in the High Court's application of the rationality principle, indicating that it drew erroneous conclusions by suggesting alternative measures without addressing the link between the regulations and their intended purpose, which according to the Minister was to mitigate the spread of COVID-19.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

[16] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, concluding that the respondents’ case against the regulations was insubstantively pleaded. The High Court's ruling to invalidate the regulations was set aside, mandating that the application be dismissed entirely. This decision reaffirmed that to mount an effective constitutional challenge, plaintiffs must clearly articulate allegations, supported by admissible evidence.

[18] The Court signaled a decisive move against a vague and broad application of constitutional rights violations and set a precedent emphasizing the necessity of clarity and specificity in constitutional pleadings. The judgment further underscored the judiciary's role in ensuring a balanced application of laws during emergencies, preventing overreach, and maintaining well-defined separations of powers.

In summary, this case serves as a vital reference point for future constitutional challenges, particularly those involving government regulations enacted under extraordinary circumstances such as a health crisis. It also demonstrates the importance of structured legal frameworks and procedural adherence in constitutional litigation.
