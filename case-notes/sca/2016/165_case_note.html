FIRAC Case Note: University of the Free State v Afriforum & Another [2016] ZASCA 165

F – Facts ([paras 1–3])

The appeal concerns the University of the Free State (UFS), which sought to overturn a ruling made by the Full Court of the Free State Division of the High Court on July 21, 2016. The Full Court had ordered that the UFS's decision to adopt a new multilingual language policy, which made English the primary medium of instruction (with Afrikaans and Sesotho as supplementary languages in certain faculties), should not be suspended pending appeal. This was a significant shift from the previous parallel medium instruction policy established in 2003. In response to the UFS's policy change on March 11, 2016, the first respondent, Afriforum, a non-profit organization, sought a review of the UFS's decision. Following the Full Court's judgment, the UFS applied for direct leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, which was denied. Instead, Afriforum approached the Full Court for an order to implement its judgment pending the UFS's appeal.

I – Issues ([paras 1–3])

The key legal issues in this case revolve around the interpretation and application of section 18 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 (the Act). Specifically, the issues included:

  1. Whether exceptional circumstances existed to justify the Full Court's order to implement its decision pending appeal.
  2. Whether Afriforum could demonstrate irreparable harm if the order was not granted, as required by section 18(3) of the Act.
  3. Whether UFS would suffer irreparable harm if Afriforum's application was granted.

R – Rules ([paras 5–10])

Section 18 of the Superior Courts Act stipulates the conditions under which an order may be executed pending appeal:

  1. Subsection (1): Generally suspends the operation of a decision pending appeal unless a court orders otherwise under exceptional circumstances.
  2. Subsection (3): Requires the applicant to prove on a balance of probabilities that they will suffer irreparable harm if the order is not granted, and that the other party will not suffer irreparable harm if the order is made.

The common law principles established in South Cape Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Engineering Management Services (Pty) Ltd [1977] (3) SA 534 (A) serve as a foundation for understanding these statutory requirements, emphasizing the necessity of demonstrating substantial harm to justify overriding the automatic suspension of a judgment pending appeal.

A – Application ([paras 16–31])

The court examined the arguments presented by Afriforum to establish exceptional circumstances. The court found that Afriforum’s claims regarding the uncertainty faced by prospective Afrikaans-speaking students were overstated. With only 386 prospective students potentially affected—a mere 1 to 2 percent of UFS's student body—the court held that Afriforum had not provided sufficient evidence that any student would indeed suffer irreparable harm as a result of the implementation of the new language policy. The evidentiary basis for their claims was lacking; no concrete instances of harm or adverse consequences were demonstrated.

The court also evaluated UFS's argument regarding the potential harm it could suffer if implementation of the new policy was delayed. UFS presented evidence of substantial planning, human, and financial resources already allocated to the new language policy, bolstering its claim that halting implementation would result in irreversible harm to its operations and public interest.

Ultimately, the court found Afriforum’s failure to prove exceptional circumstances and irreparable harm rendered its application meritless, concluding that the procedural and substantive requirements of section 18(3) had not been met.

C – Conclusion ([paras 32–33])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the UFS's appeal, concluding that Afriforum failed to demonstrate the required exceptional circumstances or the irreparable harm necessary to justify an order. The Full Court’s order was set aside, and the application by Afriforum was dismissed with costs. This ruling underscores the court's strict interpretation of section 18's requirements, reinforcing the notion that deviation from the principle of automatic suspension pending appeal must be firmly substantiated. The implications also clarify the threshold for future applications under section 18, highlighting the need for concrete evidence of exceptional circumstances and the careful balancing of harm and circumstance in matters involving public institutions and policy changes.
