# FIRAC Case Note: Die Staat teen Julies (CCT 7/96)

## F – Facts
This case originated in the Cape Provincial Division of the High Court and was referred to the Constitutional Court concerning the constitutionality of Section 21(1)(a)(iii) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act No. 140 of 1992 (the "Act") ([1]). The accused, Julies, was convicted of dealing in three metacaloone tablets (commonly known as Mandrax), a substance identified as undesirable and dependency-forming, contrary to the stipulated provisions of the Act ([2]). 

The provision in question presumes that a person found in possession of an undesirable dependency-forming substance is dealing with it unless proven otherwise ([2]). This legal presumption is critical, as the referring court and the Western Cape Director of Public Prosecutions had no doubts regarding the probable unconstitutionality of the provision and sought clarity from the Constitutional Court, which holds exclusive jurisdiction to declare legislation unconstitutional ([1]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue confronting the court was whether Section 21(1)(a)(iii) of the Act contravenes the rights enshrined in the Interim Constitution, particularly the right to a fair trial, which includes the presumption of innocence and the right to remain silent as set out in Section 25(3)(c) ([1]). The case emerged primarily from concerns about the constitutionally permissible limits related to legal presumptions of dealing in drugs based on mere possession.

## R – Rules
The court referenced previous rulings, notably the case of *S v Bhulwana; S v Gwadiso*, which elucidated the conflict between presumptions of guilt in drug-related offences and the right to a fair trial ([3]). The Court reaffirmed that the presumption of innocence, as delineated in Section 25(3)(c) of the Interim Constitution, must not be undermined by legislative provisions. The principle laid down in *S v Bhulwana; S v Gwadiso* stressed that presuming someone to be guilty based solely on possession of a substance violates fundamental rights ([4]). The Court also considered statutory provisions ensuring that any deprivation of rights must be justifiable under Section 33(1) of the Interim Constitution ([3][4]).

## A – Application
The Constitutional Court applied the reasoning established in prior judgments around the principle of the presumption of innocence ([3][4]). It concluded that the mere possession of a relatively small quantity of an undesirable dependency-forming substance, such as half a Mandrax tablet, could not reasonably indicate an intent to deal. The court emphasized that possession alone, without accompanying factors indicating intent for dealing, constitutes an insufficient basis to trigger the presumption outlined in Section 21(1)(a)(iii).

The court found that the provision imposed an untenable burden on the accused, compelling them to prove their innocence, which is contrary to the constitutional guarantee of a fair trial as expressed in Section 25. The reasoning posited that all individuals must be presumed innocent until proven guilty in a court of law, underscoring that the mere possession of a minor amount of a controlled substance does not logically support an inference of dealing ([3][4]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately held that the provisions of Section 21(1)(a)(iii) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act No. 140 of 1992 were unconstitutional and invalid. The court declared that this invalidity would take effect as of the date of the judgment, thus ensuring that any application of this provision in criminal proceedings where conviction occurred post-establishment of the Constitution, but where an appeal or review was pending, would also be impacted ([5]). 

The ruling highlights the delicate balance between societal interests in controlling drug use and trafficking against the imperative of safeguarding individual rights within the criminal justice system. This case serves as a pivotal point in affirming the constitutional rights of individuals against broad legislative presumptions regarding possession and intent in drug-related cases. The decision reaffirms the supremacy of constitutional rights over statutory provisions that contravene fundamental protections ([5]).
