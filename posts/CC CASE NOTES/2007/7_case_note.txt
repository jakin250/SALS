Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2007

# Case Note: The Road Accident Fund v Vusumzi Mdeyide and Minister of Transport

**Court**: Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number**: CCT 70/06  
**Heard On**: 27 February 2007  
**Decided On**: 4 April 2007  
**Judgment by**: Navsa AJ  

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
This case arose from a High Court ruling on 3 October 2006, where section 23(1) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 was declared unconstitutional. The High Court had ruled that the provision infringed on the rights of individuals seeking access to courts, as it placed a strict limitation on the time after which claims could be made (subparagraphs [1]–[2]). 

The respondent, Vusumzi Mdeyide, was a blind and illiterate individual who had been struck by a vehicle on 8 March 1999. After the incident, he had no independent recollection of the accident and was unable to manage his own affairs ([5]–[6]). His attorney, Mr. Niehaus, lodged a claim on behalf of Mdeyide three years and three days after the accident, which the Road Accident Fund (RAF) argued had prescribed under section 23(1) of the Act, which prescribed claims after three years unless specific conditions were met ([11]–[12]).

The High Court found that section 23(1) does not account for circumstances wherein claimants, like Mdeyide, might be ignorant of the law or incapacitated, which infringed upon their constitutional right of access to the courts, particularly in light of section 39(2) of the Constitution ([24]–[26]).

The RAF appealed this ruling, contesting both the High Court's constitutional analysis and the failure to consider the Minister of Transport as an interested party due to the Act’s administration ([2]–[4]; [27]). 

### I – Issues
1. **Constitutionality of Section 23(1)**: Whether section 23(1) of the Road Accident Fund Act is consistent with the Constitution, particularly concerning access to courts ([24]–[26]).
2. **Prescriptive Period**: Whether the claim had prescribed based on the provisions of section 23(1) and the Prescription Act.
3. **Plaintiff’s Capacity**: Whether Mdeyide had the capacity to initiate and pursue legal action regarding his injury ([22]).

### R – Rules
1. **Constitutional Context**: Section 39(2) of the Constitution requires that courts promote the spirit and objects of the Bill of Rights ([24]).
2. **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**: Section 12(3) allows for the non-completion of prescription until a creditor has knowledge of the debtor's identity and the facts giving rise to the claim. Section 23(1) of the Road Accident Fund Act does not accommodate for contextual variables that may affect a claimant's awareness of their legal position ([14]).
3. **Common Law Principles**: The common law principle that underlines the necessity of a curator for individuals who lack the legal capacity to manage their affairs.

### A – Application
The Constitutional Court analyzed the arguments surrounding the constitutionality of section 23(1). It examined how strict time limits—which did not consider an individual’s capacity—could lead to a denial of justice for vulnerable individuals like Mdeyide ([24]–[26]). 

The Court emphasized that section 23(1) barred access to justice for those who were unaware or unable to manage their legal affairs due to incapacitation or ignorance of their rights. Hence, the provision was held to infringe upon the constitutional principle of fair access to legal remedy ([25]–[26]).

Addressing the claim's prescription, the Court agreed with the High Court's observation that it failed to consider the implications of the plaintiff's incapacity and lack of awareness at the relevant time. This failure undermined the notion of justice when applied too rigidly in cases of serious disabilities ([35]–[37]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately held that the High Court's order of constitutional invalidity was not to be confirmed due to the premature consideration of the constitutionality of section 23(1). The Court mandated a further inquiry into Mdeyide's mental capacity, suggesting a need for evidence from expert witnesses. The matter was remitted back to the High Court for proper examination of the plaintiff's capacity in light of the procedural inadequacies at the trial level ([48]).

The implications of this case underscore the significance of ensuring that legislation does not infringe upon individuals' rights, especially for those unable to navigate the legal system due to mental or physical barriers. It also reiterated the utility of the Prescription Act's provisions in protecting vulnerable claimants while balancing the needs of the legal system against the interests of justice. Consequently, this case offers a roadmap for future claims involving individuals with similar disabilities, ensuring they receive due consideration in the eyes of the law.
