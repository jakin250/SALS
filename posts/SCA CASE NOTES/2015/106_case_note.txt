Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Mandla Sithole v The State (20393/2014) [2015] ZASCA 106

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1]–[4])

The case centers around Mandla Sithole, who was convicted of two counts of murder in the Regional Court at Standerton, Mpumalanga, following a traffic incident that resulted in the deaths of Ms. Jeaneth Nontlantla Ngema and Mr. Vusimuzi Petrus Ngema on 4 April 2009 ([2]). After a trial that included evidence from both the State and the appellant, Sithole was sentenced to eight years’ imprisonment on each count, with the sentences running concurrently ([3]).

Subsequent to his conviction, Sithole sought leave to appeal against both the conviction and the sentence. On 18 April 2012, his application was partially granted; he was permitted to appeal only regarding the sentence, not the conviction ([4]). Discontented with this outcome, Sithole lodged a petition in the High Court on 3 November 2012 requesting leave to appeal the refusal concerning his convictions. The High Court ultimately granted him leave to appeal against this refusal on 19 July 2013, establishing the jurisdiction for this appeal (pursuant to the provisions of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013) ([4]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([5])

The primary legal issue posed before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the High Court was correct in refusing to grant leave for Sithole to appeal against his murder convictions. This necessitated a determination of whether there existed reasonable prospects of success for such an appeal ([5]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([6])

In determining the appeal's outcome, the Court considered established jurisprudence surrounding the granting of leave to appeal in criminal matters. Reference was made to precedents such as *S v Khoasasa* ([2003] 1 SACR 123 (SCA), paras 14 and 19–22) and *S v Tonkin* ([2014] 1 SACR 583 (SCA)), which articulate the principles governing leave to appeal in South African law. The Court applied these principles to assess whether the appellant had demonstrated sufficient prospects of success regarding his challenge against the convictions ([6]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([6])

In its deliberations, the SCA considered the counsel’s submission that Sithole possessed reasonable prospects of success on appeal concerning the murder convictions. The State, recognizing this assertion, made a concession in favor of the appellant's claim, affirming that there were indeed arguable grounds for an appeal on its merits ([6]). This concession by the State significantly influenced the Court's decision, underlining the importance of the trial's previous handling and potential for error in convicting the appellant on both murder counts.

Given the acknowledgment by both parties of the appellant’s reasonable prospects of success, the SCA found that the High Court's refusal to grant leave to appeal could not stand. This understanding led to the Court's decision to uphold the appeal and overturn the High Court's order ([6]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([6])

The SCA ultimately upheld the appeal, reversing the High Court’s order and granting Sithole leave to appeal his convictions of two counts of murder. The case was remitted to the Gauteng Division of the High Court to revisit the merits of the appeal ([6]). 

The ratio decidendi of this case emphasizes the legal principle that a mere concession from the State regarding the prospects of appeal can significantly affect the judicial determination of whether leave to appeal should be granted. This case further illustrates the judiciary's commitment to ensuring that individuals are afforded the opportunity for fair appeal in criminal matters, aligning with the constitutional mandate for justice and due process within South Africa’s legal system.

The implications of the decision resonate not only for the appellant but also set a precedent regarding the treatment of similar appeals concerning the refusal of leave to appeal, reinforcing a judicial outlook that favors thorough consideration of an accused's rights in the criminal process.
