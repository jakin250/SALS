Supreme Court of Appeal

Msimango v The State (698/2017) [2017] ZASCA 181

The appellant, Mandla Msimango, was convicted in a regional court of robbery with aggravating circumstances and attempted murder.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 181

F - Facts

The appellant, Mandla Msimango, was convicted in a regional court of robbery with aggravating circumstances and attempted murder. The robbery charge was based on the use of a firearm, as per section 1 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA), and was to be read in conjunction with section 51(2) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (CLAA), which prescribes a minimum sentence of fifteen years unless substantial and compelling circumstances justified a lesser sentence ([paras 1–3]).

The prosecution presented evidence that Msimango, alongside an accomplice referred to as Simphiwe, attacked Mr. Samuel Marumenya and others on 18 May 2008, exploiting a backdrop of xenophobia in Johannesburg. They threatened the victims with violence, physically assaulted them, and Msimango allegedly shot Mr. Dixon Kasinga in the mouth during the altercation. Post-trial, the regional magistrate sentenced Msimango to 20 years for the robbery and 5 years for the attempted murder, with the sentences running concurrently, resulting in an effective term of 20 years ([paras 2–4]).

Msimango appealed against both his convictions and the severity of his sentences. The South Gauteng High Court dismissed the conviction appeal but ordered the sentences to run concurrently, allowing Msimango to petition the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) after being granted special leave ([paras 3–5]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issues at hand included:

  1. Conviction Based on Common Purpose: Whether the application of the doctrine of common purpose in convicting Msimango for attempted murder was legally justified given that it was not included in the charge sheet or formally presented during the trial ([para 14]).
  2. Appropriateness of Sentence: Whether the effective sentence of 20 years' imprisonment for robbery with aggravating circumstances remained appropriate after the conviction for attempted murder (count 3) was overturned ([para 19]).

R - Rules

The Supreme Court of Appeal considered various legal principles:

  1. Right to a Fair Trial: Section 35(3)(a) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa mandates that every accused be informed of the charges with sufficient specificity to prepare a defense. This includes the prohibition against trial by ambush, which the invocation of common purpose against Msimango violated ([paras 15–16]).
  2. Minimum Sentence Legislation: Under section 51(2) of the CLAA, a minimum sentence of 15 years is prescribed if no substantial and compelling circumstances exist. The court emphasized that any departure from this minimum must be reasoned ([para 24]).
  3. Importance of Judicial Discretion and Transparency: Judicial officers are required to provide reasons for their decisions, especially in sentencing, to ensure accountability and transparency ([para 25]).

A - Application

The SCA found merit in Msimango’s appeal regarding count 3, as the regional magistrate improperly applied the doctrine of common purpose without it being raised in the charge or tested in evidence, violating the provisions of the CPA and the Constitution ([para 18]). Consequently, the court set aside the conviction and sentencing related to both count 3 ([paras 3, 4]).

Regarding the sentence for count 1, the SCA noted that the regional magistrate's sentencing of 20 years was influenced by a conviction on count 3, which was now invalid. The court examined whether the remaining sentence was disproportionate to the crime and if substantial and compelling circumstances existed. Here, Msimango's personal circumstances—his age, parental responsibilities, and time served pre-trial—were discussed as mitigating factors, emphasizing fairness in sentencing ([paras 19, 26]).

Moreover, the evidence of Msimango's violent actions during the robbery, including the extortion of money from victims during a period of xenophobic attacks, was highlighted as aggravating conduct. However, the court concluded that a revised sentence of 13 years was more proportionate given the circumstances ([paras 20–22, 27]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the conviction on count 1 but upheld the conviction for robbery with aggravating circumstances, modifying the sentence from 20 years to 13 years. The conviction regarding attempted murder (count 3) was set aside due to improper application of the common purpose doctrine ([paras 1–4]). This case underlines the crucial balance between ensuring justice through the application of the law and adhering strictly to procedural protections that uphold the constitutional rights of individuals within the criminal justice system. The ruling serves as a reminder of the necessity for courts to ensure clarity in charges and fairness in the application of justice, reflecting the ideals embodied in the South African Constitution.
