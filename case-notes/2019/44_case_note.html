Minister of Justice and Correctional Services v Ramuhovhi and Others [2019] ZACC 44

The case Minister of Justice and Correctional Services v Ramuhovhi and Others arose from a procedural follow-up to a previous decision by the Constitutional Court, known as Ramuhovhi I.

Constitutional Court 2019 [2019] ZACC 44

F – Facts

The case Minister of Justice and Correctional Services v Ramuhovhi and Others arose from a procedural follow-up to a previous decision by the Constitutional Court, known as Ramuhovhi I. In this earlier case, the Court had determined that section 7(1) of the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 unjustifiably limited the rights to dignity and non-discrimination, leading to its declaration of constitutional invalidity ([1]–[2]). The Court suspended this declaration for 24 months to allow Parliament the opportunity to amend the statutory framework governing polygamous customary marriages ([2]). The Court also outlined specific interim arrangements for the management and control of marital property during this suspension period ([2]).

As the deadline approached for this statutory amendment, on 15 October 2019, the Minister of Justice and Correctional Services sought an extension of the suspension period by an additional 12 months, indicating that due to various interruptions, notably the 2019 electoral processes, Parliament had yet to finalize the necessary legislative changes ([4]–[6]). This application was unopposed, with several respondents indicating their intent to abide by the Court’s decision ([4]).

I – Issues

The principal legal question is whether the Constitutional Court should grant the Minister's application for an extension of the suspension period with respect to the declaration of invalidity of section 7(1) of the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act ([8]). This involves consideration of several factors, including compliance with the original order, the adequacy of the Minister's explanation for the delay, and the potential implications of refusing the extension ([8]–[10]).

R – Rules

The rules applicable to this case involve the powers of the Constitutional Court to grant extensions of time concerning suspended declarations of constitutional invalidity. In Sibiya II, the Court held that such extensions should be granted if deemed in the interests of justice and must be grounded in sufficient explanation regarding the failure to comply within the prescribed timeframe ([9]). Key factors include the adequacy of the justification for non-compliance, potential prejudice to parties involved, and the likelihood of achieving legislative remedy ([9]).

The threshold for granting extensions is high, as indicated by the requirement for the Court to exercise its discretion sparingly ([10]). The prior order in Ramuhovhi I also contains specific provisions that must be considered while assessing the Minister's request ([10]–[12]).

A – Application

In analyzing the application, the Court emphasized that the Minister's justification regarding the legislative delays, whilst acknowledging the tumultuous nature of the legislative environment due to recent elections, did not align with the clarity and purpose of its earlier ruling in Ramuhovhi I ([10]–[12]).

The Court pointed out that the original order clearly stipulated that an extension was not required if Parliament failed to enact the legislation within the specified period; rather, it instituted interim arrangements that would become permanent if Parliament defaulted ([12]). The emphasis on compliance with judicial orders underscores the necessity of legislative bodies to act within the timelines afforded to them.

Moreover, the Court expressed concern regarding the timing of the Minister's application, which was made only six weeks before the expiry of the suspension, suggesting a lack of urgency and appropriate oversight in addressing the legislative needs ([14]).

The Court concluded that given the clarity of the original order and the lack of new substantive reasons provided for requiring an extension, the discretionary power to extend the suspension could not be warranted ([15]).

C – Conclusion

The Court ultimately dismissed the Minister’s application for an extension, reinforcing that the previously mandated regime concerning polygamous customary marriages would remain in effect post-expiry of the suspension period ([16]). The judgment thereby held Parliament accountable for its legislative duties while respecting the rights affected by the lack of initial compliance with the constitutional framework ([16]).

The implications of this decision serve as a reminder of the importance of timely compliance with court orders and legislative responsibility. By declining to extend the suspension, the Court not only affirmed the necessity of upholding the rule of law but also underscored the judiciary's role in maintaining a check on legislative inaction. This case exemplifies the delicate balance between legislative processes and judicial mandates within South Africa's constitutional democracy.
