Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: Republic of South Africa (Gamevest (Pty) Limited v The Regional Land Claims Commissioner for the Northern Province and Mpumalanga & Others)

## F - Facts:
The appeal arises from decisions made in the Land Claims Court concerning land claims filed by the Ba-Phalaborwa tribes, specifically the Fourth and Fifth Respondents, under the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 (the Restitution Act). The Appellant, Gamevest (Pty) Limited, contested the claims over several farms registered in its name, collectively known as Croc Ranch, located in the Northern Province. These farms were historically occupied by the Ba-Phalaborwa people until dispossession initiated by racially discriminatory laws, notably the Black Land Act 27 of 1913 ([paras 1-4]).

In 1995 and 1996, the Ba-Phalaborwa leadership lodged initial land claims, which were consolidated, with the Fourth and Fifth Respondents making further claims in 1998. The Appellant challenged the legitimacy of these claims, alleging that the addition of claimants was made unlawfully after the statutory cut-off date of 31 December 1998, and contended that no valid claim existed due to inadequate specifications concerning dispossession ([paras 12, 13, 19, 25]).

The Land Claims Court dismissed the Appellant's application, which led to this appeal against both the dismissal and associated costs orders imposed by the lower court ([paras 1, 8]).

## I - Issues:
The primary legal issues for consideration were:

1. **Administrative Law Reviewability**: Did the actions of the Regional Land Claims Commissioner and other Respondents constitute reviewable administrative decisions?
2. **Substitution of Claimants**: Was the substitution of claimants by the Fourth and Fifth Respondents after the cut-off date valid in terms of the Restitution Act?
3. **Compliance with Claim Specifications**: Did the claims lodged comply with the requirements set forth in the Restitution Act regarding the specification of dispossession acts? ([paras 9, 30]).

## R - Rules:
The legal framework pertinent to the considerations included:

1. **Restitution of Land Rights Act (Act 22/1994)** and its provisions regarding the lodgment and acceptance of claims, particularly sections pertaining to the requirements for lodging claims, such as proper representation and the timing of claims ([paras 5-7]).
2. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa**, specifically section 33 which enshrines the right to lawful and procedurally fair administrative action ([para 10]).
3. **Case Law on Administrative Review**: Standards for determining whether an action qualifies as 'administrative action' for the purposes of review were considered, drawing on precedent such as *Hira & Another v Booysen & Another* 1992 (4) SA 69 (A) regarding non-performance of statutory duties ([para 12]).

## A - Application:
In assessing the first issue concerning reviewability, the court found that the respondents had not taken any administrative decision at the time the application was filed by the Appellant. The argument centered on whether the Regional Land Claims Commissioner had made a decision regarding the acceptance of claims which could be reviewed, leading to an indication that without such a decision, the review application was premature ([paras 24, 30]).

Regarding the substitution of claimants, the court determined that the advice given prior to lodging claims did not constitute an administrative decision but was simply anecdotal guidance. Thus, any later changes made regarding claims could not be challenged as unlawful acts since no decisions were made to accept or reject claims prior to the cut-off date ([paras 20, 28]). 

On the requirement of compliance with specifications of dispossession, the court held that merely receiving claims did not equate to a determination of their validity. The court emphasized that the section detailing the receipt of claims is procedural and does not extend to judging the merits of claims at that stage. Only after the claims were lodged could the relevant commissioners examine them under section 11 of the Restitution Act ([paras 25, 31]).

## C - Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the decision of the Land Claims Court, concluding that the arguments premised on the non-existence of reviewable administrative action were valid. Consequently, the claims were deemed appropriately lodged based on the criteria established within the Restitution Act. The appeal was dismissed, and the orders regarding costs were adjusted to reflect the circumstances of the postponement occasioned by the Respondents’ procedural deficiencies, requiring the Respondents to bear those specific costs, while the rest of the Appellant's financial obligations to the Respondents were upheld ([paras 37]). 

This case underscores the complexity of land restitution claims in South Africa’s post-apartheid legal landscape, highlighting the stringent procedural safeguards and standards of administrative law necessary to adjudicate such cases effectively. Further implications include a reinforcement of procedural fairness in administrative actions related to land claims against historical dispossession.
