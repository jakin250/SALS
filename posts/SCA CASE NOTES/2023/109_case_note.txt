Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: The Municipal Manager: The City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality and Others v San Ridge Heights Rental Property (Pty) Ltd (517/2022) [2023] ZASCA 109

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case under review involves a dispute between various arms of the City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality (appellants) and San Ridge Heights Rental Property (Pty) Ltd (respondent) regarding the classification of a property. The respondent owns Erf 827, Erand Gardens, a 5.2929-hectare plot with 42 buildings containing 470 flats ([para 2], [para 3]). The City, through Johannesburg Water, provides sewerage and sanitation services to this property and had previously classified it as a ‘multi dwelling’, which, according to their tariff policy, incurs charges of R416.47 per month per unit ([para 4], [para 5]).

Upon the property's previous ownership, Zotec Developments lodged an internal appeal against this classification, which went unaddressed. Zotec indicated in a letter that a lack of response would imply an unsuccessful appeal ([para 6]). The absence of communication led to significant financial losses for San Ridge Heights, prompting them to initiate review proceedings under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), claiming violations of their right to just administrative action as guaranteed by Section 33(2) of the South African Constitution ([para 7], [para 13]).

### I – Issues
The legal questions addressed by the court include:
1. Whether the appellants’ classification of the property under the term ‘multi dwelling’ constituted administrative action subject to judicial review under PAJA.
2. Whether the appellants provided adequate reasons for this administrative action, as required by Section 5 of PAJA and the constitutional duty to give written reasons for decisions that adversely affect rights ([para 10], [para 14]).
3. Whether the high court was correct to substitute its decision for that of the administrative body or whether the decision should instead be remitted to the appellants for reconsideration ([para 20]).

### R – Rules
This case involves the judicial interpretation of administrative action, reviewed under PAJA, which stipulates the requirements for fair administrative procedures. Section 33(2) of the Constitution mandates that individuals whose rights are adversely affected must be provided with written reasons for administrative actions ([para 13]). Furthermore, in terms of Section 8 of PAJA, the court can make ‘just and equitable’ orders. Previous case law, such as *Trencon Construction (Pty) Limited v Industrial Development Corporation of South Africa Limited and Another*, articulates that remittal rather than substitution is typically appropriate unless exceptional circumstances exist ([para 18]).

### A – Application
The court established common ground regarding the administrative nature of the classification decision, admitting that it was indeed subject to review under PAJA. The appellants conceded that they did not provide San Ridge with any reasons for reclassifying the property, significantly undermining the legality of their decision as outlined by PAJA ([para 10], [para 12]).

Notably, the failure to provide reasons, as defined in the law, creates a presumption of invalidity regarding the administrative action taken. The City and/or Johannesburg Water’s argument—that the classification was evident through the tariff policy—was deemed insufficient, as mere definitions provided in the document failed to justify how they arrived at the ‘multi dwelling’ designation without further explanation ([para 15], [para 16]).

The court concluded that the high court had erred by substituting its decision regarding the classification of the property without adequate information or reasons for such a determination. Given that there were no supporting valuations or detailed information presented regarding the classification, the court ordered a remittal rather than a substitution, thus demonstrating deference to the administrative authority's discretion ([para 20]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal on limited grounds, remitting the case back to the City and/or Johannesburg Water for reconsideration regarding San Ridge Heights' classification. The decision underscores the critical importance of providing adequate reasons for administrative decisions, aligning with the principles of good governance as per the Constitution. Each party was ordered to bear its own costs due to the mixed outcomes of the appeal, affirming that neither party had achieved complete success ([para 21]).

The implications of this case emphasize the judiciary's watchfulness over administrative accountability and the necessity for public bodies to adhere to constitutional mandates, thus ensuring individuals’ rights are protected against arbitrary decision-making.
