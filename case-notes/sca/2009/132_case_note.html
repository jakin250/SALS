Supreme Court of Appeal

Tuch v Myerson (447/09) [2009] ZASCA 132

The appellants, Michael Tuch and others, serve as executors of the estate of Nathan Myerson, who initiated defamation proceedings against Jeffrey Harold Myerson and Dion Barry Masureik, the first and third respondents.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 132

FIRAC Method

F – Facts ([1]–[8])

The appellants, Michael Tuch and others, serve as executors of the estate of Nathan Myerson, who initiated defamation proceedings against Jeffrey Harold Myerson and Dion Barry Masureik, the first and third respondents. The deceased’s application for the delivery of share certificates in Jazz Spirit 46 (Pty) Ltd was dismissed in the Johannesburg High Court, leading to this appeal ([1]).

The underlying dispute arose from an affidavit by the first respondent during application proceedings initiated by the deceased. The deceased sought to assert his entitlement to shares based on a written undertaking from the first and third respondents, which they subsequently denied. In their answering affidavits, the respondents made damaging allegations against the deceased, including claims of misappropriating R5 to R6 million from the first respondent’s late father and assertions that the deceased would be a disruptive shareholder ([2]–[5]).

The malicious nature of the statements, purportedly made during judicial proceedings, is central to the defamation claim. In their defense, the respondents claimed qualified privilege due to the context of judicial proceedings, while the deceased contended the statements were made with malice and were thus actionable ([8]).

I – Issues ([9]–[12])

The primary legal questions before the court were:

  1. Were the statements made by the respondents defamatory, and did they constitute a publication made on a privileged occasion?
  2. Did the respondents act with malice, thereby losing the protection afforded by qualified privilege?
  3. Should the court determine the quantum of damages in light of the findings?

R – Rules ([10]–[11])

The legal principles governing the case included the presumption of unlawfulness in cases of defamation and the doctrine of qualified privilege. A statement published in a judicial context may be deemed privileged, provided it is relevant to the issues being contested. However, this privilege can be forfeited if the statements are proven to be made with malice, as noted in cases such as Basner v Trigger 1946 AD 83 and Suid-Afrikaanse Uitsaaikorporasie v O’Malley 1977 (3) SA 394 (A) ([10]).

Malice was defined in jurisprudence as improper motives or a desire to harm, which can often be inferred from the circumstances or from how the statements were presented ([11]).

A – Application ([12]–[20])

The court first evaluated the defamation claims by confirming that both statements were per se defamatory, indicating reputational harm towards the deceased as both a businessman and an individual. The court noted that the claim of misappropriation directly suggested dishonesty, while the assertion regarding potential shareholder conduct implied untrustworthiness ([9]).

The court agreed with the lower court's ruling that the statements were made in a context that could initially invoke qualified privilege. However, it found that the first respondent's claim of ingratitude, drawn from events prior to the accused donation and lacking in evidentiary support, raised questions of malicious intent. The court identified that the absence of merit in the allegations led to the inference that they were motivated by an ulterior purpose, aimed at damaging the deceased’s reputation rather than truly furthering the litigation ([14]).

Regarding the damages, the court recognized that while the respondents argued for nominal damages due to lack of reputation evidence, the court countered that reputational harm exists inherently and must be taken seriously ([17]). The court ultimately found that the severe nature of the allegations warranted a substantial damages award of R30,000 ([19]).

C – Conclusion ([20])

The Supreme Court of Appeal partially upheld the appeal, concluding that the first and third respondents acted with malice in making the defamatory statement regarding misappropriation. The court varied the lower court's judgment, ordering the first and third respondents to pay R30,000 in damages, while upholding the dismissal of claims against the second respondent. The ruling reinforced the idea that statements made under the guise of privilege but lacking in merit can indeed attract liability for defamation when motivation is found to be malicious ([20]).

Thus, the implications of this case re-affirm the importance of evaluating the balance between judicial privilege and the protection of individual reputations in legal contexts, underscoring that the latter cannot be undermined by malicious allegations.
