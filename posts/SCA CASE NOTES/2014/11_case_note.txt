Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Adams & Adams v Pointer Fashion International (324/2013) [2014] ZASCA 11

## F – Facts

The legal dispute at the heart of this case involves the ownership of five trademarks previously registered to Pointer Fashion International CC (Pointer) but later acquired by Deluxe Holdings AG (Deluxe), a Swiss company, after a sale in execution on 8 September 2011. The sale was initiated by Adams & Adams Attorneys (Adams & Adams), who had obtained two judgments against Pointer (dated 15 July 2005 and 16 October 2006) for costs and outstanding legal fees ([1]).

On 27 February 2012, Pointer filed an application with the North Gauteng High Court seeking to annul the sale in execution, invalidate the registration of the trademarks in Deluxe’s name, and restore its ownership rights. The application was divided into two parts: Part A for interim relief to prevent Deluxe from dealing with the trademarks, and Part B for final relief ([2]). During the proceedings, Pointer sought to introduce a new argument claiming that the judgments held by Adams & Adams were superannuated according to Section 63 of the Magistrates’ Courts Act 32 of 1944, a point which was initially opposed by Adams & Adams and Deluxe and subsequently adjourned sine die ([2]).

Afterward, Pointer attempted to file a supplementary affidavit to address this new argument under Rule 6(5)(e) of the High Court Rules, which was opposed by the appellants. Nonetheless, the High Court, presided over by Prinsloo J, permitted Pointer to file the further affidavit ([3]). Ancillary orders were issued allowing for subsequent affidavits, and Prinsloo J’s decision to allow the introduction of new evidence led to the filing of an appeal by the appellants ([4]).

### Procedural History

The order made by Prinsloo J was purely procedural in nature, leading the appellants to challenge the order based on jurisdictional arguments. Notably, Deluxe contended that it was a peregrinus and thus not subject to the court's jurisdiction without an attachment of its property within the court's jurisdiction ([5]). However, this jurisdictional argument was separate from the interlocutory matter at hand ([6]). 

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the appeal was against an interlocutory order and thus was not appealable ([10]). 

## I – Issues

The central legal issue presented in this case pertains to the appealability of the order granted by the North Gauteng High Court. Specifically, the question arises whether an order permitting the introduction of additional evidence in the form of a supplementary affidavit, which was necessary for the ongoing litigation between the parties, is subject to immediate appeal or is classified as an interlocutory order which cannot be contested at this stage ([4]).

Another related issue considered was the determination of whether the High Court possessed personal jurisdiction over Deluxe in regards to the proceedings, particularly in light of its status as a peregrinus and the requirement for an attachment of property within the court's jurisdiction ([5]-[7]).

## R – Rules 

The legal framework applicable to this case includes the South African common law on appealability, particularly the established principle that interlocutory orders are generally not appealable unless they have a definitive effect on the rights of the parties involved ([4]). This principle has been reinforced in various precedential cases, including Zweni v Minister of Law and Order 1993 (1) SA 523 (A) and Jacobs and Others v Baumann NO and Others 2009 (5) SA 432 (SCA), which provide guidance on the interpretation of interlocutory orders ([4]).

Additionally, Rule 6(5)(e) of the High Court Rules, while ostensibly relevant to the delivery of replying affidavits in conventional application proceedings, must be considered within the context of Rule 6(11) to determine the appropriate avenue for introducing supplementary evidence ([3]).

## A – Application 

In applying the rules to the facts of the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal observed that the order from the North Gauteng High Court was purely procedural, allowing Pointer to present new legal arguments without resolving any substantive issues pertinent to the main application ([4]). Consequently, the appellants' contention that the order was appealable based on jurisdictional arguments was misplaced, as the High Court had jurisdiction to make a ruling concerning the introduction of new evidence, which was a significant but separate issue from the broader merits of the case ([7]).

The Court examined the arguments related to jurisdiction, noting that Deluxe, having engaged in the proceedings by submitting an answering affidavit, had effectively submitted to the jurisdiction of the High Court for the interlocutory proceedings ([6]). The court emphasized that its determination was confined to the interlocutory matter - whether to admit the supplementary affidavit - without making substantive adjudications regarding the main application ([6]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal against the interlocutory order was not permissible, as it did not concern a final resolution of the parties’ rights but was rather a procedural matter on whether to permit the introduction of new evidence ([10]). The ruling reinforced the principle that interlocutory orders, by nature, should not divert the course of ongoing litigation unless they fundamentally affect the rights of the parties involved.

Thus, the appeal was struck off the roll with costs, which included the costs associated with the employment of two counsel ([10]). The implications of this case highlight the importance of maintaining procedural clarity in litigation processes and reaffirm the principles governing jurisdiction and appealability in South African law. Future litigants should be aware of the boundaries distinguishing interlocutory and final orders to navigate their legal strategies effectively.
