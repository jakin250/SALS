Constitutional Court

Director of Public Prosecutions, Western Cape v Tucker [2021] ZACC 25

During the extradition inquiry under section 9 of the Extradition Act 67 of 1962, the DPP presented evidence that established Tucker's liability for extradition.

Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 25

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

On 4 October 2000, Lee Nigel Tucker was convicted in absentia by the Swindon Crown Court in the United Kingdom for nine charges related to sexual offenses against minors. Following his conviction, Tucker absconded to South Africa, prompting the issuance of a warrant for his arrest on 2 October 2000. Subsequently, on 4 March 2016, Tucker was provisionally arrested in South Africa following an extradition request from the UK. This request included charges of serious sexual offenses against minors, leading the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP), Western Cape, to commence extradition proceedings ([paras 1–10]).

During the extradition inquiry under section 9 of the Extradition Act 67 of 1962, the DPP presented evidence that established Tucker's liability for extradition. Tucker, who did not dispute the sufficiency of evidence to warrant prosecution (the second jurisdictional fact), challenged the establishment of the first jurisdictional fact on three grounds. Firstly, he claimed that he could not be charged under the UK’s Criminal Appeal Act regarding charges for which he was not convicted. Secondly, he argued that extradition would infringe on his constitutional rights, particularly concerning equality and a fair trial. Lastly, he raised concerns about media bias affecting his right to a fair trial ([paras 11–25]).

The magistrate presiding over the inquiry ruled that evidence not pertinent to the inquiry, primarily relating to the Minister's decision under section 11 of the Extradition Act, should not be included. Following this ruling, Tucker was committed to prison pending the Minister's decision to surrender him to the UK ([paras 26–40]).

Following his committal, Tucker appealed to the High Court. The High Court dismissed much of Tucker’s appeal but resonated with one point: that the magistrate had improperly failed to allow Tucker to present evidence relating to his rights, which could influence the Minister’s decision. It ordered the reopening of the proceedings to allow such evidence ([paras 41–52]).

I – Issues

The central legal question presented in this case is whether a magistrate conducting an extradition inquiry under section 10 of the *Extradition Act*:

  1. Must accept evidence relevant to the Minister’s decision to surrender a sought person, and
  2. Is authorized to reopen the inquiry to allow a sought person to adduce such evidence after a committal order has been issued ([paras 53–59]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing extradition in South Africa is primarily defined by the Extradition Act 67 of 1962. It fundamentally delineates three phases in extradition: the administrative, the judicial, and the executive. The judicial phase mandates that a magistrate must determine if:

  1. The person is liable for extradition based on the charges.
  2. There is sufficient evidence to warrant prosecution in the requesting state ([para 60]).

Section 10(2) provides for a certificate from the foreign authority as conclusive proof regarding the second jurisdictional fact. Furthermore, it has been established through precedent that a magistrate may consider evidence that is relevant to the Minister's decision under section 11 of the Extradition Act, as affirmed in the decisions of Geuking v President of the Republic of South Africa and Garrido v Director of Public Prosecutions ([paras 61–75]).

A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the Constitutional Court analyzed the High Court's decision to reopen the proceedings. The court emphasized that the magistrate is not solely limited to an inquiry regarding whether a person is liable for extradition but must also facilitate the collection of evidence relevant to the decision-making process of the Minister under section 11. This includes considerations of the protection of constitutional rights ([paras 76–90]).

The Court noted that evidence presented at the judicial phase could bear on the Minister's decision and, thus, must be allowed. The refusal of the magistrate to accept Tucker's evidence regarding potential violations of his rights was deemed an error. The reopening of the inquiry, ordered by the High Court, was recognized as a necessary step to allow Tucker to present further evidence that could influence the Minister's decision ([paras 91–110]).

C – Conclusion

The court ultimately upheld the appeal brought by the DPP regarding the interpretation of the magistrate's role under the Extradition Act, establishing that a magistrate must allow the introduction of evidence relevant to the Minister's surrender decision. It clarified that the inquiry process is not merely about the committal but should also encompass a consideration of evidence that can inform the Minister's discretion.

Consequently, the order made by the High Court to reopen the proceedings was upheld. The Court confirmed that while the DPP's concerns were valid regarding the procedural integrity of the original inquiry, the necessity for a comprehensive and just process warranted a reopening of the inquiry solely for the purpose of receiving relevant evidence ([paras 111–127]). The final determination, thereby, maintained that Tucker would remain in custody pending the Minister's decision regarding his extradition ([paras 128–130]).

This case emphasizes the intersection of extradition law and constitutional protections, particularly the right to a fair trial and the importance of evidence in judicial processes.
