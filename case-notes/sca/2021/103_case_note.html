Supreme Court of Appeal

Electoral Commission of South Africa v Democratic Alliance and Others [2021] ZASCA 103

The case centers on the powers of the Electoral Commission of South Africa (the Commission) regarding allegations of false information published during the election period.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 103

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case centers on the powers of the Electoral Commission of South Africa (the Commission) regarding allegations of false information published during the election period. The matter arose from a complaint lodged on March 2, 2019, by the second respondent, the Good Party, alleging that the first respondent, the Democratic Alliance (DA), contravened certain provisions of the Electoral Act 73 of 1998 (the Electoral Act) and the Code of Conduct (the Code) by disseminating false statements about Ms. Patricia De Lille, the leader of the Good Party ([paras 1-2]).

The crux of the complaint pertained to statements in the DA's campaign guidelines, which suggested that De Lille was "fired" for wrongdoing. De Lille contested that this characterization was not only false but defamatory and undermined her candidacy ([para 6]). The Commission found that the statement that De Lille had been "fired" was indeed false and directed the DA to issue a public apology and cease further false statements about her ([paras 13-16]).

Subsequently, the DA sought a review of the Commission's decision, arguing that the Commission lacked the authority to adjudicate such complaints and impose remedies as it did. The case progressed to the Electoral Court, which ultimately upheld the DA's assertion, leading to the Commission's appeal in the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 17-24]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue in this case revolves around the powers of the Electoral Commission under the Electoral Act and whether it has jurisdiction to rule on contraventions of the Code of Conduct and impose sanctions for such breaches ([para 1]). Specifically, the questions are:

  1. Does the Electoral Commission have the authority to determine violations of the Electoral Act or the Code by political parties?
  2. Can the Commission impose sanctions for breaches of the Code, or must such matters be adjudicated in court? ([para 1])

R – Rules

The applicable legal framework comprises the Electoral Act 73 of 1998, particularly Sections 89(2), 94, and 97, as well as the Electoral Commission Act 51 of 1996, which outlines the Commission's functions ([paras 4-5, 22-24]).

  • Section 89(2) prohibits false statements intended to influence the electoral outcome.
  • Item 9(1)(b) of the Code forbids political parties from publishing false or defamatory allegations regarding other parties or candidates.
  • Section 5(1)(o) of the Electoral Commission Act limits the Commission's powers to adjudication of disputes of an administrative nature only. This section is vital as it delineates the scope of the Commission's authority and expressly excludes adjudication of matters characterized as litigative in nature ([paras 22-24, 32-34]).

Precedents, including Democratic Alliance v African National Congress and Another ([2015] ZACC 1), interpret these statutes and provide a foundation for the principles involved ([para 23]).

A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the court evaluated whether the Commission overstepped its bounds in determining that the DA breached the Code and sanctions accordingly. The court concluded that the Commission's finding was made under a misapprehension of its statutory authority. It determined that the Commission lacks the jurisdiction to enforce compliance with the Code, and any actions taken by it to implicate contraventions must be viewed as contrary to the statutory scheme designed by Parliament ([paras 39-48]).

The court emphasized that the Code does not permit the Commission to adjudicate complaints that do not directly pertain to the administrative aspects of electoral conduct. Since De Lille's complaint and the context of the DA's statements did not equate to "administrative disputes" as framed under section 5(1)(o) of the ECA, the judgment reinforced the notion that judicial review of such matters must occur in a court, not through the Commission’s finding ([paras 30-34]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Electoral Court's decision to set aside the Commission’s ruling. It firmly established that the Electoral Commission does not possess the jurisdiction to adjudicate claims pertaining to breaches of the Code or to impose sanctions. This judgment underscores the separation of powers as dictated by the legislative framework; enforcement of electoral conduct must be overseen by the courts rather than by an executive body like the Commission ([para 59]).

The implications of this case extend to the electoral integrity framework, calling attention to the symbiotic relationship between legislative authority and administrative discretion, thereby encapsulating the delicate balance necessary for ensuring fair electoral processes in South Africa. The ruling affirms the judicial route as the proper venue for resolving disputes arising from alleged violations of electoral conduct, emphasizing the need for heightened scrutiny in politically charged interpretations of electoral legislation ([paras 58-59]).

This case not only clarifies the legal scope of the Commission but also serves as a remedial charter for ensuring electoral fairness—an essential tenet in a democratic society.
