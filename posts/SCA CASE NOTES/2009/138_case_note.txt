# Case Note: Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl (Schoombee v Marais)

### FIRAC Case Note

#### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–8])

In **Schoombee v Marais** (635/08) [2009] ZASCA 138, the appellant, Andries Gottlieb Schoombee, a practicing attorney, provided legal representation to the respondent, Anna-Marie Marais, in her divorce proceedings against her then-husband, Mr. Marais. The divorce was settled on the basis that Mr. Marais would pay a sum of R600,000 to Mrs. Marais, and she would waive her right to maintenance and receive a contribution of R15,000 towards her costs. Following the issuance of the divorce order, Mrs. Marais initiated a claim against Schoombee in the Transvaal Provincial Division of the High Court for damages, alleging that he had breached their contract by negligently providing her with incorrect legal advice.

The court of first instance, presided over by Van Rooyen Wn R, found that the appellant had indeed provided negligent advice and awarded damages amounting to R1.1 million, plus interest. This judgment served as the basis for Schoombee's appeal.

#### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1–2])

The primary legal issues arising from this case include:

1. Whether the appellant was negligent in providing legal advice to the respondent in relation to the divorce settlement, specifically regarding the classification of marital property and financial entitlements.
2. Whether the failure to secure a valuation of Mr. Marais's assets constituted a breach of duty resulting in quantifiable damages to the respondent.

#### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 4–10])

The court referenced various legal principles regarding professional negligence and the obligations of attorneys within the attorney-client relationship. In determining negligence, the court cited **Mouton v Mynwerkersunie** 1977 (1) SA 119 (A), particularly concerning the nature of judgment errors that do not give rise to liability due to the absence of negligence.

Additionally, the court considered prior judgments involving loss of opportunity claims, including **De Klerk v Absa Bank Ltd and others** 2003 (4) SA 315 (SCA) and emphasized causation in the context of damages, as articulated in **Burger v Union National South British Insurance Company** 1975 (4) SA 72 (W).

#### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 8–11])

Upon review, the appellate court noted the findings of negligence made by the lower court but disagreed with its conclusion that such negligence ultimately resulted in damage to Mrs. Marais. The court opined that her ability to litigate was not affected by the lack of a property valuation; rather, it was tantamount to a tactical decision on her part.

The argument posited by the lower court that the lack of a valuation deprived her of a genuine choice to litigate was determined to be fundamentally flawed. The appellate court concluded that Mrs. Marais suffered no actionable damage as she had not been denied the opportunity to pursue her legal entitlements meaningfully. The evidence revealed that she believed the properties were worth significantly more than what was represented by Mr. Marais, indicating that she would have settled on similar or potentially less favorable terms even had she obtained a valuation.

The evidence showed that Schoombee advised her adequately about the ramifications of pursuing the divorce case and recommended against settling based on the terms she eventually accepted. The court determined that her voluntary choice to resolve her divorce in the manner she did negated any claim of actionable negligence on Schoombee's part.

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 12–13])

Ultimately, the **Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl** upheld the initial judgment of the lower court, but on different grounds, concluding that the respondent's claims against Schoombee for professional negligence were without merit. The court replaced the findings of the lower court, dismissing the respondent's action with costs.

This ruling reinforces the principles governing the attorney-client relationship, specifically concerning the evidentiary burdens required in proving negligence and causation in professional liability cases. It highlights the significance of client agency in litigation decisions and establishes a clear standard that asserts an attorney is not liable for the outcomes of cases when clients make informed decisions, even in scenarios where the attorney's advice may have been flawed.

The decision serves as a precedent illustrating the boundaries of an attorney's liability for negligent advice, particularly within the context of contractual breaches and professional duties in family law cases.
