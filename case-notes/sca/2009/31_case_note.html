FIRAC Case Note: Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development v Chonco and Others [2009] ZASCA 31

F – Facts

The appellant in this case, the Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development, contested a judgment issued by Seriti J in the Pretoria High Court. The judgment mandated the Minister's predecessor to take necessary steps within three months to enable the President to consider 384 applications for presidential pardon referenced in the case ([1]). The applications were submitted between September and October 2003 on behalf of Mqabukeni Chonco and 383 others, all imprisoned for crimes associated with the political struggle during South Africa's apartheid era. These individuals did not apply for amnesty through the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) because their political party, the Inkatha Freedom Party (IFP), did not support the TRC process ([2]).

By the time the case was heard, the applications had not been processed, despite over four years since their submission ([3]). The Minister's lack of action despite parliamentary inquiries highlighted the neglect surrounding the applications ([4]-[8]). The Minister acknowledged complications due to the complexity of the applications and a lack of established guidelines ([12]-[14]). Appellants argued that the delay in processing violated constitutional rights, leading to the High Court's intervention ([15]-[22]).

I – Issues

The central legal question was whether the Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development had a constitutional obligation to process the applications for pardon before they could be submitted to the President ([28]). Specifically, the case examined:

  1. The nature of constitutional obligations concerning prisoner pardon applications.
  2. The legal consequences of the Department of Justice's inaction in processing these applications ([32]-[42]).

R – Rules

This case primarily engages the interpretation of sections 84 and 92 of the South African Constitution, which delineate the powers of the President and the responsibilities of ministers, respectively. Section 84(2)(j) grants the President the power to pardon offenders or remit fines ([28]). Section 92 asserts that Cabinet members must act in accordance with the Constitution, paralleling the obligations outlined in section 237 that mandates all constitutional duties to be carried out diligently and without delay ([30]-[32]).

Additionally, the judgment references the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) but notes that the obligations described are constitutional rather than administrative, thereby not requiring a PAJA analysis ([40]-[42]).

A – Application

The court, led by FARLAM JA, analyzed whether the Minister’s conduct constituted compliance with the constitutional obligation to process the pardon applications. Citing the significant delay in handling the applications and the Minister's failure to establish proper procedures or guidelines for pardon applications, the court affirmed that there was a clear failure to act diligently as mandated by section 237 ([34]-[36]).

Seriti J in the court a quo had already concluded the Minister must have some obligation to process these applications to facilitate the President’s exercise of discretion under section 84(2)(j). It was stated that someone must act in response to prisoners' rights to apply for pardon, establishing the connection between the Minister's actions (or inactions) and the constitutional framework governing these processes ([39]-[44]).

The court cited the duty to perform executive functions—which included the processing of pardon applications—and concluded there was no justification offered by the Minister for the undue delay. The lack of action was characterized as a violation of the constitutional duties binding public officials, emphasizing the necessity of procedural diligence to uphold the rights of the applicants ([41]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Minister's appeal, affirming that she indeed had a constitutional obligation to process the applications expeditiously so that the President could exercise her pardoning powers appropriately. The judgment reaffirmed the principle that constitutional obligations must be executed diligently and without delay, thereby holding the Minister accountable for her inaction ([44]). The implications of this ruling extend to how public officials manage applications that implicate fundamental rights, reinforcing the necessity of procedural integrity and responsiveness within the framework of constitutional governance. The court's decision not only enforces accountability but also underscores the critical intersection of justice and constitutional mandates in a democratic society ([45]).
