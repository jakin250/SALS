Road Accident Fund v Myhill NO (505/2012) [2013] ZASCA 73

The case centers on the Road Accident Fund (RAF) appealing a decision by the South Gauteng High Court, which had set aside settlement agreements entered into on behalf of two minor children, P[...] and L[...], after they suffered serious injuries in a 1997 motor vehicle accident...

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 73

1. Facts

The case centers on the Road Accident Fund (RAF) appealing a decision by the South Gauteng High Court, which had set aside settlement agreements entered into on behalf of two minor children, P[...] and L[...], after they suffered serious injuries in a 1997 motor vehicle accident ([para 1]). The plaintiffs, represented by their mother, Ms S[…] S[…], claimed compensation from the RAF due to injuries sustained when a vehicle allegedly collided with them as they walked along an unpaved road ([para 2]).

Both minors were subsequently hospitalized with head injuries—P[...] for ten days and L[...] for six days ([paras 3, 14]). The claims, which totaled R57,260 for P[...] and R60,260 for L[...], primarily sought compensation for general damages, as well as hospital and medical expenses ([paras 4, 19]). Initially assessed at R10,000 each by RAF, the claims were settled in 1999 for lower amounts after an alleged 30% apportionment due to contributory negligence attributed to the mother ([paras 6, 8, 22]). Unfortunately, the mother’s attorney absconded with the settlement amounts, leading the children to lose their entitlement to adequate compensation ([para 9]).

Ten years later, Advocate Myhill was appointed curator ad litem for the minors and initiated action to have the settlements set aside, arguing that they were prejudicial to the children ([para 10]). The court first assessed liability, later leading to the issue of the adequacy of the settlements being raised ([para 11]).

2. Issues

The core legal questions concerns whether the agreements settling the claims on behalf of the minors should be regarded as binding or void due to the substantial prejudice suffered by the minors, specifically in light of the following considerations:

  1. Prejudice to Minors: Did the settlements inadequately account for the children's medical needs now and in the future, specifically regarding potential complications like post-traumatic epilepsy? ([para 11]).
  2. Applicability of Set-off: Was it permissible for the RAF to apply a set-off against the claims of the minors based on alleged personal debts owed by their custodian parent? ([para 23]).

3. Rules

The legal principles pertinent to this case include:

  1. Rescission of Contracts for Minors: It is established that contracts entered into on behalf of minors can be rescinded under the principle of restitutio in integrum if shown to be prejudicial to the minor at the time of contract ([para 12]).
  2. Assessment of Prejudice: The law necessitates that courts evaluate whether the prejudice was serious or substantial, while not allowing speculation on unknown future developments ([para 13]).
  3. Set-off Principles: The general principle of set-off requires reciprocal indebtedness. Personal debts cannot be set off against debts owed to minors by their custodian parents, maintaining the minor's right to full compensation ([paras 23, 28]).

4. Application

The court assessed the nature of the settlement agreements. The RAF's settlement offers were deemed grossly inadequate, particularly when considering the subsequent medical evaluations which revealed potential long-term complications, including epilepsy. The initial assessments suggested an understanding of the medical injuries that were considered serious, and there was documentation indicating that both children might have suffered from post-traumatic seizures ([paras 18, 21]).

The court noted that, at the time of the settlements, the medical assessments were insufficient and did not account for future medical needs. Thus, the absence of provisions for potential ongoing treatment rendered these settlements prejudicial ([para 19]).

Moreover, the argument regarding contributory negligence was also scrutinized; the court found the basis for attributing negligence to the mother tenuous, given the conditions under which the accident occurred ([paras 22, 28]). It reaffirmed that minors should not suffer due to their custodian's liabilities under their personal capacity ([para 29]).

5. Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's ruling, concluding that the settlement agreements were substantially prejudicial to P[...] and L[...] given their serious injuries and potential future medical needs. The court emphasized the principles that guide settlements involving minors must prioritize the minors' rights and well-being, and thus rescinded the agreements per the doctrine of restitutio in integrum ([paras 31, 32]). The appeal was dismissed, with costs awarded to the respondent, reflecting a broader legal principle to protect minors from disadvantageous settlements ([para 32]).

This case reinforces the need for strict adherence to legal standards when settling claims on behalf of minors, ensuring that such agreements truly safeguard the interests of the vulnerable parties involved.
