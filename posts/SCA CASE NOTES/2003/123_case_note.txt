Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2003

# FIRAC Case Note: Ganesh & Another v Telecom Namibia Ltd

## F – Facts ([1]–[30])
This case involves an appeal against a final sequestration order granted by Knoll J in the Cape of Good Hope Provincial Division. Appellants L I Ganes and L G Ganes, who are married in community of property, were alleged to be insolvent by the respondent, Telecom Namibia Limited.

The first appellant, L I Ganes, served as the Manager: Procurement for the respondent from 1995 until his resignation on 2 July 2000. His responsibilities included overseeing a contract with an entity known as Dresselhaus Scrap Metal CC, which was the exclusive purchaser of the respondent's scrap copper ([2]).

On 12 July 2001, Telecom Namibia initiated an urgent ex parte application for sequestration citing that the joint estate of the appellants was insolvent, supported by a founding affidavit from Gunther Hanke, who asserted claims against the appellants totaling approximately N$2,648,948.62 ([3]).

The various claims included deliberate under-invoicing of sales, overpayments to the Receiver of Revenue, unauthorized housing subsidies, and a significant amount for copper received but not invoiced ([3]). Hanke alleged that the first appellant had been engaged in numerous unlawful transactions while employed by Telecom Namibia, receiving bribes and secret commissions from various entities ([3]–[4]).

The court initially granted a provisional sequestration order, and the appellants were interrogated as per section 152 of the Insolvency Act 36 of 1936. Following the appellants’ denial of the allegations, the respondent's replying affidavit revealed further admissions by the first appellant regarding his acceptance of illicit payments and bribes ([5]–[10]). A subsequent application to strike out certain paragraphs of the respondent’s affidavit was dismissed, leading to the current appeal ([10]–[12]).

## I – Issues ([17]–[29])
The principal legal issues in this case are:

1. **Authority to Institute Proceedings**: Did the respondent have the necessary authority to initiate sequestration proceedings against the appellants ([17]–[19])?
  
2. **Striking Out of New Matter**: Was it appropriate for the court to allow the respondent's replying affidavit, which included new evidence of further liabilities owed by the appellants, to stand ([20]–[22])?

3. **Insolvency Determination**: Did the first appellant breach his fiduciary duty to the respondent, and as a result, was the joint estate of the appellants insolvent ([23]–[30])?

## R – Rules ([18]–[27])
1. **Authority to Institute Proceedings**: Under South African procedural law, particularly motions, the requirements of authority are governed by rules pertaining to affidavits wherein the necessity of formal authorization by a party to initiate proceedings is generally waived, focusing instead on who instituted and prosecuted the proceedings ([19]).

2. **Striking Out Applications**: Courts have the discretion to rule on applications for striking out new matter in replying papers, as per section 12(2) of the Insolvency Act, allowing further proof of insolvency but requiring the debtor to have the opportunity to respond ([21]).

3. **Fiduciary Duty**: Established principles state that employees owe fiduciary duties to their employers, which include not engaging in conduct against the employer’s interests or deriving secret profits without consent ([25]–[26]). Breaches of this duty establish grounds for claims of recovery against employees for any illicit profits made ([27]).

## A – Application ([23]–[30])
The Supreme Court of Appeal applied these legal principles methodically:

1. **Authority to Institute Proceedings**: The court determined that since the founding affidavit from Gunther Hanke was accepted as duly authorized, the initiation of the sequestration was valid. The appellants’ failure to challenge this effectively legitimized the entire process ([19]).

2. **Striking Out New Matter**: The court found that the information about further liabilities introduced in the replying affidavit, deemed as new matter, was admissible. The lower court had exercised discretion properly by allowing such proof, as the appellants could address these claims adequately in their further affidavits ([21]).

3. **Insolvency Findings**: The court confirmed that the first appellant had effectively admitted to receiving bribes that breached his fiduciary duty, rendering him liable to account to the employer. This vital governance underpinned the insolvency claim, where the respondent proved that appellants' liabilities surpassed assets, amounting to insolvency ([23]–[30]).

## C – Conclusion ([31])
The appeal by L I Ganes and L G Ganes against the final sequestration order was dismissed, affirming the decision of the lower court. The court found that sufficient evidence substantiated the claims of insolvency and breach of fiduciary duty. The implications of this decision extend to the enforcement of strict fiduciary standards within employer-employee relationships, highlighting the repercussions of illicit conduct in corporate governance.

The case reaffirms the legal authority of courts to evaluate the grounds of insolvency based on evidence provided, thereby reinforcing the diligence required in employment conducted within fiduciary bounds, while also clarifying the procedural niceties regarding the authorization of representatives in legal proceedings.
