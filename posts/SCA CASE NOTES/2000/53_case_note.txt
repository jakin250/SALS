Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2000

# FIRAC Case Note for Antonica Moloi & Others v Road Accident Fund (Case No. 413/98)

## F - Facts

This case originates from an appeal against a judgment of Claassen J in the Witwatersrand Local Division of the High Court, which upheld a special plea from the Road Accident Fund (the Fund) dismissing the plaintiffs’ claims on the grounds of prescription ([1]). The plaintiffs, Antonica Moloi, Dinah Gambu, and Evelinah Ngwenya, brought the action in their capacity as mothers and guardians of their minor children, who reportedly sustained injuries in a collision with an unidentified vehicle on 30 September 1994. The collision allegedly involved negligence by an unnamed driver ([3–4]). 

The plaintiffs delivered their claims to the Fund in October or November 1996, more than two years after the incident. The Fund maintained that, according to Regulation 3(2)(a)(i) of the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Regulations, 1989, these claims had to be filed within two years to be valid. This regulation notes that claims from third parties, including minors, are subject to the same time limitations as those for other claimants, irrespective of legal disabilities like minority ([5–9]). Although the plaintiffs acknowledged the late filing of their claims, they argued that the regulation in question was ultra vires, failing to consider minors’ legal rights protected under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, which allows for the non-running of prescription against minors ([6–10]).

### Procedural History  
The upper court heard the case based on a stated case where the essential legal query was whether the minors’ claims were prescribed due to the late filing of claims under the Fund ([11–13]). Claassen J found that the regulations imposing conditions for filing claims were valid and align with the Act 93 of 1989, thus dismissing the plaintiffs' contentions ([11–12]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal issue at hand is whether the minors’ claims under Article 40 of the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act became prescribed after the expiry of the two-year period set by Regulation 3(2)(a)(i), particularly when those claims involve unidentified vehicles ([2–6], [9]). Another significant issue arises from the question of whether the provisions of the Prescription Act (namely Sections 13 and 16) should override the regulations intended to limit legal claims from minors within this specific statute ([10–12]).

## R - Rules

The rules applied in this case primarily stem from:

1. **Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act 93 of 1989 (as amended)** - specifically Section 6, which empowers the Minister to create regulations to enforce provisions of the Agreement ([8]).
  
2. **Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Regulations, 1989** - particularly Regulation 3(2)(a)(i) and (ii), which establishes a framework stipulating that claims must be filed within a two-year period, applicable to all claimants, including those with legal disabilities ([9]).
  
3. **Prescription Act 68 of 1969** - Sections 13 and 16, which protect minors from having their claims prescribed during their minority, thereby initiating a debate over the interaction between statutory and regulatory frameworks ([10]).

4. **Judicial Precedents** - Notably, **Mbatha v Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund** and **Road Accident Fund v Smith N O**, which the lower court relied upon asserting that the regulatory framework is valid under the overarching legislation ([11]).

## A - Application

In considering whether regulation 3(2)(a)(i) is ultra vires, the court juxtaposed the provisions of the Act and the included regulations. Claassen J held that the regulations effectively form part of the Act, and thus, they impose enforceable conditions on claims ([11]). The contention of the plaintiffs, that prescription should not run against minors, conflicted with the provisions of the regulations that explicitly stated otherwise ([12]). 

Farlam AJA, in the appeal, revisited the issue of whether classification under section 16 of the Prescription Act could apply to minors claiming compensation from the Fund. He argued that if the regulations do not possess the authority equivalent to parliamentary enactments, they cannot negate the protection offered to minors under the Prescription Act ([13–16]). Furthermore, the necessary comparison with the law regarding liability and claims for identified versus unidentified vehicles illustrated that the Minister lacked the power to condition claims that otherwise appear unconditional under Article 40 of the Agreement ([27]).

The analysis extended to the very nature of the "condition" set forth in the regulation. It was argued that the prescribed timeline is not a condition that suspends rights but rather a prescriptive period that serves to limit the time frame within which a claim must be filed ([19–22]). The appeal underscored a critical examination of whether the rights created under the primary Act could be relegated to conditional status merely through regulations fashioned by ministerial power, thereby influencing the validity of claims brought forward on behalf of the minors ([25–27]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately determined that Claassen J's interpretation of the regulations as valid was erroneous, siding instead with the assertion that the regulatory framework could not validly impose conditions that conflict with the unconditional rights as provided for by Article 40 of the Agreement ([27]). Thus, the appeal was upheld with costs, and the initial ruling dismissing the minor plaintiffs' claims was overturned, allowing the claims to proceed within the protective garnishing of the Prescription Act as it pertains to minors ([28]).  

This decision reaffirms statutory protections over minors in the face of rigid regulatory conditions, emphasizing the intention of the legislature to shield the vulnerable in matters of legal claims arising from motor vehicle accidents, thereby broadening the horizons for similar future cases. The ruling engenders critical implications for both regulatory authority and legislative intent across the broader spectrum of South African personal injury law.
