Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Case Title:** The Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism v Scenematic Fourteen (Pty) Ltd  
**Case No:** 85/04  
**Coram:** Scott JA, Farla, Cameron, Lewis JJA, Comrie AJA  
**Date of Hearing:** 15 February 2005  
**Date of Delivery:** 22 March 2005  

## F - Facts
The case concerns the allocation of commercial fishing rights in the hake longline sector for the 2002-2005 fishing season, under s 18(1) of the Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998 (the Act) ([1]). The Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism (the Minister) is the first appellant, with Mr. Horst Kleinschmidt (the Deputy Director-General, DDG) as the second appellant. The respondent, Scenematic Fourteen (Pty) Ltd, applied for fishing rights but did not succeed.

The total allowable catch (TAC) for the sector was set at 10,840 tons, with about 9825 tons allocated to successful applicants out of over 5000 applications ([2]). Following an appeal process under s 80 of the Act, some disappointed applicants, including Scenematic, opted for judicial review against the DDG's decision to deny their application. The review in the High Court resulted in the setting aside of the DDG's decision not to grant fishing rights to Scenematic ([3]). The court made no order regarding the Minister’s decision ([2-3]).

From a historical perspective, various administrative actions had been taken concerning fishing rights in the past, including a significant shift in policy regarding the duration of fishing rights from annual to medium-term allocations, intended to enhance investment and stability ([4-5]). The Department had to evolve in terms of transformation, particularly regarding historically disadvantaged individuals ([1]).

## I - Issues
The case posed several legal questions:

1. **Procedural Fairness:** Whether the procedures followed in denying the fishing rights were procedurally fair ([15])?
2. **Discretionary Power:** Whether the DDG had improperly delegated his discretionary powers to the advisory committee and if this constituted a failure to exercise his discretion ([19])?
3. **Streaming and Weighting:** Whether the streaming of applicants into two categories (2001 rights holders and potential new entrants), with a bias toward transformation criteria, was unreasonable and procedurally unfair ([14-15])?

## R - Rules
The relevant legal statutes and principles include:

1. **Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998**: It requires that no person may engage in fishing unless granted a right by the Minister, and establishes the framework for fair allocation of fishing rights.
2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Specifically, s 6(2) provides grounds for judicial review of administrative action based on procedural unfairness, unreasonableness, abdication of discretion, and other factors affecting the decision-making process ([14]).
3. **Case Laws**:
   - In *Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Environmental Affairs*, it was emphasised that processes must be fair and justifiable to achieve the objectives of transformative legislation ([15]).

## A - Application
Upon analyzing the submissions, the Court found that:

1. **Procedural Fairness**: The court ruled that the process adhered to proper frameworks outlined in the Act. The applicants were made aware of the guidelines and criteria for evaluation ([18]). Detailed applications were submitted containing necessary information informing decisions on the allocation of fishing rights, validating the procedural fairness ([9-18]).

2. **Delegated Authority**: The court established that the DDG’s reliance on the advisory committee did not equate to sub-delegation in an unlawful sense. It underscored that while the DDG must exercise discretion, it is permissible for him to take guidance from a committee of experts, particularly given the administrative context of handling numerous applications ([25-29]).

3. **Streaming Rationale**: The division into two categories was justified as a means to balance historical precedence of rights holders and the need to include new entrants from disadvantaged communities in line with the Act’s objectives. The court determined that the streaming mechanisms, though perhaps imperfect, were not inherently unreasonable or procedurally unfair ([15-16], [25]).

## C - Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the High Court's decision, ruling that the DDG's decision to deny fishing rights followed lawful procedures and properly considered relevant criteria. The process allowed for fair engagement with all applicants, adhering to the principles of the Act and necessary discretion allowed statutory guidance. The appeal was upheld with costs. The order from the court a quo was set aside, affirming that the DDG’s application of the law and subsequent decisions were lawful, with the implications reinforcing adherence to statutory frameworks while maintaining the balance sought in transitional justice within the industry ([36]).
