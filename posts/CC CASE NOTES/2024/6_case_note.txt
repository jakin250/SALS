Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

## FIRAC Case Note: Electoral Commission of South Africa v Umkhonto Wesizwe Political Party and Others [2024] ZACC 6

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–9])
On **28 January 2021**, the Constitutional Court of South Africa mandated former President **Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma** to testify before the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into State Capture, which he failed to comply with. Subsequently, he was found guilty of contempt of court and sentenced to **15 months’ imprisonment** on **29 June 2021**. Mr. Zuma's imprisonment began on **8 July 2021**, but he was released on **medical parole** shortly thereafter. However, on **15 December 2021**, the High Court ruled this release unlawful. Following an appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court’s decision on **21 November 2022**. 

After various legal proceedings regarding his parole and the legality of his release, Mr. Zuma was remitted for early release on **11 August 2023**, having served only **three months** of his sentence. 

Meanwhile, on **8 March 2024**, the **Umkhonto Wesizwe Political Party (MK Party)** submitted a candidate list for the National Assembly elections, which included Mr. Zuma. The **Electoral Commission of South Africa** (EMS) received **22 objections** to his candidacy based on **section 47(1)(e)** of the Constitution, which disqualifies individuals convicted of serious crimes from standing for election. The Commission upheld two objections regarding his eligibility on **28 March 2024**.

The **MK Party** and Mr. Zuma appealed the Commission's decision to the **Electoral Court**, which ruled in favor of the respondents, reversing the Commission's decision. The Commission then sought leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 14–15])
The Court addressed multiple interrelated legal questions, primarily:
1. Whether the **Electoral Commission** had jurisdiction to make determinations regarding candidates' eligibility based on section 47(1)(e) of the Constitution.
2. The significance of **remission** of Mr. Zuma’s sentence and whether it affected his eligibility.
3. Whether there was a reasonable apprehension of bias in the Commission’s decision-making process.
4. The proper interpretation of section 47(1)(e) in the context of Mr. Zuma's conviction and sentencing.

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 1–3, 32–39])
The primary legal rule at issue is **section 47(1)(e)** of the Constitution, which specifies that:
- Any citizen convicted and sentenced to more than 12 months’ imprisonment without the option of a fine is disqualified from being a member of the National Assembly.
- The disqualification ends five years after the completion of such a sentence.

The Court relied upon previous rulings and statutes, including relevant interpretations from the **Electoral Act 73 of 1998**, which outlines the Commission's powers under **section 30** for determining candidate eligibility. The judgment in **Secretary of the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into State Capture, Corruption and Fraud in the Public Sector** ([2021] ZACC 18) served as a critical reference point for establishing Mr. Zuma’s conviction and sentence for contempt of court.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 28–64])
The Court first stated that the absence of an appeal does not impede the enforcement of a sentence that stands as a conviction for **section 47(1)(e)** purposes. The majority opinion underscored that the language within section 47(1)(e) does not stipulate any requirement for a pending appeal for disqualification to apply. Thus, Mr. Zuma’s **15-month sentence**, affirmed by the contempt ruling, failed to be overturned or construed as expired due to remission. 

The ruling highlighted the **proviso** concerning appeals as a timing mechanism: it allows for the disqualification's onset once all appeal options are exhausted or settled. This interpretation emphasized the legislative intent to ensure that only serious offenders remain ineligible for parliamentary roles while acknowledging the right to appeal as a safeguard against wrongful convictions.

On the issue of bias, the Court examined the respondents' claims, finding that there was insufficient basis for doubting the integrity of the Commission. The existence of a statement by Commissioner Love was not enough to infer a reasonable apprehension of bias. 

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 87–108])
The Constitutional Court held that the **Electoral Commission** correctly determined Mr. Zuma’s ineligibility under section 47(1)(e). It declared that he was indeed convicted and sentenced to more than 12 months' imprisonment and therefore disqualified from holding office until five years post-sentence. 

The ruling reversed the **Electoral Court's** decision and reinforced the Commission's role in safeguarding electoral integrity. The decision underscored a commitment to the rule of law and the Constitution, emphasizing that disqualifications based on serious offenses are essential for maintaining the credibility of democratic institutions. 

In summary, the implications of this ruling extend beyond Mr. Zuma, reaffirming that electoral bodies must ensure candidates meet eligibility requirements, thus reinforcing public confidence in the integrity of elected representatives.
