# Case Note: **Association of Mineworkers and Construction Union and Others v Chamber of Mines of South Africa and Others** [2017] ZACC 3

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The dispute in this case revolves around an agreement reached on September 10, 2013, between the Chamber of Mines of South Africa and three unions—National Union of Mineworkers (NUM), Solidarity, and United Association of South Africa (UASA)—which represented the majority of employees in the gold mining sector ([6]–[7]). The agreement stipulated wage and working conditions and was implicitly binding on employees of member companies, regardless of whether they were union members. 

The Association of Mineworkers and Construction Union (AMCU), representing a majority of workers at five individual gold mines operated by the Chamber’s member companies, was not a party to this agreement. Therefore, AMCU contended that its members were not bound by the terms, which included a prohibition on strikes ([1]–[2]). On January 20, 2014, AMCU notified the mining companies of its members’ intention to strike starting January 23, 2014 ([8]). Subsequently, the Chamber sought an urgent interdict from the Labour Court, which granted an interim interdict, subsequently confirmed by the Labour Court ([10]).

After an unsuccessful direct application for leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, AMCU was granted leave to appeal from the Labour Appeal Court, which had dismissed its appeal without a definitive resolution of the ‘workplace’ definition and its broader implications ([9]).

### I – Issues
The core legal questions facing the Court were:
1. Whether the agreement that prohibited strikes constituted a binding resolution for AMCU members at the five individual mines, where they were the majority union ([10]).
2. The definitive meaning and implications of the term "workplace" as articulated in section 23(1)(d) of the Labour Relations Act (LRA) ([10]).
3. Whether section 23(1)(d), as applied, infringed upon constitutional rights—particularly the rights to freedom of association, collective bargaining, and the right to strike ([17]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework underpinning this case primarily consisted of:
- **Section 23(1)(d) of the Labour Relations Act** (66 of 1995), which allows for the binding of non-party employees to a collective agreement if certain conditions are met, notably if the formal agreement identifies employees and the majority of employees are represented by unions that are parties to the agreement ([3]).
- **Definition of “workplace” under Section 213 of the LRA**, which stipulates that it signifies the place where employees work collectively, with an exception allowing for distinct independent operations, thus affecting whether different mining operations could be treated as separate workplaces ([4] and [5]).
- Relevant case law reinforcing the principle of majoritarianism, underpinning much of the LRA's structure concerning workers' rights ([43]).

### A – Application
In the application of the law, the Court applied the statutory definition of "workplace," emphasizing its interpretive center around collective rights rather than geographical distinctions. The definitions indicated that the concept was intended to encapsulate the collective as opposed to individual interests ([24]–[27]). 

The Tribunal noted that the Labour Appeal Court had established that the operations at AMCU-majority mines were not independent of one another, suggesting that they should be viewed as part of a broader industry-wide workplace rather than as isolated entities ([31]). Significantly, the argument that the majority union’s rights should dictate the framework of the entire workforce, rather than the localized dynamics of each mine, reinforced the interpretation of “workplace” to mean the collective of operations under the Chamber’s agreed framework. 

On the constitutional challenge, AMCU argued that section 23(1)(d) unjustifiably limited their right to strike and collective bargaining. However, the Court noted that provisions of the LRA, especially under section 65(1)(a), justified such limitations in favor of promoting orderly collective bargaining within the mining sector ([54]). The justification for limiting such rights was premised on the vital importance of creating effective collective agreements and the instability that could arise from competing unions within individual workplaces ([52]–[56]).

### C – Conclusion
The Court, affirming the decisions of the lower courts, concluded that the agreement in question was validly extended to AMCU members. The definition of "workplace" applied in this instance was determined from a broader collective standpoint, encapsulating the general operational framework of mining companies rather than treating each operation as an isolated entity. 

Moreover, the limitation placed on AMCU’s right to strike was deemed justifiable under the principles of majoritarianism embedded in the LRA. The benefits of encouraging stable and orderly collective bargaining outweighed the individual rights potentially infringed by the extension of collective agreements ([90]).

**Final Order**: The Court granted leave to appeal but ultimately dismissed the appeal, reinforcing the binding nature of the collective agreement on AMCU members and affirming the constitutionality of section 23(1)(d) as it stood ([90]).
