Jointwo Holdings (Pty) Ltd v Old Mutual Life Assurance Company (South Africa) Ltd [2007] SCA 5 (RSA)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007

F – Facts

In Jointwo Holdings (Pty) Ltd v Old Mutual Life Assurance Company (South Africa) Ltd ([1]), the first and second appellants, Jointwo Holdings (Pty) Ltd and Malentino Holdings (Pty) Ltd, were private companies that entered into lease agreements with the respondent, Old Mutual Life Assurance Company, for restaurant premises located in the Riverside Mall, Nelspruit. The third and fourth appellants, Pierre Vosloo and Daniel Francois de Villiers Steyn, acted as sureties for the obligations of the companies under these leases.

Old Mutual, as the plaintiff, initiated legal action against all four appellants for arrears owed under the leases. The claims of Old Mutual were undisputed. The appellants, however, counterclaimed for damages, asserting that Old Mutual's decision to allow a competing restaurant, Spur, to operate in the Mall negatively impacted the profitability of their businesses. This counterclaim necessitated a rectification of the lease agreements.

The Pretoria High Court, presided over by Van der Merwe J, denied the requested rectification but permitted the appellants to appeal the ruling ([1]). The key point of contention on appeal involved a clause in the lease stating that the tenant had no exclusive rights to a specific type of business in the mall ([2]). The appellants sought to amend this clause to reflect Old Mutual's original purported intention of excluding Spur, which had not been properly documented.

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case revolves around whether the lease agreements should be rectified to reflect the parties' original common intention regarding the operation of a competing restaurant ([2], [4]). Sub-issues include determining if the misrepresentations made by Old Mutual's agent could establish grounds for rectification and whether the appellants had sufficient grounds for their claims as sureties under the original terms ([6]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing rectification of contracts in South Africa mandates that parties seeking rectification must prove that the written document fails to accurately express their common intention ([5]). This principle is supported by judicial precedent, notably in Meyer v Merchants’ Trust Ltd 1942 AD 244, where it is established that clear and convincing evidence of the antecedent agreement establishes the common intention that a written contract fails to express in accurate terms ([5]).

The distinction between an agent who conveys a current policy and one who agrees to a term of a contract is critical: an agent can relay intentions, but cannot bind the principal unless specifically authorized to do so ([6]).

A – Application

In adjudicating whether the leases warranted rectification, the court underscored that Old Mutual had not intended to bind itself to a policy communicated by its agent, Dukes. The agent had merely relayed current corporate policy rather than agreeing to contract-specific terms. The court emphasized that, for rectification to be valid, the written document must inaccurately reflect the mutual intention of both parties, which was not adequately substantiated in this case ([5], [6]).

The court also deliberated on the nature of the engagement between the parties. While the appellants relied on assurances from Old Mutual's agent (Dukes), these assurances did not constitute binding representations because Dukes lacked the requisite authority to negotiate such terms ([6]). Thus, Old Mutual's actual intention—as conveyed through the lease’s language—did not support the claim for rectification.

It was part of the appellants' argument that Duques conveyed the agreement that there would be no Spur in the shopping center prior to signing. However, as cited in Ravene Plantations Ltd v Estate Abrey 1928 AD 143, this did not entitle them to rectification in light of the contract’s explicit terms and the absence of authority on the part of Dukes to commit Old Mutual ([6]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the refusal to grant rectification by the Pretoria High Court was justified ([7]). The crux of the decision rested on the principle that Old Mutual had not intended to contractually bind itself to a policy that was subject to change over time. The court's ruling provided clarity on the need for clear evidence of intention for rectification and reinforced the importance of agent authority in contractual relationships.

As a result, the appeal was dismissed, and the appellants were ordered to bear the costs of the appeal. This case serves as a significant point of reference for understanding the boundaries of contractual representations and the requisite standards for achieving rectification in South African contract law ([7]).
