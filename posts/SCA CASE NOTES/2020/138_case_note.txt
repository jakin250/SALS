Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma v The Office of the Public Protector and Others (1447/18) [2020] ZASCA 138

### F – Facts

The genesis of this matter is rooted in significant corruption allegations against Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma, the former President of South Africa, as detailed in the Public Protector's 'State of Capture Report' published in 2016 ([2]). The Report outlined collusion between Zuma, government officials, and the Gupta family, implicating the President in numerous instances of corruption, including undue financial offers made to government officials to secure favorable appointments ([3]–[4]).

In response to the Report, Mr. Zuma initially launched an urgent application seeking to stop the publication of the findings, which he later withdrew ([5]), instead seeking to review and set aside the remedial actions prescribed by the Public Protector ([7]). The remedial actions mandated the establishment of a commission of inquiry to investigate these allegations, but Mr. Zuma delayed compliance, citing constitutional concerns including a purported breach of separation of powers, as outlined in section 84(2)(f) of the Constitution ([8]). After the High Court dismissed his review application, it ordered Mr. Zuma to pay costs on a punitive scale, resulting in this appeal ([12]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether Mr. Zuma should be granted leave to appeal against the High Court's costs order, particularly the appropriateness of the order made against him personally ([13]). Additionally, the court considered whether the appeal had a reasonable prospect of success given the previous dismissal of the review application ([18]).

### R – Rules

The rules relevant to this case were primarily derived from the Constitution of South Africa, and the case law surrounding the exercise of judicial discretion in awarding costs. The principles guiding costs orders include:

1. **Discretionary Standard**: The High Court has a broad discretion to award costs, which will not be interfered with by a higher court unless a material misdirection is shown ([19]).
2. **Precedents**: The Constitutional Court's pronouncements in both Public Protector v SARB and Biowatch Trust v Registrar Genetic Resources established frameworks for assessing costs in constitutional litigation ([20], [23]).
3. **Public Interest**: Courts bear the responsibility to ensure public officials uphold constitutional mandates and must assess their litigative conduct accordingly ([31]).

### A – Application

In analyzing the High Court's decision concerning costs, the Supreme Court reiterated that the order aimed to mark the court's displeasure at Mr. Zuma's conduct, which was deemed reckless and counterproductive to the public interest ([26]–[34]). The Court found substantial evidence suggesting that Mr. Zuma was motivated by personal interest rather than a genuine concern for constitutional principles in launching the review ([34]).

The High Court's judgment underscored that public officials, especially the President, have an enhanced duty to act in alignment with constitutional values and standards ([31]). Furthermore, the Supreme Court held that Mr. Zuma's conduct in not establishing the Commission as mandated contributed significantly to the delay and undermined the public's interest ([36]). His reliance on legal advice to justify his challenge to the remedial action was considered insufficient, as it did not absolve him from his obligations to act without unnecessary delay ([28]–[34]).

The Supreme Court found that the High Court correctly applied legal principles concerning punitive costs, emphasizing that the order was justified given the extraordinary misconduct involved ([30]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court ultimately dismissed Mr. Zuma's application for leave to appeal with costs, including the costs of two counsel on the scale between attorney and client ([42]). The judgment affirmed that the lower court's discretion regarding costs was properly exercised and that there were no grounds to overturn its finding. The ruling emphasized the responsibilities of public officials in maintaining integrity and accountability, thereby contributing to an effective rule of law and democracy in South Africa. 

This case serves as an important precedent in the context of public accountability and the legal ramifications of misconduct within government, underscoring the judiciary's role in supporting constitutional governance and the integrity of public office.
