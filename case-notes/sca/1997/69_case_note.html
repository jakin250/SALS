Supreme Court of Appeal

Republic of South Africa Case No: 334/96

The case revolves around a dispute between two companies: Kate's Hope Game Farm (Pty) Limited (the Appellant) and Terblanchehoek Game Farm (Pty) Limited (the Respondent).

Supreme Court of Appeal 1997

F – Facts ([paras 2–17])

The case revolves around a dispute between two companies: Kate's Hope Game Farm (Pty) Limited (the Appellant) and Terblanchehoek Game Farm (Pty) Limited (the Respondent). The properties in question, located in the northern region of South Africa near the Limpopo River, are separated by the Njelele River, with the official boundary being the river’s midline. The river’s behavior (dry in drought and flowing in good years) complicated boundary management. This dispute arose after the fencing that previously existed between the two farms was removed following a 1992 agreement to form a voluntary association known as the Phillip Herd Private Nature Reserve Association (the Association), aimed at pooling resources for wildlife conservation.

The Appellant withdrew from the Association in May 1992, which subsequently led to the current litigation regarding fence erection rights. The Respondent sought to establish a give-and-take line for a fence, as permitted under the Fencing Act, 1963 (Act 31 of 1963), due to the impracticability of fencing exactly along the river. The Appellant contested this, claiming a pre-existing agreement under the Association's constitution was sufficient for a boundary dispute resolution.

After several proceedings, notably a hearing before a Board established under s 16 of the Act, an in limine objection was raised by the Appellant claiming the Board did not have jurisdiction due to a supposed existing valid agreement concerning the boundary line. The Respondent was ultimately compelled to seek a legal declaration regarding the constitutionality and applicability of the Act in the matter.

The matter was resolved in the Transvaal Provincial Division by Preiss J, who favored the Respondent, highlighting the non-applicability of the Association's constitution given that juristic entities could not be members. Subsequent appeals ensued following Preiss J's ruling favoring the applicability of s 16 of the Act.

I – Issues ([paras 8–12])

The central legal questions in the case are:

  1. Whether the constitution of the Phillip Herd Association restricted both companies from erecting a fence without consent, thus affecting the enforceability of the terms of the agreement related to boundary management?
  2. Is the Respondent justified in invoking s 16 of the Fencing Act given that the Appellant claims an existing agreement applies?
  3. Whether the composition of the Board constituted under the Act was appropriate, affecting the jurisdiction and powers of the Board?

R – Rules ([paras 4–5, 8, 10, 11])

The key legal principles and rules governing the case include:

  1. Fencing Act, 1963 (Act 31 of 1963), particularly s 16, which stipulates procedures for establishing a give-and-take line where natural barriers pose boundary development challenges ([para 5]).
  2. The interpretive principles governing contracts, which demand that the terms be enforceable and not merely permissive. Clause 5.10 of the constitution allows members to "may erect" a fence but does not obligate them to do so, causing it to be non-enforceable as a claim for acting against the constitution's terms ([paras 10–12]).
  3. Principles of contract law as established in Pillay v Krishna and Reisto Dairy (Pty) Ltd v Auto Protection Insurance Co Ltd, indicating that parties must both plead and show compliance with any conditions attached to a contract ([para 15]).

A – Application ([paras 9–12, 14–17])

Olivier JA analyzed the enforceability of the constitution provisions claimed by the Appellant. First, he recognized that while the Appellant argued for the binding nature of the constitution post-withdrawal from the Association, the court deemed this argument deficient. The potential agreement found in clause 5.10 was read as offering a right rather than imposing an obligation to erect a fence. Moreover, clause 9.7 relating to fencing was found conditional upon securing the consent of the Association members who were natural persons. The Appellant could not prove the absence of that consent required for erecting a fence beyond the 5% threshold.

Overall, the court emphasized that for the Appellant to rely on clauses in the constitution, they must demonstrate the enforceability and non-fulfillment of any conditions. The Appellant's failure to provide evidence resulted in the court upholding the Respondent’s right to invoke s 16 of the Act.

Regarding the composition of the Board mentioned in s 16, Olivier JA clarified that any objections raised by the Appellant regarding the Board’s constitution were either premature or lacked sufficient factual grounding, thus did not elicit a considerable response from the court.

C – Conclusion ([paras 16–18])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the Appellant’s appeal, affirming the order of the lower court and reinforcing the applicability of s 16 of the Fencing Act to the properties involved ([para 18]). The judgment elucidates that the constitutional provisions of the Association did not create enforceable rights due to their conditional nature, and the Respondent was rightfully entitled to pursue its claim for a give-and-take fencing line under the Fencing Act. The decision holds implications for how voluntary associations govern property rights, reinforcing the need for clear, enforceable agreements and agreements that can sustain scrutiny in court. Moreover, the ruling shapes future contingencies concerning nature reserves and property boundaries, effectively delineating how mutual consent functions within such shared land management agreements.
