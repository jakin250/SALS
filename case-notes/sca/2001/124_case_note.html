Golden Fried Chicken (Pty) Ltd v Sirad Fast Foods CC and Others

Case Citation: Golden Fried Chicken (Pty) Ltd v Sirad Fast Foods CC and Others [2001] ZASCA 80 (22 November 2001)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2001 [2001] ZASCA 80 (22 November 2001) --- ## F – Facts In

Case Citation: Golden Fried Chicken (Pty) Ltd v Sirad Fast Foods CC and Others [2001] ZASCA 80 (22 November 2001)

F – Facts

In this case, Golden Fried Chicken (Pty) Ltd (Appellant) was the franchisor of the well-known fast-food outlet Chicken Licken, while Sirad Fast Foods CC (First Respondent) was one of its franchisees, with the remaining respondents being the members of the close corporation ([1]). A franchise agreement was concluded between the parties on 24 October 1988, becoming effective from 1 November 1988 for ten years, with an option to extend for an additional five years, contingent upon Sirad serving a written notice six months prior to the agreement's expiration and executing a new standard form agreement ([2]).

Sirad failed to provide the necessary notice, resulting in the initial agreement terminating on 31 October 1998 ([3]). Nevertheless, Sirad continued operations under the Chicken Licken brand, maintaining the terms of the original agreement—paying royalties, conducting quality control tests, and receiving supplies from the Appellant as before. Notably, there was ongoing communication about operational issues, including a request for renovations from the Appellant, and both parties acted as if the franchise relationship persisted ([3]). On 25 August 1999, however, the Appellant notified Sirad to cease trading by 1 October, citing the expiration of the original agreement ([3]).

I – Issues

The legal question in this case revolves around the existence of a franchise agreement between the Appellant and Sirad after the initial franchise agreement had lapsed. Specifically, did the conduct of the parties indicate a tacit relocation of the franchise agreement, thus allowing for continued use of trademarks and operational authority, or was the Appellant justified in seeking an interdict to prevent such use due to the lack of a formal agreement? ([1], [4]).

R – Rules

The concept at play is the "tacit relocation" of contracts, which, in this context, refers to the unwritten renewal of a franchise agreement based on the behavior of the parties involved ([4]). The foundational case law referenced includes:

  • Shell South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Bezuidenhout and Others: Establishing the doctrine of tacit relocation ([4]).
  • Fiat S A v Kolbe Motors: Clarifying that a tacit agreement is considered a new agreement, distinct from the old one ([4]).
  • Additionally, the S A Sentrale Ko-op Graanmaatskappy Bpk v Shifren en Andere case underscores the binding nature of non-variation and non-waiver provisions in contracts ([6]).

For a tacit relocation to be valid, the outward actions of both parties must demonstrate an intention to continue their contractual relationship under the same conditions, notwithstanding the expired formal contract ([4], [5]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the behavior of both parties post-termination of the initial agreement. It reviewed the operational continuity and mutual communications that suggested a desire to maintain the prior contractual relationship. The court found that the conduct after the lapse of the original agreement gave rise to an inescapable inference that both parties sought to revive their contractual relationship on the same terms ([4]).

The Appellant's argument, which rested on citing the non-variation and non-waiver clauses of the expired agreement, was deemed irrelevant once the contract had lapsed ([6], [7]). The judgment highlighted the principle that the expiration of the original contract nullified its binding provisions, thus allowing for the possibility of a new agreement, either written or tacit. Consequently, since the parties continued to operate as if the franchise agreement were still effective, and in light of the tacit relocation doctrine, the court deduced that a new, albeit informal, franchise agreement existed ([7]).

Furthermore, the court addressed complications concerning the term of this new agreement and the context surrounding notice periods for termination, indicating that the Appellant's notice issued on 25 August was not reasonable, particularly after previously instructing Sirad to undertake renovations ([5]).

C – Conclusion

The court concluded that there existed a tacitly relocated franchise agreement between Golden Fried Chicken (Pty) Ltd and Sirad Fast Foods CC, allowing Sirad to continue utilizing the Chicken Licken brand. The appeal was consequently dismissed, with costs awarded to the Respondents ([8], [9]). The ruling confirms the enforceability of tacit agreements under South African law, especially where the conduct of parties suggests an intention to continue a contractual relationship despite the formal termination of the original agreement. This case underscores the importance of parties’ actions in assessing contractual obligations, particularly in franchise operations, where continued cooperation can imply the existence of a new agreement, despite formal lapses.
