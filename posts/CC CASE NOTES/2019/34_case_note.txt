# FIRAC Case Note: Freedom of Religion South Africa v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Others

**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Neutral Citation:** Freedom of Religion South Africa v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Others [2019] ZACC 34  
**Date Decided:** 18 September 2019

### F – Facts

This case arose from a trial of assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm in the Johannesburg Magistrates’ Court, where a father was convicted for brutally assaulting his 13-year-old son for watching pornographic material. His punishment included violent actions such as kicking and punching, which were clearly excessive and unreasonable. The father attempted to invoke the common law defence of reasonable and moderate chastisement, but this was denied by the court, which held that such a defence could not apply to acts of severe violence. Following his conviction, the father appealed to the High Court of South Africa (Gauteng Local Division), which, without the state’s prompting, elected to declare the common law defence of reasonable and moderate chastisement unconstitutional, citing infringements of children's constitutional rights ([paras 3–6]).

The applicant in this case, Freedom of Religion South Africa, sought direct access to the Constitutional Court to challenge this declaration of constitutional invalidity, arguing that the common law defence was integral to their freedom of religion and parental rights ([paras 3, 29]). The High Court's finding effectively eliminated the defence for parents throughout South Africa, with the exception of the Gauteng region ([para 6]).

The case also involved various parties including the Ministers of Justice and Social Development, the National Director of Public Prosecutions, and various NGOs as amici curiae advocating for the protection of children's rights and the prohibition of corporal punishment ([paras 1, 2]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal questions before the court were:
1. Whether the common law defence of reasonable and moderate parental chastisement is constitutionally valid.
2. If found unconstitutional, what are the implications for parents and children regarding child discipline and parental rights?
3. Did Freedom of Religion South Africa have the standing to intervene and bring the constitutional challenge given that the parties in the original proceedings did not seek to appeal?

These issues implicate the interpretation of various constitutional rights, particularly the right of every child to be protected from violence (s 12(1)(c)), the right to dignity (s 10), and the child’s best interests (s 28(2)) ([paras 29–31, 74]).

### R – Rules

The court relied on the following legal principles and constitutional provisions to address the issues:

1. **Section 10 of the South African Constitution** protects the right to dignity, stating that everyone has inherent dignity and the right to have their dignity respected and protected.
   
2. **Section 12(1)(c)** emphasizes the right to freedom and security of the person, specifically the right to be free from all forms of violence from either public or private sources. 

3. **Section 28(2)** identifies that a child's best interests are of paramount importance in every matter concerning the child. 

4. **Judicial Precedent**: The court referred to past cases, such as *S v M*, to underline the imperative of protecting children and their rights (notably the judgment that established the inherent dignity of children).

5. **Common law doctrine** regarding reasonable chastisement, established through historical legal texts and the views of noted legal scholars ([paras 10–11]).

### A – Application

In applying these rules, the court first acknowledged the long-standing acceptance of moderate and reasonable corporal punishment in South African law as a parental right. However, the judgment shifted the analysis to assess whether such a practice aligns with constitutional protection for children against violence. 

The court reasoned that any form of corporal punishment, even if termed "reasonable" or "moderate," fundamentally involves an application of force, thereby constituting a form of violence as addressed in Section 12(1)(c). The court emphasized that the potential physical harm inflicted on children, irrespective of parental intent, raises substantive constitutional concerns ([paras 36–40]).

Moreover, when considering the right to dignity, the court posited that the very act of chastisement, even when deemed moderate, inherently degrades the dignity of the child, leading to a violation of Section 10 ([paras 45–47]). 

The court further evaluated the emotional and psychological impacts of corporal punishment, arguing that any bodily punishment might lead to long-lasting adverse effects that contradict the best interests of children stipulated in Section 28(2). The emphasis was placed on alternative, non-violent means of discipline, framing them as preferable and necessary within a democratic society that prioritizes children's rights ([paras 65–70]).

In scrutinizing the positions of the parties involved, particularly the applicant's argument that moderate chastisement is essential for parental guidance and discipline, the court concluded that such arguments fail to outweigh the constitutional imperative to safeguard children from all forms of violence. The inability to demonstrate that retaining the common law defence serves the best interests of children helped substantiate the court's findings against the applicant ([paras 63-70]).

### C – Conclusion

The court ultimately held that:
1. The common law defence of reasonable and moderate parental chastisement is inconsistent with sections 10 and 12(1)(c) of the Constitution ([para 4]).
2. The application for leave to appeal was dismissed, reaffirming the High Court's declaration of unconstitutionality.
3. Freedom of Religion South Africa was granted leave to intervene, establishing a precedent for third-party intervention where constitutional matters have broad public implications ([paras 1–3, 75]).

The implications of this judgment are far-reaching, potentially leading to increased legal scrutiny of parental discipline methods and setting a judicial standard that prioritizes the protection of children's rights over traditional parental practices. Courts and lawmakers will need to navigate child discipline mechanisms carefully, potentially reconsidering various disciplinary practices within the framework of constitutional protection ([para 74]).
