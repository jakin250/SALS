### FIRAC Case Note: REPUBLIEK VAN SUID-AFRIKA 

**Court:** Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl van Suid-Afrika  
**Case Number:** 390/2001  
**Date of Hearing:** 30 August 2002  
**Date of Judgment:** 13 September 2002  

#### F - Facts
The central question of this appeal is whether the trustees of a trust were authorized, under the terms of the trust deed, to enter into a suretyship agreement for the benefit of the respondent, Shell South Africa (Pty) Ltd, in relation to a closed corporation, Dirkies Vervoer Bk (Dirkies Vervoer) ([1]). 

1. The trust deed was executed in Potchefstroom on 14 April 1994 by Elizabeth Getruida Potgieter, designating her son Hendrik Jakobus Potgieter and his wife Christina Elizabeth Potgieter as trustees ([2]).
2. The income and capital beneficiaries include their three children—Sarel Hendrik (Dirkie), Annemarie Potgieter (First Appellant), and Christina Elizabeth Lloyd (Second Appellant)—along with their children ([2]).
3. Since 28 May 1996, Dirkie has been the sole member of Dirkies Vervoer ([2]).
4. On 24 March 1999, the then trustees executed a resolution purportedly authorizing them to bind the trust as surety for the obligations of Dirkies Vervoer to Shell, which was followed by the signing of a suretyship agreement ([2]).
5. Dirkies Vervoer was finally liquidated on 8 April 1999, and the trust became liable for an amount of R765,132.31 to Shell on 12 July 1999 ([2]).
6. On 14 July 1999, Shell initiated legal proceedings against the trust for payment ([2]).
7. The primary defense raised in court was the alleged lack of authority by the then trustees to enter into the surety contract ([2]).

The trust deed contains several relevant clauses. Notably, clause 1.2.1.1 defines the beneficiaries and clause 7.4 outlines the powers of trustees, including the authority to provide guarantees or sureties for the obligations of others ([3], [4], [5]).

#### I - Issues
The appeal raises crucial constitutional and legal questions regarding the interpretation of the trust deed and the authority of trustees: 
1. Were the trustees empowered under the provisions of the trust deed to enter into a suretyship on behalf of a closed corporation ([1], [2])?
2. Does the trust deed's language allow for suretyships beyond traditional definitions of a company to include closed corporations ([6], [7])?
3. Did the suretyship serve the interest of a beneficiary within the meaning of the trust deed's stipulations ([10], [12])?

#### R - Rules
The legal principles governing the authority of trustees are primarily outlined in the trust deed itself, which contains several key provisions:  
1. **Clause 1.2.1.1** defines the income beneficiaries of the trust and suggests that the trustees have discretionary powers to benefit these beneficiaries ([3]).
2. **Clause 7.3** provides trustees with significant power to perform any action deemed necessary for the preservation and growth of trust capital, specifically noting exceptions related to suretyship or guarantees ([3], [4]).
3. **Clause 7.4.14** explicitly allows trustees to guarantee contracts or obligations of any person or company, with a stipulation that it cannot contravene the beneficiaries' interests ([4], [5]).

Precedent from *S v Moloi and Another* further clarifies the interpretation of "ten behoeve van" (meaning "for the benefit of") in legal contexts, necessitating that actions must indeed benefit the designated beneficiaries to be valid ([11]).

#### A - Application
In the application of these rules, the court focused on interpreting clauses 7.3 and 7.4.14 within the historical context of the trust's purpose ([8]). The trustees argued that the definition of "company" within the trust deed did not encompass a closed corporation. However, the court analyzed the broader language and function of the trust deed, concluding that:

1. **Interpretation of Terms**: The court rejected the restrictive interpretation that a closed corporation falls outside the contours of a "company," determining that the trust deed used broader terminology to encompass varying business structures ([8], [9]).
2. **Beneficiary Interest**: The court examined whether the suretyship benefitted Dirkie, noting that the trustees had the burden of proof to demonstrate otherwise. The absence of counter-evidence from the trustees led to a conclusion that the suretyship was intended to benefit Dirkie indirectly through the operations of Dirkies Vervoer ([12], [13]).
3. **Trustee Authority**: Given the discretionary powers outlined in the deed and the context of aiming to support the beneficiaries, the court found sufficient grounds to validate the trustees' actions in entering the suretyship ([9], [10]).

#### C - Conclusion
The appeal was dismissed with costs, emphasizing that the trustees acted within their authority as defined by the trust deed ([14]). The court upheld the ratio decidendi that the definitions used in the trust deed did not limit the trustees' powers to guarantee obligations solely of traditional companies; rather, the language encompassed broader corporate entities, including closed corporations. This case reinforces the importance of careful interpretation of trust documentation and the broad discretion afforded to trustees in these contexts, ultimately underlining their responsibility to act in the best interests of all beneficiaries involved ([14]).
