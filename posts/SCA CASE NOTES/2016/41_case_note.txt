Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: *Adcock Ingram Intellectual Property (Pty) Ltd v Actor Holdings (Pty) Ltd* (20625/14) [2016] ZASCA 41

## F – Facts

The case involves the appellants, *Adcock Ingram Intellectual Property (Pty) Ltd* and *Adcock Ingram Healthcare (Pty) Ltd*, both part of a prominent South African healthcare group, who appealed against a decision by the North Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria ([1]). The core issue arose after the first appellant's trade mark registration for "LENTOGESIC" in class 5 was removed from the trade mark register due to non-renewal on 23 February 2009 ([3]). This non-renewal was attributed to an administrative error rather than a willful abandonment, as the trademark had been in use for over 40 years ([4]).

The respondent, *Actor Holdings (Pty) Ltd*, applied for the registration of the same mark on 4 June 2010, with the application accepted and advertised on 29 August 2012 ([4], 5). The appellants, unaware of the lapse in their registration, only became aware of the situation when served with a notice from the respondent’s attorneys on 8 January 2013 ([3]). On 11 January 2013, the appellants lodged their opposition to the respondent's application and sought condonation for the late filing, which they argued was justified given the circumstances of the administrative error ([5]).

The central argument brought forth by the respondent was that the appellants’ late application was invalid since it was brought after the expiration of the three-month opposition period prescribed by Section 21 of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([7]). The North Gauteng Division upheld this point in limine, dismissing the appellants’ application without addressing the merits of the opposition ([7], [8]).

## I – Issues

The principal legal issue for determination was whether the Registrar of Trade Marks had the authority to condone the late filing of opposition to a trademark registration application after the prescribed three-month period had elapsed. Specifically, the court had to consider whether the provisions in Sections 21 and 45(3) of the Trade Marks Act enabled such an extension after the expiry of the opposition period ([1], [11]). The appellants contested that implied limitations placed on the Registrar’s powers were inappropriate and that the provisions should be interpreted in favor of granting relief under the circumstances of administrative error and the potential for industry confusion ([4], [11]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal foundations for this case are encapsulated in the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, particularly Sections 21 and 45(3):

- **Section 21** allows any interested party to oppose a trade mark application within three months from the advertisement of the application, or within such further time as the registrar may allow ([11]).

- **Section 45(3)** states that "whenever by this Act any time is specified within which any act is to be performed... the registrar may, on application by that person, extend the time either before or after its expiration," unless expressly prohibited ([11], [12]).

Additionally, regulations pertaining to time extensions (Regulation 52) elucidate how requests for extension should be handled but also underscore that such requests should ideally be made before the expiration of the designated time frame ([8]).

## A – Application

In its analysis, the court strongly considered the interplay between the provisions of the Act. The court a quo erroneously concluded that the Registrar lacked the authority to condone late oppositions unless a request for extension was made before the expiry of the three-month period as stipulated in Section 21 ([8], [14]). The appeal court refuted this interpretation, clarifying that Section 45(3) provides the Registrar with broad authority to extend periods specified in the Act, irrespective of whether requests are made before or after expiration, unless specifically restricted by express legislative provisions ([14], [15]).

The court noted that the language of Section 21 did not impose limitations on the Registrar's powers to extend opposition periods. Instead, it was concluded that Section 29(1) does not restrict this power but merely delineates the time frame for registration upon non-opposition, indicating that extensions of the opposition period are indeed valid ([15], [16]).

The appellants also brought attention to customary practices of the Registrar in interpreting these provisions historically ([10]). Despite the respondent’s argument that allowing such condonation would lead to uncertainties in trademark registrations and contradict legislative intent, the court found no legal basis for such concerns, emphasizing that only express prohibitions can limit the Registrar's powers in this context ([14], [17]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the North Gauteng Division erred in dismissing the appellants' application for condonation without considering the merits of their opposition ([18]). The court reinstated the appellants' opportunity to challenge the respondent's trademark application and remitted the matter back to the High Court for further proceedings.

The appeal was upheld with costs, which include counsel fees, illustrating the court's recognition of the substantive legal errors made in the lower court ([18]).

The ruling underscored the critical context in which administrative errors may affect trademark rights and the importance of allowing legislative provisions to facilitate fair proceedings, promoting certainty and upholding the integrity of the intellectual property registration process. This case reinforces the notion that regulatory frameworks should be interpreted in a manner that ensures fairness and protects proprietary rights, considering the realities of business operations and administrative processes.
