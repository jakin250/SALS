Supreme Court of Appeal

Van Niekerk v Kruger and Others (20632/14) [2016] ZASCA 55

In this case, the appellant, Elizabeth Aletta van Niekerk, sought to have a copy of the disputed will of the deceased, Maria Johanna Kruger, declared a true version of her final testament.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 55

F – Facts

In this case, the appellant, Elizabeth Aletta van Niekerk, sought to have a copy of the disputed will of the deceased, Maria Johanna Kruger, declared a true version of her final testament. The deceased executed this will on 28 November 2006, but the original document was allegedly lost. The primary questions before the court were whether the deceased possessed the mental capacity necessary to execute the will, if the will had been created under undue influence, and the authenticity of the deceased's signature ([1–2]).

Prior to her death on 24 December 2006, the deceased had undergone significant health challenges, including two strokes and other complications such as hypertension and diabetes ([3–15]). The deceased's family was polarized following her health issues, with the first respondent, Gert Kruger, having been estranged from her for several years. The deceased had three natural children and two adopted children ([3–4]).

Significant medical evidence was presented regarding her mental state leading up to the execution of the disputed will. The deceased's cognitive abilities reportedly fluctuated during her hospitalizations, ultimately presenting symptoms consistent with dementia ([8–38]). Testimonies were provided by multiple healthcare professionals who assessed her abilities, with some asserting her incapacity while others contended that her will was valid ([24–42]).

Following her death, the Master of the High Court refused to accept the copy of the will, prompting the appellant to initiate legal proceedings on 28 August 2007 ([17]). The respondents challenged the capacity, assertion of undue influence, and signature validity, placing the burden on them to demonstrate the lack of testamentary capacity based on the Wills Act 7 of 1953, section 4 ([18]).

I – Issues

The case presented several pivotal legal issues:

  1. Testamentary Capacity: Did the deceased have the requisite mental capacity to execute a valid will at the time of signing ([2])?
  2. Undue Influence: Was there undue influence exerted by the appellant over the deceased in executing the will ([2], [46])?
  3. Signature Authenticity: Is the signature found on the disputed will a valid representation of the deceased's authentication ([18])?

R – Rules

Understanding testamentary capacity under South African law is largely governed by Wills Act 7 of 1953, section 4, which stipulates that a testator must be of sound mind, memory, and understanding to make a valid will ([1]).

The courts utilize established medical standards to assess cognitive function, considering the expertise of medical professionals in the analysis of the deceased’s mental state. A deviation from this standard can result in a will being deemed invalid if the testator is found to be incapable of understanding the nature of their actions ([49]).

The principle of undue influence operates on the notion that a will can be contested if the testator was under the control or coercion of another person to make decisions contrary to their own express wishes and intentions ([46], [57]).

A – Application

The court meticulously evaluated the evidence concerning the deceased's mental capacity, concluding that the appellant and the respondents had divergent views. While healthcare professionals like Dr. Edeling and Mr. Ormond-Brown indicated that the deceased had significant cognitive impairments due to her strokes, Dr. Rowji presented an alternative perspective that suggested variance in her mental faculties ([24–41], [42]).

Test results, particularly from the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE), showed that the deceased scored poorly, indicating diminished cognitive function consistent with dementia. The court emphasized the correlation between the medical documentation and the expert testimonies framing the deceased's condition prior and subsequent to the execution of the disputed will. The evidence suggested that, despite periods of clarity, the cognitive decline was progressively aggravating ([48]).

Evidence presented at trial indicated a familial split where the deceased appeared emotionally distressed and possibly influenced by the appellant’s presence and control ([50], [56]). This led the court to underscore the improbability that the deceased could possess testamentary capacity on the specific day of the will’s signing, especially considering her unstable emotional and psychological state ([46]).

In contrast, the appellant's assertions about the deceased’s soundness and relationship dynamics were scrutinized against the robust evidence of apparent cognitive impairment documented by various witnesses, including caregivers and medical professionals ([50], [55]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court's decision to dismiss the appeal, reaffirming that the deceased lacked testamentary capacity at the time of executing the disputed will. The court noted that the appellant failed to convincingly prove that her late aunt possessed the necessary mental faculties to understand the will’s implications ([58]).

The findings underscore the importance of corroborated medical evidence and coherent mental evaluations in testamentary capacity cases. This ruling has significant implications for similar cases concerning testamentary disputes, as it affirms the rigorous standards that courts will apply in evaluating cognitive impairment claims in the context of wills.

The costs of the legal proceedings were also ordered to include those of expert witnesses, highlighting the role of expert testimony in such complex cases ([58]).

This case emphasizes the necessity for both legal professionals and litigants to ensure comprehensive and reliable medical documentation to establish the mental capacity of a testator during will execution, and the potential consequences of familial dynamics on testamentary affairs.
