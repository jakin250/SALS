Case Note: DIE HOOGSTE HOF VAN APPèL
Source Folder: 2002

# Case Note: FIRAC Method for Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl, Saak No: 514/2000

### F – Facts

The case involves **Dirk Samuel Botha** (Appellant), a businessman from Bloemfontein, who commenced legal proceedings against **Coopers & Lybrand** (Respondent), a partnership of chartered accountants. Botha had operated the business of SA Truck Bodies since 1981, transferring it in 1995 to a company he indirectly owned entirely. Since 1982, the Respondent had acted as Botha's accountants until 1995, when allegations of breach of contract arose, claiming that the Respondent failed to advise Botha about possible benefits from the **Desentralisasieraad (DSR)** which he believed his business qualified for.

The crux of Botha's claim was that he had allegedly missed out on approximately R3.6 million in DSR benefits because the Respondent did not inform him that he could apply for these benefits over several years (1 March 1989 to 28 February 1995). Initially filed in the **Oranje Vrystaatse Hooggeregshof**, the trial court, led by Van Coppenhagen R, ruled in favor of the Respondent, stating that Botha could not prove the contractual term he claimed about ongoing advice regarding DSR benefits. This judgment led to the current appeal ([paras 1-4]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue revolves around whether the Respondent had a contractual obligation, either explicitly or implicitly, to continuously advise Botha on the availability of DSR benefits, which he claimed they failed to do. The procedural question arose as the trial court, based on the lack of evidence for a continuous advice obligation, awarded absolution in favor of the Respondent without delving into other disputed points ([paras 3-4], [20-22]).

### R – Rules

The legal principles applicable to this case include contract law principles regarding the existence of implied terms in contracts. The standard for implying a term in a contract relies on the "officious bystander" test, which assesses what the parties would have unambiguously agreed upon had the term been raised during negotiations. This test emphasizes that a term can only be implied if it is clear that both parties intended it to be part of the contract. Previous case law such as *South African Mutual Society v Cape Town Chamber of Commerce* and *Alfred McAlpine & Son (Pty) Ltd v Transvaal Provincial Administration* is cited to illuminate how the courts approach the implication of terms ([paras 22-25]).

### A – Application

The court critically examined whether Botha presented sufficient evidence to substantiate his claim of a continuous advisory obligation. The evidence presented included Botha's assertion that while Havenga (the respondent's partner) had expertise in DSR benefits, there was no explicit discussion regarding an ongoing obligation or duty to inform Botha about these benefits. As per the testimonies, Havenga had only advised Botha during specific instances when requested and had acted upon explicit instructions, thereby weakening the argument for an implied term of continuous advice.

The court found that Botha failed to establish that such a term was a necessary implication resulting from the nature of their agreement. Havenga's conduct did not suggest an ongoing obligation, especially given that, after 1988, Botha did not proactively seek advice regarding DSR benefits, nor had he systematically inquired about future developments requiring DSR applications ([paras 20-39]). The evidence also revealed a lack of established mechanisms for ensuring ongoing communication and advice regarding DSR benefits. Therefore, while Botha's business was explicitly linked to a critical need for such advice, the relationship's dynamics indicated that neither party expected perpetual oversight without explicit prompts from Botha ([paras 29-39]).

### C – Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed with costs, reaffirming the lower court's decision. The ratio decidendi rests on finding that Botha could not prove that an ongoing contractual term regarding the provision of advice on DSR benefits existed, whether express, implied, or customary. This ruling underscores the necessity for parties to clearly define obligations within a contractual framework, particularly when such obligations can have significant fiscal implications. The judgment implicates accountants and financial advisors, emphasizing their need to delineate responsibilities and duties to clients to avoid potential disputes regarding service expectations in future engagements ([paras 40]).
