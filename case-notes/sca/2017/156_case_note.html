Supreme Court of Appeal

Sayed v The State (530/2017) [2017] ZASCA 156 (24 November 2017)

The appellants, Mustaqh Sayed, Aasim Cotwell, and Mohamed Rawat, were convicted in the regional court of Benoni in 2006 of serious charges: murder, attempted murder, and two counts of kidnapping.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 156 (24 November 2017)

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])

The appellants, Mustaqh Sayed, Aasim Cotwell, and Mohamed Rawat, were convicted in the regional court of Benoni in 2006 of serious charges: murder, attempted murder, and two counts of kidnapping. Following their conviction, their case was referred to the North Gauteng High Court for sentencing under the former s 52 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, which allows for higher penalties than those the regional court could impose if it deemed the offences warranted harsher punishment.

In July 2009, Louw J of the High Court reviewed the case and determined that the original trial had been conducted irregularly and not in accordance with the fundamental rights and fair trial principles outlined in the Constitution. As a result, the High Court set aside the appellants' convictions, stating that they were not acquitted on their merits. Subsequently, in November 2010, fresh charges were filed against the appellants in a second trial, where they raised a special plea of autrefois acquit (previously acquitted) and sought a stay of prosecution. The regional court dismissed their special plea, leading them to appeal to the High Court.

Their appeals were dismissed, and the appellants sought special leave to appeal this ruling, which was granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) on 9 September 2014. However, the appellants failed to comply with the procedural timelines for lodging their notice of appeal and appeal record, which led to the lapse of their appeal. As a result, they brought applications for condonation for the late filings, which the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) opposed.

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 7–12])

The central issues before the SCA were:

  1. Whether the appellants’ failure to lodge their notice of appeal and appeal record within the prescribed time frames warranted condonation.
  2. The interpretation of autrefois acquit in the context of irregularities in prior proceedings.
  3. Whether there were sufficient grounds to grant a stay of prosecution due to the delays in the appellate process and the subsequent trial.

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 7–23])

The court primarily considered the principles surrounding applications for condonation for late filings. In Uitenhage Transitional Local Council v South African Revenue Service, the court emphasized that a detailed and accurate account of the reasons for the delay must be provided to assess responsibility and the importance of the case. The court also referred to the criteria for granting condonation, which includes the degree of non-compliance with the rules, the explanation provided, and the impact on the interests of justice ([para 8]).

For the issue of autrefois acquit, the SCA relied on established precedent, specifically the requirements set forth in Manasewitz and affirmed by the Constitutional Court, which stipulate that prior acquittals must be on the merits and not due to procedural irregularities ([paras 24–25]). Furthermore, the court emphasized that the right against double jeopardy is enshrined in s 35(3)(m) of the Constitution ([para 24]).

In assessing the application for a stay of prosecution, the court referred to the need to balance the public interest in finalizing cases, especially where serious charges such as murder are involved, and the accused's rights ([para 37]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 10–30])

In ruling on the applications for condonation, the SCA found that the appellants provided insufficient explanations for their delays, with long periods of inactivity and inadequate justifications for the late lodging of the notice of appeal and record ([para 10]). The attorney appointed to represent the appellants had abrogated his responsibilities partially, relying instead on an administrative assistant, which the court viewed unfavorably ([para 11]). The delays were significant, culminating in the expiration of the necessary timelines, which the court deemed unacceptable ([para 20]).

On the plea of autrefois acquit, the SCA cited Louw J's previous determination that the original conviction was set aside due to serious irregularities and thus did not constitute an acquittal on the merits ([paras 26–30]). The SCA upheld this reasoning, finding no merit in the argument that the appellants could claim protection from being retried based on their irregular earlier trial.

In addressing the stay of prosecution, the court rejected the appellants' claims, pointing out that objective delays that occurred were predominantly caused by their actions, including unsuccessful representations to the DPP ([para 36]). The court underscored that the serious nature of the charges warranted expedient action and that unnecessary delays could adversely affect the rule of law ([para 38]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 40–47])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the applications for condonation, ruling that the appeals had lapsed due to the appellants' failure to comply with procedural requirements ([para 40]). The court reinforced the importance of adhering strictly to procedural rules and emphasized that the responsibility for those failures ultimately rested with the appellants, not their counsel ([para 21]).

The SCA also underscored the disreputable conduct of the regional magistrate during the original trial, which had led to findings of bias and a miscarriage of justice. The court noted that such conduct undermines public confidence in the judiciary and directed that the matter be referred to the Magistrate's Commission for further action ([paras 41–46]).

This ruling reaffirms the stringent standards of procedural compliance that appellants must meet to have their cases heard on appeal and highlights the ongoing commitment of the judiciary to uphold the principles of fairness and justice within the legal arena of South Africa. The implications for the conduct of judicial officers are particularly significant, indicating that repeat failures could lead to disciplinary actions, thereby reinforcing the integrity of the judicial process.
