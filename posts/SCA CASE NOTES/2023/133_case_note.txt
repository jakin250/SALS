Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note - The National Credit Regulator v National Consumer Tribunal and Others (707/2022) [2023] ZASCA 133

### F - Facts

The case at hand involves an appeal by the National Credit Regulator (NCR) against the decision of the National Consumer Tribunal (the Tribunal), which had allowed the filing of a supplementary founding affidavit after a procedural dispute concerning the late filing ([1][5]). The NCR, established under section 12 of the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (the Act), sought the cancellation of the registration of Elevation Trading CC and Xcelsior Financial Services (Pty) Ltd as credit providers due to numerous complaints regarding their business practices ([2][6]).

On July 11, 2019, the NCR aimed to rectify minor issues in its founding affidavit, which had been previously filed, by submitting a supplementary affidavit to clarify the allegations against the respondents ([8]). The Tribunal initially accepted this request, claiming the power to condone such late filings under its procedural rules ([5][34]). However, subsequent review proceedings in the Gauteng Division of the High Court led to the Tribunal's decision being set aside based on the argument that the Tribunal lacked the power to grant such condonation ([19][37]).

### I - Issues

The primary legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the Tribunal had the authority to condone the late filing of a supplementary affidavit ([1][10]). This inquiry involved broader issues, such as the interpretation of the Tribunal's powers under the Act and the relevance of the rules governing proceedings before it ([20][28]).

Additionally, the case raises concerns about the procedural fairness surrounding the filing of supplementary documents and the impact of such a decision on consumer protection within the credit sector ([24][39]).

### R - Rules

The legal framework for the Tribunal’s powers comes from the National Credit Act and its corresponding rules. Notably:

1. **National Credit Act 34 of 2005**: Section 150(e) empowers the Tribunal to condone non-compliance with its rules for good cause shown. This section specifically allows the Tribunal to make orders regarding procedural matters and the conduct it oversees ([13][34]).
   
2. **Rules for the Conduct of Matters before the Tribunal**: Rule 34(1)(d) facilitates applications for condonation of "any other departure from the rules or procedures," provided good cause is shown. The Tribunal’s Rule 3 also asserts its ability to consider procedural matters flexibly ([12][34]).

3. **Legal Precedents**: The court referred to Lewis Stores (Pty) Ltd v Summit Financial Partners (Pty) Ltd and Others ([21]) to emphasize that the Tribunal is to proceed in an informal nature, which might impact its approach towards procedural compliance ([21]).

### A - Application

In applying the law to the facts, the SCA assessed the Tribunal's interpretation of its powers. The Tribunal identified the supplementary affidavit as a procedural departure that it could condone under its rules and the governing legislation ([17][34]). However, the respondents successfully argued in the high court that such actions exceeded the Tribunal’s statutory authority and did not meet the relevant procedural stipulations outlined in the rules ([19][20]).

Moreover, the SCA examined whether the supplementary affidavit introduced new evidence or if it merely sought clarification of previously stated claims. The court ultimately found that the supplementary affidavit did not submit new evidence, thus reinforcing the Tribunal's initial position that good cause was shown for the late filing ([32][36]). The Tribunal’s conduct was critiqued because the high court's second judgment restricted the Tribunal’s flexibilities as mandated under the Act, potentially limiting the pursuit of justice in consumer credit matters ([23][24]).

### C - Conclusion

The SCA held that the Tribunal did possess the requisite powers to grant condonation for the filing of the supplementary affidavit under both the Act and its procedural rules. The court reversed the high court's decision, thereby reinstating the Tribunal's order to condone the late filing and permitting the NCR to amend its founding affidavit ([39][60]).

The implications of this ruling reflect the court’s emphasis on the importance of consumer rights in the credit industry, advocating for an approach that encourages procedural flexibility to ensure that substantive justice is achieved in regulatory matters impacting consumers ([35][39]). This decision underscores the necessity for the Tribunal to operate under an interpretative lens that aligns with its objectives as outlined in the National Credit Act, reinforcing its role in upholding fairness and transparency in the credit market.
