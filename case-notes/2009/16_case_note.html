Residents of Joe Slovo Community v Thubelisha Homes and Others

[2] The inhabitants of Joe Slovo have historically been subjected to forced removals and have gradually improved their living conditions, although they continue to experience severe overcrowding, lack of basic services, and fire hazards ([24]).

Constitutional Court 2009

F – Facts
[1] This case involves an appeal by the Residents of Joe Slovo Community against an eviction order issued by the Western Cape High Court ([3]). The appeal seeks to challenge the ruling that allowed the eviction of approximately 4,386 households from their informal settlement, Joe Slovo, located in Cape Town. The settlement has existed since the early 1990s and has been maintained under precarious conditions, with the population growing amidst inadequate housing services ([20]–[21]). The City of Cape Town owns the land, and Thubelisha Homes, along with government ministers, initiated the eviction to facilitate the N2 Gateway Housing Project, a government plan aimed at upgrading informal settlements ([11]).

[2] The inhabitants of Joe Slovo have historically been subjected to forced removals and have gradually improved their living conditions, although they continue to experience severe overcrowding, lack of basic services, and fire hazards ([24]). Throughout their occupation, the municipality provided basic services, which the residents argue signifies tacit consent to their occupation of the land ([25]). The respondents counter that the services provided were merely humanitarian and did not imply legal occupancy rights ([39]).

I – Issues
[3] The critical legal questions revolve around:

  1. Whether the applicants were "unlawful occupiers" within the meaning of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) when the eviction proceedings were initiated ([3]).
  2. If deemed "unlawful occupiers," whether the respondents acted reasonably and whether their eviction would be just and equitable in terms of section 26 of the Constitution, which provides for the right to housing ([41]).

R – Rules
[4] The relevant legal principles are derived mainly from:

  • The PIE Act, which defines "unlawful occupiers" as individuals who occupy land without the express or tacit consent of the owner ([36]). Section 6 stipulates that an organ of state can institute eviction proceedings against unlawful occupiers if it is just and equitable to do so, considering all relevant circumstances ([89]).
  • Section 26 of the South African Constitution, which guarantees access to adequate housing and necessitates that no one may be evicted without a court order that considers all pertinent circumstances ([2]).

In conjunction with these statutes, case law, particularly Grootboom and Port Elizabeth Municipality v Various Occupiers, underscores the necessity for reasonable state actions in securing housing provisions and ensuring procedural fairness throughout eviction processes ([22][32]).

A – Application
[5] The court applied the law in several critical ways:

  • Lawfulness of Occupation: All judgments accepted that by the time eviction proceedings were initiated, the applicants could be categorized as "unlawful occupiers" because their occupation lacked express consent from the City ([4]). Notably, there was no formal communication terminating any tacit consent, as the applicants had been informed by the City of their need to vacate the premises in light of the N2 project initiation ([31]).
  • Just and Equitable Assessment: The Court examined various factors to establish the justness of the eviction. Yacoob J observed that evicting the residents served to assist in the provision of adequate housing for the broader community while enhancing safety conditions previously threatened by dilapidated structures prone to fires ([106]). However, opinions differed regarding the manner in which the eviction should occur. Judges Moseneke DCJ, Ngcobo J, and O'Regan J highlighted deficiencies in the engagement processes between government and residents and advocated for a plan that would respect the residents’ dignity by ensuring their participation in relocation discussions ([407]).
  • Legitimate Expectation: The applicants contended that they had a substantive legitimate expectation of being allocated a percentage of housing built under the N2 Project based on prior communications. The ruling recognized this concern but expressed that reasonable measures would be put in place to uphold this commitment ([130]), ensuring that at least 70% of new housing houses would be allocated to current residents ([5]).

C – Conclusion
[6] Ultimately, the court grants the application for leave to appeal and partially upholds the residents' objections. The eviction order is amended to include critical provisions that ensure:

  1. Meaningful engagement between the residents and government representatives to identify timelines, relocation logistics, and housing allocation ([5]).
  2. A concrete commitment that 70% of the new housing developed will be allocated to residents previously living in Joe Slovo ([7]).

The judgments collectively affirm the balance between the duty of the state to provide adequate housing and the protection against arbitrary eviction, establishing significant jurisprudential precedents for future cases involving informal settlements and urban development in South Africa. This ruling underscores the necessity for local governments to engage meaningfully with affected communities in planning housing developments, reflecting a broader commitment to human dignity and social justice ([73]).
