Morris Material Handling Ltd v Morris Material Handling (Pty) Ltd

Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa Neutral Citation: Morris Material Handling Ltd v Morris Material Handling (Pty) Ltd (829/2017) [2018] ZASCA 67 (25 May 2018) Coram: Maya P, Majiedt, Wallis, Dambuza, and Van der Merwe JJA

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 Morris Material Handling Ltd v Morris Material Handling (Pty) Ltd (829/2017) [2018] ZASCA 67 (25 May 2018)

Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Neutral Citation: Morris Material Handling Ltd v Morris Material Handling (Pty) Ltd (829/2017) [2018] ZASCA 67 (25 May 2018)
Coram: Maya P, Majiedt, Wallis, Dambuza, and Van der Merwe JJA

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–2])

The appellant, Morris Material Handling Ltd, a British company, sought to appeal a High Court decision that ordered the expungement of its registered trademark "MORRIS" from the South African Trade Marks Register under section 27(1)(b) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993. The trademark was originally registered by MHE Technologies Inc but was assigned to the appellant in 2001 as part of a larger business transaction concerning MHE’s operations.

The first respondent, Morris Material Handling SA (Pty) Ltd, a South African company with a reputation for manufacturing cranes under the MORRIS mark, attempted to register "MORRIS" but was hindered by the appellant’s existing trademark registration. The respondent thus applied for the expungement of the appellant’s mark, claiming it had not been used for a continuous period of five years prior to its application. Initially, the Gauteng Division of the High Court ruled in favor of the respondent, leading to this appeal ([paras 1–2]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–2])

The primary issue before the court was whether the appellant's trademark should be expunged based on the allegation of non-use as per section 27(1)(b) of the Act. This raised several subsidiary inquiries:

  1. Was the MORRIS mark used during the relevant period?
  2. Was such use by the registered proprietor, the appellant?
  3. If there was use, was it bona fide?
  4. Were there any other permitted uses, and were they controlled? ([para 3]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 3–4])

The relevant provision under scrutiny, section 27(1)(b) of the Trade Marks Act, specifies the criteria for removing a trademark due to non-use. It outlines that if a trademark is not utilized for a continuous period of five years preceding the application for expungement, it can be removed from the register.

Precedential case law, including Westminster Tobacco ([para 4]), indicated that bona fide use must relate to the goods or services for which the mark is registered, aimed at promoting trade. The distinction between bona fide use and token use was pivotal, as per the rulings in A M Moolla Group Ltd and Others v Gap Inc and Others and the legal reasoning expounded in New Balance Athletic Shoe Inc v Dajee and Others.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 5–24])

The court meticulously evaluated the evidence presented regarding the use of the MORRIS mark. The respondent claimed that the appellant had not used the mark in South Africa, submitting that their sales comprised solely of cranes without referencing the appellant ([para 5]).

Evidence from various documents, including emails and promotional materials from Konecranes SA, suggested some use of the MORRIS mark but were deemed ambiguous in identifying the specific entity responsible for the mark’s utilization ([para 6–12]). The court found that the complicated corporate structure and lack of definitive proof presented significant challenges.

Although the appellant claimed extensive use through Konecranes ([para 18]), it failed to provide credible evidence of direct trading activities or specific sales figures relating to the MORRIS mark, which contradicted the claims made by the respondent regarding the absence of any formal 'internal arrangement' between the entities involved ([para 16]).

Ultimately, the court found that the ambiguity surrounding the use of the trademark, compounded by the lack of clear evidentiary support and direct involvement by the appellant, undermined the appellant's position ([para 22]).

The failure to demonstrate bona fide use effectively meant that the onus was not met. Analogies were drawn from The Gap and New Balance decisions regarding evidential standards, where the absence of valid proof of use led to a conclusion of non-compliance with statutory requirements ([para 21]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 24])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal held by Morris Material Handling Ltd, upholding the High Court's decision to expunge the trademark MORRIS due to non-use. The ruling emphasized the critical requirement of establishing bona fide use by the proprietor of the trademark during the stipulated period, which the appellant failed to demonstrate adequately.

The court's holding established clarity on the necessary standards for proving active use of a trademark under South African law, particularly in complex corporate structures where the ownership and utilization of trademarks may be nebulous. The judgment serves as a significant reference in future trademark disputes concerning use and registration, underscoring the need for precise evidentiary practices to fulfill statutory obligations. Costs were also awarded against the appellant, including those for the employment of two counsels, recognizing the complexities involved in the matter ([paras 24]).
