Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: **Black Sash Trust v Minister of Social Development and Others** [2017] ZACC 8

## F – Facts
The **Black Sash Trust** initiated proceedings against various parties, including the **Minister of Social Development** and the **South African Social Security Agency (SASSA)**, concerning the administration of social grants. SASSA had entered into a contract with **Cash Paymaster Services (Pty) Limited (CPS)** in 2012 for grant payments, which was later held to be unconstitutional in **AllPay 1** ([3]). The Constitutional Court had previously issued remedial orders requiring SASSA to either award a new tender or assume the payment responsibilities by 31 March 2017, which SASSA assured the Court it would be able to do ([4]–[5]). However, SASSA later acknowledged its inability to meet this commitment, failing to inform the Court of complications encountered since at least April 2016 ([6]–[22]).

The application before the Court was brought forth under emergency conditions, with Black Sash Trust seeking direct access to resolve immediate concerns about the payment of social assistance to grant beneficiaries from 1 April 2017 onwards. The uncertainty surrounding the payment processes was described as a national crisis, potentially affecting millions reliant on social grants ([24]–[32]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues identified in this case included:
1. **Direct Access**: Whether the application for direct access by the Black Sash Trust should succeed in light of the constitutional issues at stake ([33]–[34]).
2. **Constitutional Duty**: Whether SASSA and CPS bore a continuing constitutional obligation to ensure the payment of social grants beyond the contract expiry date of 31 March 2017 ([34]–[42]).
3. **Court Supervision**: The Court's ability to reinstate its supervisory role over SASSA and CPS regarding their compliance with constitutional obligations ([40]–[62]).
4. **Personal Liability**: Whether the Minister of Social Development should be held personally liable for the costs of the application due to her role in failing to uphold constitutional mandates ([72]–[75]).

## R – Rules
The Court relied upon multiple legal rules and precedents to arrive at its decision:
- **Constitutional Obligations**: Section 27(1)(c) of the Constitution establishes the right to social security, necessitating state action to provide social assistance to those unable to support themselves ([2]–[4]).
- **Judicial Supervision**: The Court's supervisory jurisdiction under section 172(1) facilitates oversight of state action, ensuring compliance with constitutionally enshrined rights ([41]–[42]).
- **Procurement Law**: Section 217 mandates fair and transparent procurement processes for state contracts, with significant implications for the ongoing contractual relationships between state entities and private firms ([11]).

## A – Application
The Court granted the Black Sash Trust direct access, recognizing its standing as a public interest organization with legitimate concerns impacting a vulnerable population ([36]–[37]). The judgment emphasized that the urgent nature of the case merited a prompt ruling from the highest court in South Africa.

In addressing SASSA and CPS’s constitutional responsibilities, the Court articulated that their obligations did not cease with the expiration of the existing contract. The Court exercised its powers under section 172 to impose a new remedial framework, mandating SASSA and CPS to continue payments to grant beneficiaries for an additional 12 months. This was justified by the need to avert a humanitarian crisis amidst recognized failures in SASSA's and the Minister's conduct ([48], [50]).

Through a detailed enumeration of expectations from SASSA and CPS during this 12-month period, the Court imposed requirements for regular reporting, an audit trail of transactions, and the adherence to privacy safeguards regarding beneficiaries’ personal data ([67], [76]). In terms of effecting these provisions, the Court ordered that independent monitors be appointed to ensure compliance and provide necessary oversight ([69]).

## C – Conclusion
The **Constitutional Court** ultimately ruled in favor of the Black Sash Trust and its co-applicants, insisting on the necessity for ongoing governmental obligation to ensure uninterrupted social service delivery to grant beneficiaries. This landmark decision underscored the critical nature of the right to social assistance enshrined in the Constitution, holding the Minister and SASSA accountable for their constitutional duties.

The judgment carried significant implications, highlighting the judiciary's role in upholding constitutional mandates against administrative failures. Moreover, it challenged the state to adhere strictly to constitutional laws when dealing with vulnerable populations, ensuring both immediate and long-term compliance with social justice principles. As such, the case stands as a vital testament to the intersection of social rights and administrative law within South Africa's legal framework.
