Constitutional Court

Occupiers of Portion R25 of the Farm Mooiplaats v Golden Thread Limited and Others

The case involves approximately 170 families who were unlawfully occupying a parcel of land, Portion R25 of the Farm Mooiplaats, situated within the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality.

Constitutional Court 2011 [2011] ZACC 35 Decided on: 7 December 2011 ## F – Fact

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 25/11
Judgment Citation: [2011] ZACC 35
Decided on: 7 December 2011

F – Facts

The case involves approximately 170 families who were unlawfully occupying a parcel of land, Portion R25 of the Farm Mooiplaats, situated within the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality. These occupiers sought leave to appeal against an eviction order granted by the North Gauteng High Court which deemed their eviction just and equitable under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE Act) ([1]–[10]).

The first respondent, Golden Thread Limited, sought the eviction following a series of events that began when many of the occupiers, displaced from a nearby informal settlement (Itireleng), moved onto the land in December 2009. Their homes were destroyed shortly thereafter, leading to the legal proceedings initiated by Golden Thread on 21 January 2010 ([5]–[10]). The High Court considered various factors, including the absence of vulnerable groups among the occupiers and the alleged contempt shown by the applicants for earlier court rulings on land use ([7]–[9]).

Despite noting the City’s failure to attend the proceedings and its inaction regarding housing needs, the High Court concluded that the eviction was justified, leading to the appeal ([6]–[10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue presented before the Constitutional Court was whether the High Court correctly determined that the eviction of these families was just and equitable in accordance with section 4(6) of the PIE Act. Additional issues included the obligations of the City of Tshwane to provide alternative housing and the procedural fairness relating to the City's involvement in eviction proceedings ([1]–[17]).

R – Rules

The applicable law is found in the PIE Act, particularly section 4, which provides conditions under which an eviction order may be granted. The Act emphasizes a just and equitable approach and mandates courts to consider the relevant circumstances surrounding the eviction ([5]).

Key judgments were also invoked, including the Supreme Court of Appeal ruling in City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v Blue Moonlight Properties 39 (Pty) Ltd and the case of Occupiers of Erf 101, 102, 104 and 112 Shorts Retreat. These cases elucidated the responsibilities of municipalities and the need for courts to consider the broader implications of eviction orders on vulnerable populations ([11]–[14]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the Constitutional Court emphasized the necessity of a thorough justice and equity inquiry, which was inadequately addressed by the High Court. The ruling criticized the High Court for failing to investigate the City’s resources and capacity to provide alternative housing for the occupiers due to the families' significant risk of homelessness. The Court highlighted that although the applicants had occupied the land for a relatively short period, their evictions could result in serious humanitarian consequences, including displacement ([16]–[19]).

The Court also noted that the High Court improperly focused on the rights of the landowner while neglecting the obligations of the local government to mitigate the impact of evictions on the occupants ([17]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately granted leave to appeal, upheld the applicants' appeal, and set aside the order of the High Court. The matter was remitted back to the High Court for reconsideration, with specific directives to require the City to report on the housing situation of the occupiers and its potential provision of alternative housing. The Court reinforced the principle that property rights cannot be exercised in a manner that disregards the constitutional obligations to provide housing, particularly during eviction processes ([20]–[21]).

The judgment underscores the importance of municipalities in addressing housing crises and the implications of eviction on human dignity and rights to adequate housing, reflecting a broader commitment to social justice within constitutional law in South Africa.
