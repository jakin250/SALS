Supreme Court of Appeal

Gridmark CC v Razia Trading CC (349/18) [2019] ZASCA 18

On 15 February 2013, the appellant sold its business, Yorkers Superette, to the respondent for a total purchase price of R850,000.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 18

F - Facts

This case arises from an appeal against a decision of the Western Cape Division of the High Court regarding the validity of a supposed compromise between Gridmark CC (the appellant) and Razia Trading CC (the respondent). The factual background is as follows:

On 15 February 2013, the appellant sold its business, Yorkers Superette, to the respondent for a total purchase price of R850,000. The payment was structured into an initial payment of R500,000, due within five days of acceptance, followed by the remaining R350,000 in four equal installments due monthly from April to July 2013 ([paras 3–4]).

An addendum was signed on 25 February 2013, reducing the remaining balance to R302,209.33 with the same payment structure. While the respondent paid the initial R500,000, it failed to meet its obligations for the remaining balance ([paras 5–6]).

A dispute arose regarding the outstanding balance, leading the respondent to propose a settlement: a payment of R150,000 in three installments to settle the entire balance ([para 8]). The appellant counter-offered, expressing willingness to accept the R150,000 if paid by a specific date, which the respondent did not meet. Further negotiations ensued but included counter-offers from the respondent that the appellant rejected ([paras 9–10]).

Respondent eventually made partial payments totalling R150,000. However, when the respondent indicated it would make no more payments, the appellant viewed this as repudiation and initiated legal proceedings in the George Magistrate’s Court ([paras 11–12]).

I - Issues

The principal legal issue before the court was whether a compromise had been effectively established between the parties, resulting from the exchange of correspondence regarding the payments and settlement offers. Specifically, it was contested whether the correspondence altered the contract originally entered into by the parties, thus resulting in an enforceable compromise ([paras 2–13]).

R - Rules

The legal framework governing compromise and novation was central to the court’s analysis. The fundamental rules are as follows:

  1. Compromise as Novation: For a valid compromise (a form of novation) to exist, there must be a clear waiver of existing rights, which must be explicitly proven ([para 13]).
  2. Unconditional Acceptance: Acceptance must be absolute and unqualified, matching the terms of the original offer exactly. Any modification constitutes a counter-offer, not acceptance ([para 14]).
  3. Consensus and Consideration: As with any contract, a compromise requires consensus, certainty, legality, and the possibility of performance ([para 15]).

Legal precedents, including JRM Furniture Holdings v Cowlin and academic commentary by Alan Rycroft, were cited to reinforce these principles ([paras 14–15]). The court also referenced ABSA Bank Ltd v Van der Vyver NO, which underscored the requirement for effective acceptance of compromise ([para 18]).

A - Application

The court assessed the correspondence exchanged between the parties to determine if a compromise had been reached. The appellant contended that the respondent's initial offer on 4 May 2013 was met with a counter-offer, imposing conditions not present in the original proposal ([para 17]).

Notably, the appellant required payment of the full R150,000 by a specified deadline, a condition not accepted by the respondent, who instead proposed an alternative payment plan ([para 10]). The court held that since the appellant's counter-offer differed from the original offer, there was no acceptance of the initial settlement proposal.

Furthermore, the court examined whether subsequent payments constituted acceptance of a compromise. In contrast to ABSA Bank, the payments here were made following rejected settlement offers, establishing that these transactions were not made under a mutual agreement to compromise ([para 18]).

The court concluded that the evidence demonstrated a lack of consensus necessary for a valid compromise. The appellant's insistence on the full outstanding amount and rejection of the respondent’s offers supported the finding that no enforceable contract of compromise had arisen ([para 19]).

C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellant, concluding that no valid compromise was established through the exchanges between the parties. The court upheld the appeal, set aside the previous High Court decision, and reinstated the magistrate's court's order dismissing the respondent's special plea, awarding costs to the appellant ([paras 20]).

This case reinforces critical principles of contract law regarding compromises and the necessity for clear, unequivocal agreement between parties to establish any binding obligation. The judgment serves as an essential reference for future disputes regarding the formation and efficacy of compromises in contractual relationships.
