Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1999

# FIRAC Case Note: The State v Manyonyo

### CCT 36/99
**Constitutional Court of South Africa**  
Decided on: 4 November 1999  

---

### Facts
[1] The accused, Mr. Manyonyo, was convicted in the Magistrate’s Court for dealing in dagga, violating section 5(b) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992 (the Act). He received a sentence of forty-two months' imprisonment, with eighteen months conditionally suspended for five years. The conviction was subjected to an automatic review by the Supreme Court (Eastern Cape Division) as prescribed by section 302 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977.  

[2] The magistrate's conviction was predicated on presumptions arising from sections 20, 21(1)(a), and 21(1)(c) of the Act. The reviewing judges determined that the validity of section 21(1)(c) warranted constitutional scrutiny, primarily due to concerns that it could potentially infringe on the accused’s rights under the Constitution. The presumption established by section 20 also needed examination, with consideration for both provisions being deemed in the interest of justice, although section 21(1)(a) was previously declared unconstitutional by this Court.  

[3] On 5 February 1996, the Supreme Court ordered a referral of both provisions to the Constitutional Court for determination, identifying potential inconsistency with the interim Constitution.  

[4] A five-month delay in referral was noted as a procedural anomaly under section 303 of the Criminal Procedure Act, highlighting systemic issues regarding ensuring timely justice, particularly for accused persons in custody.  

[5] Notably, the State had preemptively secured Mr. Manyonyo’s release on bail pending the resolution of his case to mitigate the effects of potential constitutional invalidity.  

---

### Issues
[11] The principal legal question centered on whether section 21(1)(c) of the Act, which establishes presumptions relating to drug dealing, contravened constitutional rights, particularly the presumption of innocence guaranteed under section 25(3)(c) of the interim Constitution.  

[15] The secondary issue involved the constitutionality of section 20 of the Act, which governs presumptions regarding drug possession, and subsequent implications from the Constitutional Court’s ruling in S v Mello, which had already invalidated this provision.  

---

### Rules
[12] The constitutional validity of section 21(1)(c) was previously addressed by the Northern Cape Division in S v Mjezu, which ruled that its presumptions conflicted with rights under the interim Constitution, thus rendering them unconstitutional and invalid.  

[10] Section 172 of the 1996 Constitution necessitates the confirmation by the Constitutional Court of any order declaring provisions of an Act of Parliament invalid. The retrospective effect typically limits such declarations to cases pending judgment or where appeals are within the statutory period.

---

### Application
[11] The Court assessed the constitutionality of section 21(1)(c) within the broader context of established legal principles maintaining the presumption of innocence. The absence of opposition from the State strengthened the argument for immediate resolution regarding these provisions' validity. The Court indicated prior rulings and asserted that the declaration made in S v Mjezu should be applicable throughout South Africa, notwithstanding whether its application was initially confined to the Northern Cape.  

[15] In reviewing section 20, the Court acknowledged that its invalidation had already been established in S v Mello, clarifying that its earlier judgment had rendered further deliberation unnecessary. The application of this invalidity was confirmed to extend to cases where judgments were delivered post-interim Constitution, thereby impacting the proceedings against Mr. Manyonyo.  

---

### Conclusion
[16] The Constitutional Court held that section 21(1)(c) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act was inconsistent with the interim Constitution and thus declared it of no force and effect. The rationale was rooted in the preservation of fundamental rights, particularly the presumption of innocence, essential for justice in criminal proceedings. The Court extended the invalidation to ensure uniformity across South Africa, preventing potential injustices arising from varied applications of the law. Moreover, it entrusted the Eastern Cape High Court to reconvene the matter in alignment with its determinations, ensuring adherence to constitutional safeguards moving forward.

**Concurrence:** Justices Langa DP, Ackermann J, Goldstone J, Madala J, Mokgoro J, Ngcobo J, O’Regan J, Sachs J, Yacoob J, and Cameron AJ concurred with the judgment rendered by Chaskalson P.

**Implications:** This ruling underscored the necessity for judicial mechanisms that reinforce constitutional rights within the framework of criminal legislation, highlighting the Court's commitment to address lapses in procedural integrity that could hinder justice delivery. The consistent application of uniform constitutional principles set a vital precedent for future cases involving statutory presumptions and rights infringement.
