Supreme Court of Appeal

Rehau Polymer (Pty) Ltd v Brunettes Electrical & Others

Following the agreement dated August 2008, CDC was charged with constructing and leasing a factory to Rehau.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 101 (25 July 2019) ### Court The Supreme Court o

Neutral Citation

Rehau Polymer (Pty) Ltd v Brunettes Electrical & others (641/2018) [2019] ZASCA 101 (25 July 2019)

Court

The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Coram

Ponnan, Swain, Mocumie, Makgoka JJA and Eksteen AJA

F – Facts

In the matter at hand, the appellant, Rehau Polymer (Pty) Ltd, is the sub-lessee of factory premises located in Uitenhage, Port Elizabeth, leased from the sixth respondent, Coega Development Corporation (CDC) ([1]). A significant incident occurred on 6 February 2010 when a fire broke out in the low voltage room of the factory, resulting in considerable damage. Consequently, Rehau sought damages amounting to R13,407,025.38 from the respondents, based on various causes of action. While the fourth and fifth respondents were held liable in delict, Rehau's contractual claim against CDC was dismissed by the High Court on 26 June 2014 ([1]).

Following the agreement dated August 2008, CDC was charged with constructing and leasing a factory to Rehau. The contract comprised several warranties concerning the premises, notably pertaining to safety and legality of construction ([2]). Among them was an obligation to install an effective gas fire suppression system required by the Nelson Mandela Bay Municipality's fire department ([3]). Although this system was purported to be installed, a failure occurred; the gas suppression system was not operational at the time of the fire due to the gas cylinders remaining unarmed ([6], [8]).

During the construction phase, inspections were conducted by the municipality, leading to the issuance of a certificate of occupancy on 22 September 2009. However, unknown to Rehau, the fire suppression system was non-functional at the time of the fire ([5], [6]).

Rehau's claim against CDC concerns an alleged breach of warranties, particularly clause 9.1.8, which asserted that the premises would comply with all fire and health safety laws. The central question was whether the failure to activate the gas suppression system constituted a breach of these warranties ([7], [10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue at hand is whether the failure to activate the gas fire suppression system constituted a breach of the warranties stipulated in the lease agreement between Rehau and CDC ([9], [12]). Secondary issues include the interpretation of contractual obligations regarding fire safety compliance as related to the warranties specified within the agreement and the implications of the local authority’s involvement in certifying compliance with safety regulations ([13], [14]).

R – Rules

The court cited several legal standards concerning contract interpretation, emphasizing that warranties are binding promises regarding the condition of goods or properties ([28]). The warranties in the case were integral to the lease agreement, thus necessitating construction in accordance with applicable regulatory frameworks such as the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977, particularly sections 14(1) and (2A) ([15], [16], [21]).

The Act mandates the issuance of occupancy certificates once a local authority determines that the building’s construction complies with legal provisions. The court also discerned that the focus of sections 14(1) and (2A) relates to the installation rather than the operational status of systems once installed ([15], [18]).

In terms of agency law, the relationship between CDC and the fourth and fifth respondents— tasked with either constructing or installing the fire suppression system—was a point of contention, influencing the liability attributed to CDC for their omissions ([24], [46]).

A – Application

The court found that Rehau’s argument regarding the breach of the warranty under clause 9.1.8 was untenable. The warranty regarding compliance with safety regulations did not extend to the operational status of the suppression system; rather, it pertained to the adequacy of the installed structures ([21], [22]). Since the installation complied with the required specifications and necessary permits had been obtained, the court concluded no breach of the warranty had occurred.

Although it was accepted that the gas fire suppression system malfunctioned due to operational neglect by the fourth and fifth respondents, this failure did not constitute a breach by CDC of the warranties as delineated in the agreement. The court acknowledged that the responsibility of ensuring the proper functioning of the system post-installation was not expressly covered under the warranties provided to Rehau. Consequently, the court dismissed the appeal based on the interpretation of contractual obligations outlined in the agreement when the premises were leased ([18], [22]).

However, the dissenting opinions raised substantial arguments concerning the ongoing responsibility of CDC to ensure that the fire suppression system remained compliant and effective post-installation. This viewpoint contended that since the premises in question were high-risk due to flammable materials, the failure of CDC to ensure proper activation as required by safety regulations implicated a breach of warranty which should have extended throughout the entire lease period ([49], [60]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, concluding that Rehau had not proven a breach of the contractual warranties regarding the fire suppression system ([22]). The majority reasoned that the allegations of breach of warranty under clauses 9.1.1 and 9.1.8 were not satisfied, particularly because the fire suppression system, while unarmed, was properly designed and installed ([19], [21]).

Dissenting opinions from Justices Mocumie and Makgoka posited that the continuous obligation to ensure fire safety was inherent in the lease agreement, suggesting that the activation of the fire suppression system fell within the scope of the warranties. This case underlines the complexities intertwined in contractual obligations, particularly regarding compliance with local safety laws and operational readiness post-licensing, imbuing significant implications for future landlord-tenant relationships in high-risk industrial contexts ([54], [56]). The divergent interpretations illustrate the varying judicial perspectives concerning the ongoing responsibilities owed by landlords to tenants, especially in scenarios involving specialized safety systems.

This case establishes a critical precedent regarding the interpretation of contractual warranties in contexts of health and safety, particularly in industries dealing with hazardous materials, thus offering valuable insights for future contractual arrangements and their associated liabilities.
