Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# FIRAC Case Note: Investec Bank Limited v Lefkowitz [1996] ZASCA 84 

### F – Facts
The respondent, Mrs. Audrey Cecile Lefkowitz, was an employee of the second appellant, Reichmans Limited, a subsidiary of the first appellant, Investec Bank Limited ([2]). In 1992, she was offered options to purchase shares in Investec Bank under the Staff Share Option Scheme, which was governed by a trust deed set out in annexure PRJ3 ([2]-[3]). After accepting the first offer for 500 shares and a later offer for 700 shares, Mrs. Lefkowitz resigned her position on 24 January 1994, with her resignation effective on 28 February 1994 ([5]-[8]).

Upon resignation, a dispute arose regarding her entitlements to the shares, particularly due to a misunderstanding about the operative trust deed governing the scheme. Initially, Mrs. Lefkowitz believed she was governed by an older trust deed (annexure F), which was superseded by PRJ3 ([8]-[9]). The directors of Investec Bank expressed reluctance to allow her to exercise her options, believing she did not meet the two-year employment requirement regarding option maturity. After several communications between Mrs. Lefkowitz, her attorney, and the companies, her attempts to exercise the options were met with resistance, ultimately leading her to seek legal remedy ([9]-[10]).

### I – Issues
Three main issues emerged from the case:
1. Which trust deed governed the options awarded to Mrs. Lefkowitz ([9])?
2. Was Mrs. Lefkowitz barred from exercising her options because she resigned before completing the requisite two years of service from the dates of the offers ([10])?
3. Were the proceedings correctly instituted against the companies rather than the trust trustees, as argued by the appellants ([10])?

### R – Rules
The resolution of the disputes relied upon contractual principles surrounding option agreements and the interpretation of the terms within the relevant deeds. Specifically:
- **Trust Deed Interpretations**: The prevailing trust deed was identified as PRJ3, making it the operative document governing Mrs. Lefkowitz’s options. The rights and obligations under the options were directly governed by the terms therein ([11]-[12]).
- **Exercise of Options**: The trust deed explicitly limited the rights to exercise share options until certain timelines were reached. Clause 17 of PRJ3 outlined these conditions, including an employee's minimum service requirement for the options to be validly exercised ([14]).
- **Contractual Obligations**: The interpretation of contract terms must reflect the intentions of the parties at the time of agreement, taken from the external manifestations of such agreements rather than internal expectations ([12]-[13]).

### A – Application
In addressing the first issue, the court concluded that the operative trust deed at the time of Mrs. Lefkowitz’s acceptance of the share options was PRJ3, not the superseded annexure F ([11]). Her early reliance on annexure F was concluded to be legally irrelevant, as she was contractually bound by the terms of PRJ3 once she accepted the offers ([11]-[12]).

Regarding the second issue, the court determined that clause 17.2 of PRJ3 prevented Mrs. Lefkowitz from exercising her options before the two-year period had elapsed. The court found that the options could not be exercised prematurely and that Mrs. Lefkowitz's resignation effectively disqualified her from the share options, as she would not have completed the two years of service required ([14]-[20]).

On the third issue, the court recognized that whether proceedings should have been instituted against the trustees or the companies held less significance after determining that Mrs. Lefkowitz's purported exercise of the options was itself ineffectual. As she was no longer employed when the performance conditions expired, the issue of proper parties was rendered moot ([22]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of South Africa held that Mrs. Lefkowitz was barred from exercising her share options due to her failure to complete the necessary period of employment as outlined in the governing trust deed, PRJ3. Furthermore, it affirmed the lower court's decision was incorrect, and hence the appeal by Investec Bank Limited and Reichmans Limited was upheld. The outcome elucidated the principles of contractual interpretation, specifically regarding expectations of employment conditions related to share options. The ruling underscored corporate governance practices within employee incentive schemes, highlighting the necessity for clear communication and adherence to operative documents ([20]-[22]). 

In essence, the court's decision served as legal clarity on the execution of options and the fundamental principle that acceptance is governed by the current contractual obligations as depicted in reference documents, thereby holding sufficient weight for future similar disputes in corporate law settings.
