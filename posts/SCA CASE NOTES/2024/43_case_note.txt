Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: National Department of Public Works v Fani and 77 Others [2024] ZASCA 43

## F – Facts
The case arises from the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, wherein the appellant, the National Department of Public Works, challenged an order from the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, East London Circuit. This order, issued by Hartle J on 29 October 2020, declared unlawful the demolition of homes belonging to the first respondents, the residents of Farm Greydel, and directed the appellant to restore these homes ([1]).

The background involved an ex parte order from Stretch J on 14 March 2017, which interdicts unidentified persons (subsequently the residents) from occupying or demarcating any sites on the property. Following this order, on 27 July 2020, the appellant demolished the homes, claiming that only incomplete structures were affected. However, the court ruled that the appellant should have initiated eviction proceedings under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE Act) rather than proceeding with demolition ([3]).

The appeal lapsed due to the appellant's failure to timely file the necessary appeal documents, specifically the record and heads of argument, which were submitted significantly late, resulting in a request for condonation for this delay ([4]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions in this case include:
1. Whether the appellant's application for condonation due to the late filing of the appeal record and heads of argument should be granted ([5]).
2. The implications of the delay for both the judicial process and the respondents, specifically concerning their circumstances after the demolition of their homes ([10]).

## R – Rules
The rules applicable in this context stem from the South African Rules of Court concerning appeals and applications for condonation. The standard for granting condonation is based on the interests of justice, which incorporates considerations such as:
- The nature of the relief sought.
- The extent and cause of the delay.
- The impact of the delay on justice and other litigants.
- The reasonableness of the explanation for the delay.
- The prospects of success on appeal ([6]).

Recent precedents reaffirm that an applicant must provide a satisfactory explanation for each period of delay and must demonstrate that the court's discretion should be exercised in their favour ([7]).

## A – Application
In its examination of the appellant's application, the court noted that the explanation provided by Ms. Tyani from the State Attorney's office failed to credibly explain the entire duration of the delay, particularly from 11 February to 1 November 2022, which remained unaccounted for. The court emphasized that the record’s compilation involved straightforward tasks yet took an inordinate amount of time without adequate justification ([8]).

The court criticized the unreasonable nature of Ms. Tyani's delays and noted that the failure to engage with transcribers effectively contributed to the lapse. The impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on proceedings was acknowledged; however, it did not excuse the significant delay, especially considering that the respondents were adversely affected and left without homes ([9]).

The court found that the appeal had no merit, as it was based on an unenforceable ex parte order, lacking clarity regarding the identities of the persons against whom it was directed. The inability to enforce such an order rendered the appeal fundamentally flawed ([11]).

The overarching theme in the application was that condoning the appellant's laxity would be prejudicial to the respondents and contrary to the interests of justice, particularly given their urgent housing needs ([12]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the application for condonation with costs, reinforcing the expectation of compliance with procedural rules and highlighting the detrimental effects of inordinate delays. The court underscored the seriousness of the state attorney's conduct and hinted at the possibility of punitive measures for continued disregard of court rules. This decision has important implications not only for this case but also for future proceedings where the interests of vulnerable parties must be balanced against procedural defaults ([15]).

The court's ruling stands as a reminder of the importance of prompt and diligent legal practice, particularly in matters affecting vulnerable populations. The decision reaffirms that the court will not condone dilatory practices that undermine justice and the rights of individuals affected by state action.
