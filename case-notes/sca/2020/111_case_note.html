Case Note: Government Employees Medical Scheme and Others v The Public Protector of the Republic of South Africa and Others

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020

Case Note: Government Employees Medical Scheme and Others v The Public Protector of the Republic of South Africa and Others

FIRAC Method

F – Facts ([paras 1–10])

This case arises from a dispute regarding the jurisdiction of the Public Protector of South Africa to investigate a complaint lodged by Joel Moagl Tumelo Benedict Ngwato (hereafter "Mr Ngwato") against the Government Employees Medical Scheme (GEMS). Mr Ngwato was previously a beneficiary of GEMS through his life partner, Ms Rakgahla, who was a member of the scheme. Following her death, he sought recognition as her beneficiary but was denied this status by GEMS, who claimed he was unable to provide a marriage certificate as proof of their relationship. Following a change in GEMS policy that removed the requirement for a marriage certificate, the matter remained contentious. Mr Ngwato eventually appealed to the Registrar of Medical Schemes but was unsuccessful. He subsequently lodged a complaint with the Public Protector, claiming that GEMS's refusal to recognize him as a beneficiary was improper and lacked justification.

After some correspondence between GEMS and the Public Protector's office, during which GEMS asserted that the Public Protector lacked jurisdiction over them, subpoenas were issued for GEMS to provide documents and for its officials to testify regarding the complaints, prompting GEMS to seek a high court order declaring that the Public Protector lacked statutory authority to investigate the matter ([paras 4–16]).

I – Issues ([paras 19–36])

The primary legal issue in this case hinges on the jurisdiction of the Public Protector as set forth in sections 6(4) and 6(5) of the Public Protector Act 23 of 1994 (the "PPA") to investigate a private medical scheme, specifically GEMS, which contends that it is a private body rather than an organ of state or a public entity. The determination needed was whether GEMS performs a public function or conducts itself in a manner that allows for the Public Protector's intervention as described in the cited statutory provisions. Furthermore, there were questions regarding the nature of the complaint itself and whether it was properly within the Public Protector's mandate.

R – Rules ([paras 2, 19])

The relevant legal frameworks governing this case were:

  1. The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996: Section 182(1) empowers the Public Protector to investigate allegations of improper conduct in state affairs and has the authority to report and recommend remedial action.
  2. The Public Protector Act 23 of 1994: Specifically, sections 6(4) and 6(5) delineate the jurisdiction and investigative authority of the Public Protector. Section 6(4) provides grounds for investigation such as maladministration or improper conduct by individuals performing public functions. Section 6(5) extends this authority to institutions where the state has significant ownership or control.
  3. Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998: Governs the operations and regulations surrounding medical schemes in South Africa, including complaints procedures available to members.

A – Application ([paras 18–40])

The Supreme Court of Appeal focused on whether GEMS can be classified as performing a public function or if it is an entity subject to oversight by the Public Protector. The court rejected the notion that GEMS operates as a public entity simply because government employees are among its members and funding is sourced from the government. The court emphasized that GEMS functions similarly to other private medical schemes and operates through contracts with its members, hence not exercising a public function as defined legally.

Moreover, the court inferred that Mr Ngwato's complaint primarily pertained to the interpretation of GEMS's rules concerning his membership and the subsequent appeal process already established within the Medical Schemes Act, which he had not exhausted before approaching the Public Protector. The evaluation of the complaint revealed that critical jurisdictional preconditions for the Public Protector's investigative powers, as stipulated under the PPA, had not been met. It noted that the public interest argument presented by the Public Protector did not warrant the continuation of the investigation, as the complaint at hand was deemed moot and did not reflect a pattern requiring broader inquiry.

C – Conclusion ([paras 50–53])

The court held that the Public Protector lacked the jurisdiction to investigate the complaint lodged against GEMS by Mr Ngwato. It ruled in favor of GEMS, overturning the decision of the high court and reaffirming the need for the Public Protector to operate within the confines of the law and the specific powers delegated to her by statute. The ruling concluded that the Public Protector's insistence on continuing the investigation, despite the absence of proper jurisdiction, constituted an overreach of power, ultimately emphasizing the necessity for the Public Protector to focus on enabling accountability within lawful parameters.

Implications

This judgment reaffirms the boundaries within which the Public Protector operates, reinforcing the need for adherence to established jurisdictional limits. It presents a clear precedent for similar cases where the jurisdiction of the Public Protector may be questioned, especially in the realm of private entities functioning as medical schemes. Furthermore, it raises important considerations regarding the exhaustion of internal remedies within statutory frameworks designed to manage complaints, thereby ensuring protection against duplicative and conflicting investigative procedures.
