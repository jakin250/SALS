Case Note: DIE HOOGSTE HOF VAN APPÈL
Source Folder: 2003

### Case Note: Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl van Suid-Afrika

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])
On December 26, 1998, Sergeant John Stover of the South African Police Service responded to a complaint about an allegedly drunk man, later identified as John Christopher Geldenhuys (the respondent) ([para 1]). Upon his arrival, Stover found Geldenhuys unconscious near his vehicle, smelling of alcohol but showing no visible external injuries. Stover arrested him for public intoxication and transported him to the police station, where Geldenhuys was placed in a cell ([para 1–2]).

The following day, Geldenhuys was discovered in a severely deteriorated state by his girlfriend, including evidence of bodily fluids and significant physical injuries ([para 2–3]). He was subsequently operated on by a neurosurgeon, Dr. Wessels, who noted that Geldenhuys had sustained permanent brain damage ([para 4]). Geldenhuys then initiated legal action against the Minister of Safety and Security and the National Commissioner of the South African Police Service, claiming damages from the alleged assault by police or failure to summon medical assistance during his custody ([para 5–7]).

In the trial court, the judge partially awarded Geldenhuys damages, leading to an appeal by the police while Geldenhuys filed a cross-appeal regarding the damages awarded ([para 5]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 16–23])
The key issues before the court included:
1. Whether Geldenhuys sustained injuries from police misconduct (assault) or through the police's negligent actions in failing to summon medical assistance.
2. The determination of the standard for delictual liability with respect to negligence, wrongful conduct, and causation in the context of police responsibilities towards detainees, particularly under the constitutional framework ([paras 23–24]).

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 23–26])
The court examined the concept of delictual liability rooted in common law, emphasizing that the police have a legal obligation to ensure the well-being of individuals in their custody (Minister of Police v Skosana 1977 (1) SA 31 (A) and Corbett AR's analysis of the obligations towards prisoners) ([para 25–26]). The court also referenced constitutional provisions, particularly section 35(2)(e) of the Constitution, which guarantees every detainee access to sufficient medical treatment ([para 26]).

Precedent cases indicated expectations of conduct from law enforcement regarding the health and safety of detainees, and the threshold of proof required for negligence had to be assessed against an established standard of care ([paras 23–24]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 26–40])
In evaluating the evidence, the court found that Geldenhuys had not established, on a balance of probabilities, that police officers directly assaulted him. The testimonies provided were insufficient to justify a claim of assault due to the lack of direct evidence tying the police actions to Geldenhuys's injuries ([paras 16, 37]).

Regarding the alleged negligence of the police in failing to summon medical assistance, the court determined that the officers' failure to conduct a physical examination during their hourly checks amounted to a breach of their duty of care. The court noted that the reliance on the officers' misidentification of Geldenhuys's state as merely intoxicated prevented them from recognizing the severity of his medical condition ([paras 26–27, 31]).

The court acknowledged that the police's defenses were undermined due to unfounded credibility issues, criticizing the police for not adequately documenting Geldenhuys's condition and their vague responses during testimonies ([paras 15–34]). Ultimately, there was a significant delay in providing medical treatment, which contributed to his injuries but caused a complex legal evaluation on causation, especially given the medical evidence's conflicting expert opinions ([paras 34–42]).

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 43–44])
The appeal court held that while the police failed in their duty of care by not summoning medical help for Geldenhuys, there were fundamental issues with establishing direct causality between the police's alleged negligence and Geldenhuys's injuries. The essential finding was that any permanent damage Geldenhuys suffered related to injuries occurring before police involvement, thus undermining his claim for damages caused by governmental negligence.

Consequently, the court allowed the appeal, with the original court's decision to award damages set aside. The ruling reinforces the constitutional obligations of law enforcement to safeguard the health of detainees and clarifies the evidential standards for proving negligence in police custody cases ([para 43–44]). The case underlines the complexities of establishing liability in instances of alleged police negligence while maintaining the integrity of constitutional protections for individuals in state detention.
