Case Note: DIE HOOGSTE HOF VAN APPÈL VAN SUID-AFRIKA
Source Folder: 2013

### FIRAC Case Notes: Die Minister van Polisie v Van der Vyver (861/2011) [2013] ZASCA 39

---

#### F – Facts
This case arose from a deeply tragic and publicized event, where Inge Lotz, a promising honors student, was brutally murdered in her apartment in Stellenbosch on March 16, 2005. Following her death, Frederik Barend van der Vyver, the respondent, who had been in a relationship with the deceased, was arrested on June 15, 2005, and faced serious murder charges. Despite being released on bail the same day, various attempts by his legal representatives to persuade the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) to drop the charges were unsuccessful. The trial commenced on February 12, 2007, and concluded with a not guilty verdict on November 29, 2007 ([1]–[2]).

Subsequently, Van der Vyver sued the Minister of Police, the appellant, for damages on grounds of malicious prosecution, claiming that the police had instigated and perpetuated his prosecution based on false representations regarding forensic evidence. The primary evidence included a fingerprint purportedly matching Van der Vyver's on a DVD case, which was critical in building the case against him. Throughout the investigations, various inconsistencies and misrepresentations occurred, particularly surrounding this fingerprint and its origins, leading to a significant challenge in the prosecution's case ([2]–[3]).

The High Court initially held that the Minister was liable for damages due to the wrongful prosecution stemming from the police's actions. This decision was based on the findings of malice, negligence, and a causal link between the police’s conduct and the respondent’s prosecution ([2]).

#### I – Issues
The principal legal questions addressed in this case included:
1. Whether the police acted with malice and without reasonable grounds for the prosecution of Van der Vyver ([5]–[8]).
2. The sufficiency of evidence to establish a causal link between any alleged wrongful conduct by the police and the subsequent prosecution of the respondent ([21]–[22]).
3. Whether the respondent had proven his damages' claims under malicious prosecution and any constitutional claims for damages ([41]–[43]).

#### R – Rules
Key legal principles governing this case arose from precedents on malicious prosecution detailed in both common law and statutory frameworks. The requirements for a successful claim of malicious prosecution include:
- The defendant must have initiated the prosecution.
- The prosecution must have been carried out with malice.
- There must exist a causal link between the defendant’s conduct and the plaintiff's prosecution (see Lederman v Moharal Investments (Pty) Ltd 1969 (1) SA 190 (A); Rudolph v Minister of Safety and Security 2009 (5) SA 94 (HHA) para 16).

The court emphasized that malice does not necessarily imply evil intent but can arise from reckless disregard for the truth or falsity of information that initiates prosecution. Furthermore, to establish causation, the court applies the "but-for" test, analyzing whether the prosecution would have proceeded in the absence of the flawed evidence or actions ([24]–[26]).

#### A – Application
In applying these principles, the Appellate Court scrutinized the findings of the lower court. The court noted how the prosecution was primarily based on circumstantial evidence and flawed forensic testimony that connected Van der Vyver to the crime scene via a fingerprint, which, as later confirmed, had been improperly attributed ([5]–[8]).

The court revisited testimonies presented during the civil case, particularly focusing on the reliability of witness Constable Swartz, who failed to establish a clear and consistent chain of evidence regarding the origination of the fingerprint. It found that while there were indications of negligence or carelessness, malice was not sufficiently established because Swartz did not, at any point, know the significance of his incorrect testimony concerning the origin of the fingerprint ([37]–[38]).

Furthermore, the court recognized that the DPP assessed available evidence and determined that the prosecution should continue. This evaluation underscored a lack of causational ties leading from any police misconduct directly to the respondent's prosecution ([19]–[20]).

In terms of the respondent's constitutional claim, the court ruled that as his malicious prosecution claim failed on factual grounds, similarly, the constitutional claim grounded in rights violations under the Bill of Rights could not succeed. The appellant's obligation to uphold the law does not confer a right to damages if no wrongful actions were substantiated ([42]–[44]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Appellate Court ultimately ruled in favor of the appellant, the Minister of Police, holding that the malicious prosecution claim by Van der Vyver was not substantiated due to insufficient evidence of malice and the lack of a demonstrated causal connection between the alleged wrongful conduct and the prosecution. The court set aside the earlier ruling in favor of the respondent in the lower court ([44]) and thereby paved the way for clearer standards on police conduct related to evidence handling and prosecution initiation. This case highlights the importance of rigorous evidentiary standards in the prosecution process and reinforces the principle that damages claims must be grounded in well-established legal parameters.
