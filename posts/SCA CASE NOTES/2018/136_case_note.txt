Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Meyer & Others v Big Five Developments (Pty) Ltd (1017/17) [2018] ZASCA 136

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts ([paras 1–6])

The case revolves around an interpretation dispute concerning a Joint Venture Agreement (JVA) between Big Five Developments (Pty) Ltd and various trusts (the Trusts), represented by their trustees. This dispute arose from actions initiated by Big Five in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, seeking rectification of several clauses in the JVA, affirmation of its validity, and an interdict against the trustees' negotiations for the sale of the property to third parties ([para 1]). 

The appellants contended primarily that the JVA constituted a sale of land under the **Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981** (the Act), thereby requiring strict compliance with its provisions ([para 2]). They further argued that a single trustee, Leola Meyer, lacked the requisite authority to bind the Trusts. The high court ruled against these assertions, leading to the appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 2–3]).

During negotiations, it was established that the intended development for the Trusts’ land transitioned from a low-density to a high-density mixed-use residential scheme. Multiple agreements were executed between the parties over time ([paras 3–4]). The representatives of the Trusts indicated that the JVA was aimed solely at developing the land to elevate its market value, with future sales to follow, contrary to the appellants' claims that it was immediately enforceable as a land sale ([paras 5–6]).

### I – Issues ([paras 6–8, 19–20])

The central issues in this appeal were:

1. **Interpretation of the JVA**: Whether the agreement constitutes a sale of land, thereby invoking the provisions of the Alienation of Land Act ([para 8]).
  
2. **Authority of Trustee**: Whether Leola Meyer acted within her authority on behalf of the Trusts and whether proper trustee resolutions were in place for signing the November agreement ([para 21]).
  
3. **Claims of Rectification**: Whether the appellants' claims for rectification were properly abandoned and if this affected the standing of the respondents to bring the action ([para 24]).

### R – Rules ([paras 9–10, 19, 23])

The court drew on established principles of contract interpretation, emphasizing the intent of the parties at the time of concluding the agreement. This includes examining the factual matrix surrounding the agreement ([para 9]) and adhering to the definition and stipulations presented in the written agreement ([para 10]). 

The rules informing trustee conduct were also pivotal. According to South African law, a trust deed can specify the necessity for unanimous consent from trustees or allow decisions by majority vote. The court referenced *Land and Agricultural Bank of South Africa v Parker* to underscore that fewer than the stipulated number of trustees being in office incurs incapacity ([para 21]).

### A – Application ([paras 8–19, 21–26])

In its analysis, the court concluded that the language and context of the JVA suggested it was fundamentally a development agreement rather than an immediate sale of land. The drafting revealed no intention for Big Five to purchase the land outright. Instead, it highlighted a future resale scenario to third parties post-development ([paras 17, 18]).

The court found no inconsistency in the actions taken by trustee Meyer, rejecting claims of illegality due to lack of authority. Evidence indicated that Leola acted with the constructive authority of her co-trustees, who had knowledge of the JVA and participated in discussions regarding its terms ([paras 22, 23]). The court also viewed the abandonment of claims for rectification in clauses 8.1 and 8.2 as a genuine mistake by counsel, asserting this did not negate the respondents' standing to bring their action ([para 26]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 27])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming that the JVA was not a sale of land requiring compliance with the Alienation of Land Act. The court upheld the validity of the JVA and the authority of Leola Meyer as a trustee, while also dismissing concerns about the abandonment of rectification claims, noting they did not alter the core rights and obligations of the parties under the agreement. In conclusion, the judgment reinforced the primacy of intention in contractual interpretation and the authority structure within trusts, with significant implications for similar future disputes involving joint ventures and property development agreements.
