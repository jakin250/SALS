# FIRAC Case Note for Cartoria Motor Industries (Pty) Ltd v J J Swanepoel [2006] SCA 58 (RSA)

**F – Facts:**  
The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa concerns an appeal from the Transvaal Provincial Division involving Cartoria Motor Industries (the appellant) and J J Swanepoel (the respondent). The appellant, a dealer in motor vehicles, sold a 1995 Mitsubishi 2.8 Pajero to the respondent, who later resold it to the appellant's Vermeulen Street branch. The crux of the dispute arose when the appellant claimed that the vehicle was misrepresented as a 2.8 model when it was, in fact, a 2.5 model ([paras 2–3]). The appellant initiated legal action claiming R20,000, alleging fraudulent misrepresentation and breach of contract. The initial sale documents allowed for price adjustment based on the accuracy of vehicle descriptions, which became contentious ([para 2]).

During the trial, critical evidence was missing because the appellant's salesman had passed away. Subsequently, valuation expert Mr. Nel confirmed the vehicle's true model and value, while the respondent disputed the claims and asserted that the appellant was aware of the misrepresentation. After hearing the evidence, the magistrate granted the respondent's application for absolution from the instance, concluding that the appellant had not established a prima facie case for the claims presented ([para 5]).

**I – Issues:**  
The primary legal issues in this case revolve around:

1. The appropriateness of the magistrate’s decision to grant absolution from the instance at the close of the appellant’s case.
2. Whether the appellant successfully established a prima facie case for fraudulent misrepresentation and breach of contract, specifically regarding the damages claimed ([paras 5–6]).

**R – Rules:**  
In reviewing cases concerning absolution from the instance, the courts have established specific principles:

- The test at the close of a plaintiff’s case is not whether the evidence establishes the claim to the required standard but whether there is evidence upon which a reasonable court could find for the plaintiff ([para 5]).
- The plaintiff must make out a prima facie case with sufficient evidence to demonstrate that, without countervailing evidence, a court could potentially rule in its favor. Notably, the standard for prima facie evidence allows for reasonable inferences based on the evidence presented ([para 5]).
- The well-established precedent in *Claude Neon Lights (SA) Ltd v Daniel* outlines these principles, emphasizing that the focus should be on whether the evidence supports the conclusion and not merely on probabilities ([para 5]).

**A – Application:**  
The court found that the magistrate had misapplied the standard for absolution from the instance. The absence of direct evidence due to the salesman’s death did not negate the prima facie case. It was established that the vehicle sold to the respondent was indeed a 2.5 model, contrary to what was represented ([paras 7–8]). 

Expert testimony from Mr. Nel indicated that the appellant valued the vehicle based on its description in the sale documents, which constituted reliance on the misrepresentation ([para 8]). Further, testimonies revealed that the respondent had expressed doubts regarding the model during negotiations, corroborating a potential awareness of the misrepresentation at the time of the second sale to the appellant ([para 9]).

On the issue of damages, the court found that Mr. Nel’s testimony provided a reasonable basis for calculating loss, irrespective of how vehicle valuations were approached (be it trade-in or market value). The appellant had paid R120,000—valued under the assumption of a 2.8 model—while the vehicle's actual worth as a 2.5 model was R100,000, resulting in a provable loss of R20,000 ([paras 10–12]).

**C – Conclusion:**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the magistrate had erred by granting absolution from the instance without recognizing the prima facie case established by the appellant's evidence regarding fraudulent misrepresentation. The judgment of the lower court was set aside, with the appeal upheld. The case was remitted to the magistrate for continuation, with costs awarded to the appellant ([paras 13–14]). 

This case underscores the importance of maintaining evidential standards and the appropriate application of legal principles regarding claims of misrepresentation and breach of contract, offering a precedent for future cases involving similar factual circumstances and legal questions.
