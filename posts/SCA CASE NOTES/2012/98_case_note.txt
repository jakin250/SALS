Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

### FIRAC Case Note: The MEC for Education, KZN v Shange (529/11) [2012] ZASCA 98

#### F – Facts
The case revolves around an incident that took place in June 2003, wherein Simphiwe Shange, a 15-year-old Grade 9 student, suffered a blunt force injury to his right eye as a result of corporal punishment delivered by a teacher at a KwaZulu-Natal school. The teacher was using a belt to administer punishment and inadvertently struck Shange. Initially, the teacher explained the injury was accidental, and Shange accepted this assertion ([1]).

Upon turning 18 in January 2006, following comments from a family friend about the eye patch Shange was wearing, he was advised to contact the Public Protector's office where he learned that he might have a claim against the MEC for Education ([2]). Consequently, Shange consulted an attorney, and a notice of intention to sue pursuant to the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002 was sent to the Minister of Education on 2 February 2006 ([2]).

The summons was ultimately served on the MEC on 3 December 2008. However, the MEC filed a special plea to dismiss the claim, arguing that Shange had failed to comply with the statutory notice requirements set out in sections 3(1)(a) and 3(2)(a) of the Act. In response, Shange sought condonation for the late notice ([3]).

The court of first instance, presided over by Govindasamy AJ, granted the condonation application, leading to the MEC appealing this decision ([1], [4]).

#### I – Issues
The primary legal issues the Supreme Court of Appeal had to consider included:

1. Whether Shange's claim against the MEC was extinguished by prescription due to non-compliance with the notice requirement as stipulated in section 3 of the Act.
2. Whether there existed good cause for Shange’s delay in serving the necessary notice.
3. Whether the MEC was unreasonably prejudiced by the late notice ([5], [12], [22]).

#### R – Rules
The legal framework applicable to this case is primarily found in the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002 (the Act) and the Prescription Act 68 of 1969. Relevant sections include:

- **Section 3(1)**, which outlines the requirement for a creditor to notify the organ of state of their intention to institute legal proceedings.
- **Section 3(2)**, which requires that such notice is served within six months from the date the debt arose.
- **Section 3(4)**, which permits a court to grant condonation for non-compliance if it is satisfied that:
  - The debt has not been extinguished by prescription (s 3(4)(b)(i)),
  - Good cause for the failure exists (s 3(4)(b)(ii)),
  - The organ of state was not unreasonably prejudiced by the late notice (s 3(4)(b)(iii)).
- **Prescription Act 68 of 1969, Section 12**, which states a debt is not due until the creditor has knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the facts giving rise to the debt ([6]).

#### A – Application
The court's analysis began by acknowledging that the facts claimed by Shange regarding his knowledge of the claim were largely undisputed. The court assessed the nature of Shange's awareness regarding the incident: while he understood the injury had been caused by a teacher, it was not until January 2006, after consulting the Public Protector, that he realized the MEC could be jointly liable as a debtor ([5], [11]).

Emphasizing the importance of the knowledge threshold under section 12(3) of the Prescription Act, the court concluded that Shange was not aware of the MEC's joint liability until he received legal advice in January 2006. This knowledge, therefore, dictated the starting point for prescription. As such, the court determined that if prescription had commenced at that time, it would not have extinguished his claim by the time he served the summons on 3 December 2008 ([12]).

Furthermore, the court found good cause for the delay in notifying the MEC, emphasizing Shange's reliance on others for guidance and the attorney's oversight in addressing the notice to the wrong entity. The ruling reinforced the idea that the attorney's mistakes should not negatively impact Shange, especially given his minor status and the complex interplay of factors affecting his case ([16], [18], [19]).

Finally, on the issue of prejudice, the court noted the MEC's vague and unspecific claims of prejudice, ultimately concluding that the MEC had not demonstrated any unreasonable prejudice as a result of the delay ([22]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, affirming that the court below had correctly granted condonation for Shange’s late notice to the MEC. The findings indicated that Shange, upon receiving legal advice, had become aware of his claim before the debt could reasonably be considered extinguished by prescription. Additionally, the court rejected the argument of unreasonable prejudice, leading to the dismissal of the MEC's appeal ([24], [25]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of legal representation and accessibility for minors and those in vulnerable positions. The decision serves as a reminder of the need for courts to exercise discretion thoughtfully in condonation applications while balancing the rights and interests of all parties involved. The court's affirmation of the lower court's ruling further promotes the principle that procedural fairness must accommodate the realities faced by individuals, particularly minors who may not fully grasp their legal rights.
