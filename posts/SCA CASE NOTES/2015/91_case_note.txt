Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Safety and Security v Morudu (1084/2013) [2015] ZASCA 91

**F – Facts**

On December 24, 2001, Inspector Frans Duba (Duba), a member of the South African Police Service, fatally shot Mothibi Elias Morudu at his home in Seshego Township while searching for his wife, whom he suspected of infidelity. Duba arrived at the Morudu residence in an unmarked police vehicle and, after a confrontation with Mr. Morudu, shot him in an incident that left a profound impact on the deceased's family, particularly on his wife, Mapula Pauline Morudu, and their children ([paras 1-3]).

At the time of the shooting, Duba was off-duty but was on 'standby' status, meaning he was expected to respond to crime scenes if called. However, he had not been assigned to work at the time of the incident and was seen using his own firearm rather than an issued one. Moreover, Duba's actions represented a severe deviation from his duties, as testified by other officers who confirmed that his conduct was not authorized ([paras 6-12]).

Subsequently, the family of the deceased filed a lawsuit against the Minister of Safety and Security, claiming damages for the loss of support resulting from Duba's actions. In the initial ruling, the lower court (Molefe AJ) found the Minister vicariously liable, asserting a close link between Duba's actions and the government's responsibilities as a police employer. This conclusion was based significantly on a broad interpretation of previous case law regarding vicarious liability ([paras 16-17]).

**I – Issues**

The central legal issue upon appeal was whether the Minister of Safety and Security could be held vicariously liable for the wrongful act of Duba, given that his conduct did not directly relate to the execution of his official duties as a police officer. The court needed to determine if there was a "sufficiently close link" between Duba’s personal actions and his role as an employee of the Police Service ([paras 18-20]).

Additionally, the decision necessitated weighing constitutional considerations, especially concerning the obligations of the police to protect citizens and the public's trust in law enforcement, as discussed in recent Constitutional Court rulings ([paras 21-27]).

**R – Rules**

The legal framework for determining vicarious liability in South Africa hinges on whether an employee's wrongful acts are committed in the course of their employment or involve a deviation from their duties. The following key principles are derived from case law:

1. **Standard Test and Deviation Cases**: The courts differentiate between acts performed within the scope of employment and those classified as deviations. In deviation scenarios, to establish liability, a sufficiently close link between the personal conduct of the employee and their duties must be articulated ([paras 18-20]).
  
2. **Objective and Subjective Tests**: The assessment involves two questions: the subjective intention of the employee when committing the wrongful act and the objective nature of the link between that act and the employer's business ([paras 26-27]).

3. **Normative Content**: The application of the deviation test must respect the spirit, purport, and objects of the Bill of Rights, recognizing the broader implications of police conduct in protecting the community ([paras 21-25]).

The court borrowed heavily from precedents set in K v Minister of Safety and Security and F v Minister of Safety and Security regarding the socio-legal expectations of police officers, particularly about their duty to the public ([paras 24-26]).

**A – Application**

In assessing Duba's actions and their connection to his role as a policeman, the Supreme Court of Appeal applied both the subjective and objective tests for vicarious liability:

1. **Subjective Element**: Duba clearly acted for his personal interests, driven by jealousy. His motivation was entirely disconnected from any state duty, representing a radical deviation from his professional role as evidenced by testimonies that his conduct was not what was expected of a policeman on standby ([paras 33-34]).

2. **Objective Element**: The court scrutinized whether there was a sufficient link between Duba’s personal actions and his employment. The facts indicated that Duba was not identified as a policeman during the incident, and neither the victims nor bystanders placed any trust in him as a law enforcement agent. The use of an unmarked police vehicle did not sufficiently tie his actions back to his responsibilities as a police officer. The court further emphasized that Duba could have taken public transport, reinforcing the notion that his journey to the Morudu home was not sanctioned or necessary in the performance of his official functions ([paras 34-36]).

The appellate court concluded that the tragic consequences for the Morudu family, albeit devastating, did not suffice to establish a legal basis for holding the Minister vicariously liable, given the clear deviation from Duba’s duties ([paras 36-37]).

**C – Conclusion**

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal and set aside the lower court's ruling, concluding that there was not a sufficiently close link between Duba's wrongful act and his employment with the police. This decision underscored the necessity for a clear connection to be established between an employee's actions and their official duties before attributing liability to the employer.

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the immediate case; it invites a critical examination of how vicarious liability is applied in cases involving state agents and the constitutional obligations of police to the public. It reflects the ongoing dialogue about police responsibilities and the weight of public trust in law enforcement, aligning legal doctrines with constitutional principles ([paras 36-38]). 

The court ruled without imposing costs against the respondents, acknowledging their financial constraints and the emotional toll of the circumstances ([para 38]). Thus, the case serves as a significant precedent in the field of vicarious liability within the public service context in South Africa.
