Source Folder: 1999

# Case Note: Cape Metropolitan Council v Minister of Provincial Affairs and Constitutional Development and Others

### FIRAC Case Note

**F – Facts**:  
The case arises from an application by the Cape Metropolitan Council (the applicant) following a judgment rendered by Van Zyl J in the High Court on 22 September 1999 (see [1]). The applicant sought three primary forms of relief: an urgent hearing of the application, leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court against the High Court's judgment, and to have its appeal heard prior to the Court's judgments in two relevant cases, CCT15/99 and CCT18/99 concerning local government structures ([1]). The High Court had granted a positive certificate under rules 18(2) and 18(6) of the Constitutional Court rules ([2]).

The issues presented to the High Court largely revolved around the constitutionality of the Municipal Structures Act, No 117 of 1998 (the Structures Act), specifically relating to its alignment with sections 155 and 160 of the South African Constitution, along with concerns about municipal autonomy ([5], [7]). The applicant argued that the Structures Act undermined municipalities’ functional integrity, particularly regarding the nomination of metropolitan areas and governance structure ([5]).

Procedurally, the application was lodged following a notice given to the Registrar of the Constitutional Court ([3]). The respondents opposed the application, questioning the urgency of the matter ([3]).

**I – Issues**:  
The legal issues presented before the Constitution Court included:

1. Whether the application for urgent consideration should be granted given the claims of constitutional importance ([11]).
2. The validity of the request for leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court rather than the Supreme Court of Appeal ([12]).
3. Whether the applicant had adequately justified its absence from the proceedings in the Western Cape and KwaZulu-Natal cases where similar issues were discussed ([10], [9]).

**R – Rules**:  
The relevant legal rules and authorities include:

- **Rule 18 of the Constitutional Court's rules**, which governs direct appeals to the Court ([2]). Specifically:
  - **Rule 18(2)** stipulates procedures for those aggrieved by a decision of lower courts wishing to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court.
  - **Rule 18(6)** outlines criteria for granting leave to appeal based on the interests of justice ([2]).
  
Moreover, the constitutional provisions concerning local government, particularly sections 155 and 160 of the Constitution, were pertinent as they directly relate to the authority and autonomy of municipalities ([5]).

Further jurisprudence from previous cases, such as *Member of the Executive Council for Development Planning and Local Government, Gauteng v Democratic Party and Others* (1998) and *Amod v Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund* (1998), provided precedential value on how the Court interprets matters of importance relating to constitutional appeals ([12]).

**A – Application**:  
In assessing the application, the Constitutional Court declined to find merit in the applicant's claims of urgency regarding prayers 1 and 3 ([11], [16]). The Court emphasized that the issues were adequately explored in the parallel Western Cape and KwaZulu-Natal cases, and the necessity for the applicant to have participated as an amicus curiae was paramount, yet it failed to do so. Thus, the court highlighted that this absence rendered their current claims for urgency and priority somewhat moot ([9], [10]).

Conversely, the request for leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court was viewed more favorably given the critical nature of the constitutional issues at hand ([12]). The Court acknowledged the importance of swift resolution to facilitate impending local government elections and to dispel uncertainties surrounding local governance ([13]). The applicant was granted the opportunity to supplement its application for leave to appeal, allowing it to define further matters ostensibly worthy of consideration ([15]).

**C – Conclusion**:  
The Constitutional Court concluded by dismissing the application for urgent consideration (prayers 1 and 3) and mandated the applicant to cover the costs associated with these prayers ([17]). Nonetheless, the direct application for leave to appeal was postponed, granting the applicant time to clarify its position regarding the constitutional questions it wished to pursue ([17]). This allows for a structured approach to litigation regarding the pivotal issues peripheral to the Structures Act and local government authority, thereby maintaining the applicant’s engagement with the ongoing pivotal constitutional discourse ([5], [13]).

#### Implications  
The conduct and outcome of this case underscore the Constitutional Court’s commitment to procedural integrity and the critical nature of participatory justice, particularly for local government bodies. It presents a notable precedent regarding the role of urgency in constitutional litigation, reflecting on the importance of litigants utilizing available procedural mechanisms (such as amicus curiae) to engage meaningfully with ongoing cases of substantial constitutional relevance. Additionally, it reinforces the significance of expeditious resolutions in matters affecting local governance structures as enshrined in the South African Constitution.
