14 Case Note

FIRAC Case Note: THINT HOLDINGS (SOUTHERN AFRICA) (PTY) LTD AND ANOTHER v NATIONAL DIRECTOR OF PUBLIC PROSECUTIONS AND JACOB GEDLEYIHLEKISA ZUMA v NATIONAL DIRECTOR OF PUBLIC PROSECUTIONS [2008] ZACC 14

Constitutional Court 2008 [2008] ZACC 14 ### F – Facts The two cases, Thint Holdin

FIRAC Case Note: THINT HOLDINGS (SOUTHERN AFRICA) (PTY) LTD AND ANOTHER v NATIONAL DIRECTOR OF PUBLIC PROSECUTIONS AND JACOB GEDLEYIHLEKISA ZUMA v NATIONAL DIRECTOR OF PUBLIC PROSECUTIONS [2008] ZACC 14

F – Facts

The two cases, Thint Holdings (Southern Africa) (Pty) Ltd and Another (CCT 90/07) and Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma (CCT 92/07), were heard by the Constitutional Court of South Africa concerning the validity of a letter of request issued under section 2(2) of the International Co-operation in Criminal Matters Act 75 of 1996 (the Act). The letter in question sought the transmission of original documents from Mauritian authorities related to an investigation into alleged corruption in South Africa's arms deal ([1][2]).

The applicants included two companies, Thint Holdings (Southern Africa) and Thint (Pty) Ltd, both subsidiaries of Thales International Africa Ltd, and former Deputy President Jacob Zuma. They contested the issuance of the letter of request after a previous application under section 2(1) was declined due to the absence of ongoing criminal proceedings, as the case involving them was struck from the roll on September 20, 2006 ([9][10]). The NDPP's subsequent application for a letter of request, made ex parte under section 2(2), aimed to obtain original documents initially seized during a search in Mauritius in October 2001 ([5][10]).

The High Court ruled in favor of issuing the letter of request, asserting that the jurisdictional requirements of section 2(2) were adequately met. The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld this decision, dismissing the applicants' standing and asserting that their objections were without merit ([14][15]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues raised in the appeals were:

  1. Lawfulness of the Letter of Request: Was the NDPP justified in invoking section 2(2) of the Act despite the previous criminal proceedings concerning the applicants being struck from the roll? ([23][24])
  2. Infringement of Rights: Did the issuance of the letter of request violate the applicants' constitutional rights, including their right to dignity ([10]), fair trial ([16]), and access to courts ([34])?
  3. Standing: Did the applicants possess standing to challenge the issuance of the letter of request, considering the Supreme Court of Appeal's conclusion that they did not? ([47])

R – Rules

The Court's analysis was governed by several key legal provisions and principles:

  1. International Co-operation in Criminal Matters Act:
  • Section 2(2): Permits a judge or magistrate to issue a letter of request for information pertaining to an investigation before criminal proceedings are initiated, with specific jurisdictional requirements ([1][28]).
  • Section 2(1): Applies when the court is already seized with criminal proceedings and requires that the application be made during those proceedings ([6][7]).
  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa:
  • Section 10: Guarantees the right to dignity.
  • Section 34: Ensures access to courts for the resolution of disputes.
  • Section 35(3): Protects the rights of accused persons, specifically fair trial rights and the presumption of innocence ([15][58]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court found that the High Court's decision to issue the letter of request was lawful under section 2(2):

  1. Lawfulness: The Court held that once criminal proceedings are struck from the roll, they are no longer pending, thereby allowing the NDPP to seek assistance under section 2(2) ([41][44]). It rejected the applicants' arguments that the NDPP should not be entitled to invoke section 2(2), emphasizing that the investigatory process is integral to effectively preparing for any subsequent prosecution ([36]).
  2. Rights Infringement:
  • Dignity: The Court affirmed that Zuma’s right to dignity was not infringed. The statement made in the letter of request about him being a suspect does not entail a presumption of guilt, and the truth of his suspicion does not infringe his dignity ([50][52]).
  • Fair Trial: The Court reasoned that since the request was lawfully issued, the fair trial rights of the applicants were not compromised at this stage. Any potential infringements could be addressed during the subsequent trial phase ([55][58]).
  • Access to Courts: The Court clarified that Zuma’s right to court access was not violated because the legality of the letter did not impede his capacity to contest the admissibility of evidence in future proceedings ([62][63]).
  1. Standing: While the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled against the applicants' standing to appeal the letter's issuance, the Constitutional Court left the discussion open for future consideration. It expressed doubt regarding the correctness of the standing approach taken by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([47]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded:

  • The letter of request was lawfully issued under section 2(2) of the Act, as all jurisdictional requirements were met.
  • The issuance of the letter did not infringe the constitutional rights of the applicants, nor did it violate principles concerning fair trial or access to justice.
  • The appeals from both Thint and Zuma were dismissed. The applicants were ordered to pay the costs of the appeal, including the costs of employing two counsel ([66][69]).

The decision emphasized the balance between individual rights and the state's duties to prosecute effectively, illustrating the complexities of constitutional interpretation in criminal proceedings ([53][54]).
