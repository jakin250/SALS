Source Folder: 1997

# FIRAC Case Note: Constable S P Masuku & Others v D W Mdlalose & Others

### F – Facts
The case arose from an action initiated by the three plaintiffs against the two defendants following the alleged wrongful death of Sipho Ephraim Mdlalose ("the deceased") due to a shooting by Constable S P Masuku, an employee of the South African Police. The plaintiffs sought damages for funeral expenses and loss of support for the deceased's minor children ([paras 2–6]).

On February 14, 1992, it was alleged that Masuku, acting in the course and scope of his employment, shot and killed the deceased without justification ([para 6]). The plaintiffs claimed they complied with Section 32 of the Police Act 7 of 1958, which requires written notice to be given to defendants at least one month before commencing action ([para 13]). The defendants countered with a special plea, asserting that the plaintiffs had failed to provide timely notice as mandated by Section 32, which stipulates that any civil action arising from acts done in pursuance of the Act must be instituted within six months of the cause of action arising ([para 3]).

The defendants contended that the notice was required because the wrongful act was committed "in pursuance of" the Police Act. The plaintiffs did not file a replication to the special plea, leading the matter to proceed to trial solely on this plea ([paras 10–11]). The court a quo dismissed the special plea on an agreement between parties to decide it based solely on the particulars without leading further evidence ([para 22]).

### I – Issues
The principal legal issue was whether acting "in the course and scope of employment" equated to acting "in pursuance of" the Police Act, which would necessitate compliance with Section 32 of the Act for the plaintiffs to pursue their claim ([paras 2–3, 26]). Another relevant question was whether the plaintiffs had sufficiently alleged compliance with the notice requirement in their particulars of claim to establish a legitimate action ([para 13]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal frameworks applied in this case include:

- **Section 32 of the Police Act 7 of 1958**: Requires that any civil action against the state or person in respect of actions done in pursuance of the Act must be initiated within six months of the action's cause and that notice must be provided at least one month prior to initiation ([para 32]).
- **Vicarious Liability Principles**: Common law principles governing employer liability for the actions of employees, extending to acts of employees that fall within the "course and scope" of their employment ([para 18]). This includes the definition of an employee acting within the boundaries of their authority ([paras 6-7]).
- **Judicial Precedents**: The court referenced several decisions that illustrate differing interpretations of the relationship between "in pursuit of" and "in the course and scope of employment," arguing against a unified interpretation ([paras 12–15]).

### A – Application
The court analyzed whether the plaintiffs' allegation of Masuku acting within the course and scope of his authority inherently satisfied the requirement for actions taken in pursuance of the Police Act. Smalberger JA discussed historical precedent indicating that while these concepts may overlap, they are legally distinguishable ([paras 17–19]).

Ultimately, the plaintiffs' failure to explicitly state that Masuku was acting in pursuance of the Act in their particulars of claim led to significant ambiguity. The court concluded that had the plaintiffs intended to assert that the shooting was in pursuance of the Act, they should have explicitly stated such, allowing the defendants a clearer basis for contesting the sufficiency of notice provided ([paras 9–12]).

On evaluating the arguments, the judgment explained that the failure of the defendants to draw the necessary clarity from the particulars may have contributed to the dismissal of the special plea by the court a quo. The protracted assessment emphasized that without clear evidence or admission of compliance with Section 32, the plaintiffs could not overcome the hurdle presented by the special plea ([paras 21–22]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the dismissal of the special plea by the lower court, concluding that the plaintiffs had not sufficiently alleged that the actions of the first defendant fell within the scope of Section 32 as required. The court held that the concepts of acting in the course and scope of employment and in pursuance of the Act are distinct rather than interchangeable ([paras 21–22]). Consequently, the court determined that the plaintiffs' lack of explicit compliance with Section 32 precluded them from maintaining their action against the defendants. The implications of this case underscore the necessity for plaintiffs to meticulously frame their claims and for defendants to ensure clarity of notice requirements within the operational confines of the State’s liability framework. 

The appeal was dismissed with costs, affirming the judgment served previously in the court a quo ([para 24]).
