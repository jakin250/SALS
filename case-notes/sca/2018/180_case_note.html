Supreme Court of Appeal

Masuku & Ano v SAHRC (1062/2017)

The case revolves around the complaint lodged by the South African Jewish Board of Deputies (SAJBOD) against Bongani Masuku, who was, at the time, acting as the secretary for the International Relations arm of the Congress of South African Trade Unions (COSATU).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018

F – Facts

The case revolves around the complaint lodged by the South African Jewish Board of Deputies (SAJBOD) against Bongani Masuku, who was, at the time, acting as the secretary for the International Relations arm of the Congress of South African Trade Unions (COSATU). The complaint was based on statements made by Masuku on 6 February and 5 March 2009, which the SAJBOD alleged constituted hate speech (§1-2).

These statements were made in the context of heightened tensions arising from the Israeli military operations in Gaza during early 2009, which had led to significant civilian casualties. In contrast to the SAJBOD's supportive stance towards Israel, Masuku and COSATU organized protests denouncing these actions and expressed solidarity with the Palestinian people (§2-4).

The precipitating events included an online blog post by Masuku, wherein he described Zionists disdainfully and as "racists" and "fascists," as well as incendiary statements made during a speech at the University of the Witwatersrand, wherein he threatened South African families who supported the Israeli defense forces (§5-7). The Human Rights Commission initially found Masuku's statements to violate the principles of the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act (Equality Act) and subsequently referred the matter to the Equality Court, which confirmed this view (§8-11).

I – Issues

The central issue on appeal was whether Masuku's statements constituted hate speech as defined under section 16(2)(c) of the South African Constitution. Specifically, the court needed to ascertain whether his remarks amounted to advocacy of hatred based on religion or ethnicity, which would exclude them from the protections afforded by section 16(1), which guarantees freedom of expression (§12-13).

Moreover, a crucial secondary issue arose concerning the interpretation of "hate speech" itself—whether the Equality Court had misapplied the criteria for classifying speech as hateful by correlating support for Zionism with Jewish identity, and consequently whether Masuku's statements were indeed targeting individuals based on their race or religion (§12-14).

R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this matter is encapsulated within the South African Constitution, particularly sections 16(1) and 16(2). Section 16(1) articulates the right to freedom of expression, while section 16(2) specifically delineates exceptions to this right. Notably, it prohibits advocacy of hatred based on race, ethnicity, gender, or religion when it constitutes incitement to cause harm (§15).

Additionally, rulings from the Constitutional Court and other judicial decisions elaborate on the bounds of this constitutional guarantee. The courts maintain a strong inclination towards protecting expression, even when it may be politically inflammatory or offensive, requiring strict interpretation of any limitations (§16-19).

In Hotz & Others v University of Cape Town and Democratic Alliance v African National Congress, the importance of context in evaluating potentially harmful statements was emphasized, indicating that a mere provocative tone does not render speech automatically as hate speech (§19-31).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal undertook a meticulous review of the statements made by Masuku, emphasizing the necessity of contextual analysis. The court articulated that the hate speech provisions under section 16(2)(c) do not merely rest on the emotional impact of words but rather on their intent and actual harm caused (§31).

In examining Masuku's blog posts and speeches, the court found that although his language might be deemed inflammatory, it did not meet the threshold of incitement to cause harm or promote hatred in the manner outlined by section 16(2). The court underscored that Masuku's expressions were directed towards political ideologies rather than aimed at promoting harm against Jewish citizens per se (§26-30).

The analysis also addressed the Equality Court's reliance on international jurisprudence and expert testimony regarding anti-Zionism and anti-Semitism. It concluded that such reliance did not serve to clarify the hate speech allegations but rather muddied the understanding of the issues at hand (§20-23).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of Masuku, overturning the findings of the Equality Court. The court held that his statements did not constitute hate speech as defined by the Constitution, focusing primarily on the contextual interpretation of the statements made during a politically charged event (§32).

As a result of this ruling, the appeal was upheld, the complaint dismissed, and each party was ordered to bear its own costs, underscoring the court's commitment to defend the principle of freedom of expression within the constitutional framework (§32). This decision has significant implications for the ongoing discourse on the limits of free speech and the criteria for hate speech in South Africa, affirming the necessity for a balanced approach that protects individuals' rights to express politically charged opinions without unjustified limitations.
