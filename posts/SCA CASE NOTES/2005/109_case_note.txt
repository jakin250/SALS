Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2005

# Case Note: *Trustees for the Time Being of Two Oceans Aquarium Trust v Kantey & Templer (Pty) Ltd* 

## FIRAC 

### F – Facts:

This appeal arises from a delictual claim concerning alleged pure economic loss due to negligence in the design of exhibit tanks at the Two Oceans Aquarium. The appellants, the Trustees for the Time Being of the Two Oceans Aquarium Trust ("the trust"), operate the aquarium located at the Victoria and Alfred Waterfront in Cape Town. They initiated action in the Cape High Court against the respondent, Kantey & Templer (Pty) Ltd, along with five other defendants, seeking damages amounting to R14,924,395.00. This claim stemmed from failures in the aquarium's exhibit tanks, where the polyurethane lining used for waterproofing was purportedly porous, permitting seawater incursion, which caused internal corrosion ([1]-[3]).

The appellants argued that poor design alternatives were chosen by the project manager and the structural engineers (including the respondent) regarding the waterproofing of the tanks. They claimed damages for the costs of remedial work necessary to replace the waterproof lining and for the anticipated loss of revenue incurred during the correction of these issues, primarily from the deterioration of financial returns while remedial work was undertaken ([2]-[4]).

Three exceptions were raised by the respondent against the particulars of claim, leading to a ruling in which one exception was upheld and the associated allegations struck out with costs. The appellants were granted leave to appeal the exception upheld and the resultant costs ([2]-[2]).

### I – Issues:

The primary legal question presented on appeal is the existence of a legal duty owed by the respondent to the trust regarding the alleged negligent decision-making made prior to the formal establishment and contractual agreement with the trust. This raises several issues, particularly surrounding the concepts of “wrongfulness” and the circumstances under which a duty of care can be established for claims arising from pure economic loss ([3]-[10]).

Critical questions include:
1. Is there a legal duty owed by the respondent to the trust, given that the negligent conduct allegedly occurred before the trust was formally constituted and contracted?
2. Does the claim constitute pure economic loss, and if so, what implications does that have for establishing liability under South African delict law?
3. Can the appellants rely on delictual recovery when the claims seem to intertwine with contractual expectations? ([9]-[10]).

### R – Rules:

The decision-making framework established for determining liability in delictual claims, particularly concerning pure economic loss, is significant. The legal principle asserts that negligent conduct resulting in economic loss is only actionable if deemed "wrongful". The courts typically determine wrongfulness based on public or legal policy. Traditionally, negligent acts leading to physical damage are considered prima facie wrongful, while negligent omissions or actions causing pure economic loss require demonstrating the existence of a legal duty ([10]-[12]).

Important case law referenced includes *Minister of Safety and Security v Van Duivenboden* (2002) and *Gouda Boerdery BK v Transnet* (2005), noting that without demonstrable legal duty, liability may not be imposed. The application of the principles from *Lillicrap, Wassenaar and Partners v Pilkington Brothers (Pty) Ltd* (1985) underscores the necessity of establishing a legal duty independent of contractual terms to ground a delictual claim ([13]-[14]).

### A – Application:

In applying the rules to the facts, it became essential to determine whether any duties arising from the negligent decisions made by the respondent before the trust was formed could be construed as legally actionable. The court had to assess whether the respondent’s conduct bore the necessary ‘wrongfulness’ as required by de facto law ([10]-[19]).

The court’s analysis highlighted essential differences between this case and the precedent established in *Lillicrap*. The ruling in *Lillicrap* emphasized that contractual relationships provide a means of recourse for aggrieved parties, suggesting that overlapping claims in delict often should not arise where established contract law exists to offer remedy and redress ([14]-[19]). Here, however, the appellants contended that the absence of formal contract negated this reasoning, thereby necessitating the inquiry into the occurrence of a legal duty based on public or legal policy ([20]-[21]).

Ultimately, the court underscored the propositions that without an existing contract to encapsulate the duty of care that the trust could leverage against the respondent for the negligent conduct, no action could stand. The court cited the importance of distinguishing relational dynamics within the framework of contractual versus delictual claims, explaining why extending such liability absent existing contractual obligations was not justified ([22]-[28]).

### C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the decision by the Cape High Court to uphold the exception raised by the respondent. The court concluded that the crucial issue of establishing wrongful conduct was not satisfied; no recognizable legal duty existed since the alleged negligent conduct occurred before any contractual relationship was established between the parties. Thus, the rationale of the existing case law guided the court's decision not to extend the delictual liability in this context. 

This judgment clarifies the precarious balance courts must maintain when adjudicating claims involving delictual liability for pure economic loss, particularly in situations where contractual relationships or the lack thereof play a pivotal role in determining the availability of legal remedies ([19]-[28]). 

The implications of this case underscore the critical intersection of delict and contract law within the South African legal framework, particularly as it relates to structural engineering and professional negligence, reinforcing the importance of clearly defining legal responsibilities in contractual agreements.
