Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Minister of Police v Shawn Bosman & Others (1163/2020) [2021] ZASCA 172 (9 December 2021)

### F – Facts ([paras 1–10])
This case arises from an appeal by the Minister of Police against a judgment from the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court that confirmed the arrest and detention of the respondents—Shawn Bosman and others—as unlawful. The respondents were arrested during a high-speed chase on New Year’s Eve, 31 December 2013, after a shooting incident was reported in Malabar, Port Elizabeth. Officer Deon Goeda, while on patrol, received information regarding the shooting suspects who had fled in a black Nissan bakkie. Upon spotting the bakkie, Goeda pursued it and arrested its occupants after a brief chase. The bakkie contained nine individuals, including minors and the respondents, none of whom initially claimed possession of a firearm eventually found to have been discarded from the vehicle.

The arrests were justified by Goeda's suspicion that the occupants might be involved in the shooting incident due to the high-speed chase and the discovery of the firearm. However, the high court found these arrests to be unlawful, leading to the possibility of claims for damages from the Minister of Police, which stemmed from their detention without warrant.

The case was later brought before the Supreme Court of Appeal with the contention that the arrests were justifiable under various sections of the Criminal Procedure Act of 1977 (CPA).

### I – Issues ([paras 2–10])
The key legal issues in this case are:
1. Whether the arrests of the respondents were lawful under sections 40(1)(b), (f), and (h) of the CPA.
2. Whether the subsequent detention of the respondents was lawful.
3. The implications of the lawfulness of their arrests on the entitlement to damages for wrongful arrest and detention.

### R – Rules ([paras 11–12])
The case touches on multiple constitutional and statutory rules:
- **Constitution of South Africa**: Section 12(1)(a) protects the right to freedom and security, which includes protection against arbitrary arrest and detention.
- **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**:
  - Section 40(1) provides the conditions under which a peace officer may arrest without a warrant, specifically targeting suspicion of offenses listed in Schedule 1 or offenses related to substances or firearms.
  - Section 50 regulates the detention of individuals, requiring they be brought before a court within 48 hours of arrest.
- **Precedents**: Relevant case law includes *Duncan v Minister of Law and Order* and *Minister of Safety and Security v Sekhoto*, which outline the requirements for lawful arrests, focusing on the necessity for reasonable suspicion and the intention to bring the suspect to justice.

### A – Application ([paras 13–33])
The judgment's application of the law centered on determining whether Officer Goeda's actions met the criteria set forth in the relevant sections of the CPA. The court analyzed Goeda's rationale for arresting all occupants of the bakkie, concluding that reasonable suspicion existed based on:
- Information received about a shooting and flight from law enforcement.
- The high-speed chase and reckless driving exhibited by the respondents.
- The discovery of a firearm linked to the shooting incident.

The court emphasized the objective standard for evaluating reasonable suspicion. It stated that Goeda’s decision to arrest was based on an appropriate interpretation of the circumstances as relayed through police communications and his own experiences during the chase.

Furthermore, the court upheld that the detention of the respondents was lawful under Section 50 of the CPA, as the 48-hour rule was adhered to, and they were processed correctly at the police station. Officer Goeda's actions were deemed to be in line with the SAPS’s mandate to conduct investigations and secure evidence. The explanation provided by police on the delays in processing the respondents, particularly due to the public holiday, was determined to be reasonable.

### C – Conclusion ([paras 33–34])
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that both the arrests and subsequent detentions of the respondents were lawful and justifiable. The court overturned the high court’s finding and dismissed the respondents' claims for damages against the Minister of Police. The court emphasized the necessity of allowing law enforcement adequate discretion to act, particularly in urgent circumstances related to public safety and criminal investigation.

Furthermore, the judgment reaffirmed the significance of upholding constitutional principles while balancing the operational efficacy of the SAPS within the legal framework. The implications of this ruling underscore the judiciary's role in overseeing arrest protocols while providing leeway for police officers to act under exigent circumstances, reinforcing the importance of reasonableness and proper procedural adherence in maintaining public confidence in law enforcement methods.

In summary, the court’s ruling not only vindicated the actions of the police officers involved but also articulated a clear stance on the legality of arrest and detention protocols, contributing to the ongoing discourse regarding law enforcement in South Africa.
