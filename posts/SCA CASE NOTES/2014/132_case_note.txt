Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note: Avnit v First Rand Bank Ltd (20233/14) [2014] ZASCA 132

## F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

The case centers on an appeal by David Samuel Avnit (the appellant), following a money judgment granted against him by the South Gauteng High Court in Johannesburg in favor of First Rand Bank, trading as Wesbank (the respondent). The bank sought payment of USD 3,655,389.97 based on a surety agreement wherein Avnit acted as surety for Norse Gulfstream Ltd, the principal debtor, concerning a loan arrangement. On May 29, 2009, the High Court authorized Wesbank to take possession of a Gulfstream GIII jet aircraft owned by the principal debtor to secure the debt.

Following the adjudication of the initial judgment, Avnit’s request for leave to appeal was refused by both the High Court and subsequently, by two judges of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). Subsequently, Avnit applied to the President of the SCA under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, seeking a reconsideration of the leave refusal due to alleged "exceptional circumstances" ([paras 1-3]).

In the proceedings leading up to the appeal, Avnit contested the High Court's refusal to accept his supplementary affidavit while the respondent had opted to abandon efforts to submit a replying affidavit after having initially sought condonation for late submission. He argued that the judge's failure to consider certain admissions made by Wesbank in its abandoned affidavit resulted in an erroneous judgment, infringing upon his constitutional right to a fair trial as protected under section 34 of the Constitution of South Africa ([paras 10-11]).

## I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 4–12])

The core issue revolves around the interpretation and application of "exceptional circumstances" per section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act, specifically whether Avnit established the requisite conditions for the President of the SCA to refer the matter for reconsideration ([para 4]). 

Another significant issue involves the determination of whether the lack of consideration for the alleged admissions by Wesbank constituted a denial of justice that would support Avnit’s claims regarding the safeguarding of his rights as stipulated under section 34 of the Constitution ([para 12]).

## R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 2–6])

The decision primarily relies on section 17 of the Superior Courts Act, which delineates the procedure for applications for leave to appeal, and establishes that the President of the SCA can, in exceptional circumstances, refer a matter for reconsideration ([para 2]).

The interpretation of "exceptional circumstances" is guided by historical precedents, including *Norwich Union Life Insurance Society v Dobbs* [1912] AD 395, which emphasizes the need for a strict interpretation rather than a liberal one in determining exceptional cases ([para 4]). The court cited the rationale established in *S v Dlamini; S v Dladla and Others; S v Joubert; S v Schietekat* [1999] (4) SA 623 (CC) highlighting that the determination of exceptional circumstances is a factual inquiry rather than a matter of discretion ([para 6]).

Furthermore, the court referred to the principles set forth in prior jurisprudence concerning special leave to appeal, indicating that mere prospects of success do not suffice; rather, significant legal questions or potential for grave injustice must be demonstrated ([para 7]).

## A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 8–14])

In reviewing Avnit's application, the President concluded that the circumstances he presented did not meet the standard of "exceptional" as required by statute. The court maintained that Avnit’s argument amounted to a mere restatement of points previously considered and rejected by the lower courts, thus failing to illustrate any new or distinctive aspects that could qualify as extraordinary ([para 12]).

The court further analyzed the implications of the supplementary affidavit, reprimanding Avnit for attempting to benefit from a situation he himself had contested. The court noted that the judge’s decision to deny the supplementary affidavit had been within her authority and did not demonstrate a failure of justice or prejudice to Avnit's rights ([paras 11-13]). The judgment's merits were deemed to focus primarily on accounting issues already traceable through other evidence available to Avnit, which undermined claims of injustice ([para 13]).

## C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 14])

The SCA ultimately dismissed Avnit's application with costs, affirming the decision made by the two judges regarding the refusal of leave to appeal, maintaining that the factors advanced by Avnit did not constitute "exceptional circumstances" warranting reconsideration under the Superior Courts Act ([para 14]). 

This holding reinforces the stringent criteria necessary for invoking the court’s exceptional powers and delineates the boundaries of judicial discretion concerning statutory interpretation of circumstances that warrant revisiting earlier decisions. The judgment serves to uphold the integrity of the appellate process and underscores the necessity for compelling justification when an appellant seeks to challenge a previous refusal of leave to appeal, thereby promoting the overarching tenet of judicial efficiency and finality in litigation outcomes.
