Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

### FIRAC Case Note: Global Environmental Trust and Others v Tendele Coal Mining (Pty) Ltd and Others (1105/2019) [2021] ZASCA 13

**F – Facts**
The case arose from an appeal by the appellants, Global Environmental Trust and Mfolozi Community Environmental Justice Organisation, against a decision from the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, which had dismissed their application for an interdict against Tendele Coal Mining (Pty) Ltd. Tendele was challenged on several grounds, with the appellants alleging that it lacked the necessary statutory authorizations to conduct mining operations at the Somkhele mine in Mtubatuba, KwaZulu-Natal ([paras 1-8]). 

Tendele's mining operations commenced in 2006 under an "old order" mining licence and various mining rights and Environmental Management Programmes (EMPs) approved under the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA) [paras 4-5]. Tendele claimed to be mining in compliance with the requirements set forth by the MPRDA, whereas the appellants alleged failures in obtaining essential authorizations under the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA), local planning laws, and waste management legislation, along with breaches of the KwaZulu-Natal Heritage Act 4 of 2008 relating to grave relocations ([paras 8-9]).

The High Court had found that the appellants failed to establish a proper cause of action, particularly regarding Tendele’s supposed lack of compliance with the necessary environmental authorizations. It determined that Tendele’s EMPs were considered valid under transitional provisions that followed the introduction of the "One Environmental System" ([paras 11-13]).

**I – Issues**
The central issue under appeal was whether Tendele’s mining operations were conducted unlawfully due to a lack of necessary statutory authorizations, specifically:
1. Whether Tendele required environmental authorizations under NEMA for mining-related activities classified as "listed activities" ([paras 14-15]).
2. Whether municipal approval for land use was applicable to Tendele's operations in light of the commencement of relevant local planning laws ([paras 61-62]).
3. Whether Tendele needed a waste management licence as per the Waste Management Act 59 of 2008 ([paras 68-70]).
4. Whether Tendele’s actions in relation to the relocation of graves contravened the KwaZulu-Natal Heritage Act ([paras 73-75]).

**R – Rules**
The key legal frameworks analyzed in this case included:
- The **National Environmental Management Act** (NEMA), which outlines the need for environmental authorizations for activities with the potential to impact the environment ([paras 24-39]).
- The **Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act** (MPRDA), which governs the granting of mining rights and the approval of EMPs ([paras 28-31]).
- The **KwaZulu-Natal Planning and Development Act** which stipulates requirements for land development approvals ([paras 61-62]).
- The **Waste Management Act**, which regulates waste management activities and requires specific authorizations ([paras 68-70]).
- The **KwaZulu-Natal Heritage Act** which protects traditional graves and requires prior consent for their relocation ([paras 73-74]).

The court leaned heavily on established legal principles concerning statutory construction, interpretive frameworks, and the implications of transitional provisions introduced with the One Environmental System ([paras 33-35],[49]).

**A – Application**
The application of these legal principles unfolded throughout the court's analysis, notably:
- The court underscored the interplay between NEMA and the MPRDA, asserting that compliance with environmental standards is essential regardless of mining rights granted under the MPRDA, and that transitional provisions do not provide blanket immunity for compliance failures ([paras 52-56]).
- The absence of environmental authorization for Tendele’s operations was acknowledged by the court, with the judgment emphasizing that mining operations inherently invoke numerous listed activities, necessitating environmental scrutiny ([paras 60-61]).
- Tendele’s claims regarding compliance with municipal regulations and land use approvals were evaluated, leading to a determination that operations which began before the enactment of local laws could be viewed as legitimate ([paras 61-67]).
- Regarding waste management, the court found that Tendele's activities fell under transitional provisions, which implied Tendele was still permitted to operate until called upon by the Minister to obtain a waste management licence ([paras 68-72]).
- The court concluded that despite Tendele's previous failings in handling grave relocations, the absence of a reasonable apprehension of future harm indicated that an interdict was unwarranted ([paras 73-76]).

**C – Conclusion**
The appeal was ultimately dismissed, upholding the High Court's findings. The key holding was that Tendele’s operations, while compliant with certain older statutes, required environmental authorizations for specific listed activities under NEMA, impacting the validity of its operations ([paras 85-90]). The court’s ruling illuminated the ongoing tension between mining regulations and environmental protection mandates, and the need for clarity to preserve ecological integrity while balancing economic interests ([paras 61-68]).

This case represents a pivotal judgment elucidating the overlapping jurisdictions of environmental and mining laws in South Africa, illustrating the judiciary's critical role in ensuring compliance with statutory environmental protections. As such, the ruling underscores the necessity of an integrative approach to managing natural resources in line with constitutional environmental protection provisions found in Section 24 of the South African Constitution.
