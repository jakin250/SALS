Supreme Court of Appeal

Stoltz v Steenkamp (127/17) [2018] ZASCA 84 (31 May 2018)

The essential facts concerning the sale are largely undisputed, except for the purchase price.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 84 (31 May 2018)

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

The appellant, Mr Jan George Gabriel Stoltz, sold a 1995 model Case harvester to the respondent, Prof L J S Steenkamp, in 2006 under controversial circumstances regarding the purchase price ([1]). Their longstanding friendship of nearly 40 years plays a backdrop to the unraveling dispute.

The essential facts concerning the sale are largely undisputed, except for the purchase price. The transaction is said to have been formalized through an oral agreement between December 2005 and July 2006, with the harvester delivered by the end of August 2006 ([2]). Stoltz claims the purchase price was R750,000, while Steenkamp asserts it was simply the outstanding balance owed on the harvester to Absa Bank, approximately R341,134.55, thus leading to a claim for the difference: R408,865.45 payable in three equal installments.

Stoltz had acquired the harvester for R525,000 in 2001 and rejected a R600,000 offer at a subsequent auction ([3]). After multiple requests for the owed balance were ignored, Stoltz began recording telephone conversations with Steenkamp, which were submitted as evidence ([3]).

Steenkamp's arguments asserted that he only agreed to pay the amount owed to Absa, countering Stoltz's claim ([4]). An assessment from Mr Roelf van der Merwe, a loss adjuster, valued the harvester between R320,000 and R400,000 as of May 2006 ([5]).

In his initial ruling, Du Plessis J found for Stoltz, but this was later overturned after new evidence emerged relating to a handwritten document that seemed to validate Steenkamp's position ([6]–[9]). This document indicated that Steenkamp had paid a deposit of R35,000 and intended to cover the remaining balance owed to Absa; however, there were discrepancies regarding its authenticity and the signing circumstances that cast doubts on its validity ([8]–[10]).

Ultimately, the trial judge found that Stoltz failed to sufficiently prove the purchase price amidst conflicting testimonies and circumstantial evidence ([11]).

I – Issues

The case presents several legal questions primarily revolving around:

  1. What constitutes sufficient proof of the agreed purchase price in an oral agreement?
  2. How should courts approach mutually destructive versions when evaluating witness credibility?
  3. Is the trial court's factual determination regarding the purchase price and the nature of the agreement adequate to warrant appeal? ([12]–[14]).

R – Rules

The legal principles applicable to this case include the following:

  • The standard for proving the terms of an oral contract, especially regarding purchase price, as elucidated in R v Dhlumayo & another 1948 (2) SA 677 (A), which stipulates that findings by the trial court are generally presumptively correct unless a blatant misdirection occurs ([11]).
  • The approach to assessing mutually destructive versions is drawn from Stellenbosch Farmers’ Winery Group Ltd and another v Martell & Cie SA and others [2002] ZASCA 98; 2003 (1) SA 11 (SCA), emphasizing that in instances where both versions cannot stand, the court must determine the most credible based on available evidence and probabilities ([12]).

A – Application

The court, led by Majiedt JA, scrutinized the factual context and witness credibility extensively ([11]). The judgment highlighted that both Stoltz and Steenkamp lacked reliability as witnesses, indicating they were inconsistent and evasive in their testimonies ([12]).

Key elements considered included the existence of the disputed document, market valuations, and the nature of prior dealings between the parties. The court determined that the submitted valuation of the harvester substantially undermined Stoltz's claims regarding its worth ([11]–[13]). It noted that if the purchase price had indeed been R750,000, it would have been expected to be referenced in the documentary evidence provided.

The court found that while the credibility of witness testimonies had diminished, the probabilities, especially with Steenkamp’s financial dealings and the harvester's valuation, slightly favored the respondent’s version that he was merely paying off his debt to Absa. Accordingly, the trial judge's finding that Stoltz could not substantiate his claim was deemed correct ([14]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Stoltz did not provide satisfactory proof of the alleged purchase price, affirming that the oral agreement’s parameters were not sufficiently established in light of the evidence and testimonies presented. Therefore, the court dismissed the appeal with costs ([1], [15]).

The ruling underscores the stringent standards of proof needed in oral agreements and highlights the court's role in weighing the credibility of conflicting versions. The appeal’s dismissal reinforces the notion that contractual obligations must be demonstrably established, particularly when monetary sums are at stake, thus impacting future cases regarding oral contracts within similar contexts.

In essence, the court’s decision clarified aspects of evidentiary requirements and the rigorous examination mandated in disputes involving mutually destructive claims, thereby contributing to the corpus juris on contract law and oral agreements in South Africa.
