# FIRAC Case Note: NSS obo AS v MEC for Health, Eastern Cape Province [2023] ZASCA 41

### F – Facts  
The case revolves around a claim for compensation filed by NSS (the appellant), the mother and natural guardian of her son AS who suffered perinatal asphyxia during labor in 2006, resulting in severe lifelong disabilities, including cerebral palsy ([1]). The appellant asserts that the healthcare providers at St Patrick’s Hospital in Mthatha failed to deliver the required standard of antenatal and neonatal care, thereby breaching their duty of care ([1][2]). 

The matter was pending in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, Mthatha, when the court erroneously restricted the appellant from introducing critical expert evidence that could support her claims. The restriction was based on provisions within the Civil Proceedings Evidence Act 25 of 1965 ([2][11]). Notably, the expert opinions from Prof. J.W. Lotz and Dr. B. Alheit linked the injury to an acute profound hypoxic injury ([3][4]). 

The respondent (MEC for Health) maintained that these expert opinions constituted an admission of relevant medical facts, concluding that the injury was acute and unexpected, thereby intending to foreclose all counter-evidence ([5][8]). The high court supported this position, leading to the present appeal ([12]).

### I – Issues  
The case raises several significant legal and evidential questions: 
1. **Admission of Evidence:** Can the defendant bind the court to opinions expressed by the plaintiff's experts through their purported admissions? 
2. **Application of Section 15 of the Civil Proceedings Evidence Act:** Does Section 15, which concerns admissions, apply to informal declarations made in correspondence rather than formal admissions in court?
3. **Effect on the Best Interests of the Child:** Does the high court order, which limits the plaintiff's ability to present evidence, contravene the constitutional principle that a child's best interests are of paramount importance? 

### R – Rules  
The core legal rules and precedents relevant to this case include:
- **Civil Proceedings Evidence Act 25 of 1965:** Particularly Section 15, which states that admissions do not require proof, and any party cannot challenge facts admitted.
- **Common Law Rules on Expert Evidence:** Courts are not bound by expert opinions but must evaluate the evidence, including its reasoning process, to ensure it substantiates a justified conclusion ([25]).
- **Constitutional Framework:** Section 28(2) of the Constitution dictates that a child's best interests are of paramount importance ([2]).

The case of **Zweni v Minister of Law and Order** ([12]) holds that the appealability of decisions is based not solely on their classification but more critically on whether correcting such decisions serves the interests of justice. Additionally, **National Director of Public Prosecutions v King** provides guidance on assessing whether orders should be corrected to avoid compromising justice ([13]).

### A – Application  
In determining the appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal found the high court's order to be fundamentally flawed. The principle that a court must evaluate expert evidence was highlighted, demonstrating that the court cannot be strictly bound to an expert's opinion or an informal admission expressed in correspondence ([25][18][24]). 

The purported admissions made in correspondence (i.e., that the child’s injury was sudden and unexpected) were significantly critiqued as they did not constitute formal admissions under the Civil Proceedings Evidence Act, thereby rendering the order incorrect ([18]). The court emphasized that it is the role of the judiciary to critically assess expert testimony, regardless of outside assertions or conclusions of fact ([24][27]).

Moreover, the court noted that limiting the appellant's capacity to present counter-evidence not only hampered the adjudicative process but also jeopardized the statutory and constitutional right to seek justice for the child in question ([14][15]). The judgment referenced several previous cases to reinforce the established principles surrounding the administration of justice and the obligatory evaluative role of the court concerning expert testimony.

### C – Conclusion  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, reversing the high court's prohibition against admitting vital expert evidence ([27]). The judgment clarified that the purported admissions lacked the necessary attributes of formal admissions as outlined in Section 15 of the Civil Proceedings Evidence Act. The ruling emphasizes the judiciary's fundamental duty to assess all expert evidence presented during litigation, ensuring that all aspects of a child’s best interests are duly considered.

The implications of this ruling significantly enhance the procedural landscape for civil litigants, particularly in cases involving expert testimony. This case underlines the critical need for courts to protect the rights of vulnerable parties, such as children, ensuring that systemic failures in the judicial process do not hinder their access to justice.
