Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Scalabrini Centre of Cape Town and Another v The Minister of Home Affairs and Others [2023] ZACC 45

## F – Facts

The case originates from an application to the Constitutional Court of South Africa, seeking confirmation of an order of constitutional invalidity by the Western Cape High Court regarding subsections 22(12) and 22(13) of the Refugees Act 130 of 1998 (hereafter referred to as the Refugees Act) ([paras 1–3]). 

The applicants, the Scalabrini Centre of Cape Town and its trustees, provide essential support services to migrant communities, including asylum seekers. They contended that the impugned subsections, which deemed an asylum seeker’s application abandoned if they did not renew their visa within one month of expiry, could lead to severe consequences for asylum seekers ([paras 1–7]). The respondents included the Minister of Home Affairs, the Director-General of the Department of Home Affairs, and the Chairperson of the Standing Committee for Refugee Affairs ([para 1]).

The background of the case stems from improvements to efficiency in the handling of asylum applications, driven by an overwhelming backlog of cases in the Department of Home Affairs ([para 16]). The applicants first sought an interdict in the High Court in March 2020, arguing that the subsections were unconstitutional as they effectively barred asylum seekers from being considered legitimate claimants of asylum based on subjective administrative conditions ([paras 6–7]). The High Court issued an interdict against implementing the provisions while determining the legality of the subsections ([paras 6–9]).

After extensive litigation, the High Court found the provisions of the Refugees Act to be unconstitutional, threatening the rights of asylum seekers, and the Supreme Court confirmed this on 12 December 2023 ([paras 5–6]).

In essence, the impugned subsections allowed for an individual to be deemed to have abandoned their asylum application, violating their right to dignity and infringing upon the principle of non-refoulement ([paras 9–13]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Court were:

1. **Constitutionality of the Impugned Provisions**: Whether subsections 22(12) and 22(13) of the Refugees Act are unconstitutional due to their impact on the rights of asylum seekers, specifically relating to the principle of non-refoulement, the right to dignity, and the rights of children ([paras 4, 10]).
2. **Rationality and Justification**: Whether the provisions constituted a legitimate means to achieve the intended governmental objectives in light of the claimed administrative burdens on the Department of Home Affairs ([para 11]).
3. **Remedies**: What appropriate relief should be granted, particularly regarding the retrospective application of the declaration of invalidity ([paras 2–3]).

## R – Rules

The Court primarily invoked the South African Constitution’s Bill of Rights to govern its analysis:

- **Non-refoulement Principle**: Ensured protection for asylum seekers from being returned to countries where they face threats to life or freedom ([paras 14–15]).
- **Right to Dignity (s 10)**: Recognised the essential dignity of all individuals and its critical importance in the context of asylum seekers ([paras 17–18]).
- **Rights of Children (s 28)**: Acknowledged the special protections afforded to children under the Constitution which must be considered in policies affecting them ([paras 19–20]).
- **Section 36 - Limitation of Rights**: Established criteria to determine the constitutional validity of limitations placed on rights, requiring them to be reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([para 11]).

The Court also referenced previous judgments, notably **Ruta v Minister of Home Affairs** and **Abore v Minister of Home Affairs**, which articulated the substantial protections owed to asylum seekers and reinforced the principle of non-refoulement as imperative in South African law ([para 12]).

## A – Application

In assessing the constitutionality of the impugned subsections, the Constitutional Court examined:

1. **Impact on Asylum Seekers**: The Court noted that the provisions circumvented an assessment of the merits of asylum claims, leading to the automatic assumption that an application was abandoned due to procedural failure, without considering the potential consequences of such action, especially regarding non-refoulement ([para 23]). Asylum seekers are particularly vulnerable, and their claims require careful consideration to avoid expulsion into perilous situations ([paras 24–27]).

2. **Irrationality and Arbitrariness**: The Court found no legitimate governmental purpose served by the automatic abandonment of asylum applications. The stated objectives, such as alleviating administrative burdens and reducing backlogs, were found to be inadequately justified given the severe repercussions faced by individuals ([para 30]). The provisions were deemed to be both irrational and arbitrary as they treated all failures to renew visas as equal, ignoring systemic barriers that impacted applicants ([para 31]).

3. **Rights Violation**: The Court recognised that the subsection violated the right to dignity and disproportionately affected children's rights as the automatic abandonment of asylum applications had significant adverse effects on families, often leaving children vulnerable and without access to education and healthcare ([para 32]).

4. **Remedial Action**: In rejecting the respondents’ claims that measures needed to retain some validity, the Court maintained that proper interactive processes had been excluded by the provisions, warranting the declaration of invalidity. The order indicated a retrospective application from the date the provisions became operational, thus ensuring relief for affected asylum seekers back to 1 January 2020 ([paras 33–36]).

## C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court affirmed the decision of the Western Cape High Court to declare subsections 22(12) and 22(13) of the Refugees Act unconstitutional. The Court underscored that these provisions contravened the fundamental rights of asylum seekers, particularly the principle of non-refoulement, the inherent dignity of individuals, and the rights of children. The ruling reaffirmed the importance of treating asylum seekers as presumptive refugees deserving of protection and fair administrative processes ([paras 37–39]).

The declaration of invalidity not only recognized the immediate constitutional breaches but also prompted the need for legislative action to ensure compliance with the Constitution in the management of asylum processes. The Court’s judgment emphasizes the necessity for humane treatment of vulnerable communities and the integrity of systems intended to protect those fleeing persecution ([para 40]).

The implications of this ruling extend beyond this case, reinforcing the protection of asylum seekers against arbitrary administrative actions and affirming the foundational principles of dignity and human rights embedded in the South African constitutional framework ([para 41]).
