Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Credit Europe Bank N.V. v The Fund Comprising the Proceeds of the Sale of the MV Tarik III and Others [2022] ZASCA 136

**F – Facts**  
This case originates from an appeal involving claims against a fund constituted from the judicial sale of the motor vessel Tarik III (the vessel) ([1]). The appellant, Credit Europe Bank N.V. (CEB), asserted that the claims of several respondents recommended for payment by a referee should have been rejected. Consequently, CEB sought an order for its claims to be prioritized for payment from the fund ([2]). 

The facts leading to this litigation began with a memorandum of agreement (MoA) concerning the vessel originally named MV Arisbe. It was sold to Garanti Finansal Kiralama A.S. (Garanti) in January 2008 after Herex Trading Ltd assigned its rights under the MoA to Garanti ([3]). Subsequently, Garanti entered into a bareboat charter with Hazar Denizcilik, which was later substituted by Caliskan as the charterer. This agreement was extended multiple times, most recently until December 2015 ([4]).

In March 2014, Caliskan obtained a preliminary injunction under the Turkish Enforcement and Bankruptcy Code that provided it protection from creditor actions for one year, which complicated the carriage of contractual obligations ([5]). On April 15, 2014, Garanti issued a notice of termination to Caliskan for non-payment ([6]). Despite this, a revised payment plan was agreed upon shortly thereafter. 

The vessel was arrested on behalf of CEB on two occasions in 2014, which prompted a high court application to sell the vessel due to insufficient funds for repairs. The vessel was sold at a judicial auction in February 2015, and claims against the fund were lodged by several parties, including CEB and others who had been suppliers to the vessel ([7]–[10]). 

The referee recommended payment for claims by various respondents while recommending the rejection of Garanti's claims, concluding that the charter agreement remained extant ([10]). Following these proceedings, CEB contended that the claims of these respondents were invalid as they did not arrest the vessel before the sale, thereby asserting a better entitlement to the fund ([13]).

**I – Issues**  
The primary legal questions at dispute were:  
1. Whether the claims of the opposing suppliers against the fund were valid given the alleged termination of the charterparty ([13]–[19]).  
2. Whether claimants, who did not arrest the vessel prior to its sale, could still rely on the deeming provisions under the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983 ([35]–[39]).

**R – Rules**  
This case’s analysis was grounded in several legal tenets, notably:  
- The **Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983**, specifically section 1(3), which states that a charterer by demise is deemed the owner of the ship for the duration of the charter. This provision is crucial for claims lodged against the fund ([8], [35]).  
- **Onus of proof**: Traditionally, the party asserting the existence of a fact bears the onus of proving it ([25]–[34]). The principles from *Pillay v Krishna* and *The Kingston* were discussed regarding burden allocations in disputes over claim validity and entitlement to claims ([26], [60]–[66]).

**A – Application**  
The court's analysis focused on the onus regarding the termination of the charterparty ([14]–[20]), noting that CEB bore the burden of proof in asserting that the charter arrangement had been terminated at the relevant times. The court stressed that the direct evidence from Garanti and affirmations from Caliskan contradicted CEB's assertions about a termination ([19]).  

The court emphasized that another claimant's alleged lack of arrest did not inhibit their ability to lodge a claim against the fund, following the precedent that once a vessel has been sold, the requirement for arrest dissipates ([42]–[47]). The legislative framework aims for expediency in maritime claims, indicating that procedural technicalities should not bar rightful claims ([44]–[46]). 

Analyzing the circumstances surrounding the claims, the court observed that the referee’s suggestion to pay the claims of respondents was predicated on their validity under prevailing maritime law. Ultimately, it held that the claims were appropriately lodged and substantiated, given the necessity to establish wrongdoing by other claimants by the appellant ([18], [40]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed CEB’s appeal, confirming the validity of the opposing suppliers' claims to the fund. The ruling underscored the principle that the right to claim from the fund does not necessarily hinge on prior vessel arrest where statutory provisions and circumstances allow for claims to be made based on provided evidential support ([48]). 

CEB's contention that the charter agreement had been terminated was not upheld, as the evidence presented illustrated its continuation leading up to the vessel's sale. The court confirmed that Claims against the fund would be honored, maintaining a focus on legislative intent to favor expediency in maritime transactions and dispute resolution ([48]).

In dissent, Justices Gorven expressed concern about the consequences of the evidentiary burden displacement and the legal effect of charter agreements under Turkish law, advocating that the ambiguity surrounding the termination incident necessitated further evidence before ruling ([49]–[59]).
