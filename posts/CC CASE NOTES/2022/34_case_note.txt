# Case Note: United Democratic Movement v Lebashe Investment Group (CCT 39/21)

**FIRAC Structure**

### F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–15])

The case arises from the appeal by the United Democratic Movement (UDM) and its leader, Bantubonke Harrington Holomisa, against the decision of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) to strike their appeal off the roll. The dispute originated in an interim interdict ordered by the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria, on 16 July 2018. This interdict temporarily restrained the applicants from making or repeating statements addressed to the President alleging corruption and mismanagement associated with the operations of the Public Investment Corporation (PIC) and its investments linked to the first and third respondents, Lebashe Investment Group (Pty) Ltd and Harith Fund Managers (Pty) Ltd, respectively.

The interim interdict was sought by the respondents after Holomisa published letters alleging corrupt practices among the respondents. Following the issuance of the interim order, the UDM argued that it was an infringement on their right to freedom of expression and political activity, seeking to overturn the order and uphold their constitutional rights. The High Court ultimately ruled in favor of the respondent companies, leading to an appeal that was struck off the roll by a split decision of the SCA that deemed the interim order unappealable due to its interim nature despite the applicants contending its finality in effect.

### I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 38–38])

The following key issues arise from the facts:

1. **Appealability of the Interim Order**: Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) erred in holding that the interim interdict was not appealable, despite the applicants’ claims regarding the implications of free speech and the political context of their statements.
   
2. **Constitutional Rights**: Whether the interim interdict unjustifiably infringed the applicants' rights to freedom of expression and their ability to operate as political actors under the South African Constitution.

3. **Proper Justification for the Interdict**: Whether the High Court was justified in granting the interim interdict pending resolution of the defamation action.

### R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 31–46])

The turning points for the Court's ruling drew on various legal principles:

1. **Section 16 of the South African Constitution**: Affords the right to freedom of expression, including the right to receive and impart information and ideas.

2. **Zweni v Minister of Law and Order**: Established the common law test for appealability of interim orders, outlining that an interim order can be appealable if it disposes of substantial relief or finalizes rights temporarily.

3. **Health Professions Council of South Africa**: Cited for the proposition that injunctive relief (like interdicts) can amount to a "decision" when they pose a substantial infringement on constitutional rights.

4. **Case Law on Defamation**: Established principles around the requirements to sufficiently prove statements made in defamation claims—namely, that truth and public interest serve as valid defenses.

### A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 49–73])

In analyzing the appealability of the interim interdict, the Constitutional Court emphasized the broad interpretation of the appealability test under the interests of justice, moving away from rigid adherence to common law precedents that strictly limited review of interim orders. The Court found that the SCA's majority decision erroneously applied these tests by failing to consider how the interim order significantly impeded the applicants' ability to communicate regarding supposed corruption in government, thus constituting a grave intrusion on constitutional freedoms.

The Court further noted that the interdict, by qualitatively limiting political discourse, had negative implications not only on the applicants but also on the public’s right to engage with political issues of public interest. The applicants were unable to justify the defamatory nature of their statements as proven, as they widely lacked verification of the truthfulness of their claims before public dissemination. The conclusion reached by the Court acknowledged that a distinction must be drawn between allegations made under the guise of political action and verifiable claims posing substantive evidence of misconduct.

### C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 74–75])

The Constitutional Court ruled unanimously in favor of the applicants. It held that the interim interdict imposed by the High Court was, contrary to the SCA’s assertion, appealable as it significantly affected the fundamental rights of the applicants under the South African Constitution. The Court set aside the SCA's ruling and upheld the appeal against the order, reinforcing the need for careful judicial consideration of interim orders that restrict free expression, especially in politically charged contexts.

The judgment advocates a robust interpretation of political rights and freedom of expression, underscoring the need to safeguard these rights against undue restrictions and recognizing the constitutional imperative to inform and engage the public on matters of public interest. The case ultimately paves the way for affected individuals to challenge interim orders that may constrain their rights to express and share information, thereby reaffirming the judiciary's role in upholding constitutional democracy.
