# Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA 

**Case No:** 103/2001  
**In the matter between:** VALASHIYA: ELIZABETH LAWUKAZI (Appellant) and ROAD ACCIDENT FUND (Respondent)  
**Coram:** Marais, Streicher, Farlam, Mthiyane, JJA and Heher, AJA  
**Heard:** 16 May 2002  
**Delivered:** 29 May 2002  

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])

The appellant, Elizabeth Lawukazi Valashiya, initiated legal action against the Road Accident Fund, claiming damages for the death of her husband resulting from a motor vehicle accident on 14 August 1994. The case was adjudicated in the Witwatersrand Local Division. The trial court, after hearing evidence, determined that the respondent was liable and subsequently ordered it to pay the costs. However, a full bench appeal reversed this decision, leading to an order of absolution from the instance ([para 1]).

Key facts established during the trial indicated that the deceased was found on a road in Katlehong by a witness, Mr. Mohlala, who transported him to the hospital where he died. The particulars surrounding the accident included the testimony of a secondary witness, Miss Konyana, who claimed to have seen a vehicle colliding with the deceased while driving at high speed. However, her credibility was challenged due to inconsistencies in her statements and evasiveness on the witness stand ([paras 2–5]).

Mohlala's evidence also suggested the possible negligence of an unidentified driver; however, the details surrounding the collision were uncertain, including the exact position of the deceased when found and the nature of his injuries, which raised questions about the circumstances of the collision ([paras 3–6]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 7–12])

The central legal issue was whether the conduct of the unidentified driver, who allegedly left the scene post-collision, constituted negligence, particularly considering the lack of direct evidence linking the driver to the accident. The appellant contended that an inference of negligence could be drawn from the fact that the driver left the scene without rendering assistance ([para 12]).

Additionally, the case raised questions about the admissibility of witness statements, particularly regarding the credibility of Konyana's testimony and the evidential weight given to hearsay statements recorded by Mr. Ratsaka, an assessor present during the initial investigation ([paras 8–10]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 11–12])

The court referenced *Motor Vehicle Assurance Fund v Dubuzane* 1984 (1) SA 700 (A), which established that a driver's failure to stop after causing an injury could raise a presumption of negligence. According to Botha JA in that case, such conduct leads to a strong inference of awareness of the collision and a probable negligent mindset.

Furthermore, the court emphasized the necessity of establishing the driver's knowledge or awareness of the collision before inferring negligence. It was reiterated that circumstantial evidence alone, such as fleeing the scene, may not suffice to establish liability without concrete proof that the driver was aware of the pedestrian's presence ([para 12]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 13–14])

In applying the legal principles to the case at hand, the Supreme Court of Appeal consistently found a notable absence of concrete evidence directly linking the driver to the accident. Factors such as the nature of visibility at the time of the incident and the location where the deceased was found underscored the uncertainty surrounding the driver’s awareness of the collision.

The court critiqued the credibility of both Mohlala and Konyana. Mohlala's evidence was deemed reliable, while Konyana’s statements exhibited significant inconsistencies, prompting the court to dismiss her claims regarding the driver's behavior during the alleged incident. The possibility was also acknowledged that the deceased's own actions might have contributed to the accident, especially considering he was reportedly under the influence of alcohol and possibly engaging in reckless behavior ([paras 11–14]).

Consequently, the court concluded that since the appellant failed to establish that the unidentified driver was aware he had struck a pedestrian, it could not draw an inference of negligence based solely on the driver’s departure from the accident scene. Hence, the appeal was dismissed, upholding the full bench’s decision ([paras 12–14]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 15])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, agreeing with the full bench's finding that the trial court erred in drawing an inference of negligence based solely on the unidentified driver's conduct post-accident. The decision reinforced the principle that negligence cannot be assumed merely from the fact that a driver left the scene without assistance, particularly when no definitive evidence was presented to establish the nature of the collision.

**Implications**: This ruling emphasizes the critical need for clear evidence in establishing liability in motor accident claims, particularly concerning the conduct and state of mind of the involved parties. It reflects the broader legal principle that a mere absence from the scene after an accident is insufficient to establish negligence, placing the onus on plaintiffs to provide reliable and consistent evidence to support their claims ([para 15]).
