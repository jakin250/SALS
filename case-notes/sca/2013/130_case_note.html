Supreme Court of Appeal

Bornman v National Credit Regulator (798/12) [2013] ZASCA 130

The Tribunal's proceedings were initiated following a comprehensive investigation by the National Credit Regulator (NCR), which revealed that Bornman had failed to comply with the obligations imposed upon him as a debt counsellor ([1], [6]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 130

F - Facts

The case of Bornman v National Credit Regulator ([1]) revolves around the cancellation of Christoph Bornman's registration as a debt counsellor by the National Consumer Tribunal (the Tribunal). Bornman, also an attorney, was registered under the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA) on 22 February 2008 ([8]). The Tribunal determined that Bornman had contravened multiple general and specific conditions of his registration, as well as various sections and regulations of the NCA ([6], [14]).

The Tribunal's proceedings were initiated following a comprehensive investigation by the National Credit Regulator (NCR), which revealed that Bornman had failed to comply with the obligations imposed upon him as a debt counsellor ([1], [6]). Specifically, the Tribunal found that Bornman did not adequately assess the over-indebtedness of consumers and, moreover, engaged in practices that were contrary to the best interests of these consumers ([10], [12]).

Bornman managed a significant number of debt review cases—initially including over 4,800 consumers—but had his practices called into question after it was shown that he collected fees improperly, which were not aligned with the NCA and its regulations ([9], [19]). The Tribunal ordered him to refund all fees unlawfully collected ([6]).

Bornman appealed the Tribunal's decision to the North Gauteng High Court, which upheld the Tribunal's orders, dismissing his appeal. A subsequent appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ultimately concluded with the dismissal of any further relief to Bornman ([7]).

I - Issues

The core issues presented before the SCA involve several legal questions:

  1. Procedural Compliance: Did Bornman contravene the procedural requirements set forth in the NCA regarding the assessment of over-indebtedness and the communication of such determinations to consumers and credit providers? ([14], [18])
  2. Breach of Conditions of Registration: Were the findings of the Tribunal regarding Bornman's repeated contraventions of the conditions of his registration accurate and justified? ([6], [21])
  3. Authority of the Tribunal: Did the Tribunal possess the jurisdiction and authority to mandate a refund of fees received by Bornman under circumstances determined to be in breach of the NCA? ([27])

R - Rules

The applicable legal frameworks and regulatory provisions considered in this case include:

  1. National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA): This Act governs the functioning of debt counsellors and was designed to shield consumers from over-indebtedness. Key sections involved in the ruling included:
  • Section 86: Outlines procedural requirements for debt review, defining roles and responsibilities of debt counsellors concerning over-indebtedness assessments ([15], [16]).
  • Section 150: Confers powers on the Tribunal to issue orders related to consumer protection, including the requirement for repayment of excessive charges ([27]).
  1. Regulations under the NCA, particularly Regulation 24, which details obligations around the processing of debt review applications and the necessary determinations to be made by debt counsellors ([15]).
  2. Debt Counselling Fee Guidelines: These guidelines delineate how and when fees can be charged by debt counsellors and emphasize that any fees must be clearly disclosed and agreed upon with consumers ([8], [22]).
  3. Precedent Cases: The SCA referred to established case laws such as the National Credit Regulator v Nedbank Ltd ([20]), emphasizing the obligation of debt counsellors to adhere strictly to the regulations imposed by the NCA.

A - Application

The SCA applied the relevant statutory provisions and regulatory requirements directly to the conduct of Bornman in several notable ways:

  1. Failure to Comply with Section 86 Procedures: The SCA found that Bornman undertook a non-compliant form of debt restructuring, referred to as an "expedited" process, which compromised the rights of consumers and failed to reflect the necessary determinations as mandated by the NCA ([18], [19]). His actions not only lacked proper documentation but also exposed consumers to potential financial detriment.
  2. Improper Collection of Fees: The court scrutinized Bornman's practice of charging fees, notably a collection fee described as a retainer. The mischaracterization and misapplication of this fee structure contradicted the explicit prohibitions outlined in his registration conditions and the Debt Counselling Fee Guidelines ([23], [24]). The court emphasized that the NCA does not allow the accumulation of retainer fees in the manner that Bornman had employed in practice.
  3. Tribunal's Authority to Order Refunds: Addressing Bornman's argument against the Tribunal's power to mandate the refund of fees, the SCA held that the statutory provisions under Section 150 indeed grant such authority, especially in instances where fees are charged in breach of the NCA ([27]). The court found the Tribunal’s decision to require refunds justified, denoting that it served to enforce consumer protection standards outlined in the NCA.

C - Conclusion

In its ruling, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the Tribunal’s findings and orders ([28]). The SCA concluded that Bornman had repeatedly contravened the provisions of the NCA and consequently failed to uphold the integrity of his role as a debt counsellor.

The implications of this decision underscore the stringent regulatory environment governing debt counselling in South Africa and highlight the critical importance of compliance with statutory requirements to protect consumers from exploitation in debt review processes. The ruling reinforces the ethical obligations imposed upon legal professionals who assume the dual role of debt counsellor and attorney, ensuring that the interests of consumers are accurately prioritized within the frameworks established by the NCA.

In upholding the Tribunal’s decision, the SCA not only reaffirmed the necessity for strict adherence to regulatory provisions but also sent a clear message regarding the accountability mechanisms in place within the debt counselling sector ([6]).
