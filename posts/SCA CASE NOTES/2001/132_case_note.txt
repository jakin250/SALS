# FIRAC Case Note: **Republic of South Africa (BoE Bank Ltd v Sonja Mathilda Ries)**

## F - Facts

[1] This case involves an appeal by BoE Bank Ltd (the appellant) against a decision made in the Cape Provincial Division where the trial court had ruled in favor of Sonja Mathilda Ries (the respondent). The events leading up to the dispute occurred in December 1994, when Sonja (the intended beneficiary) was employed by Ries (the deceased), who wished to change the beneficiary on his life insurance policy issued by Old Mutual [2]. The appeal arises from the fact that the deceased had not formally completed the required documentation before his death, leading to the proceeds of the policy being paid to a different beneficiary, one Mr. Strahlendorf, rather than to Mrs. Ries.

[3] The deceased had two insurance policies, one with Liberty Life, which was taken out shortly before or after 22 December 1994, and the other with Old Mutual, where the beneficiary had not been changed to reflect Mrs. Ries. Groenewald, who was employed as a broker at the bank, had facilitated the drafting of a new will for the deceased. During this process, the deceased mentioned a desire to change the beneficiary on the Old Mutual policy [4]. Groenewald arranged to bring back the appropriate form to effect this change, yet the document remained unsigned at the time of the deceased's death, and he did not follow through to ensure the form was completed and submitted [5–7].

[8] Following the death of the deceased in 1996, Mrs. Ries claimed for damages equivalent to the policy proceeds of the Old Mutual insurance, which the trial judge awarded after finding that Groenewald had a duty of care towards her. The Appellant subsequently appealed against this decision, leading to the present proceedings [9].

## I - Issues

[10] The core legal questions in this case relate to:
1. Whether the bank or its employee, Groenewald, owed a legal duty to Mrs. Ries to take further steps to ensure the beneficiary change was finalized.
2. Whether the trial court correctly determined issues of wrongfulness, negligence, and causation, particularly in the context of a disappointed beneficiary claim [10].

## R - Rules

[11] The standard for determining the existence of a duty of care within South African delictual law is well established. The court evaluated precedents concerning “disappointed beneficiaries,” where previous judgments acknowledged that a beneficiary has a delictual action if a third party's negligence causes them to lose an expected benefit [10]. Key cases include *Arthur E Abrahams & Gross v Cohen and Others* and *Pretorius and Others v McCallum* which support this view.

[12] However, the court pointed out that establishing wrongfulness involves assessing the community's legal convictions and whether the defendant had a legal duty to act positively versus merely a moral obligation [13]. Importantly, principles from *Administrateur Natal v Trust Bank van Afrika Bpk* were invoked, acknowledging that pure economic loss could be claimed where omissions arise from a failure to fulfill legal duties [12–14].

## A - Application

[15] In applying these rules, the court examined whether Groenewald owed a duty of care to Mrs. Ries in the context of his involvement with the beneficiary change. The court concluded that Groenewald did not have a professional relationship with the deceased that went beyond a mere messenger service for the will, and as such, he did not assume a legal duty to ensure the form was signed and submitted [14–15]. The factors considered included the nature of Groenewald's relationship with the deceased and the professional context of his actions.

[16] The trial judge had erroneously drawn a close analogy with the responsibilities of legal professionals drafting wills, implying analogous obligations about the duty of care [16–18]. The Supreme Court rejected this line of reasoning, underscoring that Groenewald's involvement was neither formal nor was there a contractual obligation to deliver the completed form. The Appellate Court found that mere foreseeability does not create a duty of care [20–22].

## C - Conclusion

[26] Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled that the trial court's finding on wrongfulness was incorrect and dismissed Mrs. Ries's claim. It concluded that there was no established duty on Groenewald's part to obtain or submit the signed form and that the circumstances did not warrant the imposition of liability. The appeal was upheld, with the trial court's judgment set aside [24–26].

### Citation:
BoE Bank Ltd v Sonja Mathilda Ries, SCA Case No 247/2000 (2001). 

This judgment illustrates the complexities surrounding claims involving disappointed beneficiaries and the necessity for clear legal duties in establishing liability in cases of economic loss resulting from omissions. The implications serve as a guideline for similar future disputes involving broker-client relationships and the extent of responsibilities assumed in informal arrangements.
