Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Mathale v Linda and Another [2015] ZACC 38

## F – Facts

This case arose from a dispute regarding an eviction order between the applicant, Nthome Steve Mathale, and the respondents, July Janson Zenzele Linda and the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality ([1]–[4]). Mathale had been residing at stand number 8702 in Winnie Mandela Park, Tembisa, since 1994, having unlawfully occupied the land with a group of individuals ([5]–[8]). The local Municipality formalised the township in the late 1990s, leading to administrative confusion over property allocations, as many residents, including Mathale, believed their previous standings were disrupted. In 2000, the Municipality allocated Mathale an RDP house at a different location, which he rejected ([9]–[11]).

In 2011, Linda, claiming to have been allocated the same stand as Mathale, commenced eviction proceedings against him under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 ([14]–[15]). An eviction order was granted in 2012, and despite Mathale appealing this order, the High Court later dismissed his appeal and further application for special leave to appeal. Subsequently, Linda obtained an execution order to evict Mathale whilst his appeal was still pending ([16]–[18]). Mathale argued this execution order violated his housing rights protected under Section 26 of the South African Constitution and was thus appealable ([19]).

## I – Issues

The case presented several key legal questions:

1. Are execution orders granted in terms of Section 78 of the Magistrates’ Court Act appealable ([22]–[23])?
2. Did the High Court err in its approach to determining the appeal against the execution order ([33])?
3. Are there sufficient grounds to interfere with the discretion exercised by the Magistrates’ Court when granting the execution order ([39])?

## R – Rules

The legal framework guiding this case encompasses various statutes and case laws. Section 78 of the Magistrates' Court Act 32 of 1944 allows for the issuance of execution orders while appeals are pending, and Section 83(b) provides a basis for appeals against orders that have a "final judgment" effect ([24]–[27]).

The controlling precedent, **Machele and Others v Mailula and Others** [2009] ZACC 7, establishes that issues leading to “irreparable harm,” such as evictions from the home, justify appealability ([32]). Furthermore, principles of fairness and justice dictate that courts must regard the rights to housing under Section 26 of the Constitution ([22]).

## A – Application

Khampepe J articulated that Section 78 execution orders are inherently appealable when their effects are akin to a final judgment ([30]–[34]). The court highlighted that the execution order’s implementation would lead to Mathale's immediate displacement, resulting in severe and irreparable harm, further underscoring the importance of dignity and the right to housing ([28], [36]). The High Court’s assumption that the execution order was only appealable if it served the interests of justice represented a misunderstanding of the applicable legal standard.

In assessing whether the Magistrates’ Court exercised its discretion reasonably, Khampepe J found that the court did not sufficiently weigh the emotional and practical ramifications of evicting Mathale from his home, considering he had lived there for nearly two decades ([39]–[42]). The judgment underscored that the eviction's potential to render Mathale homeless while seeking relief for his rights constituted an impermissible disregard of his circumstances, necessitating reconsideration of the factual context from which the eviction order was premised.

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court unanimously held that the execution order was indeed appealable and should be set aside. The court emphasised that it is vital to uphold the dignity and housing rights of individuals—especially the vulnerable—over mere procedural expedience ([56]–[60]). The implication of this judgment reaffirms that execution orders related to housing matters carry substantial weight, thus warranting judicial scrutiny and providing a safeguard against unwarranted evictions.

In finding that the High Court had erred, the Constitutional Court not only overturned the prior rulings but also remarked on the municipalities' failure to uphold their housing obligations, highlighting the systemic issues faced by residents affected by similar disputes ([54]–[55]). The court ultimately mandated that, pending the outcome of the primary appeal regarding the eviction order, Mathale be permitted to remain in his home. Each party was ordered to bear their own costs, demonstrating the court’s commitment to fairness and justice for all involved ([58]).
