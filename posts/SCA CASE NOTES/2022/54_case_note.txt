Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Central Energy Fund SOC Ltd and Another v Venus Rays Trade (Pty) Ltd and Others [2022] ZASCA 54

## F (Facts)

This case concerns the review of decisions related to the management and rotation of South Africa's strategic oil stock, specifically involving significant transactions conducted by the Central Energy Fund SOC Limited (CEF) and its subsidiary, the Strategic Fuel Fund Association NPC (SFF). The appellants sought to invalidate decisions taken in 2015 and 2016 regarding the sale and purchase of approximately 10 million barrels of crude oil, which included Basrah Light and Bonny Light oils ([1]–[2]).

The transactions involved the SFF selling crude oil to multiple parties, including the first respondent, Venus Rays Trade (Pty) Ltd, which subsequently sold the oil to Glencore Energy UK Ltd. Financing for these transactions was provided by Contango Trading SA and Natixis SA, among others ([3]–[7]). The sales were facilitated without adherence to the SFF’s procurement policy, and significant allegations of misconduct surfaced against Mr. Sipho Gamede, the then CEO of SFF, who was accused of engaging in corrupt practices and misrepresentations to secure these transactions ([18]–[28]).

The appellants' review application was initiated more than two years after the agreements were signed, claiming that the decisions to rotate and sell the strategic stock were made unlawfully. The high court ultimately found that these transactions were indeed unlawful, citing a pervasive disregard for corporate governance and prescribed processes ([10]–[11], [14]). Though the high court confirmed the improprieties, it ruled that the parties involved in the transactions, namely Contango and Vitol, were not complicit in the wrongdoing and established a compensation structure for their incurred expenses due to the appeals ([31], [32]).

## I (Issues)

The primary issue before the court was whether the high court's order, which compensated innocent parties for out-of-pocket expenses stemming from the unlawful decisions and transactions of the appellants, was appropriate. This raises broader questions concerning the legality of the decisions under the principle of legality and the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), particularly regarding the sustenance of innocent third-party interests against the backdrop of significant state misconduct ([2], [17]).

Another related issue was whether the delay in bringing the review application was reasonable, as well as the legal implications of this delay on the remedy offered to the affected parties ([14], [21]).

## R (Rules)

The court analyzed the legality of administrative decisions based on the principles outlined in the Constitution, particularly section 172(1)(b) which allows the courts to grant any order that is just and equitable in cases of constitutional invalidity ([36]). The PAJA further enables judicial review of administrative actions that do not comply with legality or fairness standards as mandated by administrative justice.

The courts are permitted to exercise a wide remedial discretion rooted in the "no-profit-no-loss" principle and the corrective principle that advocates against undue enrichment from unlawful contracts. Courts also consider the extent of culpability of the parties involved—distinguishing between those complicit in corrupt actions and innocent third parties ([41], [42], [59]).

Relevant case law cited included the principles from *Allpay Consolidated Investment Holdings v Chief Executive Officer of the South African Social Security Agency* [2014] ZACC 12, which established that remedies should effectively rectify violations of rights ([36]).

## A (Application)

The court meticulously applied the identified legal principles to the facts at hand. Initial determinations of illegality focused on Mr. Gamede's actions, which involved direct financial misconduct ([18]–[20]). The review highlighted how the SFF engaged in arrangements that did not comply with transparency and competitive bidding processes as legally required ([17], [28]). 

While the high court had condoned the delay in bringing the review, it justified its decision by recognizing the overwhelming public interest in ensuring accountability for such significant state assets. The court acknowledged the well-established principle that illegal conduct cannot stand in the face of statutory obligations, thus ruling the decisions invalid ([14], [15]).

The court’s broader application evaluated whether innocent parties (Contango and Vitol) should bear the losses from decisions actively executed with governmental misconduct. It concluded in favor of compensating out-of-pocket expenses as a means of restitution, allowing these parties to recover their financial outlays while not permitting profit from unlawful conduct ([31], [63]). The court explicitly dismissed the notion that such compensation would undermine public interest or allow for inappropriate financial benefits for Contango and Vitol ([59], [62]).

## C (Conclusion)

The appeal against the high court's order was ultimately dismissed, confirming that the relief sought by the appellants was inappropriate given the realities of state misconduct and the importance of protecting third-party innocent interests ([33], [81]). The court asserted that the inherent public interest in maintaining the integrity and accountability of state transactions prevails over the potential deterrent effects of compensating those who acted in good faith.

Thus, the ruling emphasized that the high court properly balanced the interests of justice and equity by declaring the contracts void while simultaneously providing relief to innocent third parties, effectively restoring them to their pre-contractual positions without allowing for undue enrichment ([32], [74]). 

This decision underscores the complex interplay between state accountability and the protection of third parties affected by government actions in South Africa’s legal landscape.
