Lebea v Menye and Another [2022] ZACC 40

Constitutional Court 2022 [2022] ZACC 40

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The present case arose from a series of judicial proceedings concerning the applicant, Mr. Justice Nhlanhla Lebea, an attorney practicing in Bloemfontein. Mr. Lebea was appointed by the Provincial Department of Public Works and Infrastructure (the Department) to lead evidence in a disciplinary inquiry against Mr. Sango Menye, the first respondent ([para 2]). The inquiry faced several postponements due to the unavailability of witnesses, including the Head of the Department. On 3 October 2014, a postponement was sought by the Department, which was opposed by Mr. Menye's legal team ([para 4]).

After the inquiry was postponed, an agreement was reached regarding the payment of Mr. Menye’s legal costs due to the postponement. The circumstances of this agreement became contentious, with Mr. Lebea asserting that he only arranged for Mr. Menye's lawyers to submit a bill of costs without acknowledging any formal undertaking to pay these costs ([paras 6–7]).

Following an unfavorable ruling in the Magistrates’ Court—where the court accepted Mr. Menye's version of the events and made an adverse credibility finding against Mr. Lebea for making misrepresentations about the funding agreement—Mr. Lebea sought to challenge this credibility finding through the court ([para 11]). He applied for leave to intervene in the ongoing proceedings but was denied, under Rule 28(1) of the Magistrates’ Court Rules, because the court found he lacked a direct and substantial interest in the matter ([paras 12–13]).

After his application for leave to appeal was rejected by both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal, Mr. Lebea brought the matter before the Constitutional Court, citing potential far-reaching implications for his personal and professional reputation as a result of the adverse credibility finding ([paras 14–15]). The court had to address whether the situation engaged constitutional issues and whether Mr. Lebea's application for leave to appeal should be granted ([paras 16–18]).

I – Issues

The key issue before the Constitutional Court was whether a witness who is not a party to court proceedings can seek leave to intervene for the purpose of appealing against an adverse credibility finding made against them ([para 19]). Additionally, the court needed to determine if the matter raised a constitutional issue under sections 10 and 34 of the Constitution, concerning human dignity and the right to a fair public hearing, respectively ([paras 17–18]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal rule governing intervention in this context was Rule 28(1) of the Magistrates’ Court Rules, stating that a court may grant leave to a person to intervene if they have a direct and substantial interest in the proceedings ([para 12]). The Constitutional Court’s jurisdiction is outlined in Section 167(3) of the Constitution, allowing it to decide constitutional matters or any other matter deemed to raise an arguable point of law of general public importance ([para 16]).

Precedents relevant to this case included Wynne v Divisional Commissioner of Police and National Director of Public Prosecutions v Zuma, which emphasized the necessity of a direct and substantial interest for intervention ([paragraphs 23, 27]). The court also considered the implications of broadening the intervention rules in relation to the interests of witnesses ([para 42]).

A – Application

The court applied the law to determine that Mr. Lebea did not possess the required direct and substantial interest in the outcome of the legal proceedings between Mr. Menye and the MEC. Notably, Mr. Lebea's concern was focused on the adverse credibility finding rather than any substantive legal order affecting him directly, which was integral to the rule on intervention ([paras 30–31], [42]).

Moreover, the court discussed the implications of granting leave to appeal against credibility findings separate from the merits of the order itself. The judgment emphasized that allowing such appeals could complicate the adjudication process, lead to delays in litigation, and burden the court system unnecessarily ([paras 42–43]). In rejecting the application for leave to intervene, the court underscored the notion that adverse credibility findings could not be a valid basis for intervention as they do not directly impact an applicant’s legal interests in the way required by law.

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court refused Mr. Lebea leave to appeal, holding that he lacked the necessary direct and substantial interest in the matter for which he sought intervention ([paras 44–45]). The court reasoned that expanding the concept of direct and substantial interest could lead to an influx of similar applications from witnesses and could create confusion within the legal process. Thus, it concluded that the situation should be left for legislative consideration rather than judicial expansion of common law ([para 43]). The decision reinforces the stringent standards for intervention and highlights the importance of directly affected interests in legal proceedings, ensuring the integrity and efficiency of the judicial system.
