# FIRAC Case Note: Milner Street Properties (Pty) Ltd v Eckstein Properties (Pty) Ltd

### F – Facts
The case Milner Street Properties (Pty) Ltd v Eckstein Properties (Pty) Ltd ([1]–[2]) revolves around a dispute regarding a value-added tax (VAT) liability arising from the sale of a property situated at 37 Willowton Road, Pietermaritzburg. The appellant, Milner Street Properties, is a registered vendor under the Value Added Tax Act 89 of 1991 (the Act), and so is the respondent, Eckstein Properties. The crux of the matter stems from an agreement for the sale of immovable property, initially proposed in July 1994, shortly before a significant amendment to the VAT legislation took effect on November 25, 1994.

Initially, Eckstein Properties agreed to sell the property to Lenco Holdings Ltd for R3.5 million. Following the amendment, the parties believed that the transaction would qualify for zero rating under section 11(1)(e) of the Act, which requires certain formalities to be met—a stipulation that arose with the 1994 amendment ([6]–[8]). However, when the new agreement was drafted, neither party was aware of the new requirements, leading to a situation where the necessary formal conditions for zero rating were not included. Accordingly, the VAT assessed amounted to R429,824.56, which Milner Street Properties sought to reclaim from the Commissioner for Revenue Services ([1]–[2]).

After various correspondences and a demand for a tax invoice reflecting this VAT liability, the parties were divided over whether the agreement for sale constituted a zero-rated transaction due to these formal omissions ([17]–[19]). When litigation ensued, the initial court dismissed the appellant's claim to a VAT invoice and granted Eckstein Properties a counterclaim for rectification of the agreement to include the necessary conditions to validate the zero rating ([21]).

### I – Issues
The pivotal legal issue is whether the transaction between Milner Street Properties and Eckstein Properties met the statutory requirements for zero rating under section 11(1)(e) of the Act, particularly given the insufficient adherence to the amended formalities outlined therein ([9]–[10]). Additional questions pertain to whether the doctrine of rectification could apply, given that the apparent non-compliance was attributed to mutual ignorance of the law rather than any deliberate omission or fraudulent intent ([22]).

### R – Rules
The statutory framework at play is primarily governed by the Value Added Tax Act 89 of 1991. The pertinent provisions include:
- **Section 7(1)(a)**: VAT is levied on the supply of goods or services in furtherance of any business ([2](a)).
- **Section 11(1)(e)**: This section specifically allows for zero rating where the supply is by one registered vendor to another, and the enterprise is disposed of as a going concern—a provision that requires that both parties agree in writing to certain stipulations ([8]).

Furthermore, the principles of rectification are crucial, particularly in situations where an apparent non-compliance with a legal framework does not result from a deliberate misrepresentation or omission ([24]) but arises from mutual misunderstanding of the law. Legal precedents such as *Intercontinental Exports (Pty) Ltd v Fowles* ([24]) provide context on the limits and applicability of rectification concerning compliance with formal statutory requirements.

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal examined whether the parties had indeed met the core requirements for zero rating as established under section 11(1)(e). It was acknowledged that while the formal requirements were not satisfied in the written agreement, the court emphasized the intent and mutual understanding between both parties that the transaction should be zero rated ([22]–[23]).

The court opined that not allowing rectification would elevate formalism over substance, effectively denying a legitimate claim to the benefits of zero rating when all substantive conditions were agreed upon by the involved parties. This interpretation was bolstered by the VAT Practice Note No. 14 issued by the Commissioner, illustrating a flexible approach to transactions entered into before awareness of legislative changes. The court further noted that the requirement of written agreements was largely probative and not constitutive of validity in this specific context ([28]–[30]).

Consequently, the court found that since the parties intended the transaction to be completed under the zero rating provisions as understood at the time, a rectification was appropriate to align the written contract with the original intent, thus fulfilling the conditions stipulated by the amendment retroactively ([31]–[33]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appellant's appeal and upheld the lower court's order granting rectification. It determined that the necessary grounds were satisfied for rectification to take place and that the intentions of the parties should not be overshadowed by technical non-compliance with newly imposed statutory formalities. As a result, the agreement would be treated as if it had complied with the formal requirements from the outset, allowing for the zero rating under the Act to apply. The ramifications of this holding underscore the courts' willingness to enforce equitable remedies where parties are able to demonstrate mutual intent and the actual understanding of a contractual undertaking ([34]).

This case serves as an important precedent in the interpretation of VAT law compliance and the application of rectification in South Africa, emphasizing the significance of intent and mutual understanding in contractual agreements, particularly in the context of legislative changes.
