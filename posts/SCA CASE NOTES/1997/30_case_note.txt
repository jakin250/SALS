# FIRAC Case Note: Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl

**Case Title:** Amalgamated Banks of South Africa v Johannes Jacobus de Goede & Albert Bernard Eager [1997] SZH 318/95  
**Court:** Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl, Suid-Afrika  
**Date of Hearing:** 25 February 1997  
**Date Delivered:** 27 March 1997  
**Coram:** Hefer, Grosskopf, Marais, Schutz, Streichert AJ

---

### F – Facts

The case concerns a dispute originating from a credit facility agreement made by Volkskas Beperk (hereinafter referred to as "Volkskas") with Lydenburg Passasiersdienste B K (the "closed corporation") in September 1989. As a condition for extending overdraft facilities, Volkskas required that the members of the closed corporation provide personal surety. The first respondent, Johannes Jacobus de Goede, and the second respondent, Albert Bernard Eager, along with another individual, held a partial interest in the closed corporation, while a senior figure, J J de Goede (senior), maintained a majority interest.

On December 4, 1989, the respondents signed a limited surety agreement at the behest of Volkskas. However, the closed corporation later defaulted on its debt to the bank. Subsequently, in April 1992, the appellant (the bank) sought to recover a debt of R74,863.43 through court proceedings, naming four defendants: the closed corporation and the three individuals as sureties. Initially, a summary judgment was passed on June 16, 1992, against some parties, but the first and second respondents were allowed to defend based on their claim that they had not obtained the mandatory written consent from their spouses for the suretyship agreement as required under Section 15(2)(h) of the Marriage Act (Act 88 of 1984).

The respondents contended that they were married in community of property, making the requirements of the statute applicable. However, the appellant argued that the stipulations of Section 15(6) of the Marriage Act exempted them because the suretyship was executed in the ordinary course of their business actions.

---

### I – Issues

The principal legal issues before the court were:

1. **Whether the exclusion in Section 15(6) of the Marriage Act applies, given that the suretyship was executed in the ordinary course of the respondents' business.** [para 13.1]
2. **Whether the lack of compliance with Section 15(2)(h) of the Marriage Act results in the surety agreement being declared void.** [para 13.2]

---

### R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions and rules cited in the case include:

- **Section 15(2)(h)** of the Marriage Act (Act 88 of 1984), which prohibits a spouse married in community of property from becoming a surety without the written consent of the other spouse.
- **Section 15(6)** of the Marriage Act, which provides that the requirements of subsection (2) do not apply when actions are taken in the ordinary course of a spouse's profession, trade, or business.
- **Section 46(a)** of the Close Corporations Act (Act 69 of 1984), which grants every member of a close corporation the right to participate in the business affairs of the corporation.
- Case law referenced includes *A A Mutual Insurance Association Ltd v Biddulph and Another* 1976 (1) SA 725 (A), regarding the interpretation of "business."

---

### A – Application

In the application phase, the court analyzed whether the respondents' actions fell within the ordinary course of business as allowed by Section 15(6). The court found that while the respondents were not actively involved in the day-to-day operations of the closed corporation, they had signed the suretyship as members, making their actions integral to their role within the corporation.

The court emphasized that business activities could encompass individual transactions that could be interpreted as "in the ordinary course" of business. It assessed the nature of the closed corporation’s business and determined that signing the surety was consistent with their responsibilities as members, even if those duties were not performed on a daily basis. It was also relevant that the respondents had financial interests through their loans to the corporation, adding weight to their involvement in the business.

Furthermore, the court considered previous legislative intentions behind the Marriage Act, aiming to balance equitable rights between spouses while not stifling business operations. Thus, their ruling indicated that the lack of written consent did not invalidate the suretyship when it was executed as part of their roles in the corporation's business dealings.

---

### C – Conclusion

The court concluded that the respondents’ signing of the suretyship agreement fell within the ordinary course of their business as members of the closed corporation. Therefore, the provisions of Section 15(6) of the Marriage Act were applicable, and the appellant's appeal was upheld. The court held that the questions posed in the referenced paragraph 13.1 were to be answered in the affirmative, and it dismissed the previous court's judgment regarding the invalidation of the surety agreement.

The decision effectively clarified the application of statutory obligations for spouses engaged in business relationships and underscored the principle that business operations should not be unnecessarily impeded by marital property laws.
