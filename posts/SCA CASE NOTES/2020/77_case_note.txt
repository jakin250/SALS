Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Mathekga and Another v S (Case no 717/2019) [2020] ZASCA 77 (30 June 2020)

## F – Facts
[1] In the case at hand, Constables Mmereki Welcome Mathekga and Johannes Thulani Mngomezulu, the appellants, were involved in a tragic shooting incident on 14 January 2013. Responding to an urgent call reporting that a colleague, Constable Daniel Ndima, had been shot, the appellants approached the scene in a marked police vehicle. Observing two individuals armed with firearms, who turned out to be Constables Tshomela and Khumalo, they mistakenly identified them as suspects and opened fire, resulting in the death of Constable Tshomela and severe injury to Constable Khumalo, alongside injuries to civilians and damage to surrounding property, including vehicles at a nearby taxi rank ([paras 1–5]).

[2] Both appellants were charged in the Gauteng Division of the High Court with murder, attempted murder, and malicious damage to property. They were convicted and sentenced to 15 years’ imprisonment for murder, while also receiving concurrent lesser sentences for the attempted murder charges. The appellants appealed their convictions and sentences with the leave of the trial court ([para 2]).

[3] The trial court found that the appellants acted unlawfully, lacking reasonable suspicion that their victims had committed any offense and determining that excessive force was used. The appellants argued that their actions were justified under section 49(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA), which allows for the use of force by police when making an arrest; however, the trial court concluded otherwise upon examining the evidence presented ([paras 5–12]).

## I – Issues
[4] Three main legal issues arise from this case:

1. Whether the appellants could objectively and/or subjectively justify their actions under section 49(2) of the CPA.
2. Whether the appellants were aware that the deceased was a police officer and thus whether section 51(1) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (CLAA) applied in this case regarding sentencing.
3. Whether the 15-year sentence imposed by the trial court was appropriate given the circumstances surrounding the conviction ([paras 7–8]).

## R – Rules
[5] The legal framework primarily involves several critical legislative provisions:

1. **Section 49(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**, which permits the use of force by an arrestor under specific conditions, outlines criteria that must be met for the use of deadly force to be considered justified, including whether the suspect posed a threat of serious violence ([paras 8–9]).

2. **Section 51(1) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997**, which stipulates minimum sentences for certain serious offenses, imposes a life sentence for murder when committed against a law enforcement officer in the execution of their duties. The applicability of this section depends critically on the perpetrator's knowledge of the victim's capacity as a law enforcement officer ([paras 20–21]).

3. **Relevant case law** including *S v De Blom* and *Govender v Minister of Safety and Security*, which shape the judicial interpretation of justified police actions and establish standards for assessing the proportionality of force in arrests ([paras 6, 9]).

## A – Application
[6] In evaluating the actions of the appellants, the court noted the factual background and evidence presented throughout the trial, emphasizing the absence of any immediate threat posed by the deceased and the other officer. It highlighted that the shooting was initiated almost immediately upon the appellants' arrival at the scene, without any effort made to confirm identities or issue warnings ([paras 11–15]).

[7] The court underscored that despite the appellants' training and experience as police officers, they failed to act reasonably or proportionality in their response to a perceived threat. The determination that the victims posed no immediate danger was crucial in concluding that the appellants acted outside the protective scope of section 49(2) ([paras 16–17]).

[8] On the issue of whether the appellants were acutely aware that Constable Tshomela was a police officer, the court found insufficient evidence to qualify their actions under the harsher provisions of section 51(1), noting that their failure to recognize fellow officers maximally mitigated their culpability regarding the designation of the victim as a law enforcement officer, thus rendering the application of severe sentences based on this statute inappropriate ([paras 21–23]).

[9] The court also revisited the sentencing aspect critically, noting the trial court's misdirection on applying the minimum sentence legislation incorrectly, leading to the excessive original sentences. Consequently, in consideration of the substantial and compelling circumstances present—being first offenders and the unusual context surrounding the shooting—the court found that a lesser sentence of 13 years was fitting ([paras 29–30]).

## C – Conclusion
[10] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded by dismissing the appeals regarding the murder convictions, reaffirming the trial court’s findings that the appellants acted with intent to kill, thus aligning with the legal standards for murder ([paras 31–33]). However, in light of the judicial oversight identified in sentencing, the court modified the sentences imposed, reducing them from 15 years to 13 years’ imprisonment, establishing a precedent aimed at ensuring proportionate responses for police conduct in the face of extraordinary circumstances ([paras 29–30], [32]).

[11] The implications of this judgment are significant, as it mandates a careful reassessment of the application of deadly force by law enforcement personnel and highlights the necessity for adherence to proper protocol, reinforcing that the extent of the police's authority is necessarily bounded by the principles of lawfulness and proportionality in both action and consequence.
