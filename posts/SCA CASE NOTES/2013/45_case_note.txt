Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Food & Allied Workers Union v Ngcobo (353/12) [2013] ZASCA 45

## F – Facts
The appellant, Food and Allied Workers Union (FAWU), a trade union registered under the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA), faced claims from the respondents, L Ngcobo, as executrix of the estate of the late Mandla Ndlela, and M Mkhize, for damages due to FAWU's failure to adequately represent them after their dismissals from Nestlé South Africa (Pty) Ltd (Nestlé) ([1]).

Ndlela and Mkhize were employed as sales representatives by Nestlé for approximately 22 and 20 years, respectively. In late 2001, Nestlé initiated a restructuring process that affected their positions. Although neither Ndlela nor Mkhize were FAWU members at the time, they sought to join FAWU amidst these developments ([11]–[15]). Following their dismissal on 15 May 2002, they were represented by FAWU at a conciliation hearing held on 18 June 2002, where the union raised an objection concerning their membership status. This issue was resolved in the respondents' favor, leading to the issuance of a non-resolution certificate ([17]).

Subsequent to this, FAWU failed to refer the dispute to the Labour Court within the stipulated timeframe, neglecting to inform the respondents of this failure. Over time, the respondents sought the assistance of the University of Durban-Westville Law Clinic, where it was discovered that no proceedings had been initiated in the Labour Court ([19]–[20]). In August 2004, both respondents instituted actions for damages in the KwaZulu-Natal High Court ([27]).

The court awarded each respondent damages of R107 232 plus interest, finding FAWU liable for breach of mandate ([1],[29]). However, FAWU appealed against this ruling, arguing that the respondents' claims should have been dismissed, while the respondents sought increased damages through a cross-appeal ([1]). The appeal and cross-appeal were ultimately dismissed by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]).

## I – Issues
The case presented several legal issues, particularly concerning:
1. Whether Ndlela and Mkhize were members of FAWU at the relevant times.
2. The nature of the agreement or mandate between FAWU and the respondents.
3. Whether FAWU breached that agreement and if it bore liability for the resulting damages.
4. Whether the respondents could reasonably have mitigated their losses by taking independent action.
5. The quantum of damages recoverable if liability was established ([29]).

## R – Rules
The legal foundation of the case is derived from the LRA, which stipulates employees’ rights regarding unfair dismissal and the obligations of trade unions in representing their members ([3]–[5]). Key provisions included:
- Section 185 of the LRA which ensures the right to not be unfairly dismissed.
- Section 191 concerning the referral of disputes to the Labour Court.
- Provisions for unfair dismissal, which allow for reinstatement, re-employment, or compensation as articulated in Sections 193 and 194 of the LRA ([4]).

The court's analysis was influenced by established contract law principles, particularly regarding the obligation of agents to perform mandated tasks with diligence and care ([46], [51]). The union's failure to act adequately constituted a breach of its duty, exposing it to liability for consequential damages stemming from that breach ([30]).

## A – Application
In its analysis, the court upheld that the respondents had established their case for breach of contract against FAWU. The union accepted the mandate to represent the respondents yet failed to act timely and competently in lodging their claims in the Labour Court ([32]–[34]). 

The court recognized that although the respondents did not apply for condonation for the late filing, which could have been seen as a mitigating factor, FAWU’s failures predominantly contributed to the failure to secure a successful outcome in the Labour Court ([36], [38]). The delay of over 20 months in filing the relevant papers was significant, yet the court stated that it was probable that an application for condonation would have been granted considering the merits of the case and the historical context of the dismissals ([39]–[42]).

In evaluating damages, the court ultimately focused on whether the respondents would have succeeded in the Labour Court had FAWU properly discharged its obligations. Given the evidence presented, including the nature of the dismissals and the lack of consultation, the court found significant grounds suggesting that the dismissals were unfair, justifying the compensation sought by the respondents ([46]–[55]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court's decision, reaffirming FAWU's liability for failing to perform its mandate adequately. The court emphasized that the respondents had been deprived of their fair opportunity to seek relief from unfair dismissal due to FAWU's negligence. Importantly, the legal standing of the respondents as members at the time of their dismissal was upheld, and they were entitled to compensation reflective of the damages incurred due to this breach. 

The appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the principle that trade unions are accountable for the representation provided to their members ([43], [63]). In terms of public policy and the fair treatment of employees, the case exemplifies the critical balance between the obligations of unions and the protections afforded to workers under the LRA.
