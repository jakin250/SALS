# Case Note: Malindi v Commercial Union Insurance Company Ltd (Case No 103/95)

## F - Facts
The appellant, Enoch Malindi, was involved in a motor vehicle accident on 7 February 1987, which resulted in injuries that led to a claim for compensation under the Motor Vehicle Accidents Act 84 of 1986 ('the Act') ([1]). Following the submission of a completed claim form, the respondent, Commercial Union Insurance Company Ltd, did not object to the validity of the claim. On 19 December 1990, the respondent offered to settle the claim, specifying amounts for future medical expenses (R2,250.00), general damages (R2,000.00), and legal costs (R300.00), while denying liability for future earnings ([2]–[3]). This offer was stated to be valid for 60 days and was made “without prejudice” to avoid litigation ([3]). 

Malindi did not accept this offer and initiated legal proceedings in June 1993. The respondent raised the defense of prescription, asserting that the notice served to the appellant was a valid offer under section 14(2) of the Act, which interrupted the running of prescription for 90 days from the date of the offer ([4]). The appellant contested the validity of the respondent’s offer on two grounds: (1) it was only open for acceptance for 60 days, contending that offers must remain open for 90 days, and (2) the reference to future earnings suggested that the offer was contingent upon further documentation and was therefore incomplete ([5]).

## I - Issues
The legal issues revolved around the interpretation of section 14(2) of the Motor Vehicle Accidents Act and whether the offer made by the respondent constituted a valid offer that would revive the running of prescription. Key questions included:
1. Does an offer to settle made for a period less than 90 days satisfy the criteria for interrupting prescription under section 14(2)(b) of the Act? ([6]).
2. Did the appellant appropriately reject the offer regarding future earnings, or did the offer remain valid to address the entire claim? ([18]).

## R - Rules
The relevant rule derived from the Motor Vehicle Accidents Act, particularly section 14, stipulates that:
- If a claims agent does not object to a claim's validity within 60 days, prescription is interrupted until either repudiation of liability or conveyance of an offer of settlement occurs ([3], [5]). 
- Section 14(2) specifies that for interruption of prescription, an offer must be "unqualified and unconditional" to trigger its resumption ([7]). 

Conflicting precedents from previous cases, such as *Ngantweni v National Employers General Insurance Co Ltd* 1991 (2) SA 645 (C) and *Lucas v Sentraboer (Co-operative) Ltd* 1995 (1) SA 334 (W), ruled that offers must remain valid for 90 days, whereas *Joka v Commercial Union Insurance Co of SA Ltd* 1994 (3) SA 391 (W) interpreted the provision differently, allowing for shorter periods ([5]). 

## A - Application
In reviewing the facts, the Court noted the plain language of section 14(2) does not explicitly require an offer to remain open for 90 days. The judges emphasized that the legislature's intent was to enable the claims agent to either repudiate liability or make a bona fide offer substantially sufficient to allow the claimant to consider whether to settle or pursue litigation. The Court affirmed that the validity and sufficiency of the respondent’s offer were not compromised by its 60-day limitation ([17]–[19]). 

On the appellant's point that the respondent’s offer regarding future earnings was nullified due to inadequate documentation, the Court found this argument unconvincing, stating that the offer’s intent was clearly to dispose of the claim entirely, and the explanation for refusing future earnings did not imply an invitation for further proof ([18]). Thus, the Court ruled that the prescription was indeed effective after the expiration of the 90-day window following the offer, thereby validating the respondent's defense ([19]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the offer made by the respondent did constitute a valid offer under section 14(2) of the Act, effectively resuming the running of prescription after 90 days post-offer. The Court ruled that appellant's failure to accept the offer within the specified 60 days led to the prescriptive period expiring before he instituted action in June 1993, rendering the claim extinguished ([19]). 

As such, the Court dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the decision from the court a quo that had upheld the plea of prescription ([19]). The case clarifies essential aspects of interpreting settlement offers within the context of prescription under the Motor Vehicle Accidents Act, reinforcing the principle that the legislative intent was not to provide an automatic 90-day acceptance period but to facilitate a claimant's choice between acceptance and pursuing legal recourse. This delineation underscores the need for clarity in claims processing while balancing the rights of claimants and insurers.
