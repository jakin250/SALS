# FIRAC Case Note: Swanepoel N.O. v Profmed Medical Scheme [2024] ZACC 23

### F – Facts:
The case concerns **Carlo Swanepoel** (the executor of the estate of **Mignon Adelia Steyn**, the original applicant who passed away) and **Profmed Medical Scheme** ([1]). Ms Steyn, a member of Profmed, had her membership terminated by the scheme citing alleged non-disclosure of material medical conditions on her application form ([4]–[5]). The termination was based on claims that she failed to disclose significant medical history, which included conditions like gastritis (later misconstrued as a gastric ulcer) ([8]–[9]).

The procedural history began with a complaint made by Ms Steyn to the **Registrar of Medical Schemes**, which was dismissed. Subsequent appeals to both the **Council for Medical Schemes** and the **Appeal Board** were also unsuccessful ([10]–[15]). Ms Steyn sought judicial review in the High Court, which ruled in her favor, setting aside the Appeal Board's decision and ordering Profmed to honor its contractual obligations ([19]–[29]).

However, Profmed appealed this decision, and the Full Court of the High Court overturned the High Court's ruling, reinstating the Appeal Board's decision ([30]–[32]). This led to an unsuccessful application for leave to appeal to the **Supreme Court of Appeal** before requesting that this court hears the matter ([33]).

### I – Issues:
Several legal and constitutional issues arose:
1. Whether the executor of a deceased estate is entitled to continue a review application under the **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act** (PAJA).
2. The materiality of the alleged non-disclosed medical conditions and whether there was a duty to disclose them ([34]).
3. Issues of procedural fairness in the hearings before the various Appeal Bodies ([25]).

### R – Rules:
The court considered several legal principles:
1. **PAJA**: It emphasizes the right to just administrative action and guarantees procedural fairness ([19]–[24]).
2. Statutory provisions concerning **material non-disclosure** within the **Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998** ([74]–[75]).
3. Case law, including *Regent Insurance* and *Oudtshoorn Municipality*, that established the standard for assessing materiality and the insurer's obligation to prove that the non-disclosure induced the contract ([17], [79]).

### A – Application:
The court assessed whether the Appeal Board acted fairly and correctly regarding procedural matters and the materiality of Ms Steyn’s alleged non-disclosures. 

1. **Procedural Fairness**: The court found that Ms Steyn was not given the opportunity to present evidence regarding new claims raised by Profmed at the Appeal Board level, violating her procedural rights ([58]–[80]). The failure to allow her full participation constituted a serious irregularity ([81]–[83]).

2. **Materiality of Non-Disclosures**: The court scrutinized the previous decisions regarding whether gastritis and a hip arthroscopy were material non-disclosures. The court determined that both were either immaterial or mischaracterized, arguing that gastritis is common and typically does not pose a risk warranting non-disclosure ([89]–[100]). The court emphasized that a rigorous test for materiality includes an objective consideration of whether a reasonable person would have deemed the undisclosed information relevant to the insurer ([79]–[98]).

3. **Substitution Application**: After considering the executor's standing and whether the PAJA claims were transmissible to the estate, the court found it appropriate to substitute the executor as the applicant given that the estate had a financial interest in the administrative action ([55]–[58]), asserting that Mr. Swanepoel was entitled to represent the estate under section 6(1) of PAJA ([57]).

### C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court of South Africa upheld the application for substitution of the executor as the applicant and granted leave to appeal. The appeal was upheld, revoking the Full Court's decision and reinstating the High Court's ruling, thereby ruling that Profmed must honor its commitments under Ms Steyn’s policy ([112]). The case established important principles regarding the transmissibility of administrative justice rights posthumously and reinforced protections against unfair administrative action, particularly in the context of medical schemes ([112], [107]). 

The implications of the judgment highlight the necessity for medical schemes to adequately justify materiality claims and ensure procedural fairness in their dealings with clients ([110]–[111]).
