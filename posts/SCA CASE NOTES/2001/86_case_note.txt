# FIRAC Case Note: Capri Oro (Pty) Limited and Others v The Commissioner of Customs & Excise and Others

---

### F – Facts

[1] This case arises from an appeal against a judgment that involved the interpretation of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 (“the Act”), particularly regarding the seizure and forfeiture of goods brought into South Africa without appropriate declaration. The first appellant, Capri Oro (Pty) Limited, and the second and third appellants, David Mazor and Pentagold Srl, respectively, challenged the actions of customs authorities related to jewellery seized at Johannesburg International Airport.

[2] The factual background centers around the second appellant, David Mazor, who arrived from overseas at Johannesburg Airport carrying 77 kg of jewellery belonging to the third appellant. His intention was to fly to Namibia later that day to facilitate a sale of the jewellery. Upon arrival, he experienced a three-and-a-half-hour delay before departing and obtained permission to leave the transit area to meet his father, passing through the “nothing to declare” channel. Shortly thereafter, he was detained by police who seized the jewellery. Additionally, the following day, further jewellery was confiscated from the business premises of the first appellant, where the second appellant was employed.

[3] The primary relief sought by the appellants in the lower court was denied, with MacArthur J delivering a judgment reported at 1998(3) SA 571(T). The appellants were granted leave to appeal this decision.

### I – Issues

[4] The central issue on appeal is whether the trial court correctly determined that the jewellery seized from Mazor was liable to forfeiture under sections 87(1) and 15(1) of the Act. Specifically, the court needed to ascertain whether the lack of unreserved declaration of the goods upon entry amounted to a contravention of the Act, thus rendering the goods subject to forfeiture.

[5] Additional questions included whether the respondents returned all the additional jewellery seized and the appropriateness of the costs order made by the trial court.

### R – Rules

[6] The relevant provisions of the Act include:

- **Section 15(1)**: Specifies that persons entering the Republic must unreservedly declare goods upon entry, which includes goods purchased abroad, modified, or controlled under law.
  
- **Section 87(1)**: Stipulates that goods dealt with contrary to the Act or in respect of which an offence has been committed are liable to forfeiture regardless of the possessor.

- **Section 88(1)**: Permits the detention of goods by customs officials to establish their liability for forfeiture.

- **Section 89(1)**: Establishes a process for the notice of claim by the owner concerning seized goods.

- **Section 93**: Provides the Commissioner with discretion over the remission or mitigation of penalties and forfeiture.

### A – Application

[7] The trial court found that the second appellant had contravened section 15(1) by failing to make the requisite declaration upon entering South Africa, even though the appellants argued that the second appellant had not committed a criminal offence as required under section 87(1) for forfeiture. The court highlighted that an offence under the Act is not necessary to render goods liable to forfeiture, as the phrase "contrary to the provisions of this Act" in section 87(1) includes actions that do not amount to criminal conduct.

[8] The appellants' defense was weakened by their prior admission that they had not declared the jewellery as required by section 15(1). The court dismissed arguments claiming the declaration process was not properly communicated or that Mazor had declared the goods to South African police officials post-hoc. The seizure occurred after Mazor had already traversed the customs point without declaring the goods, invalidating any subsequent declaration.

[9] The court further evaluated the distinction between goods merely in transit versus those brought into South Africa with clear intent to enter the local economy. It determined that the jewellery, having physically entered South Africa under the appellants' control, was subject to the forfeiture provisions of the Act despite the intended destination being Namibia.

[10] Regarding the additional jewellery seized from the first appellant's premises, the court upheld the trial judge's findings that all appropriate goods had been returned and dismissed the appeals against the costs order as lacking merit.

### C – Conclusion

[11] The court concluded that the trial court correctly applied sections 87(1) and 15(1) of the Act, thus ruling that the jewellery seized at Johannesburg Airport was lawfully subject to forfeiture due to non-compliance with declaration requirements. The court affirmed the trial court’s judgment and ordered the appellants to pay the costs associated with both the appeal and their applications for condonation. 

[12] The ruling underscores the strict enforcement of customs laws and the responsibilities of individuals bringing goods into the country, emphasizing that intent to export goods does not negate the requirement for declaration upon entry. This decision emphasizes the importance of compliance with customs regulations, serving as a precedent for similar cases involving the seizure of goods under the Customs and Excise Act.

[25] The appeal was dismissed on all counts, with the appellants bearing the costs, reinforcing the principle that adherence to customs regulations is paramount and that failure to comply can result in serious financial repercussions.
