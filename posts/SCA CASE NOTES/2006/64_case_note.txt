# FIRAC Case Note: Ralukukwe v S [2006] SCA 64 (RSA)

## F – Facts
On 27 March 2000, Samson Shonisani Ralukukwe (the appellant) and five other co-accused were convicted by the Venda High Court for murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances. The appellant received a life sentence for murder and a 15-year sentence for robbery. The trial judge, Hetisani J, denied leave to appeal, necessitating a subsequent appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]).

The critical evidence against the appellant emerged from the events of 15 November 1998. Accused 1, who traveled with accused 2, 3, 4, and 5 to Venda ostensibly to consult the appellant on security business matters, was later implicated in a murder ([2]). The appellant organized accommodation for the co-accused, including spending the night at his home with some ([3]).

The next day, after experiencing mechanical issues, accused 1 and 2 approached the residence of the deceased to inquire about a mechanic, during which a confrontation ensued, culminating in the murder of the deceased ([4]–[5]). Subsequent police investigation led to the arrest of the co-accused, with crucial evidence garnered from accused 1, who made an incriminating statement to the magistrate after being apprehended ([6]–[8]).

During the appeal, the appellant's counsel contested the admissibility of accused 1's statement against him, invoking section 219 of the Criminal Procedure Act, 51 of 1977, which prohibits confessions made by one accused from being used against another. In contrast, the State sought admission under the Law of Evidence Amendment Act, 45 of 1988, specifically section 3, which governs the admissibility of hearsay ([8]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues in this case revolve around the application of sections 219 of the Criminal Procedure Act and 3 of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act in determining the admissibility of a co-accused's statement against the appellant. Specifically, the critical questions were:

1. Can a confession by one accused be used against a co-accused in the context of the Criminal Procedure Act?
2. Under what conditions can an admission be used against a co-accused, particularly when considering the requirements laid out in the Law of Evidence Amendment Act? ([8]–[9]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal provisions include:
- **Section 219 of the Criminal Procedure Act, 51 of 1977**: No confession made by any person shall be admissible as evidence against another.
- **Section 3(1)(c) of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act, 45 of 1988**: Allows for hearsay evidence under specific considerations relating to the nature of the proceedings and the probative value of the evidence ([9]).

The determination of what constitutes a "confession" versus an "admission" is also critical in this analysis. The definition in **R v Becker** distinguishes a confession as an unequivocal acknowledgment of all elements of the offense charged, while an admission need not meet such stringent criteria and can be admissible against the declarant ([9]).

## A – Application
In applying these legal principles to the case, the court examined the implication of Section 219. The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the confession by accused 1 did not entail an acknowledgment of the specifics needed for it to be considered a confession capable of being applied against the appellant. As a result, the appellant's claim under section 219 was found to lack merit ([12]).

The court also scrutinized the State's reliance on section 3(1) of the 1988 Act. It ruled that this section had not been invoked during the original trial; therefore, it could not subsequently be used against the appellant without causing unfair prejudice, as he was never allowed to confront the statement made by accused 1 ([13]).

Thus, the court determined that the evidence from accused 1 must be excluded entirely when assessing the appellant's guilt. Without this statement, the remaining admissible evidence did not substantiate the charges, leading to the conclusion that there was insufficient evidence to prove the appellant's mens rea or connection to the crimes committed ([14]–[15]).

## C – Conclusion
The appeal was upheld, and the convictions against the appellant were overturned. The Supreme Court emphasized the importance of a fair trial, establishing that no admissible evidence linked the appellant to the crimes charged. Consequently, the court's decision underscored the constitutional requirement for due process in criminal proceedings, reiterating that mere suspicion is inadequate for conviction ([16]–[19]).

The judgment not only affected the appellant but hinted that co-accused 4 and 5 might have similarly been wrongfully convicted, prompting the court to recommend further investigation into their cases. The implications of this judgment reaffirm the stringent standards of admissibility and the protection of the accused's rights within the South African legal framework ([17]).
