FIRAC Case Note: The National Prosecuting Authority v PSA obo Meintjies & Others [2021] ZASCA 160

F – Facts

The case revolves around a dispute regarding the Occupational Specific Dispensation (OSD) salary structure, specifically whether it applied to Deputy Directors of Public Prosecution (DDPPs) and Chief Prosecutors (CPs) under the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA). The Public Servants Association (PSA), acting on behalf of 56 DDPPs and CPs, initiated legal proceedings in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, arguing that their remuneration should be aligned with the OSD structure. The NPA, the Minister of Justice, the Director-General of the Department of Justice, and other state officials contested the application ([1–14]).

Negotiations regarding wages were conducted at the Public Service Co-ordinating Bargaining Council (PSCBC) in 2007 and subsequently formalized in collective agreements. These negotiations led to the introduction of GPSSBC Resolution 1 of 2008, which established the OSD salary structure for legally qualified personnel in the justice sector ([2–5]). However, the NPA’s subsequent salary determinations did not mention the OSD, leading to disagreements regarding its applicability to DDPPs and CPs ([6–10]).

The High Court, under Judge Van der Westhuizen, initially ruled in favor of the PSA, asserting jurisdiction to hear the application based on the arguments presented, which included claims of unfair labor practices and rights under the Basic Conditions of Employment Act ([11–13]).

I – Issues

The principal issue was whether the High Court had jurisdiction to adjudicate the application concerning the OSD and the claims made by the PSA. Specifically, did the legal basis of the claim, rooted in alleged unfair labor practices and collective agreements, fall within the exclusive or concurrent jurisdiction of the Labour Court and High Court as defined by the Labour Relations Act and the Basic Conditions of Employment Act? Moreover, the court had to address whether the NPA’s refusal to implement the OSD constituted an unfair labor practice ([15–14], [57–58]).

R – Rules

The court considered several statutory provisions, including:

  • Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA): Section 157 stipulates the jurisdictional framework for labor disputes, highlighting concurrent jurisdiction between the Labour Court and the High Court in issues not exclusive to the Labour Court ([57–58]).
  • Basic Conditions of Employment Act 75 of 1997 (BCEA): Section 77(3) provides for concurrent jurisdiction over contracts of employment ([57–59]).
  • Precedent cases, notably Chirwa v Transnet Ltd and Gcaba v Minister of Safety and Security, underscored the importance of assessing jurisdiction based on the pleadings and the nature of the claims presented ([58–60],[62–71]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal conducted a thorough analysis of the pleadings from the PSA, noting that jurisdiction must be determined based on the legal basis underpinning the applicant's claim. The PSA's claims were primarily framed around enforcing collective agreements and the 2010 Determination regarding OSD, which implied contractual obligations ([16–23]).

The court assessed whether the PSA successfully established a contractual basis for its claims by referencing the 2010 Determination and purported actions taken by the NPA. It was noted, however, that the PSA's claims revolved around general obligations arising from collective agreements rather than enforceable individual employment contracts. Furthermore, the court emphasized that agreements and resolutions did not encompass an automatic entitlement to OSD without individual consent and compliance with requirements established in prior negotiations ([26–36]).

The appeal court reviewed the evidence, including minutes from meetings and correspondence, determining that the PSA had not substantiated its claims regarding the consent of DDPPs and CPs to be translated to OSD. The court concluded that the NPA did not unlawfully refuse to implement the OSD, and the approvals given were conditional, lacking the finality needed to compel compliance ([31–43]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the High Court did not possess the jurisdiction to adjudicate the matter as it should have been addressed through labor dispute mechanisms per the LRA. The appeal was upheld with costs, and the High Court’s order was set aside, with the application struck from the roll due to lack of jurisdiction ([1–2], [54–72]).

The implications of the decision reaffirmed the jurisdictional demarcations established by labor law in South Africa and underscored the necessity for parties to comply with appropriate dispute resolution avenues, particularly in employment-related conflicts. The judgment also raised significant considerations regarding the enforceability of collective agreements within administrative frameworks of public service employment.
