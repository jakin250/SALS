Supreme Court of Appeal

Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund v Nkosi & Lekhuleni [1999] ZASCA 44

The Fund raised special pleas based on regulation 3(1)(a)(v) of the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act 93 of 1989 (the Act), asserting no liability because there was no physical contact between the Golf and the other vehicles involved.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1999 [1999] ZASCA 44

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts:

The case concerns a collision occurring on 16 April 1992, involving three vehicles on the N4 near Johannesburg. A driver of an unidentified Golf vehicle (the Golf) was negligent, leading to a situation where neither the Golf nor its driver was identified. The first respondent, Daniël Makhingila Nkosi, sought damages for injuries sustained while a passenger in a Toyota minibus that collided with another vehicle (the Ford) that had been impacted by the Golf. The second respondent, Tryphinah Ntombi Lekhuleni, sought loss of support due to her husband's death, who was the driver of the Toyota. Both respondents cited the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund (the Fund) and Santam Limited as defendants, under provisions allowing claims against unidentified vehicles and negligent drivers, respectively ([1]–[3]).

The Fund raised special pleas based on regulation 3(1)(a)(v) of the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act 93 of 1989 (the Act), asserting no liability because there was no physical contact between the Golf and the other vehicles involved. The respondents contended that this regulation was ultra vires and thus invalid, and the trial court (Spoelstra J) supported the respondents on this point ([4]–[6]).

The appeals followed the trial court's determination of liability, particularly concerning whether the regulation was ultra vires and whether the driver of the Ford (Perumal) was negligent. The appeal was consolidated with a similar case questioning the validity of the regulation ([7]).

I – Issues:

The core legal issues of this case involve:

  1. Whether regulation 3(1)(a)(v) is ultra vires the provisions of section 6 of the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act, thus rendering it invalid.
  2. Whether the driver of the Ford was negligent and liable for the damages sustained in the collision, despite the lack of direct contact with the Golf ([6]–[8]).

R – Rules:

Key rules and legal authorities applicable to this case include:

  • Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act 93 of 1989: This Act establishes the framework for liability against the Fund and outlines the roles of the Minister in making regulations ([3], [4]).
  • Regulation 3(1)(a)(v): Prohibits the Fund from being liable if there is no physical contact with the unidentified vehicle.
  • Common law principles regarding negligence as articulated in cases such as Kruger v Coetzee 1966 (2) SA 428 (A) and SAR v Symington 1935 AD 37, which provide the standard for assessing the conduct of drivers in terms of reasonable care under similar circumstances ([12]–[16]).

A – Application:

Upon reviewing the evidence, the court evaluated the circumstances around the collision that transpired under challenging weather conditions, highlighting the chaotic nature of the traffic on the road. The incident was considered severe enough that the Golf's reckless driving necessitated a response from the other drivers to avoid a collision ([9]–[10]).

The court found insufficient evidence for concluding that Perumal's response to the situation was negligent. Evidence presented indicated that his reaction could have been a reasonable response to an unexpected and dangerous situation caused by the Golf’s actions. The court emphasized that a driver is not expected to have their eyes fixed on their rearview mirror constantly, nor could they anticipate all actions of approaching vehicles ([12]–[16]).

The court rejected findings from the trial that suggested Perumal should have inferred danger from an oncoming vehicle’s flashing lights, highlighting the ambiguity of such signals and the lack of a factual basis to ascribe negligence under those circumstances ([11]–[17]).

C – Conclusion:

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Fund’s appeal regarding the ultra vires finding but ruled in favor of the second appellant, Santam. The court held that Perumal was not negligent, overturning the liability imposed by the lower court. The judgment clarified that the regulation was indeed ultra vires, but the evidence did not substantiate claims against Santam due to lack of demonstrated negligent conduct on Perumal's part.

The implications of this decision underscore the challenges in determining liability in motor vehicle accidents involving unidentified vehicles while reinforcing the principle that negligence must be evidenced by actual failure to meet reasonable standards under specific conditions ([1]–[23]).

Overall, while the respondents were initially victorious at trial, the Supreme Court's judgments rolled back some determinations on negligence and liability, rendering the Fund accountable for the collision with respect to the unidentified Golf, and establishing a precedent for future cases involving similar circumstances.
