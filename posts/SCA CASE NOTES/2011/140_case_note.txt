Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

## FIRAC Case Note: Van der Watt v Jonker (837/2010) [2011] ZASCA 140

### F – Facts
The case arose from a dispute concerning a restraint of trade agreement following the separation of business interests between Mr. Gert Jakobus van der Watt and Mr. Christiaan Jacobus Jonker, who had previously worked together in various petroleum distribution businesses in the Free State Province ([1]–[5]). The Van der Watts, specifically Gert and his wife Martha, were involved in the business of Dynamic Fuels, which operated in areas serviced by Jonker's companies, Agriwen (Pty) Ltd, Agrigen Petroleum (Pty) Ltd, and Agrigen Diesel Bultfontein (Pty) Ltd ([2]–[5]). 

Jonker initiated legal proceedings against the Van der Watts in the Free State High Court after alleging that they breached a 10-year restraint clause by soliciting customers from the Agri group ([6]). In a prior agreement dated April 25, 2007, the parties decided to separate their business ventures, with Jonker paying R2 million in cash to the Van der Watts and each party agreeing to non-competition clauses that were deemed fair and reasonable ([5], [6]).

The high court ruled in favor of Jonker, enforcing the restraint and holding the Van der Watts accountable. The appellants then appealed on several grounds, chiefly contesting Jonker’s locus standi and the enforceability of the restraint agreements ([6]–[14]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:
1. Whether Jonker had locus standi to enforce the restraint clause despite not being a direct owner of the goodwill associated with the Agri group businesses ([7]–[9]).
2. Whether the restraint could be deemed enforceable, particularly in light of claims that Jonker lacked a protectable interest ([7]–[8]).
3. Whether the duration of the restraint, set at ten years, was reasonable and aligned with the interests of the business ([14]).

### R – Rules
The court's decision relied heavily on several legal principles and precedents regarding restraints of trade and the protection of goodwill:
- **Goodwill as Property**: Goodwill is considered an incorporeal property right that must be protected when sold with a business. It includes the advantages stemming from a business’s reputation and established customer relationships ([10], [11]).
- **Locus Standi**: The court established that a contracting party may enforce contractual rights even if they lack ownership of certain underlying assets, as long as their interest arises from the contract itself as per Basson v Chilwan ([7], [8]).
- **Reasonableness of Restraint**: Restraint clauses are examined closely to determine if they are reasonable in duration and geographical scope, as established in previous rulings. Notably, the reciprocity of restraint agreements plays a critical role in their enforceability ([14]).

### A – Application
In evaluating Jonker's right to enforce the restraint, the court clarified that he indeed had locus standi. As the sole shareholder post-agreement and a party to the contract, he had a vested interest in enforcing the restraint, countering the argument that goodwill resided solely with the Agri companies ([7], [9]). The court emphasized that while Jonker did not directly own the goodwill, his contractual position afforded him sufficient protectable interests ([8]).

Regarding the enforceability of the restraint, the court further explored the interconnectedness of goodwill and restraint at sale. As established in A Becker & Co v Becker, the seller of goodwill cannot indiscriminately solicit former customers once the goodwill is conveyed ([10], [11]). The indisputable fact that Van der Watt began soliciting former clients from the Agri group while operating Dynamic Fuels demonstrated a breach of the restraint ([11], [12]).

Moreover, the court found the duration of the ten-year restraint reasonable, noting various considerations that justified its length, including the nature of the businesses involved, their competitive landscape, and the transition dynamics between the parties ([14]). Each factor weighed heavily against a finding that the duration was excessive.

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the high court's ruling. The court reiterated that the separate business entities involved had sold and transferred goodwill, hence the restraint agreement was enforceable against the Van der Watts ([14], [15]). The judgment emphasized the importance of maintaining contractual integrity in matters of business goodwill, highlighting that parties engaged in similar commercial ventures are bound by agreements established during their separation. The court's ruling underscored the necessity of fairness in restraint agreements while protecting business interests, with significant implications for future dealings in similar contexts.
