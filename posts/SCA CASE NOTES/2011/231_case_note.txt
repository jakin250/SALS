Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: FIRAC Method

## Case Title:
**Harold Gunase v Ramesh Anirudh (826/10) [2011] ZASCA 231**

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–12])
The case arises from a traffic accident on 9 July 1992, where the respondent, Ramesh Anirudh, was injured after being hit by a motor vehicle driven by Mr Goundon. Anirudh sustained serious injuries and was hospitalized for about a year ([para 3]).

In October 1993, Anirudh appointed the appellant, Harold Gunase, to pursue a third-party claim against the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund (MVA Fund) under the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act 93 of 1989 ([para 4]). He provided necessary documentation to Gunase and was regularly informed about the status of his claim until he discovered in 2001 that Gunase's practice had closed down ([paras 5–7]). 

During a period between 2000 and 2004, Anirudh did not follow up on his claim but later attempted to ascertain its status through another attorney, Ms. Aggie Govender, who could not find his file ([paras 7–8]). In April 2005, Anirudh sought the assistance of another attorney, Anushka Parbhoo, who ultimately discovered that his claim had not been lodged with the MVA Fund ([para 9]).

By January 2006, it was confirmed that the claim was never submitted, and Anirudh was advised to take legal action against Gunase for negligence due to the alleged prescription of his claim ([paras 10–12]). On 21 October 2008, after extensive correspondence that failed to yield results, summons was issued against Gunase.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 13–17])
The core legal issue revolves around whether Anirudh's claim had prescribed by the time he initiated action against Gunase. Specifically, the court needed to ascertain whether Anirudh exercised reasonable care to obtain knowledge regarding his claim, as delineated by sections 11 and 12 of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([para 14]).

Key issues include:
1. Did Anirudh have knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the facts of the debt on time?
2. Did he exercise the requisite reasonable care under section 12(3) of the Prescription Act that would have prevented the prescription of his claim?
3. What constitutes "reasonable care" in the context of exercising one’s legal rights and seeking remedy after an injury?

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 13-22])
The court based its determination primarily on the provisions of the Prescription Act, specifically sections 11, 12(1), and 12(3). Section 11(d) establishes that a debt shall prescribe after three years unless otherwise provided by law. Section 12(1) states that prescription commences as soon as the debt is due, while section 12(3) establishes that a creditor is deemed to have knowledge of their claim if they could have acquired it by exercising reasonable care ([paras 13–14]).

Judicial precedents, including *Burley Appliances Ltd v Grobbelaar NO & others* 2004 (1) SA 602 (C) and *Drennan Maud & Partners v Pennington Town Board* 1998 (3) SA 200 (SCA), were referenced to highlight that a creditor’s inaction does not extend the period of prescription. The court reiterated that reasonable care requires diligence in ascertaining facts and that the standard of evaluation must be objective rather than subjective ([paras 15–19]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 18–22])
In applying the law, the court found that the respondent's inactions—failing to inquire about his claim from 2001 until 2005—demonstrated a lack of reasonable care under section 12(3) of the Prescription Act. Despite having the opportunity to consult another attorney or inquire with the MVA Fund, Anirudh neglected to pursue steps that would have clarified the status of his claim ([paras 18–19]).

The court explicitly noted that Anirudh's claim against the MVA Fund had prescribed by 8 July 1995, three years after the incident, due to his lack of due diligence from 2001 onwards, when he discovered that Gunase's office was closed ([paras 16–18]). The initial trial court had erred by failing to consider the implications of section 12(3), resulting in a miscalculation of the claim’s status ([para 21]).

Thus, the Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Anirudh did not observe the requisite care and should have reasonably suspected the non-filing of his claim much earlier than he did. His failure to act promptly regarding his legal rights led to the prescription of his claim.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 22–22])
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal with costs and set aside the previous ruling, concluding that the special plea of prescription was valid and the claim must be dismissed. The decision emphasized that the burden is on the claimant to demonstrate reasonable care in securing knowledge about their claim ([para 22]). 

The ratio decidendi illustrates the importance of timely legal action after an injury to avoid the prescription of claims, reaffirming the principle that creditors cannot remain inactive in the preservation of their rights without consequences. This ruling affirms that knowledge, or the opportunity to acquire such knowledge, triggers the start of the prescription period, serving as a cautionary measure for future claimants regarding their responsibilities in seeking legal remedies in a timely manner. 

This judgment serves not only to provide clarity on the application of prescription law but also emphasizes the critical necessity for claimants to be proactive in pursuing their claims—especially in personal injury scenarios where delays can significantly impact the outcome.
