Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 125 F – Facts: Relevant background and proce

FIRAC Case Note: Nkosi v The State (20727/14) [2015] ZASCA 125

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–2])
Thabo Macbeth Nkosi, the appellant, was convicted along with co-accused of murder and robbery following an armed robbery at a coal yard in Wattville, Benoni. The robbery took place around 6 PM when Mr. Dennis Sikhumbuso Ngobese, the coal yard owner, was closing his business. Nkosi was part of a gang that entered Ngobese's office demanding money, while armed with firearms. During the robbery, Mr. Bongani Jabulani Skhosana, one of Nkosi's accomplices, was fatally shot by Ngobese, who acted in self-defense after Skhosana shot at him and wounded an employee, Mr. Dennis Mabaso. Following Ngobese's testimony, which detailed the chaotic shootout, Nkosi was convicted of murder alongside two counts of robbery with aggravating circumstances and unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition ([paras 2–3]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 3–4])
The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the trial court correctly convicted Nkosi for the murder of Skhosana, given that his death was the result of lawful self-defense by Ngobese. Specifically, the court needed to address Nkosi’s argument that Skhosana had acted independently in a "frolic of his own," which would absolve Nkosi of responsibility under the doctrine of common purpose. Furthermore, the court considered whether the necessary intent, or dolus eventualis, was proven in relation to Nkosi's culpability ([paras 3–4]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 4–10])
Central to the court's determination was the doctrine of common purpose as applied in prior cases such as S v Molimi & another ([2006] ZASCA 43) and S v Nkombani & another (1963 (4) SA 877 (A)). The court affirmed that participants in a joint criminal venture may be held liable for the unforeseen consequences of their actions, including the death of a co-actor, if it could be shown they foresaw the possibility of such outcomes. The criteria for proving dolus eventualis were outlined, asserting that a subjective foresight of death during the commission of a robbery, coupled with the acceptance of that risk, binds co-actors to liability ([paras 4–7]).

Additionally, the court addressed the issue of self-defense and its legal implications, particularly emphasizing that a lawful act of defense does not negate the prior unlawfulness of a robbery in determining liability for murder under common purpose ([paras 8–10]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 5–12])
The court found that the circumstances surrounding the robbery involved a high likelihood of armed resistance, which the co-actors were acutely aware of when they armed themselves for the crime. Unlike in Molimi, where the death was caused by an act independent of the robbery, in Nkosi’s case, the onset of violence was directly linked to the unlawful act of the robbery itself. The court rejected Nkosi's assertion that Skhosana's actions constituted a separate and independent frolic.

The court also distinguished Nkosi's case from Mkhwanazi, arguing that postulations of foreseeability and intent must consider the nature of the circumstances in which the robbery was executed. The evidential context, including testimonies about the armed nature of the robbery and the ensuing shootout, established that all parties were aware of the inherent risks, thus satisfying the requirements for dolus eventualis.

In evaluating the overall culpability, the court concluded that by entering into a joint agreement to commit robbery armed with loaded firearms, Nkosi and his co-actors inherently accepted the potential for violence and the resultant death, reinforcing the convictions ([paras 5–12]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 13–14])
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Nkosi’s appeal, affirming his conviction for murder and robbery. The court emphasized that engaging in an armed robbery while knowing the risks associated with such criminal actions implicates all participants in foreseeable consequences, including the potential death of co-actors. The ruling underscores the responsible legal interpretation of the common purpose doctrine, reinforcing that participants cannot evade responsibility for outcomes they foreseeably bring about through their collective, unlawful actions.

The decision illustrates the balance between individual accountability and collective criminal responsibility under South African law, highlighting how perpetrators of a violent crime bear the risks that accompany their unlawful intentions. The ruling also reiterates the importance of assessing criminal liability based on the established facts, making it a significant reference point for future cases involving the complexities of joint criminal ventures ([paras 13–14]).
