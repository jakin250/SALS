Supreme Court of Appeal

Wishart v Justice P Blieden NO 659/13 [2014] ZASCA 120 (19 September 2014)

The appellants argued that, although they were never formal clients of the lawyers, their prior connections as directors of companies that had instructed the lawyers created a "quasi-client" relationship warranting the requested interdict.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 120 (19 September 2014)

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–31])

The appellants, Grant Logan Wishart, Malcolm Grant Wishart, and Shabier Bhayat, sought an interdict from the KwaZulu-Natal High Court to prevent a group of lawyers (the respondents) from interrogating them in an insolvency inquiry into Avstar Aviation (Pty) Ltd, initiated under sections 417 and 418 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973. The inquiry was ordered by the appointed commissioner, P Blieden, at the request of BHP Billiton Energy Coal South Africa Ltd, which had previously been represented by the lawyers in various legal matters concerning companies connected to the appellants.

The appellants argued that, although they were never formal clients of the lawyers, their prior connections as directors of companies that had instructed the lawyers created a "quasi-client" relationship warranting the requested interdict. The high court dismissed their application, finding no grounds to substantiate their claims of conflict of interest, as the lawyers did not possess any confidential information pertaining to the appellants ([paras 1–6], [31]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 29–34])

The principal legal issue was whether the common law should be developed to protect individuals, such as the appellants, who claimed to have a quasi-client relationship with lawyers acting against them in legal proceedings. The appellants contended that, based on public policy considerations, it was improper for lawyers who had previously represented companies associated with them to act adversarially without disclosing any confidential information.

Furthermore, the case raised questions about the extent to which the common law provides protections against conflicts of interest for non-clients, specifically whether the established principles in South African law regarding former client protections extend to those who are merely related or connected to the client companies ([paras 29–34]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 32–40])

The case examined the common law principles established in Robinson v Van Hulsteyn Feltham and Ford which focuses on the necessity for lawyers to preserve confidences imparted by clients, thus providing a foundation for protecting former clients against conflicts of interest.

The Prince Jefri Bolkiah v KPMG case was also referenced, which established the judicial framework for restraining lawyers from acting against former clients where confidential information is involved. The English law precedents were contrasted with the approach taken by courts in jurisdictions such as the United States and Australia, demonstrating a spectrum of protective measures and the necessity to maintain the integrity of the judicial process ([paras 32–34], [37–40]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 41–56])

The court concluded that the appellants' assertion of a quasi-client status was untenable, as they had never been formal clients of the lawyers nor had they disclosed any confidential information to them during prior representations. It emphasized that the purpose of protecting former clients is to safeguard against the misuse of confidential information, a principle not applicable to the appellants in this case.

The court declined to extend the common law to include "quasi-clients," maintaining that this could undermine the rights of lawyers to choose their clients freely and impede the administration of justice. It stressed that allowing the appellants' request would lead to potential delays in ascertaining the truth regarding Avstar's affairs, thus contravening public interest ([paras 41–56]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 55–56])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, confirming the high court's ruling that the appellants did not demonstrate a clear right to the interdict nor established potential injury from the lawyers’ interrogation. The judgment clarified that existing legal frameworks adequately protect against conflicts of interest primarily through the lens of direct client relationships.

The ratio decidendi emphasized the importance of preserving the integrity of legal representation and the administration of justice while balancing litigants' rights to select their counsel without undue restrictions. This ruling reinforced the boundaries of the attorney-client privilege and highlighted the judiciary's reluctance to extend these protections beyond traditional client relationships, reaffirming the status quo of South African common law ([paras 55–56]).
