Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: Jochen Kofahl v S L Keiley [2007] SCA 41 (RSA)

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–12])

This case involves an appeal by Jochen Kofahl (the appellant) against a judgment by Willis J in the Witwatersrand Local Division, which awarded S L Keiley (the respondent) US $100,000 in damages. The dispute arose from the appellant's alleged repudiation of a contract with the respondent regarding the marketing and sale of the ‘Hydraform Machine,’ a brick-making machine invented by the appellant in 1988. The machine was patented and sold primarily in South Africa and some African countries.

The relationship between the parties began in 1995 when they were introduced by John Carter. The respondent, an American businessman with substantial academic credentials, intended to partner with the appellant to expand the market for the Hydraform machine worldwide, especially outside Africa. In November 1995, they formed an oral agreement wherein the appellant would grant the respondent and Carter shares in a new company, Palmerfield Ltd, to market the machine internationally.

Despite initial optimism regarding the venture, including projections of high sales in Argentina, by early 1997, the joint venture had failed to generate substantial sales, with only eight machines sold by the valuation date (6 January 1997). Following the deterioration of their working relationship, the appellant repudiated the agreement in September 1996. The respondent cancelled the agreement and subsequently sought compensation for 10% of Palmerfield's purported value, which was determined by the court to be US $1 million.

After Blieden J found the appellant liable for repudiation, the matter of quantum was referred to Willis J, who awarded the respondent US $100,000. The appellant’s appeal focused solely on whether the respondent met the burden of proving the value of the shares.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11–13])

The main issue on appeal was whether the respondent established the value of the shares in Palmerfield as of 6 January 1997, satisfying the onus of proof placed upon him. The appeal court particularly scrutinized whether the valuation was credible given that Palmerfield possessed no assets outside of a disputed exclusive license to sell the Hydraform machines.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 14–17])

The appeal court considered various precedents and principles in assessing share valuations, relying heavily on the established legal rule that the valuation should reflect what a willing buyer would pay a willing seller in an open market. The court cited Holt and Others v Inland Revenue Commissioners [1953] 2 All ER 1499, emphasizing the need to presume the foresight of a prospective buyer who would conduct due diligence.

In addition, the court referenced Illovo Sugar Estates Ltd v South African Railways & Harbours 1947 (1) SA 58 (W), reinforcing the necessity of analyzing all relevant factors impacting potential earnings and market viability. Furthermore, in Scott and Another v Poupard and Another 1971 (2) SA 373 (A), the court reiterated that a prudent buyer would conduct comprehensive inquiries into the subject matter's true worth, taking into account all pertinent data available concerning the company’s operational context and market conditions.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 18–20])

In applying the law to the facts, the appeal court critically analyzed the evidence presented regarding the value of Palmerfield’s shares. It noted the optimism surrounding the housing market in Argentina but also recognized that this optimism was not matched by actual sales, reflecting a significant disconnect between market interest and tangible commercial success.

The evidence presented by the respondent concerning sales projections and market potential was found wanting. Notably, the testimony by Patricia Scott, who had minimal relevant experience, and Professor Wainer, who based valuations on questionable projections, contributed little factual basis to establish a credible market for the shares. Moreover, the court highlighted the absence of adequate market research and the lack of concrete sales due diligence, which could have provided the necessary grounding for justifying the valuation.

Furthermore, the prospective buyer's hypothetical inquiries would have revealed numerous factors that detracted from the perceived value, such as operational challenges, lack of assets, and the presence of competing products with established market acceptance. Consequently, the court agreed that due to the lack of credible evidence from the respondent, the lower court’s valuation of US $100,000 was not supported factually.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 21–22])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the respondent failed to meet the onus of establishing a credible value for the shares in Palmerfield as of 6 January 1997. The judgment of the lower court was reversed, and the appeal was upheld, leading to an order for “absolution from the instance with costs.”

This decision underscores the imperative of substantively supporting assertions regarding valuation with appropriate and credible evidence, particularly where substantial financial implications are at stake. The court's reflection on the necessity for due diligence and comprehensive market understanding presents a reiteration of the standards expected in contractual and commercial disputes, reinforcing the criticality of thorough evidence when asserting claims in business ventures.

This ruling thereby not only affects the parties involved but also serves as a cautionary tale for future parties engaged in similar business arrangements, emphasizing the importance of robust market research and valuation methodologies in commercial ventures.
