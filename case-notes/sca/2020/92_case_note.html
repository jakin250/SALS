Tshiki v The State (358/2019) [2020] ZASCA 92

Aggrieved by the convictions and resultant sentences, Tshiki's application for leave to appeal was partially granted, with the full court dismissing his appeal against his convictions ([02]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 92

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

The appellant, Mr. Mpho Robinson Tshiki, along with co-accused Mr. Thomas Maluleke, faced charges of murder (count one), robbery with aggravating circumstances (count two), unlawful possession of a firearm (count three), and unlawful possession of ammunition (count four) in the North West Division of the High Court ([01]). They were convicted despite Tshiki's plea of not guilty, as the trial court found that no substantial and compelling circumstances warranted a deviation from the minimum sentences prescribed by the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([01]). Tshiki received a life sentence for murder, 15 years for robbery, three years for unlawful possession of a firearm, and two years for unlawful possession of ammunition ([01]).

Aggrieved by the convictions and resultant sentences, Tshiki's application for leave to appeal was partially granted, with the full court dismissing his appeal against his convictions ([02]). Subsequently, the Supreme Court of Appeal granted him special leave to appeal against this dismissal ([02]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the court were:

  1. Application of the cautionary rule regarding the evidence of a single witness who was an accomplice—whether the trial court correctly applied this rule in its findings ([11]).
  2. Whether the appellant's alibi and his denial of complicity were reasonably possibly true ([12]).

R – Rules

The constitutional and statutory rules pertinent to this case can be summarized as follows:

  1. Onus of Proof: The State bears the burden of proving the accused's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, and the accused’s version cannot be dismissed merely as improbable without the trial court having credible evidence to assert its falsehood ([13]).
  2. Section 208 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA): An accused may be convicted on the single evidence of any competent witness. The credibility of such a witness must be scrutinized, particularly if they are an accomplice ([14],[15]).
  3. Cautionary Rule: Evidence by an accomplice must be approached with caution due to the risks of self-serving testimonies and the potential for fabrication. This principle requires corroboration to reduce the risk of wrongful conviction ([15]).
  4. Assessment of Evidence: A trial court must weigh the evidence presented to them, considering inherent strengths, weaknesses, probabilities, and improbabilities ([23],[46]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) analyzed the evidence primarily from Maluleke, the accomplice whose testimony was crucial to the conviction. The court noted several contradictions within Maluleke's previous statements and his trial testimony, particularly regarding the sequence of events leading to the murder and robbery ([16],[24]).

  1. Cautionary Rule Application: The court found that the trial court erred in relying solely on Maluleke’s testimony without adequately applying the cautionary rule or considering the lack of corroboration ([24],[27]). Maluleke’s self-incriminating statements were contradicted by other evidence presented, which did not link Tshiki to the commission of the offences.
  2. Tshiki’s Alibi: Tshiki's alibi rested on evidence that he was employed at Morula Sun and worked during the hours the crime occurred. The trial court initially dismissed this when it was confronted with a document confirming that Tshiki left work earlier than claimed ([30]). However, according to the SCA, even if this alibi was contradicted, it remained a credible defence when considering the totality of evidence. The court concluded that Tshiki’s version, asserting he was with his girlfriend during the time of the crime or at work, was not definitively disproven ([32]).
  3. Credibility Assessment: The SCA underscored the need to assess all evidence to determine if Tshiki's alibi was "reasonably possibly true." The presence of witnesses who could potentially exonerate him was overlooked by the trial court, leading to a flawed verdict ([35]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the SCA found that the trial court misdirected itself by placing undue reliance on Maluleke's testimony, which was fraught with inconsistencies and lacked corroboration ([36]). The appeal was granted, and the SCA would have set aside the convictions and resultant sentences, arguing that the State failed to prove Tshiki’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt ([36]).

The implications of this case are significant, particularly concerning the evidentiary standards required in criminal trials, especially when testimony originates from accomplices. This judgment reinforces the principle that an accused's defence, particularly an alibi, must be examined holistically alongside all other evidence presented, emphasizing the state's burden of proof and the necessity of corroborative evidence when relying on an accomplice’s testimony.
