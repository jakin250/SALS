Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Frieslaar NO v Ackerman (1242/2016) [2017] ZASCA 03

## F - Facts
This matter arises from the actions of the first and second appellants, Glenwin and Angeline Frieslaar, who are the trustees of the Frieslaar Family Trust, along with the third appellant, G & I Plumbers CC. They initiated an action against the first respondent, Petrus Andre Ackerman, and the second respondent, Adlu Projects CC, seeking orders related to the transfer of specific immovable properties (Kaldi Place and Barrish Place) sold under agreements dated 25 February 2010 ([para 1]). 

The appellants claimed that the respondents had purportedly cancelled the agreements on 4 July 2012, which was not accepted, and therefore the contracts remained in force. They sought specific performance for the transfer of the properties, among other forms of relief, including an alternative claim for damages amounting to R2,160,000 for unpaid goods and services provided by G & I Plumbers CC to Adlu Projects CC ([paras 1–2]). 

The legal issue presented hinged on whether the appellants' claims had prescribed under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969. The respondents argued that since the debt arose when the sale agreements were concluded on 25 February 2010, and the summons was served on 7 March 2013, the claim had already prescribed ([para 5]).

## I - Issues
The central legal issue considered by the court was the timing of when the debt triggered the prescription period under Sections 10, 11, and 12(1) of the Prescription Act. Specifically, the court needed to determine:
1. When did the debt related to the transfer of the properties become due?
2. Did the appellants' claim for the transfer costs and related obligations constitute a debt as defined under the Prescription Act?
3. What effect did the purported cancellation of the contract by the respondents have on the running of prescription? ([para 9]).

## R - Rules
The rules relevant to this appeal are primarily found within the Prescription Act 68 of 1969:
- Section 10: "A debt shall be extinguished by prescription after the lapse of the period which in terms of the relevant law applies in respect of prescription of such debt."
- Section 11: Defines the period of prescription, stating general claims are subject to a three-year period.
- Section 12(1): Asserts that prescription begins to run as soon as the debt is due.

The court referenced earlier case law, such as *Desai NO v Desai NNO & others* and *Mohlomi v Minister of Defence*, to establish that the obligation to perform creates conditions where a debt is considered due when the creditor is in a position to enforce their rights ([paras 15, 27]).

## A - Application
In applying the established rules, the court assessed that the relevant agreements stipulated an obligation for the respondents to undertake the payment of transfer costs immediately upon the sale's conclusion. The absence of a specific timeline for payment meant that the obligation was immediately claimable ([para 31]). 

Despite the appellants’ arguments that the prescription period did not commence until a reasonable time post-agreement for the payment of transfer costs, the court found that the obligation to perform, as stipulated under clause 7.1 of the agreements, translated to an immediate debt as per the Prescription Act. The respondents' assertion that the debt was due from 25 February 2010 was upheld ([paras 28–40]). The assertion that the debt would not be due until the completion of transfer costs was rejected, illustrating that the arguments to delay the commencement of prescription were inconsistent with the statutory provisions ([paras 39–42]).

Importantly, the court noted that prescription cannot be affected by the creditor’s independent decision to delay enforcing their rights ([para 41]). The court upheld the finding from the court of first instance that prescription commenced running as soon as the agreements were concluded and concluded that the appellants failed to act within the prescriptive period ([para 48]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal with costs, confirming that the obligations outlined in the sale agreements constituted a debt as per the Prescription Act. The court's ruling emphasized that prescription commenced immediately upon the due date, which logically followed from the nature of the agreements being silent on exact timelines for performance. This case illustrates the court's adherence to the clear parameters set by the Prescription Act while concurrently highlighting the necessity for creditors to act promptly in enforcing their rights. Dismissal of the appellants’ claim reaffirmed the principle that inaction within the statute of limitations leads to the extinguishment of debts, promoting judicial economy and the finality of disputes ([para 42]).
