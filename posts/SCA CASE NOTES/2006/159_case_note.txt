Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH
Source Folder: 2006

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Safety & Security v N Folo & 2 Others [2006] SCA 117 (RSA)

### F – Facts

This case arose from a tragic incident on 28 November 1997, where Mr. Sivuyile Sweleka (the deceased) was shot and killed by Inspector Jameson Dingiso, a member of the South African Police Service, during an attempted arrest ([1]). The deceased, who was driving a stolen vehicle, fled after the car crashed. Dingiso pursued him on foot but, after failing to apprehend him, fired four shots, three of which struck the deceased from behind, causing his death ([1]).

The respondents, consisting of the deceased's wife and children, initiated a lawsuit against the Minister of Safety and Security in the Transkei High Court, seeking damages for the death of their husband and father ([1]). At trial, Dingiso claimed that his use of lethal force was justified under Section 49(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which permitted the use of deadly force under certain conditions to prevent an escape ([2]).

The trial court found that Dingiso's actions were not justified and that he had failed to meet the criteria for lawful harm as determined in the precedent case, **Govender v Minister of Safety and Security** ([5]). This finding was upheld by the full bench of the Transkei Division of the High Court, where the court also cited that reading the requirements for lawful injury from **Govender** into Section 49(2) was necessary, as failing to do so could create an untenable situation ([6]).

### I – Issues

The central issue on appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether Section 49(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act should be interpreted with reference to the requirements established in **Govender**, which involved a standard of imminent serious harm ([7]). Specifically, the question was whether Dingiso had acted within the legal limits of justifiable homicide under the statute as it was worded before being declared unconstitutional ([7]).

Additionally, the court had to consider whether the interpretation of Section 49(2) should allow for reading in the requirements of reasonableness found in Section 49(1) ([8]). This implicated a deeper constitutional question regarding the retrospective application of the now-unconstitutional statute ([10]).

### R – Rules

The primary statutory rule at the heart of the case was Section 49(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which provided the conditions under which the killing of a suspect could be deemed justifiable homicide during an arrest. The relevant part reads as follows:

> "Where the person concerned is to be arrested... and the person authorized... cannot arrest him...other than by killing him, the killing shall be deemed to be justifiable homicide." ([2])

The interpretation of this statute was informed by the preceding case law, particularly **Govender v Minister of Safety and Security** 2001 (4) SA 273 (SCA), and **Ex Parte Minister of Safety and Security and others: In re: S v Walters and another** 2002 (4) SA 613 (CC). In **Govender**, the court laid down that the use of lethal force was only justified if the arrestor had reasonable grounds to believe that the suspect posed an immediate threat of serious bodily harm to themselves or others ([4]).

In **Walters**, the Constitutional Court declared Section 49(2) invalid and unconstitutional, but did so prospectively, thereby allowing the application of the old version of the statute for incidents occurring before the decision ([10]).

### A – Application

The court analysed whether Dingiso's actions fell within the justifiable homicide provisions of Section 49(2) as it previously stood. Mthiyane JA concluded that the previous lower courts had erred by reading the requirements from **Govender** into Section 49(2). Such an interpretation would effectively "save" the statutory provision from being unconstitutional, undermining the clear declarations made in **Walters** ([9]).

Dingiso argued that his chase of the deceased justified the shooting; however, he did not assert that he faced any imminent threat of harm from the deceased or that the theft of the vehicle involved violence that could justify his use of lethal force. Therefore, if the section were interpreted in its ordinary sense, and not in conjunction with **Govender**'s assessments of seriousness and immediacy, Dingiso's reliance on Section 49(2) would fail ([8]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court ultimately allowed the appeal, rejecting the lower courts' interpretations that imposed the **Govender** criteria onto Section 49(2) ([12]). The implications of this ruling reinforced the idea that constitutional validity must adhere strictly to the provisions set forth at the time of the incident, effectively maintaining the protection afforded to public officials under the older statute when it was still in force.

The order of the court a quo was set aside, and the respondents' claims were dismissed with costs, including the costs of two counsel ([13]). This decision reaffirmed the difficulties inherent in reconciling the demands of constitutional law with the realities of enforcement by state actors, particularly in contexts marked by urgency and potential harm.
