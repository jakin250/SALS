Supreme Court of Appeal

The President of the RSA v Democratic Alliance & Others (664/17) [2018] ZASCA 79

This case originates from an appeal by the President of the Republic of South Africa, Jacob Zuma, against an interlocutory ruling made by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 79

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([para 1–8])

This case originates from an appeal by the President of the Republic of South Africa, Jacob Zuma, against an interlocutory ruling made by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria ([para 1]). The appeal was initiated after the court ordered the President to disclose the record of decision regarding a cabinet reshuffle announced on 31 March 2017, which led to the dismissal of Minister of Finance Pravin Gordhan and his deputy Mcebisi Jonas. The Democratic Alliance (1st respondent) challenged the constitutional validity of this decision ([para 3]–[4]).

After the High Court's order to compel the President to provide the record—a provision sought under rule 53 of the Uniform Rules of the Court—the President obtained leave to appeal ([para 6]). However, developments unfolded leading up to the appeal hearing: President Zuma resigned from office, a new cabinet reshuffle was conducted by newly elected President Cyril Ramaphosa, and the review application was subsequently withdrawn by agreement of both parties ([para 7]–[8]).

The primary issue thus arose from whether the appeal had become moot due to these developments and whether the Court had any practical effect on the outcome.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([para 1–2, 10])

The central legal questions before the Court primarily revolved around the concept of mootness as articulated in section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which asserts that if the issues presented will have no practical effect or result, an appeal may be dismissed on that basis alone ([para 2]).

Additionally, the Court needed to assess:

  1. Whether the High Court’s ruling established a precedent extending the scope of rule 53 to executive functions, an argument posited by the appellant.
  2. The relevance of addressing the legality or merit of the High Court's ruling if the underlying application had been withdrawn ([paras 10]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([para 2, 11–19])

The Court primarily referred to section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which addresses mootness ([para 2]). The principles of justiciability were reinforced through previous case law, with specific attention to the precedent articulated in National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality & Others v Minister of Home Affairs 2000 (2) SA 1 (CC), which emphasizes that cases lacking an existing or live controversy are non-justiciable ([para 11]).

Moreover, the court scrutinized whether any exceptions to the rule of mootness existed, referencing case law such as Independent Electoral Commission v Langeberg Municipality 2001 (3) SA 925 (CC), where the court retained discretion to entertain moot appeals under limited conditions ([para 12]). Precedents addressing rule 53's applicability to executive decisions, including Van Zyl & Others v Government of the Republic of South Africa 2008 (3) SA 294 (SCA), were also invoked to illuminate arguments pertaining to executive functions ([para 14]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([para 9–19])

The court's analysis commenced with the mootness question, where it noted that the primary relief sought—disclosure of the record—lacked practical implications following the withdrawal of the review application and the changes in presidency; thus, any ruling from the Court would amount to an advisory opinion ([para 18]). The appellant’s contentions regarding the establishment of a new precedent through the High Court’s ruling were examined critically but ultimately dismissed, as courts have historically applied rule 53 to executive actions. The Court found that the High Court's order did not create a novel precedent impacting future cabinet reshuffles, given the existence of case law affirming the applicability of rule 53 to executive actions ([para 19]).

Furthermore, the Court posited that exploring the implications of the High Court’s interpretations and their potential impact on future executive decision-making would require a grounded factual matrix and context, which was absent due to the matter’s withdrawal ([para 15]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([para 20])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, establishing that there was no practical effect to the relief sought. The judgment explicitly observed that it would not endorse a review of the High Court's decision on its merits, given that the circumstances had left the appeal moot ([para 20]). The ruling serves to underscore judicial restraint in matters that lack concrete implications, reinforcing the necessity for active, live disputes to warrant judicial intervention.

The implications of this decision are significant for future interlocutory applications and the application of rule 53 to executive functions. It delineates the boundaries within which the judiciary may engage with executive actions, ensuring that procedural rules do not encroach on the doctrines of separation of powers, thus preserving the autonomy of the Rules Board to determine the contours of court processes.
