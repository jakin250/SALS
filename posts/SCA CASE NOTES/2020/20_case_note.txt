# Case Note: Nature's Choice Farms (Pty) Ltd v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality ([2020] ZASCA 20)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1]–[3])

The appeal arose from a dispute concerning the alleged prescription of a claim for service charges related to water usage, initiated by Nature’s Choice Farms (Pty) Ltd (NCF) against Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (Ekurhuleni). The proceedings began on 13 March 2014, when NCF sought a declaratory order asserting it owed no debt of R3,863,147.90 for water services from 3 September 2007 to 7 July 2010, claiming that the amount had become prescribed under relevant provisions of Ekurhuleni’s schedule of tariffs ([1]–[2]).

Initial findings in the Gauteng High Court favored NCF, agreeing that the debt had prescribed and issuing an order against Ekurhuleni to reverse the charge. However, the full court reverse the high court’s decision, leading NCF to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([1]). A critical aspect of the case involved the interpretation and enforceability of the schedule of tariffs relevant to the municipal services ([2]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([3])

Key legal questions arose that the Court needed to determine:
1. The precise scope of the issues separated according to rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules of Court.
2. The legal interpretation of the relevant provisions under Ekurhuleni’s tariff schedule, specifically regarding the prescription of the debt.
3. The validity and legality of the conclusions reached by the full court regarding these matters, including potential misunderstandings about the provisions of the Prescription Act ([3]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([18]–[19])

To arrive at its conclusions, the court examined various legal frameworks:
- **Rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules of Court**: Authorized the separation of issues in trial based on practicality for adjudication ([15]).
- **Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000**: Sections 74, 75, 75A, and 95 guided Ekurhuleni's tariff policy, requiring adherence to principles of fairness, accountability, and accurate billing for municipal services ([24]–[29]).
- **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**: Defined the conditions under which prescription occurs, elucidating that a debt becomes unenforceable once it has prescribed ([41]).
- The case **Denel (Edms) Bpk v Vorster** provided precedents on the appropriate use of rule 33(4), specifically cautioning against poorly defined issues that could mislead the court ([16]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([25]–[46])

The SCA analyzed the factual background of how service charges were assessed and billed by Ekurhuleni, noting that an inspection revealed NCF was billed for only 10% of actual water usage due to a documented error in the water meter ([5]–[6]). Furthermore, the court found that the separation of issues under rule 33(4) had not been adequately circumscribed, leading to the misinterpretation of whether the debt had prescribed before Ekurhuleni rendered the disputed invoice ([17]).

The application of section 12 of the Prescription Act was particularly scrutinized, determining that the debt related to the claim arose on the date of the discovery of the billing error, thereby establishing a potential running time for prescription ([41],[42]). The court ultimately concluded that NCF had not properly anchored its claims in the dictates of the Prescription Act but rather rested its argument on the interpretation of the tariff schedule ([21]).

The SCA ruled that without a proper assertion regarding prescription and the unambiguous framing of the issues, the findings of the full court could not stand ([44]). The nature of the charges billed and their validity were also examined through the lens of Ekurhuleni’s obligation under the local government systems, demonstrating that inaccuracies in billing and errors in reporting outcomes do not terminate a municipality's right to recover legitimate debts ([24]–[29]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([45]–[46])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal by NCF was justified, setting aside the order of the full court, thus reinstating the trial court's original order, albeit dismissing NCF’s claim due to the failure to substantiate the claim of prescription satisfactorily. Each party was ordered to bear its own costs, recognizing the complexities and redundancies that arose from the poorly defined separation of issues in litigation ([45]).

This judgment serves as a critical reminder of the necessity for clarity in pleadings and the articulation of issues for trial proceedings. The implications extend beyond this case, underscoring the need for municipalities to enforce their entitlement to recover debts arising from utility provision effectively while adhering strictly to procedural rules. The case also reflects the intersection of local government law, public utility regulation, and the principles governing legislative interpretation.
