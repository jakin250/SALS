Supreme Court of Appeal 2001 1962 (“the Act”) in revised assessments for the tax years 1989

Case Note: The Commissioner for South African Revenue Service v R.M. Woulidge

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The respondent, R.M. Woulidge, established two trusts for his minor children, Laura and Douglas, in 1982, making them both the income and capital beneficiaries ([1]). Subsequently, Woulidge sold shares in four companies to these trusts, which at that time had no assets to cover the purchase price. To facilitate this transaction, he granted the trusts an interest-free credit to cover the purchase cost, although the sale agreement permitted him to charge interest on the unpaid balance. From 1982 to 1987, the trusts received no income from the shares, as the companies declared no dividends ([2]).

In 1988, following a restructuring involving a company called CTP Ltd, the trusts sold half of their shares in a holding company formed to hold the shares of the original companies for R1,642,500. Consequently, the appellant, the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS), sought to tax Woulidge on this income under section 7(3) of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 (“the Act”) in revised assessments for the tax years 1989, 1990, and 1991 ([2], [3]). Woulidge's objection was partially upheld by the Cape Special Court for Income Tax Appeals, which ruled that he should be taxed only on the forsaken interest for the unpaid price of the shares, subject to the in duplum rule that limited the interest deemed to have been accrued ([3]).

The appellant contested the lower court’s finding, arguing that the sale was a simulated transaction and therefore all income from it should be taxable in Woulidge's hands. This argument was contested procedurally, as it was not clearly articulated in earlier correspondence in the case ([5], [6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions concerned the interpretation of section 7(3) of the Income Tax Act, specifically:

  1. Whether the income arising from the sale of shares to the children's trusts should be taxed in Woulidge's hands as income accruing from a donation or disposition under section 7(3) ([1], [3]).
  2. Whether the in duplum rule should apply, limiting the amount of interest deemed to have accrued to Woulidge to the amount of the initial purchase price ([14]).

The procedural issue arose regarding the validity of the appellant's attempt to frame the sale as a simulated transaction at the appellate stage, given that the broader concerns had not been adequately presented at earlier hearings ([5], [8]).

R – Rules

Section 7(3) of the Income Tax Act states, "Income shall be deemed to have been received by the parent of any minor child," if it is received by or for the benefit of the child through various dispositions made by the parent ([1]). This section aims to ensure that any income, which flows to a minor child through parental actions, is treated as income for tax purposes in the parent's hands.

Additionally, the in duplum rule, which limits the recoverable interest to the principal amount, applies in circumstances where interest accrues and serves as a consumer protection mechanism ([12]). This rule was central to the appellant’s attempt to limit the amount of income deemed to have accrued to Woulidge.

Caselaw pertinent to section 7(3) includes Ovenstone v Secretary for Inland Revenue 1980 (2) SA 721 (A) and Joss v Secretary for Inland Revenue 1980 (1) SA 674 (T), both discussing the treatment of donations and considerations included within the framework of the taxation of trusts ([10]).

A – Application

In examining the applicability of section 7(3), the court noted that Woulidge conceded that the forbearance of interest on the unpaid purchase price qualified as a donation under the section due to the nature of the transaction ([4], [13]). Therefore, any income generated by the trusts that related to this donation was indeed taxable in Woulidge's hands.

However, the court differentiated between the mere act of forbearance and the entirety of the sale transaction. The evidence proposed by Woulidge illustrated that the sale itself was not gratuitous, as it involved a proper commercial transaction for a notional market-related price. The failure to charge interest on the credit provided was viewed as a continuing donation, yet that alone did not render the entire sale a sham or simulating transaction ([10], [13]).

The court also confronted the in duplum rule's relevance in this context. Given that no actual interest had ever been charged or accrued, and that Woulidge had enacted a legitimate sale arrangement, the court concluded that the in duplum rule did not apply to limit the income derived from the sale of shares ([12], [14]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, ruling that the application of the in duplum rule by the lower courts was erroneous. The court concluded that while Woulidge's act of not charging interest constituted a periodic donation, the sale of shares itself was valid and should not be treated as a sham. Consequently, Woulidge was required to pay tax on the full amount deemed to have accrued under section 7(3) without the limitations imposed by the in duplum rule.

In terms of costs related to the appeal, the court found that given the partial success of both parties on different arguments, no costs order was made, reflecting a balanced approach to the legal complexities presented in this case ([16], [15]).
