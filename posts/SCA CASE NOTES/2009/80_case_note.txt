# Case Note: The Occupiers of Erf 101, 102, 104 and 112, Shorts Retreat, Pietermaritzburg v Daisy Dear Investments (Pty) Ltd 
**(Supreme Court of Appeal, Case No. 245/08) [2009] ZASCA 80 (3 July 2009)**

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

The case revolves around a group of approximately 2000 occupants (the Appellants), predominantly unemployed and impoverished, who settled illegally on portions of land in Shorts Retreat, Pietermaritzburg. They erected informal dwellings known as shacks, and some even occupied existing buildings on the property ([2–3]). Despite their illegal occupation, which had persisted for over five years, the landowners, Daisy Dear Investments (Pty) Ltd (1st Respondent), did not initiate eviction proceedings until April 2006, prompted by a demand from the Msunduzi Municipality. This demand claimed that the shacks contravened municipal health bye-laws, leading to a formal eviction application filed in October 2006 ([4]).

The High Court (Jappie J) initially ordered the Appellants to demolish their homes and vacate the land, based on the assertion that the requirements established by the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) were met. The Appellants contested this ruling, arguing that the necessary statutory requirements for eviction under PIE were not fulfilled, primarily because no adequate consideration was given to their circumstances or the municipality's obligations to provide alternative accommodation ([5]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented for consideration were:
1. Were the requirements for eviction as set out in PIE satisfied, particularly regarding the just and equitable nature of the eviction?
2. Did the court adequately consider the rights and needs of vulnerable community members, including elderly persons, children, and women-headed households?
3. Was it appropriate to join the Msunduzi Municipality in the proceedings, given its constitutional obligations to facilitate housing for those facing eviction? ([6–13]).

### R – Rules

The crucial rules applicable to this case stem from PIE and relevant constitutional provisions. Sections 26 and 34 of the Constitution of South Africa guarantee the right to housing and access to fair legal processes, respectively. Section 4 of PIE prescribes conditions for eviction, which include:
- A minimum of 14 days' written notice to both the unlawful occupier and the relevant municipality ([5]).
- An assessment of whether an eviction would be just and equitable, based on all relevant circumstances, including available alternative accommodation ([6–7]).

The precedent set in *Port Elizabeth Municipality v Various Occupiers* is significant as it articulates the necessity for mediation in eviction disputes, emphasizing the court's discretion to ensure outcomes that respect the rights of unlawful occupiers ([9]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the initial proceedings to ascertain whether the High Court adhered to the statutory mandates of PIE. It found several lapses:

1. **Lack of Justification for Eviction:** The Court noted that information about potential alternative accommodation was not collected or presented adequately. The failed attempts by the municipality to provide housing options further exacerbated the situation ([6–8]). The High Court did not entertain evidence that could have shown feasible relocation alternatives, highlighting a significant oversight in applying PIE’s provisions.

2. **Inclusion of Vulnerable Groups**: The Court emphasized that the needs of vulnerable groups within the community were not adequately addressed. The absence of considerations regarding elderly persons, children, and female-headed households called into question the justice and equity of the eviction order ([6–10]).

3. **Failure to Mediate**: The second significant oversight was the absence of mediation efforts. The appeal highlighted that the municipality had a role in mediating disputes before eviction orders are issued, which was neglected in the High Court's approach ([9–10]).

4. **Joinder of the Municipality**: On procedural grounds, the Court underscored the necessity of joining the municipality to the case in order to allow it the opportunity to respond and to consider its constitutional obligations towards providing adequate housing for those affected by eviction ([11–14]).

As a result, the appellate court found the previous order of eviction premature and unjust under the current legislative framework ([10]).

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, set aside the High Court's order, and mandated the inclusion of the Msunduzi Municipality in the proceedings. The Court directed the municipality to file a report detailing its steps towards providing alternative accommodation and to evaluate the effects of eviction without such provisions ([1–4],[11–12]). 

The implications of this ruling clarify that compliance with PIE’s requirements is non-negotiable and that municipalities have a vital role in ensuring equitable resolutions to complex eviction cases, particularly when large communities are involved. This judgment serves as a crucial affirmation of the constitutional rights to housing and due process in the context of eviction proceedings. 

Through these measures, the Supreme Court of Appeal sought not only to protect the rights of the occupants but also to engage the municipality in proactive dispute resolution, exemplifying the judiciary's commitment to uphold social justice in housing access.
