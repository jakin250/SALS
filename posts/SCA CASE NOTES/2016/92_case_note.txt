# Case Note: National Tertiary Retirement Fund v Mokadi [2016] ZASCA 92

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case of **National Tertiary Retirement Fund v Mokadi** [2016] ZASCA 92 arises from the employment termination and subsequent pension benefit withholding of Professor A T Mokadi, the former Rector and Vice-Chancellor of the Vaal University of Technology ([1]). Following allegations of severe misconduct, including fraud and corruption, Mokadi was dismissed from the University on 11 July 2006 ([1]). The University then requested the National Tertiary Retirement Fund (the Fund) to withhold Mokadi’s pension benefits pending the resolution of criminal and civil proceedings against him ([2]).

Initially, Mokadi was criminally charged; however, he was acquitted in February 2009. Subsequently, the University pursued civil action against him for damages amounting to R6,073,215.01 ([2]). The Fund resolved to withhold Mokadi's pension benefit under section 37D(1)(b) of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 (the PFA), citing the outstanding civil action and various cost orders against him ([3]). Mokadi lodged a complaint against the Fund with the Deputy Pension Funds Adjudicator on 3 January 2010 ([3]).

The Fund contended that Mokadi’s complaint was time-barred under section 30I of the PFA and asserted that withholding his benefit was justified due to the pending civil action, alongside reference to prior cost orders ([4]–[5]). The Adjudicator ultimately determined that the Fund withheld Mokadi's pension benefit unlawfully and ordered the Fund to pay Mokadi his benefit, including interest from a specified date ([10]).

Following an unsuccessful appeal from the Fund in the Gauteng Local Division ([12]), the matter proceeded to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

### I – Issues
The case presents critical legal issues concerning:
1. The lawfulness of the Fund's withholding of Mokadi's pension benefit and its repercussions under the PFA.
2. The Adjudicator's authority to award interest on the pension benefit.
3. The interpretation and application of sections of the Pension Funds Act, especially concerning conditions under which the Fund may withhold benefits ([1], [12]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework applicable to this case is primarily structured around the **Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956**. Key provisions include:
- **Section 30N**: Allows the Adjudicator to determine whether interest shall accrue when a determination consists of an obligation to pay money. It grants discretion to decide the rate and commencement date for such interest ([14]).
- **Section 30I**: Prohibits complaints relating to acts or omissions occurring more than three years prior to the complaint ([4]).
- **Section 37D(1)**: Permits a pension fund to withhold payments due to members under specific circumstances, particularly when damages arise from misconduct ([5]).

The case also references judicial precedents like **Highveld Steel & Vanadium Corporation Ltd v Oosthuizen** [2008] ZASCA 164, which interpreted the provisions of section 37D and the objectives of the PFA ([5], [21]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal, affirming the decisions of both the Adjudicator and the court a quo, established that the Fund was not justified in withholding Mokadi’s benefits post-acquittal from the criminal charges. The Fund's assertion rested upon responsibility to adhere to the sections of the PFA authorizing withholding of benefits only until finalisation of civil proceedings ([25]).

Analyzing **Section 30N**, the court underscored the Adjudicator's discretion in directing interest on payments. The rationale behind this provision aims to compensate for late payments to ensure that beneficiaries do not suffer financially due to delays in accessing their funds ([14], [15]). It further emphasized that interest is separate from the fund return, clarifying that the former compensates for loss of use of funds ([26]).

The Fund’s arguments regarding non-liability for withholding payments were dismissed, as the court confirmed the Fund should have taken proactive measures to ascertain the status of the civil action ([23]). The Adjudicator's ruling to apply interest from 2 June 2010 was deemed appropriate since it was the date the civil action was effectively abandoned, and no reasonable justification existed for withholding payment thereafter ([24]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the Fund’s appeal, reinforcing the Adjudicator's authority to award interest under section 30N and the appropriate timing for its application ([28]). The case underscores the protective intent of the Pension Funds Act and the importance of fair treatment for members seeking their benefits post-employment. The court’s decision elucidates the necessity for pension funds to maintain active oversight of pending claims to avoid prolonged withholding of due benefits ([25], [28]). The ruling highlights not only the statutory framework’s emphasis on financial protection for pension members but also elucidates clear principles regarding interest entitlement in pension benefit determinations.
