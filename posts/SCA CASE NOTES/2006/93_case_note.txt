Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2006

### Case Note: Mittalsteel SA Ltd v Hlatshwayo [2006] SCA 94 (RSA)

#### FIRAC Method

---

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–2])

Mr. Mondli Hlatshwayo (the respondent), an industrious student, focused his Master's thesis on the factory regimes of state corporations during the 1960s and 1970s, specifically examining ISCOR (now known as Mittalsteel South Africa Limited), the largest steel producer in South Africa. Hlatshwayo sought documentary evidence for his research that was exclusively held by the appellant, Mittalsteel. Although certain records were disclosed, the appellant refused to provide others, claiming no obligation to do so.

This refusal led Hlatshwayo to apply to the Pretoria High Court, where he succeeded in obtaining an order under the Promotion of Access to Information Act 2 of 2000 (PAIA). The court mandated Mittalsteel to provide specific documents related to labour relations, wages, and health and safety for the period 1965-1973 ([paras 1–2]). The appellant sought an appeal against this order, specifically contesting its classification as a "public body" under PAIA, which determines obligations to disclose records ([para 3]).

---

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 3–6])

The crux of the appeal raised a singular issue: whether Mittalsteel, at the time of the creation of the requested documents, was a "public body" as defined under PAIA, thereby obligating it to grant access to the records requested by Hlatshwayo. This question becomes pivotal given the appellant's transition from a public entity to a private company and the implications of such a transition in terms of public access to information ([para 3]).

According to section 32(1) of the Constitution of 1996, everyone has the right of access to information held by the state or any individual required for the exercise and protection of any rights. The conversation around how PAIA's implementation is impacted by the changing status of an entity encapsulates the broader legal discourse on public vs. private bodies and their obligations regarding transparency ([para 4]).

---

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 6–10])

The legal framework governing the matter primarily hinges on PAIA and the constitutional right of access to information as affirmed in section 32. Specifically, a "public body" is defined in PAIA to include entities performing public functions under legislation, which are essential for determining the appellant's obligations despite its current private status ([para 7]).

Past case law played an influential role in delineating the characteristics of an "organ of state" and by extension, a "public body." The rulings in *Directory Advertising Cost Cutters v Minister for Posts, Telecommunications and Broadcasting* and various other cases examined the concept of government control alongside public function delivery, articulating that the pivotal test revolves around whether the state exercises control over the entity and whether it serves governmental functions ([paras 13–19]).

---

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 10–28])

The court embarked on a thorough analysis distinguishing between public and private bodies based on the functions and control exerted by the state. It was noted that Mittalsteel was created under legislative mandates that historically positioned it as a public body, executing functions that were critical to national interests ([para 23]).

While the appellant now operates as a private company, the context of the requested records being generated while it operated as a public entity (where it was substantially controlled by the government) maintained its classification as a public body under PAIA. The court underscored that public bodies must adhere to requests for access to information generated in the performance of their public functions ([para 28]).

The court also addressed the point of whether the requested documents still existed, allowing the appellant time to undertake a reasonable search for them. This indicates the court's intent to balance procedural fairness with the right to access information ([paras 29–30]).

---

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 28–30])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Mittalsteel had indeed been a public body at the time the records were produced, thus affirming Hlatshwayo's entitlement to access the requested documents. The appeal was dismissed, with costs awarded to the respondent, reinforcing the precedent that entities historically linked to public functions do not escape scrutiny and obligations under PAIA by virtue of changing their operational status to private entities ([para 30]).

This judgment reaffirms the constitutional commitment to transparency and the public's right to access information, illustrating that the shifting nature of an entity does not diminish historical obligations. It serves as a significant ruling in the realm of access to information and the operational legitimacy of public bodies post-privatization.

--- 

This case underscores the importance of judicial oversight in ensuring that access to information rights are upheld, illustrating a broader commitment to transparency in governance and accountability in the context of South Africa's legal landscape.
