Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Africa Charter Airline CC v AviSys Aviation Systems CC (421/2018) [2019] ZASCA 16

## F – Facts

The appellant, Africa Charter Airline CC, operates an aircraft charter business, while the respondent, AviSys Aviation Systems CC, specializes in servicing and overhauling aircraft components. In November 2011, Africa Charter requested a quotation from AviSys for the overhaul of major components of its Boeing 737's main landing gear. AviSys quoted R550,000, which Africa Charter accepted, leading to the transfer of the components to AviSys.

Upon completion of the overhaul in December 2011, AviSys returned the components to Africa Charter, asserting that it had fulfilled its obligations under the agreement. Africa Charter had already paid R460,000 to a subcontractor of AviSys for the work done. The key issue arose from AviSys's failure to remove the cadmium plating from the components during the overhaul. Africa Charter contended that the removal of this plating was a contractual obligation, constituting a material breach that justified the cancellation of the agreement and led to the lawsuit for the recovery of the R460,000 as well as consequential damages of US$648,000.

The litigation commenced in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, with Mayat J presiding over the initial proceedings. However, Mayat J passed away prior to delivering a judgment. Consequently, Matojane J took over, considering the pleadings and evidence presented, and ultimately ruled in favor of AviSys, dismissing Africa Charter's claims and allowing AviSys's counterclaim for R90,000.

As a result, Africa Charter was granted leave to appeal on the basis that the primary issue pertained to whether AviSys had a contractual obligation to remove the cadmium plating ([paras 1–4]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issue was whether AviSys was contractually obligated to remove the cadmium plating from the Boeing 737's main landing gear during its overhaul. This issue necessitated an interpretation of the relevant contractual provisions and regulatory standards outlined in the Maintenance Support Agreement ([paras 3-5]). Additional considerations involved the enforceability of any exclusions on consequential damages as stipulated in the agreement and the sufficiency of Africa Charter's claims for damages ([paras 25–30]).

## R – Rules

The case was governed by several key legal principles, notably the terms of the Maintenance Support Agreement, which included clauses mandating compliance with manufacturer specifications and relevant Civil Aviation Regulations. Specifically, Clause 2.1 required adherence to Component Owner/Operator specifications and approved Maintenance Technical Documentation ([para 5]).

The governing regulations, as per the Civil Aviation Regulations, stipulate that maintenance must conform to the manufacturer's maintenance manual, reflecting best practices and safety standards ([paras 6–7]). Relevant sections from the Boeing Component Maintenance Manual (CMM) and the Standard Overhaul Practices Manual (SOPM) were cited, particularly sections 32-00-05 and 32-11-11, which prescribed specific requirements and general practices for overhauling high-strength steel landing gear components ([paras 8–12]).

Moreover, a Boeing Service Letter stressed the necessity of removing cadmium plating to prevent potential corrosion and structural failure, emphasizing the safety implications of adhering to these maintenance practices ([paras 13–14]).

## A – Application

The court engaged in a thorough analysis of the contractual interpretation of the CMM sections to determine whether removing the cadmium plating constituted an obligatory step in the overhaul process. It identified that section 32-00-05 explicitly mandated the removal of protective finishes, including cadmium plating, as a part of the repair process, indicating this step was critical to properly inspecting for cracks or corrosion ([paras 19–23]).

The court dismissed the respondent's argument that only section 32-11-11 applied to the components, asserting that section 32-00-05 provided general repair procedures that must be followed first and were incorporated into section 32-11-11 where specific procedures necessitated referencing general practices. The failure to ensure proper removal of cadmium plating was determined to be a significant oversight that compromised the safety and operational integrity of the landing gear—a primary function of the maintenance and overhaul manuals ([paras 20–22]).

As such, the court concluded that AviSys had indeed failed to fulfill its contractual obligations, justifying Africa Charter's cancellation of the agreement and the request for recouping the R460,000 paid for the substandard services rendered ([paras 23–24]).

Regarding claim B, which sought consequential damages, the court noted that Africa Charter had failed to establish specific evidence that it had suffered financial losses as a result of AviSys's breach since it was able to utilize another aircraft for its operations ([paras 26–30]). Additionally, the existence of a clause limiting AviSys's liability for consequential damages raised further complexities in assessing the validity of Africa Charter’s claims under the agreements. However, since this aspect was not pursued in the appeal due to the dismissal of claim A, the court did not further engage with the merits of the damages claim ([paras 25–30]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that AviSys was contractually obliged to remove and reapply the cadmium plating during the overhaul, due to the clear requirements set out in the applicable maintenance manuals and safety letter. The court upheld Africa Charter’s appeal, set aside the lower court's judgment, and ruled in favor of Africa Charter for the refund of R460,000 along with interest and costs while dismissing the counterclaim ([paras 31]).

This case underscores the critical nature of adherence to regulatory standards within aviation maintenance, emphasizing contractual obligations rooted in safety. The ruling also highlights the importance of comprehensive and clear agreements that account for potential liability limitations as well as the need for diligence in fulfilling maintenance obligations to avoid breaches that could have severe operational consequences.
