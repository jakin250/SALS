Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Others v Ntuli (Judicial Inspectorate for Correctional Services intervening as amicus curiae) [2023] ZASCA 146

### Facts
[1] The respondent, Mbalenhle Sidney Ntuli, is a prisoner serving a 20-year sentence at the Johannesburg Medium C Correctional Centre. Since July 2018, he enrolled in a computer studies course at Oxbridge Academy, requiring the use of a personal computer. On 6 August 2018, Ntuli requested permission to use his computer in his cell. This request was denied by the head of the prison on 16 August 2018, citing the prison’s educational policy, which prohibited personal computers in cells. 

[2] After being similarly rejected by the Regional Commissioner, Ntuli approached the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, contesting the policy. The High Court, presided by Matsemela AJ, found that the policy limited Ntuli’s constitutional right to further education under section 29(1)(b) of the Constitution and constituted unfair discrimination as per the Equality Act (Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000).

[3] The appellants, comprising the Minister of Justice, the National Commissioner of Correctional Services, and the Head of Leeuwkop Medium "C" Correctional Centre, sought leave to appeal against the High Court's decision, which had refused their application. The Supreme Court of Appeal granted leave and addressed issues regarding late applications for condonation and reinstatement of the appeal, which had lapsed.

### Issues
[4] The primary issues for determination include:
- Whether the appellants should be granted condonation for the late filing of the appeal record.
- Whether the educational policy that prohibits the use of computers in cells constitutes an infringement of the right to further education as laid out in section 29(1)(b) of the Constitution.
- Whether the policy engages with issues of unfair discrimination under the Equality Act.

### Rules
[5] The court relied on several constitutional provisions and legal principles:
- **Constitution of South Africa, s 29(1)(b)** states that everyone has the right to further education, which the state must make progressively available and accessible.
- **Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act** provides grounds for challenging unfair discrimination.
  
[6] Relevant case law included:
- The principles derived from *Whittaker & Morant v Roos & Batemen* and *Goldberg & others v Minister of Prisons*, which affirm that prisoners retain rights consistent with human dignity despite incarceration.
- *Minister of Home Affairs v NICRO* emphasized that limitation of rights must be justifiable with demonstrable justification.

### Application
[7] The court first examined whether to condone the late filing of the appeal record. It ruled that the appellants' inexperience did not justify the significant delay in compliance with procedural rules but acknowledged the importance of the case, warranting the reinstatement of the appeal ([5]–[6]).

[8] Turning to the merits of the policy, the court noted that the blanket prohibition on the use of personal computers in cells was an unjustified infringement of the right to further education. It recognized that access to a computer was essential for completing a course focused on data processing, thereby limiting prisoners' potential for self-development and rehabilitation. The court emphasized that prisoners should not be denied educational opportunities due to blanket policies lacking sufficient justification ([22]–[23]).

[9] Furthermore, the court evaluated the appellants' justifications for the policy, including security concerns related to smuggling technology into prisons. It found these justifications unconvincing, as they failed to provide a clear and objective basis for the blanket prohibition. The court concluded that the policy inadequately justified its limitations on prisoners' rights under section 36 of the Constitution ([29]–[32]).

[10] Therefore, while recognizing the need for prison security, the court held that this concern could not overshadow the constitutional right to education that prisoners possess. The dismissal of Mr. Ntuli's use of his computer for studies was deemed an unconstitutional restriction ([35]–[36]).

### Conclusion
[11] The Supreme Court of Appeal partially upheld the appeal. It set aside the high court's declaration of unfair discrimination under the Equality Act due to procedural issues regarding the jurisdiction of the high court. However, it also invalidated the blanket prohibition against personal computers in cells, recognizing it as an infringement of the constitutional right to further education ([33]).

[12] The court ordered that the provision prohibiting the use of personal computers be invalidated, suspended the order for 12 months to allow appellants to revise the policy adequately, and established interim rights for prisoners to use personal computers under specific conditions ([37]). The appellants were directed to bear the costs associated with both the initial applications and the appeal, reflecting the court's displeasure with their administrative handling of the case. 

This ruling has significant implications for the rights of prisoners to access education and the administration of correctional facilities in South Africa, highlighting the delicate balance between security and the fundamental right to education.
