Supreme Court of Appeal

YM v LB (465/09) [2010] ZASCA 106

After B’s return to alcohol-induced altercations and contrary to expectations, M became pregnant in March 2007, convinced B was the father.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 106

FIRAC Method

F – Facts:
In this case, the appellant, Y D (now M), contested an order made by the North Gauteng High Court compelling her and her daughter, Y, to undergo DNA testing to establish whether the respondent, L B, was Y's biological father ([1]). The parties had initiated a romantic relationship in February 2006, cohabitating by October of that year, leading to an engagement in November ([3]). Following B's alleged work-related absences, M moved to Musina in March 2007.

After B’s return to alcohol-induced altercations and contrary to expectations, M became pregnant in March 2007, convinced B was the father. Their relationship deteriorated; despite B initially acknowledging paternity, he later contested it post-birth in October 2007, eventually applying for testing ([5]–[10]). The High Court determined that, despite M's assertions, the paternity dispute warranted a scientific resolution.

I – Issues:
The core issue was whether the High Court erred in compelling M and Y to submit to paternity testing, despite paternity already being established on the balance of probabilities. This raised constitutional implications relating to the right to privacy and bodily integrity, as well as considerations regarding the best interests of the child under the Children’s Act ([12]–[16]).

R – Rules:
The judgment referenced several legal principles surrounding paternity disputes and the admissibility of scientific tests, particularly under Section 37 of the Children’s Act 38 of 2005, which allows for testing where paternity is contested. Additionally, the court discussed the balancing act inherent in weighing the rights to privacy and bodily integrity against the pursuit of truth in judicial matters ([13]–[16]). Citing the Plascon-Evans principle, the court acknowledged that unresolved disputes typically lean on the assertions of the party whose allegations could only be dismissed if utterly untenable ([11]).

A – Application:
The Court underscored that B’s actions did not substantiate an actual dispute over paternity, as he had previously expressed certainty regarding his fatherhood, evidenced by financial support during M's pregnancy ([6]). The High Court's determination, influenced by an inclination towards testing to ascertain "truth," was ultimately erroneous because the factual premise of a paternity dispute was absent ([13]). Moreover, while recognizing the potential validity of compelling tests in other scenarios where paternity is genuinely contested, the Court emphasized that such a decision should not have generalized implications without considering the particularities of the child's situation and the specific case context ([15]).

C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the High Court's decision, concluding that the order demanding M and Y to undergo DNA testing was inappropriate. The Court held that paternity had been sufficiently established on a balance of probabilities and that compelling testing under these circumstances violated the rights to privacy and bodily integrity without adequate justification ([17]). The ruling establishes a significant precedent regarding the courts' discretion in ordering scientific tests in paternity cases and reinforces the need to evaluate each circumstance individually, keeping the welfare of the child paramount. The appeal was upheld with an award of costs against the respondent ([1], [17]).
