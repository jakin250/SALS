Brits v Kommandantsdrift CC and Others (143/2021) [2022] ZASCA 41

The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal centers on a dispute involving ownership of a piece of land, specifically a portion known as "the wedge," which was claimed by both the appellant, Johannes Brits, and the first respondent, Kommandantsdrift CC.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 41

F – Facts ([paras 1–9])

The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal centers on a dispute involving ownership of a piece of land, specifically a portion known as "the wedge," which was claimed by both the appellant, Johannes Brits, and the first respondent, Kommandantsdrift CC. The origins of the dispute stem from two contracts of sale concerning the property known as Oude Zandrift 446. The original farm, Onder Zandrift no: 119, was registered to the grandparents of the Le Roux brothers and underwent several subdivisions and transfers over the years.

In 1995, the original farm was transferred to Kommandantsdrift CC, with Meyer Jnr as its sole member. The CC acquired and sold farm 446 in 2000 to Nico Le Roux, and subsequently, in 2008, Nico sold the same property to Brits. It was common knowledge among the parties at the time of both sales that the wedge was not included in the sale of farm 446 and was farmed separately. However, this understanding came into question when Brits sought occupation of the wedge in 2013, leading to litigation initiated by the CC and Nico in 2015 claiming that both sale contracts were void due to a "common error." The High Court ruled in favor of the CC, declaring the contracts void, a decision that prompted Brits to appeal ([paras 1–6]).

I – Issues ([paras 10–17])

The primary legal issues at stake include:

  1. Whether the sale contracts (the 2000 sale from the CC to Nico and the 2008 sale from Nico to Brits) were void ab initio due to a common error concerning a material term (the status of the wedge) ([para 10.1]).
  2. Whether the appellant's plea of prescription was valid, arguing that the CC's claim should be dismissed because it had prescribed ([para 10.2]).

These issues led to the question of whether the original contracting parties had a mutual misunderstanding about the nature of what was sold—the wedge, which impacted the validity of the contracts. The ramifications of the High Court's judgment on ownership and the procedural issue of remittance of matters not yet determined were also critical ([para 10.3]).

R – Rules ([paras 18–23])

The legal foundations for the Court's decision rested on principles of contract law, specifically the doctrine of common mistake and the Prescription Act 68 of 1969. The doctrine of common mistake, as delineated in Dickinson Motors (Pty) Ltd v Oberholzer 1952 (1) SA 443 (A), establishes that a common error concerning a material term of a contract renders the agreement void, as both parties must have been ad idem for a contract to be solemnly enforced ([para 20]).

On the issue of prescription, established principles dictate that the onus rests on the party raising the plea to demonstrate the date when the creditor, in this case, the CC, became aware of the facts underlying their claim, as highlighted in Gericke v Sack [1978] 2 All SA 111 (A) and Lancelot Stellenbosch Mountain Retreat (Pty) Ltd v Gore N O [2015] ZASCA 37 ([para 17]).

A – Application ([paras 19–29])

The Court meticulously analyzed the facts to validate the High Court's earlier ruling regarding the contracts' void nature due to a common error. It concurred with the lower court’s findings that all parties involved were under the mistaken belief that the wedge was not included in the sales, making the error fundamental to the contractual agreement ([paras 20–23]).

On the issue of prescription, the Court determined that Brits failed to discharge his burden of proof by not establishing an inception date for the CC's knowledge of its claim. The Court emphasized that the evidence presented at trial indicated that neither Nico nor Meyer Jnr became aware of any claim related to the wedge until the summons was issued in 2013, thus rejecting Brits' assertions of prescription ([para 24]). The ruling suggested that the parties operated under a consistent understanding of the property's boundaries, reinforcing the determination that both contracts were indeed void ([paras 25–26]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 27–28])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court's finding that the contracts of sale were void ab initio due to a common error relating to a material term. As a result, both sales inherently lacked legal validity. However, the Court remitted the matter to the High Court for further adjudication regarding unresolved issues, particularly concerning the ownership records in the Deeds Office, as the question of rectification of the records had not been fully addressed ([paras 27–28]).

This judgment underscores the importance of clarity in contractual agreements, particularly concerning land transactions, as well as the necessity of thorough proof in matters concerning prescription. The implications of this case extend beyond its immediate parties, contributing to jurisprudence on contractual errors and the complexities of land ownership in South Africa.
