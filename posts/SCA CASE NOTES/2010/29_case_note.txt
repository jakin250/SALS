# Case Note: Majikazana v The State (559/09) [2010] ZASCA 29

## FIRAC Template

### F – Facts

The appellant, Mbulelo Majikazana, was convicted by the Eastern Cape High Court (Grahamstown), presided over by Froneman J, of murder (count 2), unlawful possession of a firearm (count 3), and unlawful possession of ammunition (count 4) ([1]). He was sentenced to 25 years' imprisonment on the murder conviction and two years and one year's imprisonment for the other counts, respectively, which were to run concurrently ([1]).

The incident occurred on 9 October 1999 and involved the shooting of Mandla Mda, at the Fort Beaufort Hospital. Initially, the appellant denied entering the hospital and denied killing Mda. However, during the trial, he admitted to both actions, claiming it was in self-defense ([7]). The firearm used in the shooting belonged to another accused, Mr Jacobs. The appellant argued that he had taken possession of the firearm to safeguard it as it had dropped in his vehicle during a volatile incident involving rival factions within a local taxi association ([14]).

Upon exhausting initial avenues for appeal, new counsel discovered that Judge Froneman had presided over a prior bail application for the appellant, which had been dismissed roughly seven months prior to the criminal trial. This information was not disclosed at the beginning of the trial, leading to concerns about potential bias ([4]). An application for condonation for the late filing of an appeal was granted, along with a special entry regarding the alleged irregularity ([5]).

The trial court concluded that there were no indications of bias, ruling that the relevant legal framework did not necessitate recusal in this instance ([8],[13]).

### I – Issues

The case raises several legal issues:

1. Whether the presiding judge’s previous involvement in the bail application created reasonable apprehension of bias that undermined the fairness of the trial ([6],[11]).
2. The legal interpretation of mens rea, particularly regarding the unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition under the relevant statutes ([22]).
3. The appropriateness of the sentencing imposed by the trial court in relation to statutory minimum sentences for murder and unlawful possession of a firearm ([24],[27]).

### R – Rules

The legal authorities relevant to this case include:

- Section 322(1)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act, which outlines grounds for appeal based on wrongful decisions regarding questions of law and failure of justice ([10],[12]).
- The requirement of mens rea as a crucial element for establishing guilt in unlawful possession provisions as stipulated in the Arms and Ammunition Act 75 of 1969, which asserts that knowledge of unlawfulness is essential ([22]).
- The Criminal Law Amendment Act, specifically section 51(2)(a)(i), which provides a statutory framework for minimum sentencing guidelines for murder, emphasizing that first offenders convicted of unpremeditated murder should receive a minimum sentence of 15 years ([26]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal conducted a thorough examination of the evidence and the procedural history. In relation to bias, the court agreed with counsel for the State that the absence of recorded bias during the trial indicated no true apprehension of bias existed ([6],[10],[13]). Despite the failure to seek the judge’s recusal, the court held that actual bias would have to be demonstrated for the appeal to succeed. It concluded that no indications of bias were present in the record ([13]).

Regarding the unlawful possession of the firearm, the court found that the appellant's possession could not be characterized as unlawful at the initial point of taking it into his control; he believed he was safeguarding it ([22],[23]). However, after he had used the firearm to shoot the deceased, the court determined that he must have known he was in unlawful possession given the nature of his actions at that point ([23]).

As for the sentencing issue, the appellate court highlighted the trial court’s failure to properly apply the statutory minimum sentence provisions. The original sentence of 25 years was deemed excessive considering that the appellant was a first offender and that the murder was unplanned. Thus, the sentence was reduced to 15 years' imprisonment ([24],[26],[29]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the conviction for murder and unlawful possession of a firearm while reversing the conviction for unlawful possession of ammunition, leading to the conclusion that the appellant had not illegally possessed the ammunition because he possessed a firearm capable of discharging it ([19],[29]). 

The court’s judgment affirmed the conviction on count 2 while reducing the sentence to 15 years to align with statutory requirements, given the absence of premeditation and the appellant's status as a first offender ([2],[29]). This case illustrates significant principles regarding fairness in trials, the standards for recusal in judicial proceedings, and the interpretation of statutory provisions concerning mens rea and sentencing in the South African legal context.
