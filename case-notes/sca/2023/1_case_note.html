Democratic Alliance v Rulumeni (88/2021)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 Democratic Alliance v Rulumeni (88/2021) [2023] ZASCA 1 (13 January 2023) Delivered 13 January 2023

Neutral Citation: Democratic Alliance v Rulumeni (88/2021) [2023] ZASCA 1 (13 January 2023)
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Coram: Makgoka, Nicholls and Hughes JJA, Goosen and Salie AJJA
Heard: 1 November 2022
Delivered: 13 January 2023

F - Facts

The case revolves around a delict claim by the respondent, Ms. Ntombenhle Rulumeni, against the appellant, the Democratic Alliance (DA). Ms. Rulumeni alleged that her dignity was intentionally and wrongfully harmed during an interview conducted in a female ablution block on 6 February 2016, for which she sought R10 million in damages ( [1]–[2] ).

Background:

  • Ms. Rulumeni, a member of the DA and a former municipal councillor, was not initially advanced to the selection panel after being "red-flagged" by the DA's Electoral College due to probity issues ([3]–[5]).
  • After an appeal, she was allowed to interview with the selection panel, which was held in a room adjacent to the ladies' toilets (often referred to by her as a "toilet") ([6]–[10]).

Events Leading to the Claim:

  • During the interview, Ms. Rulumeni expressed feelings of shock and humiliation, describing her experience as a serious infringement of her dignity ([13]–[15]).
  • The DA contended that the venue was chosen out of necessity due to scheduling conflicts, and Mr. Mileham, the selection panel chairperson, assured her that the room was appropriately appointed, not merely a toilet ([16]–[18]).

The Eastern Cape High Court found the DA liable for infringing Ms. Rulumeni's dignity and ordered it to pay damages, while dismissing her claim for economic loss ([2]).

I - Issues

The core issues addressed in this case included:

  1. Whether the choice of venue for the interview constituted a wrongful and intentional infringement of Ms. Rulumeni’s dignity ([21]).
  2. Whether the High Court rightly established that the DA's conduct in choosing the venue intended to harm her dignity ([27]).

R - Rules

The legal framework for this case is primarily grounded in the actio iniuriarum, where three essential elements must be proven for a successful claim:

  1. An intentional and wrongful act.
  2. The act must infringe upon the plaintiff's dignity.
  3. The conduct must meet the test of wrongfulness under societal norms ([20]).

The Court referred to several precedents, notably Delange v Costa, which emphasized that a claim for dignity infringement must focus on the character of the conduct and its perception by the community ([21]–[24]).

The objective test for wrongfulness requires that the conduct be evaluated against societal values, and any intention to harm must be shown objectively ([22]–[24]).

A - Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) undertook a detailed assessment of whether the High Court had correctly identified the conduct as wrongful.

  1. Wrongfulness: The SCA clarified that the High Court’s classification of the interview venue as a "toilet" was misleading. It emphasized the attributes of the room itself, which was carpeted and properly furnished, and noted that it did not adhere to the ordinary notion of a toilet ([25]–[26]). Therefore, the choice of venue did not objectively contravene societal norms enough to be deemed wrongful.
  2. Intention to Harm: The SCA found no evidence that the DA intended to harm Ms. Rulumeni's dignity. The prior red-flagging and events leading up to the interview were not sufficient to establish any intention to harm at the time of the interview ([27]–[28]). The available evidence supported the DA's position that there was no intended insult or humiliation linked to the choice of venue for the interview.

The Court found that the High Court had erred by not adequately addressing the wrongfulness of the conduct and by prematurely concluding that the DA's actions were wrongful based merely on the venue's nature ([24]–[26]).

C - Conclusion

The SCA upheld the appeal, concluding that Ms. Rulumeni had not established the necessary elements for her actio iniuriarum claim. The court overturned the High Court's finding that the DA was liable for infringing her dignity. As a result:

  1. Ms. Rulumeni's claim was dismissed, and the action was closed with an order that she pay the costs, including those of two counsel ([30]).

This judgment underscores the rigorous examination required to establish a claim of dignity infringement, particularly focusing on socially evolving norms and an accurate portrayal of the intent behind the alleged wrongful act. The implications of this judgment reflect a nuanced understanding of dignity in the context of societal values and the complexities surrounding the assessment of intent in cases of alleged personal injury.
