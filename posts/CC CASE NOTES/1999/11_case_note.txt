Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1999

# FIRAC Case Note: President of the Republic of South Africa v South African Rugby Football Union and Others

**A. Overview and Summary**

**F – Facts**:  
This case arises from the constitutional legitimacy of two presidential notices issued on 26 September 1997 regarding the establishment of a commission of inquiry headed by Mr. Acting Justice Browde into the administration of rugby in South Africa. The South African Rugby Football Union (SARFU), its affiliate unions, and Dr. Louis Luyt sought to have these notices set aside in the Transvaal High Court. The High Court found in favor of the respondents, leading to an appeal by the President and his co-appellants. The case discusses the dynamics of administrative actions, the powers of the President, and issues relating to procedural fairness ([1]–[24]).

**I – Issues**:  
The central legal issue pertains to the constitutional validity of the presidential notices. Specifically, it focuses on whether the President legally abdicated his responsibilities to the Minister of Sport and Recreation, whether a fair hearing was owed to the respondents before the decision was made, and if the considerations taken into account by the President were relevant to the exercise of his powers ([37]–[152]).

**R – Rules**:  
The court relied on various constitutional provisions, including Section 84(2)(f) of the South African Constitution, which outlines the President's power to appoint commissions of inquiry. The case also delves into Section 33, which posits the right to just administrative action, emphasizing the need for procedural fairness ([129]–[224]).

**A – Application**:  
The court critically assessed the High Court's findings, particularly the "abdication fallacy" posited by the High Court that the President had irrevocably delegated decision-making authority to the Minister. The Constitutional Court rejected this view, elucidating that the President retained the power to reconsider his decisions and that the process of the inquiry's appointment did not infringe upon procedural fairness due to the nature of presidential discretion ([42]–[155]).

**C – Conclusion**:  
The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal, overturning the High Court's order. It clarified that the President had not abdicated his responsibility and had acted within his constitutional powers. Further, it emphasized that the conditions for the applicability of the Commissions Act were satisfied. The court ruled unnecessary costs imposed on the appellants in previous interlocutory matters should be reversed. SARFU's claims of legitimate expectations were unfounded as they failed to adequately engage with the process or assert their rights before the commission's establishment ([260]).
