Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: The City of Johannesburg v The South African Local Authorities Pension Fund (20045/2014) [2015] ZASCA 4

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History

[1] The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) involves an appeal by the City of Johannesburg and several entities under its purview (the "Appellants") against the South African Local Authorities Pension Fund (the "First Respondent") and the trade unions representing some of the members of the pension fund, along with individual contributors to the fund (the "Respondents"). Following a decision by the appellants to terminate their contributions to the pension fund in favor of a new pension scheme (the eJoburg Retirement Fund), legal proceedings commenced when the respondents sought a declaration that this decision was unlawful.

[2] The appellants had ceased contributing to the First Respondent’s pension fund effective from January 1, 2005, resulting in two key applications: the first sought to restore prior contributions pending litigation outcomes, while the second sought to declare the cessation illegal. The case was delayed, driven by procedural merits and filings, and culminated in a judgment in October 2013 where the court a quo favored the respondents on various grounds but dismissed the appellants’ preliminary objection regarding non-joinder.

[3] The core of the respondents' challenge was premised on the assertion that the appellants had violated the rules of statutory, contractual, administrative, and labor law obligations by unilaterally withdrawing from the pension scheme, thereby rendering their action a nullity. The appellants argued that not all affected employees, particularly those whose membership had been terminated, were joined in the proceedings, which constituted a non-joinder issue critical to the litigation.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

[4] The central legal issue arose from the non-joinder of terminating pension fund members in the litigation regarding the appellants' decision to cease contributions. The Supreme Court had to determine whether the lower court's dismissal of the non-joinder objection was appropriate, considering whether the terminated employees had a direct and substantial interest in the outcome of the application. 

[5] Additionally, the court needed to address the implications of the purported breach of statutory, contractual, and common law obligations by the appellants and whether the respondents were entitled to bring the application on behalf of pension fund members without their direct involvement.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon

[6] The ruling involved principles delineated in earlier case law concerning non-joinder alongside statutory provisions from the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956. Established judgments highlighted that courts would refrain from adjudicating disputes impacting unjoined parties who have direct and substantial interests in the subject matter ([5], [9]). Key cited cases included:

- **Amalgamated Engineering Union v Minister of Labour** 1949 (3) SA 637 (A) ([9])
- **Gordon v Department of Health, KwaZulu-Natal** 2008 (6) SA 522 (SCA) ([9])
- **Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town & others** 2004 (6) SA 222 (SCA) ([8])
  
[7] The material provisions of Section 7C(2) of the Pension Funds Act delineate duties that fund trustees owe to their members, asserting a duty to act with due diligence and in their interests, thereby necessitating members' involvement in litigation that could affect their rights and entitlements under the fund ([11]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

[8] The SCA critically examined the principle that the interests of affected parties must be considered in determining the appropriateness of a party being absent from litigation ([9], [10]). It became evident that some terminating members could potentially suffer significant adverse effects from the relief sought by the respondents, thereby establishing a direct and substantial interest in the proceedings. 

[9] Addressing the lower court's rationale deriving from Section 7C(2) of the Pension Funds Act, the SCA rejected the notion that the board could act in lieu of individual members without their authorization or involvement, arguing instead for the necessity of their direct participation, especially where interests are not uniform across all members ([12], [13]). 

[10] The court reinforced that any order issued could impose liability for arrears contributions on both employers and employees, complicating rights in relation to both the prior and current pension schemes, thereby justifying the non-joinder objection ([9], [14]). Furthermore, distinctions between defined benefit and defined contribution regimes indicated that unintended consequences of the ruling could propagate inequalities among members, bolstering the argument for their necessary inclusion in the litigation.

[11] As a final note, the court addressed the respondents' assertion that some terminating members were represented by trade unions, ruling it insufficient as no explicit evidence demonstrated that all terminating members were either members of or substantially represented by these unions ([15]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

[12] The SCA concluded that the non-joinder of terminating members was critical and meritorious, upholding the appellants' objection. The court set aside the lower court's order due to the procedural irregularity and remanded the case for joinder of affected parties to ensure the integrity of the legal proceedings and the protection of all relevant interests.

[13] The implications of this ruling are significant. It not only reaffirms the necessity of including all parties with pertinent interests in litigation but also reinforces the fiduciary duty that pension fund boards have toward their members. By emphasizing individual rights in collective systems, this decision contributes substantially to the jurisprudence surrounding pension fund governance and liability in South Africa.

[14] Ultimately, this ruling signifies a pivotal outcome in pension law in South Africa, ensuring that member interests are protected and that disputes involving obligations to pension funds cannot proceed without an inclusive approach to all affected entities, thereby enhancing procedural justice and the efficacy of legal protections in financial governance.
