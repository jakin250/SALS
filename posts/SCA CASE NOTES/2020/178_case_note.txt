## Case Note: C B and Another v H B [2020] ZASCA 178

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–7])

In this appeal, the appellants, C B (the first appellant) and Sarel Louis Augustyn (the second appellant), contested a decision from the Gauteng Division of the High Court regarding the interpretation of a settlement agreement made in the course of the divorce between C B and the respondent, H B. The couple married in February 1987 and separated in June 2016, with divorce proceedings initiated by H B in 2017. They reached a settlement agreement stipulating that C B would pay H B an all-inclusive monthly maintenance until her death or remarriage ([para 2]).

The critical issue arose when H B cohabited with R V and participated in a church ceremony on 9 December 2017, which she publicised as a marriage. After discovering this ceremony, C B ceased maintenance payments, arguing that the ceremony constituted H B's remarriage, thereby terminating his obligation to maintain her ([paras 3–4]). H B then sought enforcement of the maintenance order and alleged contempt of court against C B for his failure to pay ([para 5]). The high court ruled in H B's favor, amending the settlement agreement for future clarity and holding C B in contempt of court, leading to this appeal.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1–7, 12, 14])

The primary legal issue in this case was the interpretation of the term "remarriage" in the settlement agreement, specifically whether the church ceremony between H B and R V constituted a legally binding marriage under South African law ([para 1]). Further questions included whether C B was in contempt of court due to his ceased maintenance payments, and the appropriateness of the costs awards against the second appellant ([paras 6–8]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 12–21])

The Supreme Court of Appeal referred to several statutes and precedents to inform its determination. Primarily, the court considered the **Marriage Act 25 of 1961**, which mandates specific solemnization and registration procedures for marriages ([paras 12–13]). Key sections delineated conditions under which a marriage can be recognized as legally binding, including the requirement for a marriage officer and completion of a marriage register ([para 17]).

Judicial interpretation principles cited from **Bothma-Batho Transport (Edms) Bpk v S Bothma & Seun Transport (Edms) Bpk** and **Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality** emphasized that contract interpretation hinges on the parties’ intention and the context surrounding the agreement ([para 13]). 

Furthermore, in considering contempt of court, the court reflected on precedential authority regarding costs orders in cases involving legal representation and procedural fairness ([para 19]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 14–34])

The court concluded that the term "remarriage" in the context of the settlement agreement strictly referred to a legally recognized marriage under South African law ([para 14]). This interpretation was bolstered by the context of the clause, which was embedded within a legal framework governing the dissolution of their actual marriage ([para 14]).

The court analyzed the requirements of the **Marriage Act** and established that the ceremony held on 9 December 2017 failed to meet legal standards—a marriage officer did not solemnize it according to the Act, nor did the necessary registrations occur ([para 17]). Thus, the court rejected the notion that the ceremony constituted a valid remarriage and reaffirmed C B's duty to continue maintenance payments.

Moreover, in addressing the contempt ruling, the majority opinion noted that C B acted on legal advice, which contributed to the conclusion that he lacked the requisite mala fides typically necessary to prove contempt ([para 11]). 

In respect to costs, the court recognized that procedural fairness had not been observed regarding the second appellant, who had not been a party to the original proceedings, thus vitiating any costs order against him ([para 20]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 23–36])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, overturning the high court's rulings concerning both the characterization of the church ceremony as a remarriage and the contempt finding against C B. The court amended the settlement agreement to clarify that maintenance obligations would terminate only upon the onset of a legally recognized remarriage or cohabitation, consistent with the precise terms outlined in the original agreement ([para 23]).

This ruling clarifies that while religious ceremonies may have significant personal meaning, they do not substitute for the legal characteristics required for marriage under South African law. The judgment underscores the principle that parties in legally binding agreements must adhere to the specific legal standards governing contractual definitions and obligations. It also highlights the criticality of applying established principles of procedural fairness in awarding costs in legal disputes, particularly when determining the professional conduct of legal representatives.

In dissent, Judge Makgoka argued that the context warranted a broader interpretation of “marriage,” suggesting that the relationship between H B and R V, despite lacking formal registration, constituted a remarriage for the purposes of the maintenance clause, revealing a tension between traditional interpretations of marriage and evolving societal views ([paras 24–34]).

This case elucidates ongoing discussions within South African family law regarding the definition and recognition of marriage and could have significant implications for future interpretation of similar clauses in settlement agreements, particularly in the context of cohabitation and informal unions.
