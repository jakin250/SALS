Supreme Court of Appeal

The Standard Bank of SA Ltd and Others v Thobejane and Others (38/2019 & 47/2019) and The Standard Bank of SA Ltd v Gqirana N O and Another (999/2019) [2021] ZASCA 92 (25 June 2021)

The decisions from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, and the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, Grahamstown, varied significantly, prompting the appeals ([paras 1-4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 92 (25 June 2021)

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) addressed two interconnected appeals concerning jurisdictional issues arising from civil claims by banks against debtors for repayment of debts related to mortgages and motor vehicle purchases ([paras 1-2]). These cases originated from applications where several banks sought orders for repayment of outstanding debts and special execution on mortgaged properties, with no participation from the debtors in the hearings ([para 2]).

The decisions from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, and the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, Grahamstown, varied significantly, prompting the appeals ([paras 1-4]). The Gauteng Court expressed concerns about the congestion of its roll owing to cases that could be handled in the Magistrates’ Courts, and the prejudice faced by indigent debtors who would have to travel to the High Court only to contest claims that might more suitably be adjudicated elsewhere ([paras 2, 3]).

In both cases, the Judges-President proposed questions regarding the obligation of the High Court to entertain matters within the jurisdiction of Magistrates’ Courts and whether financial institutions must consider the accessibility of courts for impoverished defendants when choosing where to sue ([paras 4-5]). Each court responded differently, leading to conflicting legal principles regarding the role and discretion of the High Courts vis-à-vis concurrent jurisdiction ([paras 6-10]).

I – Issues

The main legal questions before the SCA were:

  1. Whether the High Court can refuse to hear matters where both it and a Magistrates’ Court have concurrent jurisdiction, particularly regarding financial claims typically falling under the latter's jurisdiction ([paras 1, 10]).
  2. Whether the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA) ousts the jurisdiction of the High Court over claims pertaining to the financial matters dictated by the NCA ([paras 8, 9, 64-68]).

R – Rules

The governing legal framework consists of several provisions from the South African Constitution, the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, and the Magistrates’ Court Act 32 of 1944:

  • Constitution (s 34): Guarantees the right to access courts to resolve disputes ([para 49]).
  • Superior Courts Act (s 21): Affirms the jurisdiction of the High Court over all persons within its area of jurisdiction and causes arising therein ([paras 20-21]).
  • Magistrates’ Court Act (s 29): Specifies the jurisdictional limits of Magistrates’ Courts, including their power to hear financial claims comparable to those sought in the High Court ([paras 22-23]).
  • National Credit Act (s 3): Expresses the purpose of promoting accessible credit and establishing fair treatment, but does not explicitly oust the High Court's jurisdiction ([paras 72-83]).

These acts emphasize the concurrent jurisdiction of the High Court and Magistrates’ Courts in civil matters, illustrating the principle that no court should decline to hear a case simply due to the existence of another court with jurisdiction ([paras 11, 12, 25]).

A – Application

The SCA unfurled substantial legal reasoning in addressing the factual and legal complexities.

  1. Concurrent Jurisdiction: The Court articulated that the High Court possesses an obligation to hear cases that fall within its jurisdiction, regardless of the existence of a concurrent jurisdiction at the Magistrates’ level. This ruling contradicted the Gauteng Court's approach, which suggested the High Court could refuse to hear matters based on perceived access to justice concerns for indigent litigants ([para 38]).
  2. Abuse of Process: The notion that the banks' choice to litigate in the High Court amounted to an abuse of process was rejected. The Court asserted that opting for the High Court is a right grounded in law, and no evidence substantiated the claim that litigating in the High Court inherently caused a systemic failure of access to justice for debtors ([paras 36-38, 49]).
  3. National Credit Act: The Eastern Cape Court's interpretation that the NCA ousted the High Court’s jurisdiction was found to be inconsistent with sound legal reasoning. The SCA clarified that no legislation expressly prevented concurrent jurisdiction and that existing statutory frameworks support the High Court's role in matters falling within the Magistrates’ Courts' sphere of competence ([paras 63-68]).
  4. Rights of Debtors: The Court emphasized that the rights of financially distressed individuals to access justice are not inherently compromised merely because the choice of litigation venue is not the nearest possible court ([para 61]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA ruled in favor of the appellants, reiterating that the High Court is obligated to hear all matters within its jurisdiction, including those overlapping with the Magistrates’ Court, thus reaffirming judicial capacity and the constitutional right of access to justice. The decisions rendered by both the Gauteng and Eastern Cape courts were set aside.

Consequently, the Court held that:

  1. The High Court must hear cases within its jurisdiction that fall under the ambit of the Magistrates’ Courts without discretionary refusal or transfer to lower courts ([para 88]).
  2. There is no inherent obligation on financial institutions to factor in the cost implications for litigants when selecting the court for initiating proceedings ([para 88]).

These rulings imply broader implications for civil litigation in South Africa, particularly regarding the balance of judicial resources, access to courts for indigent defendants, and the principles of fair justice administration in cases involving financial claims against vulnerable individuals ([para 60]).
