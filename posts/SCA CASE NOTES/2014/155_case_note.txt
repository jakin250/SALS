Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: The State v Sehoole (730/13) [2014] ZASCA 155

## F – Facts
In this case, **The State v Sehoole**, the appellant, the State, appealed against the decision of the South Gauteng High Court which had set aside the convictions of the respondent, Bongani Sehoole. The respondent was initially convicted in the Kempton Park Regional Court on 14 November 2011 for the unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition, as provided in sections 3 and 90 of the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000 (the Act). He received a sentence of 10 years' and 5 years’ imprisonment for these charges, running concurrently ([paras 1–2]).

During the incident on 25 January 2011, police officers Sergeant Kladie and Constable Filtane stopped the vehicle driven by Sehoole due to suspicious behavior. Upon searching, Kladie discovered a 9mm Beretta pistol, with its serial number filed off, tucked in Sehoole’s trousers along with a magazine containing 15 rounds of ammunition. After his arrest, Sehoole contested the legality of the charges, leading to his successful appeal in the High Court, which argued that he should have been charged under section 4 of the Act concerning firearms without serial numbers rather than section 3 ([paras 3–6]).

The High Court’s ruling effectively acquitted Sehoole, a decision that the State contested on points of law ([paras 1, 2]).

## I – Issues
The primary issues raised in this case pertain to:
1. Whether the High Court correctly determined that a person found in unlawful possession of a firearm with a filed-off serial number could only be charged under section 4(1)(f)(iv) of the Firearms Control Act, thereby not allowing for a charge under section 3 of the Act ([para 2]).
2. Whether the High Court erred in requiring a ballistic report to confirm that found ammunition was indeed ammunition and thus relevant to the conviction under section 90 of the Act ([paras 16–20]).

## R – Rules
The applicable legal frameworks consist of:
- **Section 3 of the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000**: This section creates a general prohibition against the possession of a firearm without the proper licensing ([para 7]).
- **Section 4(1)(f)(iv) of the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000**: This section specifically prohibits possession of firearms where altered or removed identifying marks or serial numbers are present ([paras 7–8]).
- **Section 90 of the Act**: This section addresses the prohibition on possessing ammunition unless appropriate licensing is held ([para 18]).

Additionally, the principle of prosecutorial discretion as dominus litis grants the State authority on how to proceed with charges. Judicial intervention should only occur under exceptional circumstances where prosecutorial discretion is improperly exercised ([paras 10–12]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed both issues in the context of legal principles. It established that the State had the discretion to charge Sehoole under section 3 of the Act despite the condition of the firearm. The court emphasized that there is no statutory requirement compelling the State to proceed with the more serious charge under section 4. Thus, the High Court’s position that such a charge was warranted was deemed incorrect ([paras 12–14]).

Regarding the ammunition, the court criticized the High Court’s insistence on a ballistic report as the sole means of establishing that the retrieved items constituted ammunition. The court asserted that, in certain factual circumstances, the presence of ammunition in a properly functioning firearm could be inferred without additional evidence, hence unduly constraining the State's case ([paras 19–20]).

The court further clarified that, while each case must be evaluated on its specific facts, allowing the High Court’s ruling to stand would undermine the prosecutorial authority defined under s 179 of the Constitution, which ensures independence in prosecutorial discretion ([para 14]).

## C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the High Court erred on both points of law. It reinstated the original convictions and sentences from the regional court and remitted the matter back to the High Court for a hearing on Sehoole's appeal ([paras 22]). This case underscores the critical boundaries of judicial review over prosecutorial decisions and reaffirms the discretion afforded to the State in determining appropriate charges under the Firearms Control Act.

In essence, the judgment reinforced legal precedents on prosecutorial discretion, clarified the interpretation of possession laws within the Firearms Control Act, and delineated the evidentiary standards pertaining to ammunition possession under South African law.
