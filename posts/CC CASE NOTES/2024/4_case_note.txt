# FIRAC Case Note: Mafisa v Road Accident Fund and Another [2024] ZACC 4

### F – Facts

In the matter of **Mafisa v Road Accident Fund and Another**, the applicant, Mr. Tumelo Mafisa, sustained serious injuries in a motor vehicle accident on 31 January 2016, which rendered him eligible to claim damages against the Road Accident Fund (RAF), a statutory entity under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 ([paras 1–2]). Mr. Mafisa's injuries included a fractured left proximal humerus and other bodily impairments, leading him to lodge a claim for R2,387,568.00 for past and future medical expenses, loss of earnings, and general damages ([paras 6–8]).

After negotiations, the parties reached a settlement agreement where the RAF agreed to pay Mr. Mafisa R1,652,715.70, broken down into loss of earnings and general damages ([paras 9–10]). When the settlement was presented to the High Court, Judge Daniso expressed concerns about the adequacy of the damages related to loss of earnings but ultimately unilaterally amended the agreement, striking out the amount for loss of earnings and awarding only R350,000.00 for general damages ([paras 12–14]). This decision was contested through a series of appeals, with the High Court dismissing Mr. Mafisa's application for leave to appeal ([paras 14–15]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Constitutional Court were:
1. Whether the High Court was empowered to unilaterally amend the settlement agreement concluded by the parties without affording them a hearing ([paras 16–19]).
2. The implications of such judicial action on the parties' right to a fair hearing, equality, and the integrity of settlement agreements as binding contracts ([paras 18–19]).
3. Consideration of the principles surrounding settlement agreements, including whether the High Court exceeded its jurisdiction by acting as a custodian of public funds ([paras 22–24]).

### R – Rules

The court relied on several key legal principles:
- The right to a fair hearing as enshrined in **Section 34 of the Constitution**, which guarantees that every person has the right to have any dispute resolved by application of law in a fair public hearing ([para 30]).
- Judicial discretion to amend settlement agreements requires adherence to established precedents, such as in **Eke v Parsons**, which highlighted that a court's role is not to arbitrarily alter terms agreed upon by parties ([paras 37–40]).
- The principles of public policy relating to contract freedom and the significance of the separation of powers doctrine, upheld in **Barkhuizen v Napier**, dictate that compacts freely entered should be respected and enforced ([para 43]).

### A – Application

In applying these legal principles, the Constitutional Court found that the High Court had exceeded its jurisdiction by unilaterally altering the settlement agreement without properly engaging with the parties ([paras 47–53]). The High Court’s failure to consult the parties before changing terms violated the audi alteram partem principle, which mandates that both sides be heard, especially in decisions that negatively affect them ([para 54]).

The Court emphasized that the High Court was not justified in using external reports that had not been formally admitted into evidence to undermine the agreed-upon terms of the settlement ([paras 54–55]). The justifications used by the High Court to amend the agreement were based on discretion that is misaligned with the established practices and principles governing compromise and settlement.

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court unanimously held that the High Court’s unilateral amendment of the settlement was irregular and improper. The order of the High Court was set aside, and the original settlement agreement was reinstated as a binding order of court. Consequently, the decision reaffirmed the enforceability of settlement agreements in the context of claims against the Road Accident Fund and reinforced the necessity for courts to uphold the principles of fairness, justice, and contractual autonomy in civil proceedings ([paras 56–56]). No costs order was issued against the RAF, recognizing their non-opposition to the appeal ([para 56]).

This ruling has significant implications for future claims against the RAF, clarifying the limits of judicial intervention in settlement agreements and reinforcing the procedural rights of claimants in negotiating terms with state entities.
