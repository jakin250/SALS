Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Premier, Gauteng and Others v Democratic Alliance and Others; All Tshwane Councillors who are Members of the Economic Freedom Fighters and Another v Democratic Alliance and Others; African National Congress v Democratic Alliance and Others [2021] ZACC 34

## F – Facts
The dispute arose from the decision of the Gauteng Premier and the Provincial Executive Council (Executive Council) to dissolve the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipal Council (Municipal Council) due to a prolonged deadlock resulting in the Council's inability to fulfill its executive obligations ([1]–[5]). Following the dissolution on 4 March 2020, the Municipal Council, characterized by significant conflict between the Democratic Alliance (DA) and coalition partners (the African National Congress (ANC) and the Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF)), was left in a state of disarray, lacking a Mayor or integral governing officers ([5]–[6]).

The DA challenged the Executive Council’s dissolution decision, asserting procedural and substantive irregularities, including a failure to address the underlying issues of the Council's dysfunctionality through less drastic interventions as outlined in relevant statutes ([24]–[28]). The High Court ruled in favor of the DA, holding that the dissolution was inappropriate and that the Executive Council had ignored adequate avenues for resolution, thus upholding the principle of legality and maintaining the autonomy of local government ([25]–[30]).

The Premier and EFF subsequently sought leave to appeal the High Court's decision directly to the Constitutional Court under section 167(6)(b) of the Constitution ([49]). The main legal issue revolved around the interpretation and application of section 139(1)(c) of the Constitution, particularly concerning what constitutes “exceptional circumstances” for municipal dissolution.

## I – Issues
The principal legal questions before the Constitutional Court included:
1. Whether the decision of the Premier to dissolve the Municipal Council was lawful and effective under section 139(1)(c) of the Constitution, which requires circumstances of a markedly unusual nature.
2. Whether the High Court's determination that less intrusive measures could have been adopted before dissolution was justified and reflective of the governed parameters for intervention in municipal affairs ([48]).

## R – Rules
The case invoked several key legal provisions:
- **Section 139(1)** of the Constitution allows a provincial executive to intervene when a municipality fails to fulfill its executive obligations, with options that include issuing directives, taking over responsibilities, or dissolving the Municipal Council if exceptional circumstances warrant such action.
- **Section 154(1)** mandates that provincial and national governments support municipalities to enhance their capacity for governance ([55]–[56]).
- The **Municipal Systems Act** provides specific guidelines on the responsibilities of municipalities, including mechanisms for intervention by provincial governments in case of executive failures ([63]–[64]).

Legal precedents such as *Mnquma Local Municipality v Premier Eastern Cape* interpreted the term "exceptional circumstances" as requiring a narrow rather than a broad understanding to ensure respect for local government autonomy ([56]). The principle of legality, underscored by recent rulings mandating that public power must be exercised within legal parameters, also played a critical role in guiding the court's review ([66]).

## A – Application
In its ruling, the Constitutional Court emphasized several critical legal analyses:
1. **Legal Framework**: The Court reaffirmed the need for a stringent application of section 139(1)(c), reiterating that the dissolution of a Municipal Council must be predicated on evidence of exceptional circumstances that render continued governance impossible through more conventional means ([90]–[91]). The lack of regular council meetings, resulting primarily from political walkouts by the ANC and EFF, underscored the Council’s dysfunctionality but did not automatically validate dissolution without meeting constitutional thresholds for intervention.

2. **Assessment of Circumstances**: The Court scrutinized whether the cited failures by the Municipal Council (e.g., water service delivery issues, financial mismanagement) constituted executive obligations in need of dissolution rather than more cooperative solutions ([106]–[127]). The High Court had previously identified that the water crisis should have been subject to targeted intervention rather than leading to outright dissolution, emphasizing co-operative governance ([63]).

3. **Legitimacy of Decision**: The crux of the Court's conclusion hinged on the principle that while the provincial executive must act to ensure service delivery and governance, such actions must be rooted in constitutional and legislative procedures that respect local government autonomy ([216]–[220]). The Premier’s decision to dissolve was found to be premature, lacking the requisite engagement with the Municipal Council and reliance on less drastic measures previously identified in the Systems Act.

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the appeals of the Premier and the EFF, upholding the High Court's ruling that the dissolution of the Municipal Council was unlawful and inappropriate under section 139(1)(c) of the Constitution ([132]). The judgment reinforced the significance of legislative procedure in the realm of local governance, rejecting the unilateral decision of the Premier in favor of a structured approach that emphasizes co-operative governance and respect for municipal autonomy. The Court ordered the MEC for Co-operative Governance and Traditional Affairs to investigate the cause of the deadlock and recommend appropriate action, emphasizing a process-driven solution over immediate dissolution ([131]).

The implications of this ruling underscore a critical balance between provincial intervention and the autonomous governance of municipalities, reinforcing procedural fairness in public administrative action and the need for a collaborative approach to resolving municipal governance crises.
