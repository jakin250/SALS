Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: Municipality of Port Elizabeth v Rhona Smit (Case No: 450/2000)

## F – Facts

The case in question arises from a pedestrian accident involving the respondent, Rhona Smit, who fell into a manhole while stepping onto a concrete slab manhole cover situated on a pavement in Port Elizabeth. The appellant, the Municipality of Port Elizabeth, is responsible for over 200,000 such covers in the city. Upon falling, Smit sustained leg injuries and subsequently filed a claim for damages in the magistrate's court, where she was awarded R9,000. The municipality's appeal against this judgment was dismissed by the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, leading to this further appeal.

The manhole cover in question tilted when Smit stepped on it, primarily due to being displaced from its metal frame, which subsequently created a hazardous condition. The appellant did not dispute its duty to maintain the safety of the manhole covers but contended that it had taken reasonable measures to ensure safety. However, the magistrate found the municipality negligent in failing to secure the cover and deemed it should have affixed hinges to prevent displacement. The municipality's evidence suggested that securing the covers was not feasible due to the financial burden it would impose ([1]–[10]).

Following the grant of leave to appeal, the parties entered a settlement agreement. This agreement provided that Smit would withdraw her opposition to the appeal, allowing the municipality to pursue it at its own expense, while she would waive any rights to the damages awarded in the lower courts if the appeal succeeded. The settlement effectively eliminated any remaining dispute between the parties ([5]–[6]).

## I – Issues

The central legal question in this appeal concerns whether the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) should entertain the municipality's appeal given that there was no longer a dispute between the parties, per the terms of their settlement agreement. Furthermore, the case raises considerations regarding the interpretation of s 21A(1) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, which allows for the dismissal of appeals when the issues raised will have no practical effect ([6]–[7]). 

Additionally, there exists the question of whether there remains a valid legal or constitutional issue to be resolved that justifies the court’s engagement despite the respondents’ withdrawal of opposition ([9]–[11]).

## R – Rules

The applicable legal framework primarily comprises the provisions of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, particularly s 21A(1), which states that appeals may be dismissed when they do not bring about practical effects or when there is no existing dispute between the parties. The precedent set in *Sun Life Assurance Company of Canada v Jervis* [1944] AC 111 (HL) and further discussed in *R v Secretary of State for the Home Department, Ex parte Salem* [1999] 2 WLR 483 (HL) establishes the principle that for an appeal to be valid, a live controversy must exist between the parties ([7]–[9]).

The distinction between public law and private law cases is also noted, with implications for the court's discretion to hear appeals where no direct impact on parties' rights is evident ([9]–[11]).

## A – Application

In assessing whether to hear the appeal, the SCA focused on whether a substantive issue existed that warranted judicial consideration. The court noted that while the municipality had cited a strong interest in disestablishing the precedent set by the lower court regarding safety measures, particularly the suggestion that hinges should be attached to manhole covers, this was not sufficient given that Smit had effectively expressed no interest in the outcome of the appeal. Consequently, her withdrawal of opposition indicated that any decision from the court would not affect her significantly ([8]–[10]).

The judgment described the absence of a *lis* between the parties as a critical factor against entertaining the appeal. The argument advanced by the municipality for setting aside the lower court’s decision was fundamentally undercut by the realization that the ruling had no practical impact on future cases, nor did it represent a definitive legal obligation that would compel similar cases to follow suit ([6]–[11]).

Moreover, the court emphasized the importance of thorough legal argument from both sides in the formulation of judgements to ensure balanced adjudication and the pitfalls of considering an appeal while only one side's arguments are presented ([11]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, underscoring that no issue remained between the parties for adjudication since all disputes had been settled out of court. The decision reaffirmed the provisions of s 21A(1) of the Supreme Court Act, highlighting that without a *lis*, the court had no jurisdiction to entertain the appeal. As a consequence, the court ruling served not only to terminate the specifics of this case but also underscored the necessity for a substantive controversy for the appeal process to be valid.

The implications of this judgement extend beyond the matter at hand, suggesting that local authorities are not unduly burdened by potentially expansive precedents unless specifically demonstrated in future litigation. Thus, the court rejected the municipality's plea to assess its obligation to affix hinges, clarifying that such determinations would depend on future facts ([6]–[12]). 

In conclusion, the appeal was dismissed with costs, affirming the principle that judicial resources should not be expended on issues lacking practical ramifications for the parties involved ([12]).
