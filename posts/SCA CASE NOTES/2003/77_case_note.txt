Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2003

**Case Note: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa**

**Title:** The Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism v Pepper Bay Fishing (Pty) Ltd and The Chief Director: Marine and Coastal Management v Isak Smith

**FIRAC Method Analysis**

---

### F – Facts

The subject appeals arose from the rejection of fishing rights applications under the **Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998** (MLR Act), specifically concerning the right to fish West Coast rock lobster. The appellants were the Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism and the Chief Director of Marine and Coastal Management; the respondents were Pepper Bay Fishing (Pty) Ltd and Izak Smith (hereafter referred to as "Smith") ([1-2]).

Both applicants sought to register for fishing rights following a **General Notice 1771 of 2001**, which established procedural requirements for applications under **s 18 of the MLR Act**. The Chief Director concluded that Pepper Bay's application was defective for failure to pay the required application fee on time, while Smith's application was deemed incomplete for lack of the two required copies ([8-20]).

Pepper Bay deposited a cheque on 10 September 2001, prior to the application deadline of 13 September 2001; however, it was post-dated, leading to the reversal of the deposit by the bank ([13-15]). Smith's application faced delays for submission as he and fellow applicants could not submit required copies due to logistical issues at the submission venue on the deadline date ([17-19]).

Following the rejection of their applications, both parties sought judicial review in the Cape High Court, which initially favorably judged them. However, the appeals to the Supreme Court of Appeal contested whether the Chief Director had discretion to overlook procedural missteps in their applications ([3],[5]).

### I – Issues

The case presents two primary legal questions: 

1. Whether the Chief Director, as a delegate of the Minister, possessed the discretion to condone non-compliance with the procedural requirements set forth in the MLR Act ([2],[22]).

2. The permissible scope of administrative discretion in relation to the principles of procedural fairness as mandated by **s 33 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa** ([37]).

### R – Rules

The governing statute, **s 18 of the MLR Act**, mandates that no commercial fishing may occur without a granted right from the Minister. Compliance with the specified procedural requirements in **General Notice 1771** was critical ([5],[6]). The court examined:

- The explicit provisions delineating application requirements.
- The General Notice’s authority as subordinate legislation, as referenced in the **Interpretation Act 33 of 1957**.

In jurisprudence, particularly drawing from **SA Co-operative Citrus Exchange Ltd v Director General Trade and Industry and Another**, administrative authorities are not permitted to condone breaches of peremptory regulations unless explicitly granted discretion ([31]).

### A – Application

The court's judgment emphasized the importance of adhering strictly to the procedural directives established in the General Notice. Both cases were considered on their merits, revealing:

1. **Pepper Bay's Case**: The Chief Director properly interpreted non-timeous payment, affirming that submission without actual payment is non-compliant. The court ruled that a provisional credit does not equate to 'payment' ([23-24]).

2. **Smith's Case**: Smith argued that the deadline had been communicated erroneously, extending to the next day. However, the Chief Director’s understanding that the late submission did not confer discretion was validated, as the procedural timelines were established firmly through the General Notice ([19-22]).

Furthermore, the Chief Director's interpretation that he lacked discretion to condone deviations was upheld. The judges of the Court a quo erroneously perceived the discretion to exist within **para 20** of the Instructions, asserting that the discretionary powers mentioned could occur post-consideration, not inherently allowing for non-compliance ([28-34]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeals by the Minister and Chief Director, affirming the initial decisions to reject the applications of both Pepper Bay and Smith due to non-compliance with procedural requirements. The court emphasized that the rights conferred by the MLR Act require strict adherence to established procedures, and the absence of a statutory basis for discretion precluded the option to overlook such procedural defects ([42-43]).

The implications of this ruling elucidate the boundaries of administrative discretion in South African environmental law and reiterate the necessity of compliance with statutory and procedural obligations. This decision sets a precedent for future applications under the MLR Act, reinforcing that adherence to procedural norms is fundamental in administrative processes.
