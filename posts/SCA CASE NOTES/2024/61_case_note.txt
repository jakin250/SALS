# Case Note: Nkomo and Others v The State (Case no 130/2022) [2024] ZASCA 61

## FIRAC Case Notes

### F – Facts ([1]–[6])

This appeal arises from the judgment of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, where Nkoma Xolani Batista and three others were convicted of murder, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and malicious damage to property. The accomplices, later acquitted, had initially joined them in a common purpose to commit the crimes, as determined by the trial court ([2]). The trial took place in Palmridge, culminating in convictions and life sentences for all appellants, along with additional prison terms ([3]).

The incidents occurred on 21 June 2012, when Mr. Godknows Nkosiyazi Motloung and Mr. Memory Skhumbuzo Phiri witnessed the murder of Mr. Solomzi Livingston Jafta by the appellants and other accomplices, who were identified as undocumented immigrants from Zimbabwe involved in illegal gold mining ([5]–[6]). Mr. Motloung testified that he recognized the appellants' involvement in the attack on the deceased, identifying Xolani Batista Nkomo as pursuing Jafta while armed with a stone and a reinforcing pipe, and the others participating in the assault by using various weapons ([7]).

The trial court accepted the eyewitness testimonies, despite some inconsistencies, particularly highlighting Motloung's recognition of the appellants due to prior acquaintance, which was positioned as vital to the case ([12]). The full High Court dismissed the appellants' appeal against their conviction while modifying their robbery conviction to theft and subsequently reducing the associated sentence ([14]).

### I – Issues ([14]–[15])

Central to the appeal is the question of whether the appellants were correctly identified as the perpetrators of the violent crimes, given the severe implications of their convictions. The reliability of witness identification, especially given that both primary witnesses were undocumented immigrants with similar backgrounds to the appellants, also arises as a critical issue ([16]). The dissenting opinion raised concerns regarding the inconsistency in the identification process, particularly highlighting issues in witness testimony and identification parade protocols ([14]).

### R – Rules ([16]–[20])

In matters of identification, the court acknowledged the foundational legal principles regarding the burden of proof, emphasizing the presumption of innocence until proven guilty beyond a reasonable doubt ([15]). The court cited relevant case law, including *S v Van Der Meyden* and *S v Jackson*, underscoring that any reasonable doubt should lead to acquittal ([15]). Additionally, the reliability of eyewitness identification is profoundly influenced by circumstances surrounding the identification, as discussed in *S v Mthetwa* and *S v Sauls and Others*, stressing the importance of factors like lighting, proximity to the event, and prior acquaintance with the accused ([17]).

The distinction between recognition and mere identification was also critical, with the court asserting that the former is often deemed more reliable ([18]). Lastly, the court engaged with statutory provisions set out in the Criminal Procedure Act, affirming the validity of convictions based on single witness testimony under certain conditions, provided the evidence is credible ([20]).

### A – Application ([18]–[26])

The court primarily relied on Mr. Motloung's testimony, reasoning that his long-standing familiarity with the appellants bolstered the credibility of his recognition of them as attackers during the fatal incident. Even though inconsistencies existed in Mr. Motloung's testimonies, the court found that those did not suffice to impinge upon the overall reliability of his recognition, especially given the clear visibility conditions during the assault ([19]). 

Moreover, the court addressed procedural concerns raised about the identification parade, arguing that issues related to the missing pages and discrepancies in descriptions could not be introduced at the appeal stage as they were not raised in the lower courts ([21]). The court's evaluation led to the conclusion that there was no substantial basis for doubting the reliability of identification evidence, reaffirming that Motloung's testimony constituted adequate grounds for conviction under the principle of common purpose ([22]).

In terms of sentencing outcomes, the appellants contended that the trial court failed to consider substantial and compelling circumstances that warranted a deviation from mandatory sentencing norms. The court discussed how the trial court has a broad discretion in sentencing, evaluating that it had adequately considered all pertinent factors, including the nature of the crime and the personal circumstances of the appellants ([23]–[25]).

### C – Conclusion ([26]–[26])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal against both the conviction and the sentence upheld by the High Court, affirming the findings of the trial court on the basis of credible eyewitness identification and sufficient evidence of common purpose. The decision reinforced the standard of proof in criminal matters and the significance of recognizing prior familiarity in identification cases. The case underscores the delicate balance of witness reliability and evidentiary standards in preserving the integrity of judicial processes in a South African context.
