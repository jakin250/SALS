### FIRAC Case Note

#### Case Title: KSL v AL (Case no 356/2023) [2024] ZASCA 96 (13 June 2024)

#### F – Facts:
The case arises from divorce proceedings initiated by the appellant, KSL (the husband), against the respondent, AL (the wife), following their marriage on 3 April 1992, which was out of community of property with the accrual system as per the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984 ([2]). After several failed attempts at reconciliation, the marriage was dissolved on 14 March 2019. The issue of their proprietary rights was postponed pending a later determination ([2]).

While the divorce proceedings were ongoing, KSL established the L[...] Children Educational Trust on 19 July 2018 and donated R1,800,000 to support the couple's children. This transaction was unknown to AL until December 2018 ([3]).

KSL proposed a settlement offer to AL on 12 December 2018, suggesting R550,000 as full and final settlement of her accrual claim. AL rejected this offer ([4]). 

In May 2021, KSL sold a property for R5,114,740.75, and AL learned of this sale in June, leading her to file an anti-dissipation application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court ([5]). The High Court, after hearing the application, granted the requested anti-dissipation relief to AL, preserving the net proceeds from the property sale until the accrual claim was resolved ([5]).

Following the judgment, KSL's application for leave to appeal was dismissed, prompting him to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([6]). 

#### I – Issues:
1. Did AL satisfy the requirements for an interim anti-dissipation interdict against KSL?
2. Did the High Court apply the correct legal principles in granting the order?
3. Is an interim interdict granted appealable in the interests of justice? ([1]).

#### R – Rules:
The legal standards for granting an interim interdict in South African law, particularly an anti-dissipation interdict, were outlined in *Knox D'Arcy Ltd v Jamieson* which stipulates the following requirements:
- A prima facie right exist, despite potential doubts.
- An injury must either have occurred or be reasonably feared.
- The balance of convenience should favor the applicant.
- No alternative remedy should be available ([16], [17]).

The specific consideration of intention behind the alleged dissipation was reiterated in *Knox D'Arcy*, emphasising that such a state of mind must generally be demonstrated, barring exceptional circumstances ([17], [18]).

In the context of appeal, the court referred to *City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Afriforum*, establishing that the test for appealability has morphed into a more flexible one based on the interests of justice rather than solely on whether the order has a final effect ([31], [32]).

#### A – Application:
In examining whether AL met the requirement for a prima facie right, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that AL failed to substantiate her claim adequately. The High Court's reliance on KSL's settlement tender as an admission of his accrual was incorrect. The tender was merely an offer to settle, not an acknowledgment of liability ([19], [20]).

In discussing the alleged intention of KSL to dissipate his assets, the court noted that the trust's creation and the annuity investment took place significantly before the property sale and hence could not infer fraudulent intent ([21]). KSL contended that he needed to sell the property to settle existing debts, which aligned with intentions considered reasonable and bona fide.

Moreover, the application for further evidence introduced by KSL was dismissed as it did not meet the criteria for admission; the evidence was available before the High Court’s decision and should have been presented then ([13], [14]).

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal underscored that AL could not change her case’s basis on appeal, failing to represent exceptional circumstances that could allow bypassing the general requirement of demonstrating intention to frustrate her claim ([25], [26]).

#### C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of KSL, concluding that the High Court erred both in its findings related to the prima facie nature of AL’s claim and in its application of the legal principles governing anti-dissipation interdicts. The court emphasized the necessity of demonstrating intent to frustrate claims for such interdicts, which AL lacked ([30], [31]). As a result, KSL's appeal was upheld, the order of the High Court was set aside, and the anti-dissipation application was ultimately dismissed with costs ([34]). 

This decision clarifies the landscape surrounding anti-dissipation interdicts in matrimonial disputes, reinforcing the requirement for the applicant to substantiate claims of intention towards asset dissipation, impacting future applications of similar nature.
