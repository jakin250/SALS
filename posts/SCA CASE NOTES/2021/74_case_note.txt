Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

## FIRAC Case Note: Watson NO v Ngonyama and Another (Case no 453/2020) [2021] ZASCA 74 (9 June 2021)

### F – Facts
The case concerns an appeal against a decision by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg. The participants included Jared Michael Watson (as executor for the late Gavin Watson, the appellant) and Lulama Smuts Ngonyama along with Thunder Cats Investments 92 (Pty) Ltd (the respondents) ([1]). The dispute centered around certain shares in Ntsimbintle Mining (Pty) Ltd, which were a pertinent point of contention due to alleged misrepresentations around Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment (BBBEE) credentials ([2]–[27]).

Originally, Ngonyama, Watson, and Sabelo Macingwane established a company named Nkonjane Economic Prospecting and Investment (Pty) Ltd, poised to invest in a manganese mining opportunity ([5]–[6]). The trio agreed to allocate shares while incorporating empowerment principles by transferring portions to historically disadvantaged individuals. This was structured through Bosasa Youth, which was purported to be a BBBEE-compliant entity, to hold shares in Nkonjane ([7]–[9]).

Over time, conflicts arose among the stakeholders, particularly related to the administrative conduct and the veracity of Watson's assurances regarding the empowerment of shareholders in Bosasa Youth ([12]–[20]). In light of deteriorating relations leading to the liquidation of Nkonjane, Ngonyama argued he was misled into donating shares based on representations made by Watson ([25]–[27]). When Watson’s estate executor failed to restore the shares to Ngonyama's investment vehicle, a legal application ensued to reclaim the donated shares and associated dividends ([27]–[28]).

### I – Issues
The legal questions before the court were:

1. Did Watson misrepresent the nature of Bosasa Youth and its shareholder composition in violation of BBBEE commitments, entitling Ngonyama to recover his share donations?[42]
2. Did the Fluxmans agreement made during Nkonjane's liquidation constitute a binding compromise, extinguishing any claims Ngonyama may have had regarding the recoverability of the donated shares? ([48])

### R – Rules
In adjudicating these issues, the court relied upon several principles:

- **Misrepresentation in Contract Law:** The court referenced Tubane v Machakela for the principle that a person to whom a misrepresentation was made is not obliged to verify its veracity ([44]).
- **Commemoration of Legal Obligations:** The court reaffirmed that parties to a corporate agreement must act in good faith, especially where fiduciary duties and representations about BBBEE alignments are concerned ([44], [45]).
- **Findings on Compromise Agreements:** The distinction between a compromise (transactio) and mere agreements was emphasized, indicating that a genuine dispute should be settled before reaching a compromise ([60]–[61]).

### A – Application
The appeal court systematically evaluated the high court's findings. It noted the high court concluded that Watson reasonably failed to disclose crucial information regarding the true beneficiaries of Bosasa Youth, which was not genuinely representative of BBBEE interests as represented to Ngonyama and Macingwane ([42], [44], [45]).

While the high court found significant grounds for misrepresentation, the appeal brought attention to Ngonyama’s own commitments over time, questioning whether he had truly exercised his right to monitor or enforce BBBEE compliance as initially agreed ([56]–[59]).

Regarding the Fluxmans agreement, while the high court deemed it as ineffective in settling the claims related to the original share donations, the appeal court contended there were existing complexities and non-joined parties that inhibited the execution of any orders envisaged in the original claim ([62]–[66]). The appeal court highlighted how the binding nature of any orders against Bosasa Youth was inherently flawed due to its withdrawal from earlier proceedings while under liquidation ([63]–[65]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, overturning the high court's earlier ruling ([67]). The court found that the claims against Watson for the restitution of shares lacked legal grounds for enforceability, particularly in light of the procedural missteps that included the non-joinder of essential parties and an ambiguous order regarding Bosasa Youth's obligations in liquidation.

The implications of this case extend beyond individual restitution; they underscore the importance of transparency and accountability in corporate governance, especially in initiatives aimed at economic empowerment and transformation in post-apartheid South Africa. This judgment reinforces the caution with which parties must approach agreements regarding empowerment structures to avoid future legal entanglements.
