Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Khumalo v The State (1262/2017) ZASCA 161

## F – Facts
The appellant, Sanele Selby Khumalo, was convicted in the regional court of Piet Retief, Mpumalanga, on three counts: theft of a Norinco 9 mm firearm and six rounds of ammunition, unlawful possession of an unlicenced firearm, and unlawful possession of ammunition. The appellant was sentenced to a total of ten years' imprisonment, with the sentences for counts 1 and 3 running concurrently with count 2. Following his conviction, Khumalo's application for leave to appeal against his conviction was dismissed by the trial court. He subsequently petitioned the Gauteng Division of the High Court for leave to appeal, which was granted only in respect of his sentence. On 2 November 2017, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) granted him special leave to appeal the high court’s refusal of his petition against the conviction ([1]–[3]).

The events leading to the conviction unfolded on 19 March 2014, when the complainant, Mr. Mzuleki Daniel Nkosi, left his firearm in his vehicle while briefly attending to a personal matter. Upon returning, Khumalo had moved the vehicle and subsequently had possession of the firearm, which he was alleged to have stolen. The complainant reported the firearm as stolen to the police. On 21 March 2014, after being informed of Khumalo’s possession of the firearm, the police apprehended him. He eventually produced the firearm during a search, and it was confirmed by the complainant to be his by serial number. Khumalo claimed that he had discovered the firearm while changing a tyre, intending to return it, and suggested that it may have been placed in his taxi by the complainant’s grandson ([5]–[7]).

## I – Issues
The principal issue in this case is whether Khumalo's application for leave to appeal against his conviction should have been granted by the high court. Specifically, the court needed to determine whether there were reasonable prospects of success for Khumalo’s appeal, particularly concerning the charge of unlawful possession of a semi-automatic firearm ([4]).

## R – Rules
The body of law governing the appellant's case includes the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, particularly sections concerning the appeal process (s 309C) and the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which outlines the procedure for obtaining special leave to appeal (s 16(1)(b)). In determining reasonable prospects of success, the court utilized precedent from **S v Smith** ([2011] ZASCA 15; 2012 (1) SACR 567 (SCA)), which articulated that the test requires a dispassionate assessment recognizing that more than mere possibility of success is necessary for granting an appeal ([4]).

## A – Application
In application of the law to the facts, the court analyzed whether the state had proven beyond a reasonable doubt that the firearm in question was semi-automatic, as this was crucial for Khumalo's conviction under the relevant sections of the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000. The charge sheet specifically alleged that Khumalo unlawfully possessed a semi-automatic firearm without a licence. Despite Khumalo’s admission of possession, his counsel contended that the state needed to offer further proof regarding the classification of the firearm, as defined in the Act ([8]).

The court determined that the prosecution's evidence was sufficient to substantiate that the firearm was indeed a semi-automatic. The identification of the firearm by the complainant following its recovery, as articulated in **S v Thembalethu** ([2008] ZASCA 9; [2008] 3 All SA 417 (SCA)), implied that no further evidence was necessary to establish the firearm's classification due to the formal admission by Khumalo. Furthermore, the identification through serial numbers established a direct link between the firearm and the complainant, reinforcing the state's case ([9]–[10]).

Ultimately, the court found that Khumalo had no reasonable prospects of success on appeal concerning his conviction for the unlawful possession of the handgun, leading to the dismissal of his appeal ([11]).

## C – Conclusion
The SCA dismissed Khumalo’s appeal against the high court’s refusal to grant leave to appeal his conviction for unlawful possession of a semi-automatic firearm. The court concluded that the appellant failed to demonstrate reasonable prospects of success, which was necessary to justify an appeal ([12]). The implications of this ruling reinforce the judicial standard that a mere possibility of success is insufficient; a demonstrable and rational basis for appeal is required. Consequently, the ruling affirms the integrity of the trial process and upholds the convictions based on substantial evidence presented at the initial trial.
