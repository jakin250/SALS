Mashigo & Another v The State (20108/2014) [2015] ZASCA 65

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 65

F - Facts

This appeal arises from the decision of the North Gauteng High Court which upheld the conviction and life sentences of both appellants, Johannes Moya Mashigo (first appellant) and Mankge Rakolota (second appellant), for gang rape. The incident occurred on 18 November 2006 when the complainant, Ms P[…] P[…], returned home from her sister's house. She was assaulted by the first appellant, accompanied by two other men, including the second appellant, who she did not know prior to the incident ([1]–[2]).

After physically assaulting the complainant, all three men raped her in turn. The first appellant was apprehended at the scene while the second appellant was caught near the gate ([3]–[5]). The complainant displayed confusion in her testimony regarding the sequence of the assaults, making numerous contradictory statements about who raped her and in what order ([6]–[8]). The evidential basis for the convictions relied heavily on the complainant's identification, which was further complicated by the evidence from a witness, Morris, who provided contradictory accounts ([9]–[11]).

At trial, Dr. Carel Grovè Kleynhans, who examined the complainant, could not provide conclusive evidence of rape and noted discrepancies in the injuries reported ([9]). The appellants both denied the charges but maintained an alibi regarding the circumstances of their presence at the scene ([10]–[12]).

The regional magistrate found both men guilty, but the first appellant only appealed against his sentence of life imprisonment, contending that the circumstances of the rape did not warrant such a harsh sentence. The second appellant appealed against both conviction and sentence ([24]–[26]).

I - Issues

The key legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

  1. Sufficiency of Evidence: Whether the evidence presented by the State was sufficient and reliable enough to satisfy the burden of proof required for conviction ([15]–[16]).
  2. Identification of the Second Appellant: Whether the identification evidence against the second appellant was reliable, particularly given the contradictions in witness testimony ([22]–[23]).
  3. Appropriateness of the Sentence: In relation to the first appellant's life sentence, whether the circumstances constituted substantial and compelling reasons to depart from the prescribed minimum sentence ([26]–[36]).

R - Rules

The applicable legal standards are derived from South African statutory and case law:

  • Burden of Proof: The State bears the onus to prove an accused's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt (S v Jochems 1991 (1) SACR 208 (A) at 211E-G).
  • Credibility of Witnesses: The court must exercise caution in evaluating evidence, especially when a witness provides multiple contradictory accounts (S v V 2000 (1) SACR 453 (SCA)).
  • Minimum Sentences: According to Section 51 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, life imprisonment is a prescribed minimum sentence for certain serious offences, including rape, unless substantial and compelling circumstances are demonstrated ([30]–[34]).

A - Application

Evidence Evaluation

The Supreme Court assessed the reliability of the complainant’s testimony, noting her admitted confusion and contradictions about the sequence of events—and critical details regarding the identity of her assailants. The court emphasized that merely finding the appellants' version improbable does not suffice for conviction; substantial proof must refute their account beyond reasonable doubt ([17]–[19]).

In particular, the court scrutinised witness Morris's testimony, which contradicted the complainant's account and called into question the plausibility of the State's case against the second appellant. The testimony of Victor, who purportedly witnessed the second appellant’s arrest but was never called to testify, further weakened the State's position regarding identification ([22]).

Sentence Analysis

For the first appellant, the court considered the claims that the absence of genital injuries qualified the crime as not being among the most severe types of rape. It determined that, despite these arguments, the circumstances surrounding the assault—including the brutality described—did justify the imposition of life imprisonment ([24]–[30]). The court also rebuffed the idea that the lack of pre-sentencing reports rendered the sentence invalid, as the magistrate had sufficient information to make an informed decision ([28]–[36]).

In contrast, the court found that since the State's case against the second appellant lacked the required evidentiary reliability, both his conviction and sentence could not withstand scrutiny and should be set aside ([23]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the life sentence for the first appellant, determining that the circumstances of the crime warranted such a sentence. However, it set aside the conviction and sentence of the second appellant due to significant evidentiary contradictions and shortcomings in the State's identification evidence. This decision underscored the necessity of reliable evidence in criminal proceedings, particularly in sexual assault cases, where the stakes are high for both victims and defendants. The case reiterates the judiciary’s duty to ensure that convictions are founded on robust evidence, compliant with the standards of proof required in criminal law ([42]).

Ultimately, the court sent a copy of its judgment to the Judge President of the North Gauteng High Court to address the inadequacy of the prior judgment in the appellate process ([41]–[42]).
