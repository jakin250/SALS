Supreme Court of Appeal

LA Group (Pty) Ltd v Stable Brands (Pty) Ltd and Another (Case no 650/2020) [2022] ZASCA 20

The High Court ruled in favor of the first respondent, leading to the appellant's appeal.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 20

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])

This case revolves around the appeal made by LA Group (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) against Stable Brands (Pty) Ltd (the first respondent) concerning the latter's successful counter-application for the cancellation of 46 trade mark registrations held by the appellant. The appellant's registrations, covering 14 classes, primarily involved trade marks that feature the word “POLO” and various visual devices of polo players. In 2018, the appellant initiated an interdictory application against the first respondent for perceived trade mark infringement, which prompted the latter to counter with a motion to have the appellant’s trade marks removed from the register under various provisions of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (the Act) citing lack of distinctiveness and non-use ([paras 1–3]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the first respondent, leading to the appellant's appeal. The grounds for removal included that the marks were non-distinctive, had not been used for a period exceeding five years, and created a likelihood of confusion in the market, especially alongside similar marks owned by Ralph Lauren, with whom the appellant had an unclear coexistence agreement ([paras 4–5]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 6–12])

The principal issues addressed by the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

  1. Whether the High Court erred in granting the removal of the appellant’s trade mark registrations under sections of the Trade Marks Act, specifically in terms of distinctiveness (s 10(2)(a), (b) and (c)), non-use (s 27(1)(a) and (b)), and likelihood of confusion (s 10(13)) ([para 6]).
  2. The interpretation of the agreement between LA Group and Ralph Lauren concerning the shared use of trade marks and its implications for market confusion and deception ([para 10–12]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 7–20])

Relevant statutory provisions include:

  • Section 10 of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, which outlines unregistrable marks, focusing particularly on definitions of distinctiveness, descriptive signs, and customary use.
  • Section 27 pertains to the removal of trade marks based on non-use or lack of genuine intention to utilize the mark.
  • Precedents include discussions surrounding the distinctiveness of trade marks, specifically cases such as Royal County of Berkshire Polo Club Lt v OHIM and Cadbury (Pty) Ltd v Beacon Sweets and Chocolates (Pty) Ltd, which underscore the necessity for trade marks to serve as indicators of origin, free from ambiguity and confusion ([paras 7–8]).

The court also addressed the comparative analysis outlined in the UK Trade Marks Act and noted the lack of consensus on what constitutes 'confusion or deception,' thereby fleshing out the meaning of “manner of use” of the marks ([paras 14–20]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 21–100])

The Supreme Court of Appeal conducted a detailed scrutiny of the High Court's findings, particularly addressing whether the marks in question had become distinctive due to extensive use and consumer recognition over time ([para 21–46]). While the appellant established that it had been using its trade marks for over 40 years with substantial marketing effort and revenue generation, the court noted that such use did not automatically confer distinctiveness if the mark remained descriptive and thus incapable of exclusive association ([paras 47–50]).

The High Court’s error was highlighted in its concluding remarks that the predominant use of the term “POLO” rendered it a mere descriptor for clothing, without acknowledging the acquired distinctiveness through extensive usage by the appellant ([para 48–50]). Ultimately, the appellate court found that the trademarks had not lost their distinctiveness and were not merely descriptive at the time of action. The evidence produced by the appellant demonstrated sufficient efforts to establish the marks as indicators of origin in relation to the goods and services registered ([paras 51–70]).

In addressing the likelihood of confusion arising out of the shared-mark usage with Ralph Lauren, the court reasoned that the agreement itself did not constitute infringement, provided each party’s usage was clearly differentiated within the market ([paras 71–200]). Thus, the failure of the respondent to demonstrate actual consumer confusion effectively disbarred its claims for removal based on confusion ([paras 180–207]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 208–209])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, granting LA Group the desired relief by overturning the High Court's cancellation order on the majority of trade marks contested. It concluded that the marks retained their distinctiveness based on extensive consumer recognition forged through lawful and bona fide use over decades. Consequently, the ruling reaffirmed that merely descriptive marks could gain distinctiveness through substantial use, effectively impacting future trade mark infringement claims and the thresholds for proving usage and consumer error in analogous contexts ([paras 208–209]).

The court ordered the removal of specific trade marks where the appellant conceded their non-use while disallowing costs associated with preparing the appeal record, emphasizing the appellate scrutiny on the management of court documentation in trademark litigation ([para 209]).

This FIRAC case note provides a structured analysis of LA Group (Pty) Ltd v Stable Brands (Pty) Ltd and Another, clarifying the case's legal grounds and implications within South African trade mark law.
