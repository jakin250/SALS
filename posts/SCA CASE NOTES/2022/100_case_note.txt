# Case Note: Cochrane Steel Products Pty Ltd v Jumalu Fencing Pty Ltd (166/2021) [2022] ZASCA 100 (22 June 2022)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

The case arises from an appeal by Cochrane Steel Products (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) against a ruling by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which dismissed its application for an interdict and claims of trade mark infringement under sections 34(1)(a), (c), and 34(2)(b) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (the Act) ([1]). Cochrane and Jumalu Fencing (Pty) Ltd (the Respondent) are both competitors in the manufacturing and installation of perimeter security barriers. The core of the Appellant's argument was based on Jumalu's use of the words “clear view” in advertising its fencing products, which Cochrane claimed infringed upon its registered trade mark “CLEAR VU” ([2]–[3]).

Cochrane registered its trade mark under two classifications: Class 6 (relating to metallic goods) and Class 37 (referring to building and installation services) ([1]). The Appellant contended that Jumalu's use of "clear view" as a descriptor was not bona fide, asserting that it infringed upon its rights related to the trade mark ([2]). Despite these assertions, the High Court concluded that the words in question were commonly used descriptively within the fencing industry and thus would not mislead the average consumer ([6]).

The High Court also noted the necessary disclaimers associated with the Appellant's registration of the mark, explicitly stating that Cochrane had no exclusive rights to the separate words “clear” and “view” as they were deemed descriptive ([12]). Consequently, the inability of the Appellant to demonstrate that Jumalu's actions had led to consumer confusion or constituted passing-off resulted in the dismissal of Cochrane's application ([6],[25]).

### I – Issues

The legal issues at stake revolve around the interpretation of trade mark rights under the Trade Marks Act and the scope of protected trademarks versus descriptive use. Specifically, the Court had to address:

1. **Whether Jumalu's use of the term "clear view" constituted infringement of Cochrane's trademark rights under s 34(1)(a) and (c) of the Act** ([1],[6]).
2. **Whether Jumalu's use of "clear view" was a bona fide descriptive use as envisaged in s 34(2)(b) of the Act** ([10],[19]).
3. **Whether Cochrane sufficiently established a case for passing-off under common law** ([22],[25]).

### R – Rules

The primary legal rules articulated in this matter stem from the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993:

- **Section 34(1)(a) and (c)**: Address the unauthorized use of a registered trade mark and define the conditions under which such use constitutes infringement ([10]).
  
- **Section 34(2)(b)**: Provides a defense against infringement claims by permitting the bona fide descriptive use of terms that describe goods or services. This section suggests that parties are not precluded from using terms that are descriptively customary ([10],[12]).

- **Common Law Principles of Passing-Off**: The law pertaining to passing-off necessitates the demonstration of misrepresentation by one party that damages the goodwill of another, often through confusingly similar branding or representation ([22],[25]).

### A – Application

In applying these legal principles, the Court examined whether Jumalu's use of "clear view" fell within the scope of the permissible descriptive use under the Trade Marks Act. The Court distinguished between what could be considered infringement due to trademark rights and the bona fide use of descriptive terms:

- **Descriptive Use**: The Court found that Jumalu's usage of the words “clear view” alongside its brand “Clamberprufe” was appropriate as it descriptively characterized their fencing products. The words served to inform potential consumers about the nature of the fencing, reinforcing the description of its aesthetic attributes rather than misleading consumers about the product's source unrelated to Cochrane's brand ([17],[18]).

- **Passing-Off**: The Court determined that Cochrane also failed to meet the burden of proof in establishing any misrepresentation or confusion caused by Jumalu’s marketing practices. As such, there was no substantiation for the claim that the public was likely to believe Jumalu's products originated from Cochrane due to the descriptive nature of the terms used ([24],[25]).

The findings emphasized that the descriptive nature of the terms "clear view" negated any potential registration monopoly Cochrane might have sought. The Court reiterated that trademark protection should not inhibit the descriptive language commonly used in specific industries ([16]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court’s decision to dismiss Cochrane's claims, solidifying principles relating to the balance between protecting trademark rights and allowing fair competition through the use of descriptive terms. It concluded that:

1. **Jumalu's use of "clear view" did not infringe on Cochrane’s trade mark**, as it was descriptive and bonafide ([21]).
2. **Cochrane was unable to establish a passing-off claim**, as it could not demonstrate that Jumalu's representations led to public confusion or deception regarding the origins of the products ([25]).

Thus, the appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the critical legal distinction between descriptive use and trademark infringement within competitive trade ([28]).

The balance sought by the Court between trademark protections and descriptors commonplace in trade ensures that the market remains fair and competitive.
