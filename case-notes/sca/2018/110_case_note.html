Matoto v Free State Gambling and Liquor Authority and Others (987/2017) [2018] ZASCA 110

On September 21, 2012, a special liquor license was transferred from Ms. Senti Jane Mathekeng to Mr. Hans Seuntjie Matoto for the establishment known as Senti's Lounge, located at Erf No 132, Vetman Street, Jacobsdal, as authorized under section 116 of the Liquor Act 27 of 1989....

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 110

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts [1–6]

On September 21, 2012, a special liquor license was transferred from Ms. Senti Jane Mathekeng to Mr. Hans Seuntjie Matoto for the establishment known as Senti's Lounge, located at Erf No 132, Vetman Street, Jacobsdal, as authorized under section 116 of the Liquor Act 27 of 1989. On May 3, 2013, Matoto applied to the Free State Gambling and Liquor Authority (the Authority) to permanently transfer this registration to new premises at Erf No 338, Wolf Street, Ratanang, Jacobsdal, pursuant to section 41 of the Free State Gambling and Liquor Act 6 of 2010 (FSGLA) ([1]).

The Authority rejected Matoto’s application on November 13, 2013, prompting him to seek a review in the Free State Division of the High Court ([2]). A settlement was reached, leading to an order that rescinded the initial refusal and directed the Authority to reconsider the application ([2]).

After a reconsideration meeting on October 2, 2014, where both parties presented their cases, the Authority later issued a refusal letter on March 12, 2015, citing proximity to religious institutions and an existing liquor outlet as reasons for refusal ([3]). Following this, Matoto's attorney communicated with the Authority multiple times seeking to understand the refusal and pushing for action on a potential review application ([4]).

On September 29, 2015, Matoto filed a fresh review application aimed at overturning the second refusal. However, the application was submitted after the statutory 180-day timeframe established by section 7(1) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([5]). He sought an extension under section 9 of PAJA, which the high court denied, leading to the current appeal ([5]).

I – Issues [5–10]

The principal issue before the court was whether the high court exercised its discretion appropriately in refusing to extend the 180-day period stipulated by PAJA to lodge a review application. This required consideration of the appellant's explanation for the delay in bringing the application and whether the interests of justice necessitated an extension under section 9 of PAJA ([6]).

Further, the court needed to address whether the high court had properly defined its discretion as 'true' or 'loose' in assessing the application for extension ([7]).

R – Rules [6–8]

The application of PAJA regulates the procedural framework for judicial review of administrative actions. Section 7(1) mandates that any review proceedings must be initiated within 180 days following the notification of administrative action. Section 9 provides for the potential extension of this period, contingent on the interests of justice ([6]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal examined previous cases to clarify the nature of discretion exercised by the lower court, referencing Trencon Construction (Pty) Ltd v Industrial Development Corporation of South Africa Limited and Camps Bay Ratepayers and Residents Association and another v Harrison and another to illuminate how discretion should be interpreted in cases involving judicial review ([7]).

A – Application [9–15]

The high court concluded that Matoto failed to provide a satisfactory explanation for his delay, leading to the refusal of his extension request. The appellant’s arguments hinged on prospects of success in the underlying review application; however, the high court was justified in prioritizing the necessity for timely action over prospective outcomes. This was underscored by the acknowledgment that while prospects of success are significant, they do not solely determine whether an extension should be granted ([12], [14]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized that an extension of time for review proceedings must be strictly construed given the legislative intent behind PAJA, stating, "the interests of justice require an applicant to provide a full and reasonable explanation for the delay" ([8]). Matoto’s failure to act within the established timeframe reflected poorly on his argument.

Furthermore, the court reiterated the importance of an adequate explanation for the delay and observed how significant gaps in the narrative weakened Matoto's application ([10], [12]). The high court's interpretation of the absence of a convincing reason for the delay, coupled with the need to uphold legislative frameworks, was deemed reasonable and duly respectful of the scope of judicial review.

C – Conclusion [15–16]

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the high court's decision, confirming that the refusal to grant the extension was justified. Matoto’s appeal was dismissed, and costs were awarded against him. The ruling underscored judicial comity and the imperative for timeliness in the context of administrative reviews, reaffirming the legislature's intent behind PAJA to promote efficiency in legal proceedings. The decision highlights the critical balance courts must maintain between administrative justice and adherence to procedural timelines.

Overall, the decision serves as a clarion call for litigants to comply rigorously with statutory deadlines, particularly within the administrative realm where adherence to procedures is essential for effective governance ([16]).
