# FIRAC Case Note: Olitzki Property Holdings v. State Tender Board & Premier of the Province of Gauteng (2001)

## F – Facts
This appeal arises from a dispute regarding a tender process managed by the State Tender Board, in which the appellant, Olitzki Property Holdings, claimed damages after its tender for office accommodation was not accepted ([4]–[6]). The provincial government of Gauteng sought office space, and after issuing a tender invitation, awarded the contract to a competing supplier. The appellant alleged that improper conduct interfered with the tender process, breaching its rights under the interim Constitution ([1]–[6]). The damages claimed were based on lost profits from the rentals the appellant contended it would have earned had it been awarded the tender ([4]). The appellant advanced two claims: Claim A asserted a breach of the procurement provisions established under section 187 of the interim Constitution, while Claim B contended a breach of the right to administrative justice as enshrined in section 24 ([5]–[6]).

The first court to hear the matter dismissed both claims, deeming them unsustainable due to the existence of an alternative remedy via administrative review ([6], [8]). The appellant subsequently appealed that decision.

## I – Issues
This case raises significant legal questions, primarily concerning:
1. Whether the procurement provisions of the interim Constitution create a civil right to claim damages for lost profits due to an alleged breach regarding tender administration ([1]).
2. Whether the administrative justice provisions of the same Constitution confer similar rights for damages, especially when alternative remedies like review and interdict are available ([1]).

## R – Rules
The provisions relevant to the court's decision include:
1. **Section 187 of the interim Constitution**, which governs procurement processes and stipulates that tendering must be fair, public, and competitive ([9]–[10]).
2. **Section 24 of the interim Constitution**, pertaining to administrative justice, guarantees the right to lawful and procedurally fair administrative actions ([32]).
3. Prior case law, notably *Fose v Minister of Safety and Security*, which distinguished between ordinary common-law damages and 'constitutional damages,' emphasizing that an appropriate remedy must be effective and take into account community values and interests ([34]).

The court also draws upon principles dictating the existence of delictual liability stemming from a statutory duty, which revolves around the overarching question of whether the breached statutory provision created a legal duty to refrain from causing loss ([11]).

## A – Application
The court systematically applied both statutory frameworks to address the appellant's claims. 

**Claim A:** The court concluded that section 187 imposes duties primarily on the legislatures to establish a fair system of procurement and does not grant individuals a right to claim damages for losses incurred due to non-adherence by officials ([23]–[30]). The court emphasized that the constitutional provisions create duties for the legislature, not individual rights actionable in tort, thus the appellant's claim for lost profits lacked a legal basis.

**Claim B:** The court considered the implications of the administrative justice provisions. It observed that while the appellant had a right to fair processes, claiming lost profits for a failure to have the tender awarded constituted an inappropriate form of relief. The court reasoned that an interdict could have provided the necessary remedy by stopping the tender allocation process, thus precluding the loss of profits ([39]–[42]). The court further noted that the potential for significant financial repercussions on the public purse due to such claims added a layer of public policy considerations against recognizing such damages ([42]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appellant's appeal, affirming the lower court's conclusion that neither the procurement nor the administrative justice provisions of the interim Constitution provided a substantive legal ground for the appellant’s claims for lost profit. As both claims were rightly deemed unsustainable due to the absence of direct legislative enforcement mechanisms and the existence of adequate alternative remedies, the ruling reinforces the constitutional framework delineating the interstices of public procurement and administrative justice ([42]).

The implications of this case highlight the necessity for tenderers to not only seek statutory compliance but also to be cognizant of their available legal remedies in the administrative landscape, reasserting that claims for lost profits require clear legislative foundations to bridge constitutional protections and state actions.
