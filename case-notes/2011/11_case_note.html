FIRAC Case Note: The Citizen 1978 Pty Ltd v McBride [2011] ZACC 11

F – Facts

The case revolves around the impact of an amnesty granted under the Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995, specifically regarding its implications for the law of defamation ([1]–[6]). Robert John McBride, a former operative of the African National Congress (ANC), was convicted in 1988 for a car bombing incident that killed three individuals. He received a death sentence but was later reprieved and released. After applying for amnesty under the Reconciliation Act, the amnesty was granted on April 19, 2001, expunging his record of conviction ([3]).

In light of his candidacy for a senior police post in Ekurhuleni in 2003, The Citizen newspaper (applicants: Citizen, Kevin Keogh, Martin Williams, Andrew Kenny) published articles contesting McBride's suitability, labeling him a "criminal" and "murderer" ([4]–[5]). In response, McBride filed a defamation claim against The Citizen and the journalists for damages of R3.6 million, alongside a demand for a public apology ([5]). The South Gauteng High Court awarded McBride R200,000, which was reduced to R150,000 by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([6]).

I – Issues

The legal questions revolve around the following:

  1. Whether McBride can be labeled a "criminal" or "murderer" post-amnesty in the context of defamation.
  2. The relationship between McBride’s amnesty and the right to freedom of expression, particularly in journalism ([2], [6], [7]).

R – Rules

The court cited several legal principles relevant to defamation:

  • Common Law of Defamation: An individual’s right to dignity and reputation is protected. The defense of fair comment hinges on the expression of opinion based on true, accurately stated facts ([79]).
  • Constitutional Rights: Section 16 of the Constitution guarantees freedom of expression but does not extend to defamatory statements that injure another's dignity ([3], [79]).
  • Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act: Section 20(10) indicates that amnesty results in a conviction being treated as if it never took place, thus raising complex questions about referencing such convictions ([49]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court concluded that while McBride’s conviction is legally expunged, this does not prevent factual recounting of past actions in public discourse. The public interest and the context are crucial when determining whether statements are defamatory ([29], [60]).

  • Murderer Label: The court agreed that McBride’s actions constituted murder at the time of his conviction. However, post-amnesty, calling him a murderer was deemed inaccurate due to the statutory expunging of his conviction ([29], [162], [185]). Yet, it observed that his actions leading to the conviction remain historical facts, thus not obliterated by the amnesty, allowing discussion around the past in context ([29], [60]).
  • Contrition: The Citizen's claim that McBride lacked contrition was ruled false. Testimonies demonstrated McBride's remorse about the bombing and his commitment to reconciliation. This aspect was particularly underscored by the absence of any supportive evidence from The Citizen regarding their assertion of McBride's moral disposition ([190]–[121]).
  • Flirtation with Gun Dealers: Regarding the references to McBride’s alleged dealings with questionable parties in Mozambique, the lack of factual accuracy surrounding his arrest and the failure to mention that the charges were dropped rendered the allegations misleading ([199], [203]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court found in favor of McBride regarding the defamatory claim of him lacking contrition, accepting that such a statement caused significant damage to his reputation ([133]). However, the statement labeling him as a murderer was deemed untrue post-amnesty; thus, that specific claim was not upheld against The Citizen.

The court awarded McBride R50,000 in damages, following the principle that while his past actions can be discussed, the implications of those actions must be contextually accurate and respect his achieved amnesty ([130], [136]). The balance between freedom of the press and protecting individual dignity within the framework of South African law highlights the delicate interplay between these competing rights amidst a historical backdrop of reconciliation.
