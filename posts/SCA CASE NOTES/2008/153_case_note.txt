# FIRAC Case Note: ATM Solutions (Pty) Ltd v Olkru Handelaars CC & Another 
**Neutral citation:** [2008] ZASCA 153  
**Case No:** 739/07  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Date Delivered:** 27 November 2008  
**Coram:** Streicher, Cloete, Lewis, Jafta, and Ponnan JJA  

## F – Facts
The matter at hand revolves around an appeal by ATM Solutions (Pty) Ltd (hereafter referred to as "ATM Solutions") against a decision made by the Cape High Court (Griesel J), which refused ATM Solutions' application for a mandament van spolie. The dispute originated from a contractual relationship between ATM Solutions and Olkru Handelaars CC (hereafter "Olkru"), which operates a convenience store branded Kwikspar Breedevallei in Worcester ([1]).

In February 2007, ATM Solutions and Olkru entered into a "Site Location Agreement." Under this contract, Olkru was to provide space for an ATM supplied by ATM Solutions. The ATM was intended for use by customers of Kwikspar and was physically installed and affixed within the store premises ([3]–[4]). 

On 17 September 2007, Olkru disconnected the ATM's electricity supply without consent and moved the device to a storeroom while simultaneously installing an ATM belonging to the second respondent, Absa Bank Ltd ([5]). ATM Solutions claimed that both Olkru and Absa were guilty of spoliation as Olkru had interfered with its installed ATM, which ATM Solutions contended was a violation of its rights under the contractual agreement ([1]).

The High Court dismissed the application for a spoliation order on the grounds that ATM Solutions' rights were purely contractual and not deserving of protection under spoliation law ([2]). 

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue in this appeal is whether ATM Solutions is entitled to a mandament van spolie to restore its ATM following its unauthorized removal by Olkru, considering that the rights upon which ATM Solutions relied were of a contractual nature ([2]–[4]). Specifically, the court needed to determine:

1. If ATM Solutions possessed any genuine rights beyond mere contractual rights that would entitle it to a spoliation order.
2. Whether a mandament van spolie can be employed to enforce rights based solely on a contractual relationship, in light of precedents that construe the mandament as protective of use rights to property or possession ([9]).

## R – Rules
The rules relevant to the case center around the doctrine of the mandament van spolie, a legal remedy aimed at restoring possession to a party dispossessed of property without due legal process. The doctrine asserts that protection is available not for personal contractual rights but specifically for rights that entail the actual possession or use of property (gebruiksregte) ([2], [9]). 

Key cases that provided precedence for this ruling include:

- **Telkom SA Ltd v Xsinet (Pty) Ltd** 2003 (5) SA 309 (SCA), which reinforced the limitation of spoliation orders to instances of actual possession or use rights ([2]).
- **First Rand Ltd t/a Rand Merchant Bank v Scholtz NO** [2007] 1 All SA 436 (SCA); 2008 (2) SA 503, which confirmed that a spoliation order cannot serve as a catch-all remedy for contractual disputes but is reserved for cases involving possession of property or established use rights ([2], [9]).

## A – Application
The court analyzed the nature of ATM Solutions' rights in relation to the spoliation order sought. It noted that, although ATM Solutions claimed a right to have its ATM remain installed and operational, these claims were grounded in a contractual relationship with Olkru ([3]–[6]). The court emphasized that Olkru maintained physical possession of both the ATM and the space, which was essential to determine the underlying rights related to spoliation ([7]–[8]).

Moreover, ATM Solutions argued for "quasi-possession," claiming that its contractual rights and physical installation constituted a form of possession. However, the court clarified that quasi-possession, as recognized in prior rulings, specifically concerns the conditions under which property rights are exercised—primarily, instances involving the use of property that is under actual control ([9]–[14]). The court found that the nature of ATM Solutions' claim was fundamentally contractual and thus did not warrant the application of spoliation law. 

The court also rejected the assertion that recent case law distinguishing between contractual and property rights was inapplicable here, affirming that a request for reinstallation and reconnection of the ATM was essentially a claim for specific performance, which fell outside the remit of spoliation orders ([10], [15]). 

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the High Court, concluding that ATM Solutions' request for a mandament van spolie, predicated solely on contractual rights without actual possession, was not valid. The court reaffirmed that the mandament is not designed to enforce mere contractual rights and dismissed the appeal with costs ([15]). The ruling emphasizes critical limits on the applicability of spoliation orders in South African property law, thereby clarifying the boundaries between contractual relationships and property rights. The judgment has significant implications for future cases involving spoliation, reinforcing the need for parties to establish genuine possessory rights before seeking such remedies.
