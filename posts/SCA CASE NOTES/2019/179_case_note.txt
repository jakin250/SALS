Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Donald Khobane v The State (1255/2017) [2019] ZASCA 179

## F - Facts

The case at hand concerns the appellant, Donald Khobane, who was convicted of theft in the Alexandra Regional Court for stealing R3 million from Nedbank during his employment as an ATM consultant. Following his conviction, Khobane was sentenced to 15 years’ imprisonment under section 51(2) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (the CLAA). The appellant was accused of being absent from work on the day his colleagues discovered the shortfall in the ATMs, which he later confessed to stealing under the influence of a spiritual healer purportedly promising financial liberation ([1]–[4]). 

Khobane's initial appeal against the sentence was summarily dismissed, prompting him to seek further appeal options through various legal channels, including a petition to the High Court and ultimately a special leave application to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([6]–[8]). His appeal was focused solely on the sentence, arguing that he was not warned about the minimum sentence provisions in the charge sheet, and that the trial court had failed to consider substantial and compelling circumstances justifying a deviation from the minimum sentence ([12]–[13]). 

In his submissions to the SCA, Khobane contended that the absence of a reference to section 51(2) in the charge sheet infringed upon his right to a fair trial and resulted in prejudice ([16]).

## I - Issues

The core issues before the SCA revolved around:
1. Whether the failure to reference the minimum sentencing legislation (s 51(2) of the CLAA) in the charge sheet constituted a breach of Khobane’s right to a fair trial and led to prejudicial effects on his defense ([9]–[10]).
2. Whether special circumstances existed to justify a further appeal against the sentence in accordance with the standard set in **S v Van Wyk** [2015] (1) SACR 584 (SCA) ([10]).

## R - Rules

The SCA referred to established principles from **S v Van Wyk** on special leave to appeal, stipulating that an applicant must demonstrate:
- Reasonable prospects of success.
- Existence of special circumstances warranting the court's attention, such as substantial points of law or greater importance of the matter ([10]).

Additionally, the court referenced **S v Kolea** [2013] (1) SACR 409 (SCA), emphasizing the accused’s right, under section 35(3)(a) of the Constitution, to be adequately informed of the charges against him to ensure a fair trial. A failure to mention the minimum sentence provisions at the commencement of the proceedings could potentially violate this right, though the specifics of each case need to be assessed carefully ([14], [19]–[20]).

## A - Application

The SCA scrutinized whether Khobane had demonstrated any substantial prejudice stemming from the alleged failure to reference the minimum sentence provisions. Initially, although the facts surrounding Khobane's employment and his eventual confession presented significant issues, the SCA noted his legal representation during trial had addressed the nature of the charges effectively. This raised questions about whether the absence of a formal warning regarding the minimum sentence genuinely impaired his ability to mount a proper defense ([5], [20]–[21]).

The SCA examined the trial court’s handling of the sentence hearing, where Khobane's attorney did engage the magistrate on the question of substantial and compelling circumstances for a deviation from the mandatory 15-year sentence. The record showed that the trial did not unfold in a manner that left Khobane blindsided regarding the potential legal ramifications of his conviction ([18], [21]).

Consequently, the SCA found that there was inadequate evidence to support Khobane's claims of unfair trial or resulting prejudice, undermining his argument that the mere omission of the minimum sentencing references in the charge sheet constituted a procedural irregularity warranting appeal ([12], [15], [22]).

## C - Conclusion

In its determination, the SCA held that there were no special circumstances justifying Khobane’s appeal. The court asserted that the appellant did not demonstrate any procedural prejudice arising from the failure to cite the minimum sentencing provision in the charge sheet. Consequently, the appeal was struck off the roll ([22]).

The implications of this ruling are significant as they reaffirm the courts' position on the threshold for 'special circumstances' necessary to secure a further appeal and emphasize the centrality of effective legal representation within the trial process. It underlines the requirement that claims of procedural inadequacy must be substantiated with clear evidence of an actual impact on the accused's right to a fair trial, aligning with constitutional guarantees ([9]–[10], [19]).
