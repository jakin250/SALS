Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: Wilhelm Ignaz Erich Geuking v. President of the Republic of South Africa

**CCT 35/02**

**Heard on:** 21 November 2002  
**Decided on:** 12 December 2002  

### F – Facts 
The appellant, Wilhelm Ignaz Erich Geuking, was convicted of fraud and arson in the Federal Republic of Germany (FRG) in December 1990. He fled to South Africa and acquired South African citizenship in 1995 after giving up his German citizenship. In a verbal note dated 25 November 1996, the German embassy requested South Africa to extradite Geuking to serve a sentence and face additional charges for fraud. 

This request was processed under the South African Extradition Act of 1962, particularly section 3(2), which allows extradition with the President's consent even in the absence of a treaty. On 30 May 1997, the President consented to extradition without knowledge of Geuking's change in citizenship status. Subsequently, the Minister of Justice notified the Magistrate of the extradition request, leading to Geuking’s arrest on 20 April 1998.

On 13 May 1998, Geuking filed an application in the Cape High Court, arguing the invalidity of the President's consent and the constitutionality of sections 10(2) and 3(2) of the Act. The High Court dismissed his application on 11 June 2001, and he sought further redress in the Constitutional Court ([paras 1-12]).

### I – Issues 
The primary legal questions included:
1. Whether section 3(2) empowered the President to consent to extradition despite Geuking's status as a South African citizen ([paras 19-20]).
2. Whether the consent granted by the President was constitutionally valid, especially given misunderstanding regarding Geuking’s citizenship ([paras 25-28]).
3. The constitutionality of section 10(2), which mandates that magistrates accept a foreign certificate as conclusive proof for extradition ([paras 31-35]).

### R – Rules 
1. **Extradition Act of 1962**: Sections 3(2) and 10(2) govern the consent for extradition and the evidentiary burdens in such lawful proceedings. Section 3(2) allows the President to consent to extradition, while section 10(2) instructs magistrates to accept foreign certificates as proof of sufficient grounds for prosecution in the requesting jurisdiction ([paras 6-8, 16-17]).
2. **Constitutional Principles**: The case revolves around constitutional provisions safeguarding citizens' rights, particularly sections 21(3), 34, 35(3), and 12(1) concerning fair trials, access to courts, and protection from arbitrary detention ([paras 26, 43-48]).
3. **Judicial Precedent**: Previous cases like *Harksen v President of the Republic of South Africa* elucidate the government’s obligations concerning extradition under constitutional scrutiny ([para 12]).

### A – Application 
The court carefully assessed the power of the President under section 3(2). It emphasized that consent was a policy decision regarding foreign relations that must be respected unless it violated constitutional principles. It was found that Geuking’s citizenship status was relevant but did not invalidate the President’s decision because South African policy underlined the need to prosecute citizens for crimes committed abroad.

Regarding section 10(2), the Court found that despite the appellant's claims of undermining the right to a fair trial and the independence of the judiciary, the provisions were constitutional. The magistrate’s role in extradition is limited to determining the extraditability of the offender rather than adjudicating their guilt. The use of a foreign certificate did not breach the judicial independence as the proceedings were deemed sui generis, tailored specifically for issues of extradition ([paras 41-50]).

### C – Conclusion 
The Constitutional Court dismissed Geuking's appeal, affirming the constitutionality of both the President's consent and section 10(2). The decision reinforced the notion that South Africa must maintain its obligations to international law while balancing domestic citizens' rights. The court underscored the importance of reciprocal legal assistance in combating international crime, despite any constitutional concerns raised by the appellant.

The specific order was as follows: the appeal was dismissed, and there was no order made regarding costs, acknowledging that the issues raised were of public interest and not frivolous ([paras 51-52]). 

This case is significant in defining the limits and operational processes of extradition in South Africa, particularly concerning citizens’ rights and the delicate balance between justice and international cooperation.
