Phumlani Nicholas Khathide v The State (840/2020) [2022] ZASCA 17

The case under review is an appeal from the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa concerning Phumlani Nicholas Khathide (the appellant) and The State (the respondent).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 17

F – Facts

The case under review is an appeal from the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa concerning Phumlani Nicholas Khathide (the appellant) and The State (the respondent). On 24 November 2014, the appellant and his co-accused, Sibusiso Ndaba, pleaded guilty to robbery with aggravating circumstances in the Umzimkulu Regional Court. The appellant was sentenced to 15 years' imprisonment, while Ndaba received a 17-year sentence.

In October 2018, Khathide and Ndaba filed applications for leave to appeal their sentences. The regional court denied their applications. The two men then appealed to the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, which, on 19 February 2019, allowed Ndaba to appeal, but denied Khathide's request.

On 9 July 2020, Khathide sought special leave to appeal this refusal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which was granted on 9 September 2020. The pivotal issue emerged: whether the high court correctly denied Khathide leave to appeal against his sentence ([paras 1–3]).

I – Issues

The main legal questions presented to the court for determination were:

  1. Did the high court err in denying the appellant leave to appeal against his sentence?
  2. Did the trial court misdirect itself by considering factors not presented in the appellant's statement of plea?

These issues necessitated an examination of the procedural fairness in the sentencing process and the appropriateness of the trial court's reliance on aggravating factors in rendering its decision ([paras 3–4]).

R – Rules

The legal framework for determining leave to appeal is articulated in Section 17(1) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013. This section states that leave to appeal can only be granted if the presiding judge forms the opinion that:

  • (a)(i) the appeal would have a reasonable prospect of success; or
  • (ii) some compelling reason exists for the appeal to be heard.

Additionally, the judgment must not fall under the ambit of Section 16(2)(a) and, if it does not resolve all issues, the appeal must contribute to a just and prompt resolution between the parties ([para 4]).

Precedents cited include S v S ([1988] 1 SA 120 (A)) where the court considered whether previous convictions, which had not yet yielded a retributive effect, could be used in aggravation of a subsequent sentence. Another significant authority is S v Witbooi ([1978] 3 SA 590 (TPD)), which elaborated on the principles governing plea explanations corresponding with the state's case ([para 9–11]).

A – Application

In reviewing the appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized whether the high court’s refusal to grant Khathide leave to appeal was justified. The court highlighted that both Khathide and Ndaba were similarly situated, sharing identical procedural paths and circumstances regarding their earlier convictions for theft and robbery ([paras 5, 6]).

Khathide's appeal was rooted in two main claims: that the trial magistrate incorrectly accounted for his prior convictions not yet experienced in a punitive capacity, and that the trial court improperly considered an oral statement by the prosecutor regarding alleged gunfire exchanges with police, which was not mentioned in Khathide's plea ([paras 7–18]).

Firstly, with respect to the prior convictions, the Supreme Court noted that since Khathide and Ndaba were both on bail for their earlier convictions when the robbery occurred, the magistrate was justified in considering their prior criminal history to assess character, as guided by S v S. Therefore, the court found insufficient grounds to grant an appeal based on this point ([para 10]).

However, on the second ground concerning the oral remarks by the prosecutor, the court explicated that the magistrate’s reliance on the prosecutor's statement during sentencing — which suggested an exchange of gunfire that was absent from Khathide’s plea explanation — constituted a critical misdirection. The prosecutor’s comments, having no evidential support prior to conviction, should not have been considered as they contradicted the established facts of the case provided in the plea ([para 19]).

This misdirection was deemed material enough to undermine the integrity of the sentencing process, establishing reasonable prospects for success on appeal ([para 20]).

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the high court's refusal to grant Khathide leave to appeal was improper. The appeal was thus upheld, resulting in the earlier order set aside and a new directive alleging that the petition for leave to appeal against sentence should be granted. This decision underscores the importance of adherence to procedural fairness in sentencing practices, specifically regarding the integrity of evidence considered during such proceedings. It also highlights the potential implications for similar cases where the reliance on unsubstantiated claims can lead to substantial miscarriages of justice ([paras 1, 21]).

The judgment serves as a reminder of the essentiality of ensuring that all mitigating factors and procedural aspects are duly considered to uphold the principles of justice in the sentencing phase of criminal proceedings.
